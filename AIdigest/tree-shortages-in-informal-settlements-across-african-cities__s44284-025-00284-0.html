Tree shortages in informal settlements across African cities

Xihong Lian · Wei Liu · Zejin Liu · Limin Jiao · Charlie M. Shackleton · Bingjie Li · Jing Zhong · Rui Yang · Mengjing Fu · Haoran Zhang · Neema Simon Sumari · Yaolin Liu Published 2025-08-04 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00284-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

By mapping 53 million individual trees across 54 African cities with deep-learning tree detection on satellite imagery, the study shows that the most informal neighborhoods suffer the largest shortage of green infrastructure per capita along a gradient of urban informality. Tree cover declines as informality, building density and population density rise, so the residents most exposed to environmental stress have the least access to trees. Under continued urban expansion, 28 of the cities are projected to lose tree cover beyond their current boundaries by 2050, making tree retention an urgent, equity-critical component of informal-settlement upgrading.

Why It Matters

  • Makes green inequity visible at the individual-tree scale. Instead of coarse canopy percentages, the work resolves 53 million single trees across 54 cities, exposing exactly where the green deficit concentrates inside informal neighborhoods.
  • Links informality directly to environmental injustice. Ordering neighborhoods along a gradient of urban informality shows the poorest, densest, most building-packed areas are also the most tree-deprived — a double burden for the most vulnerable residents.
  • Turns the deficit into a forward-looking warning. Coupling tree maps with future land-cover and population projections, it forecasts that 28 cities will lose tree cover beyond present boundaries by 2050, framing tree retention as a planning decision, not an afterthought.
  • Speaks to a region under-represented in green-space science. African cities are urbanizing fastest yet are rarely studied at this granularity; the fine-grained data give planners concrete tree-retention targets for upgrading and expansion.

Core Methods

  • Individual-tree detection: the HR-SFANet deep-learning model generates a confidence map from high-resolution Google imagery to locate individual trees, instead of classifying canopy fraction at the pixel level.
  • Tree-crown segmentation: a segmentation-curve module automatically delineates tree crowns from the confidence maps and predicted tree locations, converting points into mapped canopy (Extended Data Fig. 1 flowchart).
  • Informality gradient analysis: neighborhoods are ordered along a gradient of urban informality and tree metrics (cover, per-capita green infrastructure) are related to building density, population density and informality.
  • Per-capita green-infrastructure accounting: tree cover is normalized by population/built area to expose the shortage per capita in the most informal neighborhoods.
  • 2050 scenario projection: four assumptions — external expansion, building densification, rising population density and neighborhood upgrading (Extended Data Fig. 2) — are combined with future land-cover and population data to project tree-cover loss for each city.
  • Open, reproducible stack: imagery download via GeoPandas, rasterio and PIL; model and segmentation executable released publicly.

① Turn the informality–greenness gradient into an explainable accessibility deficit

Extending this paperThe paper establishes that tree cover declines with informality but treats the relationship largely descriptively. A direct extension: feed the 54-city, individual-tree dataset into an explainable ML model (e.g. MGWR or SHAP-attributed gradient boosting) that quantifies how much each driver — building density, population density, BSA informality, climate zone — contributes to the per-capita green deficit, and whether that attribution is spatially non-stationary across cities. This converts the qualitative gradient into a transferable, interpretable deficit law.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my explainable-spatial-prediction line: it gives me a Global-South testbed for attributing environmental inequity, and the per-capita green-infrastructure metric maps cleanly onto a 2SFCA-style green-space accessibility score I can fold into my health-geography work.

② Couple individual-tree maps with a 2SFCA green-space accessibility model

Extending this paperThe paper reports green infrastructure per capita within neighborhoods but does not model access — how far residents must travel to reach usable tree canopy or parks. Extending it: combine the individual-tree layer with the Million Neighborhoods street network to compute a walk-network green-space accessibility surface, revealing informal blocks that are tree-poor and access-poor versus those merely sparse. This adds a behaviorally meaningful access dimension to the supply-side canopy count.

Added value to my researchIt lets me transplant my R2SFCA service-area delineation from healthcare to green-space-as-health-resource, and to test distance-decay assumptions in dense, irregular informal street networks where Euclidean buffers fail.

③ Link the tree deficit to heat and health outcomes

Extending this paperThe paper stops at mapping the deficit; it does not quantify the health or thermal cost of missing trees in informal settlements. Extending it: overlay the individual-tree maps with land-surface-temperature and (where available) morbidity/mortality data to estimate the cooling and health benefit forgone in the most tree-deprived informal blocks — turning a green-equity finding into an environmental-health argument with concrete avoidable burden.

Added value to my researchThis connects directly to my urban-health and healthy-cities themes: the resulting heat–health exposure surface is a natural input for spatiotemporal KDE and SIR-style exposure modeling, and for prioritizing greening where health returns are highest.

④ Replace static 2050 assumptions with a physics-informed graph forecast of canopy loss

Extending this paperThe 2050 projection rests on four fixed assumptions (external expansion, densification, population growth, upgrading). Extending it: cast each city as a spatial graph of blocks and learn a physics-informed graph operator that propagates expansion and densification dynamics, with conservation/scaling priors (e.g. tree loss bounded by impervious-area gain) baked in, so canopy-loss forecasts extrapolate across cities rather than being recomputed per scenario.

Added value to my researchThis is a concrete application of my physics-informed graph-learning agenda to a real environmental-forecasting problem, and the 54-city panel is an ideal transfer-learning benchmark for a reusable GeoAI canopy-change solver.

⑤ Fine-tune a geospatial foundation model for individual-tree mapping at scale

Extending this paperHR-SFANet is a bespoke detector trained for this study; scaling it to all of Africa (or globally) and across imagery sources is costly and brittle. Extending it: fine-tune a geospatial foundation model on the released 40-city tree dataset as labels, enabling few-shot transfer to new cities and sensors, and benchmark against the 3-30-300 canopy rule so the deficit is comparable to global cities, not just within Africa.
