State media control influences large language models

Hannah Waight · Eddie Yang · Yin Yuan · Solomon Messing · Margaret E. Roberts · Brandon M. Stewart · Joshua A. Tucker Published 2026-05-13 DOI 10.1038/s41586-026-10506-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across six studies, the authors show that government control of media already shapes LLM outputs through the training data pipeline. LLMs queried in the language of a country with lower media freedom return systematically more pro-government responses than the same queries in higher-freedom languages. A controlled pretraining experiment on Chinese state-coordinated media confirms a plausible causal mechanism, and cross-language audits of commercial models demonstrate that this effect extends beyond open-weight models — creating strong strategic incentives for states to leverage media control to influence AI at scale.

★

Why It Matters

  • First systematic, cross-national evidence that state media control leaks into LLMs. Prior work documented LLM persuasive potential; this paper is the first to trace the upstream influence pathway — from state-scripted news, through web-scale training corpora, to model outputs across languages.
  • Establishes a causal mechanism, not just correlation. By combining a cross-national audit with a controlled pretraining experiment on an open-weight model, the authors triangulate how state-curated text propagates pro-government valence into model weights.
  • Implicates commercial models already in wide deployment. Cross-language audits reveal that GPT-4o, DeepSeek-R1, and similar commercial models respond more favourably to China’s institutions when queried in Chinese than in English — without any targeted fine-tuning.
  • Raises urgent AI governance implications. States have already shaped LLM training data in measurable, transferable ways, calling for transparency requirements around training data provenance and multilingual auditing standards for deployed models.
  • Ships as a living, replicable benchmark. An open replication archive (Harvard Dataverse DVN/NECR2K) and a live replications site (state-media-influence-llm.github.io) extend the audit to the latest models as they are released.

Core Methods

  • Cross-national LLM audit (Studies 1 & 6): query multiple commercial LLMs with political prompts in the dominant language of each country; measure response valence toward that country’s government; correlate with RSF media freedom scores across language-exclusive countries. Study 6 replicates with three reference languages (English, Spanish, Chinese) for robustness.
  • Training-data overlap detection (Study 1): compute 5-word cosine similarity (≥ 0.25 threshold) between 189,486,611 Chinese-language CulturaX documents and state-controlled media documents; calculate the proportion of CulturaX matched per source type (state-run media, scripted news, Xuexi Qiangguo).
  • Domain provenance analysis (Extended Data Fig. 2): extract URL domains from CulturaX documents (excluding OSCAR-2019 and OSCAR-21.09 which lack URL metadata); compare rates of gov.cn / chinacourt.org origins vs. Chinese Wikipedia domains.
  • Memorization audit (Study 2): probe LLMs with verbatim phrase completions drawn from Chinese state-coordinated media as a direct test of training-data inclusion and memorization.
  • Controlled pretraining experiment (Study 3): apply additional pretraining on Chinese state-coordinated media to an open-weight LLM; measure change in response valence vs. the base model to establish a plausible causal pathway from state-media inclusion to model output.
  • Cross-language commercial model audit (Studies 4 & 5): administer the same political prompts in Chinese and English to GPT-4o, DeepSeek-R1, and Claude Sonnet; evaluate relative favourability via human RA labels (3 per pair, majority vote) and LLM-as-judge with design-based supervised learning (DSL) debiasing against gold-standard RA labels.

① Spatialize the audit: map LLM valence bias onto geographic media-freedom gradients

Extending this paperThe paper’s cross-national audit treats countries as discrete units ranked by RSF score, but media freedom is spatially heterogeneous even within countries — border regions, diaspora communities, and multilingual cities experience different information environments. A direct extension would geolocate prompt-response pairs by linking query languages to subnational regions or diaspora populations, overlay RSF scores with satellite-derived internet penetration and mobile-network coverage, and ask whether the LLM valence gradient is also spatial: do users in peri-urban or border areas with mixed-language exposure encounter attenuated or amplified bias? This converts the paper’s national-level audit into a fine-grained geopolitical risk surface.

The RSF + connectivity overlay is the same multi-source data fusion logic I apply to mobile-phone records and remote sensing — now applied to an information-access problem with real political consequences.

② Replace the LLM-as-judge with an explainable, calibrated evaluator with uncertainty quantification

Extending this paperThe paper uses LLM-as-judge with DSL debiasing to compare response favourability — a pragmatic but opaque pipeline. A direct extension: replace the black-box judge with an explainable scoring model that (a) identifies which specific phrases or framing devices drive a “pro-government” rating, (b) quantifies uncertainty via semantic entropy, and (c) produces calibrated confidence intervals per prompt rather than pooled regression coefficients. This would make the paper’s audit infrastructure interpretable to policymakers and more robust to adversarial prompt reformulations by state actors seeking to evade detection.

③ Extend the pretraining experiment to a multi-country, multi-volume dose-response design

Extending this paperStudy 3 demonstrates a plausible causal effect of state-media pretraining using one open-weight model and one country (China). The critical extension is a dose-response experiment across multiple countries (Russia, Iran, Turkey, Hungary — varying RSF scores) and multiple pretraining volumes (e.g., 0%, 5%, 10%, 25% of the corpus replaced by state media). Fitting a dose-response curve would quantify how much state-media saturation is needed to produce a measurable valence shift, converting the paper’s existence proof into a quantitative risk model: given the fraction of a language’s web content that is state-controlled, predict the expected LLM bias at deployment.

I could apply the same regression discontinuity or instrumental-variable toolkit I use for spatial accessibility studies to the media-saturation → LLM-bias relationship, testing whether the RSF free/not-free threshold is also a discontinuity in model output valence — a genuinely cross-domain methodological transfer.

④ Detect state-media fingerprints in training corpora using hidden behavioural signals beyond surface similarity

Extending this paperThe paper uses 5-word cosine similarity to identify state-media documents in CulturaX — a surface-level text matching approach that is vulnerable to paraphrasing or machine translation laundering. A more sensitive extension: exploit the finding that language models transmit behavioural traits through hidden signals in data to look for stylometric fingerprints of state-coordinated media (sentence-level rhythm, formulaic phrasing patterns, citation and attribution conventions) that survive paraphrasing. This would make the paper’s detection pipeline robust to adversarial content laundering — a realistic near-term escalation by state actors.

⑤ Close the causal chain: measure downstream political persuasion on real users exposed to biased LLM outputs

Extending this paperThe paper establishes that LLM outputs are biased by state-media training data, and cites existing work on LLM persuasive potential, but stops short of connecting the two in a single study. The natural extension is a field experiment: expose participants in multilingual countries to LLM-generated political summaries (in their native language vs. English), measure attitude change toward the country’s government, and estimate the downstream persuasion effect of the combined training-data bias and language-channel effect. This would complete the causal chain from state media → training data → LLM output → voter attitude, documented only in separate literatures.
