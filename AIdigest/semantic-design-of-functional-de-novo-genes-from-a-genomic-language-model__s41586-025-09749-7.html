Semantic design of functional de novo genes from a genomic language model

Aditi T. Merchant · Samuel H. King · Eric Nguyen · Brian L. Hie Published 2025-11-19 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09749-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The genomic language model Evo can perform function-guided sequence design by leveraging the genomic context of prokaryotic operons — a strategy the authors call "semantic design." Providing a DNA prompt encoding a known functional gene steers Evo to autocomplete novel, functionally related yet sequence-diverse genes, including de novo anti-CRISPR proteins and toxin–antitoxin pairs with no detectable similarity to natural proteins. The approach achieves robust experimental activity (up to 100% cell-growth rescue) without structural priors, evolutionary conservation, or task-specific fine-tuning, and scales to SynGenome — a publicly available database of over 120 billion AI-generated base pairs spanning 9,000 functional terms.

★

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the sequence-similarity barrier in protein design. Two of five validated anti-CRISPR candidates (EvoAcr1, EvoAcr2) show no significant sequence or structural similarity to any known protein, demonstrating that generative genomics can access entirely unexplored areas of functional sequence space.
  • Validates an elegant in-context design principle. By treating prokaryotic operons as the biological analogue of distributional word semantics ("you shall know a gene by the company it keeps"), the paper formalizes a principled, prompt-based design strategy requiring no task-specific model fine-tuning.
  • Delivers a large-scale open resource. SynGenome (evodesign.org/syngenome) provides over 120 billion base pairs of AI-generated genomic sequences organized by Gene Ontology and InterPro annotations — immediately usable by the broader community for gene discovery and screening libraries.
  • Demonstrates cross-toxin breadth not seen in nature. Several generated antitoxins (EvoAT2, EvoAT4) neutralize multiple structurally unrelated toxins, suggesting that semantic design can uncover protein–protein interaction compatibilities that evolution has not sampled.
  • Achieves high experimental success rates. Among all tested anti-CRISPR candidates 17% showed measurable activity in a single-Cas9 screening round — a notably high hit rate given the absence of structural or evolutionary priors.

Core Methods

  • Genomic language model (Evo 1.5): a single-nucleotide-resolution autoregressive transformer pretrained on prokaryotic DNA from OpenGenome (450 billion tokens); the 1.5 variant extends Evo 1 8K pretraining by 50% and achieves the best sequence-recovery across tested genes and prompt lengths.
  • Semantic design / in-context DNA autocomplete: a known functional gene (or its flanking/operon context, reverse complement, etc.) is supplied as a DNA prompt; the model generates a response sequence enriched for related functions via the learned distributional semantics of prokaryotic gene co-occurrence.
  • Multi-prompt strategy: for each target function, six prompt types are curated per gene (upstream, CDS, downstream regions and their reverse complements), yielding diverse functional conditioning without fine-tuning.
  • Bioinformatic filtering pipeline: generated sequences filtered by length, low-complexity removal, predicted complex formation (AlphaFold 3 / ESMFold), repeat structure (T3TA), and PaCRISPR classification (Acrs) before experimental testing.
  • Experimental validation: bacterial growth inhibition/rescue assays (arabinose-inducible expression in E. coli); T4 phage plaque assays for Acr activity; one-sided Student's t-test for significance.
  • Similarity and novelty analysis: BLAST, HHpred, Foldseek, Dali, and residue-coverage compositionality analysis to characterize sequence and structural novelty of generated proteins relative to natural proteins and known databases.
  • SynGenome characterization: Prodigal ORF prediction, codon-usage analysis, Leiden clustering in Evo embedding space, Pfam/InterPro domain annotation, protein-family co-occurrence network construction (Circos), and ESMFold structure prediction.

① Inject double-strand DNA geometry into Evo's semantic design

Extending this paperEvo 1.5 treats DNA as a linear sequence of nucleotides, ignoring the base-pairing symmetry between the two strands. The paper partially compensates by using reverse-complement prompts, but the model itself does not explicitly model cross-strand dependencies — a limitation noted in the context-sensitivity experiments. Directly extending this paper by incorporating explicit cross-strand interaction layers into Evo would give the model a richer representation of the DNA duplex, potentially improving recovery of sequences where promoter or stem-loop structures span both strands, as observed for T3TA RNA antitoxins. Such a model could also produce more realistic non-coding RNA designs, a domain where single-strand language models systematically underestimate secondary structure constraints.

Added value to my researchThis connects to my interest in fine-tuning geospatial foundation models: the challenge of building domain-aware inductive biases into a general-purpose language model mirrors the need to encode topological constraints (network connectivity, spatial autocorrelation) into geospatial transformers. The cross-strand attention mechanism is structurally analogous to graph attention over dual spatial layers (e.g., road network + land-use topology), informing design choices for spatially-aware pre-training.

② Extend semantic design from prokaryotic operons to eukaryotic regulatory programs

Extending this paperEvo 1.5 was trained exclusively on prokaryotic sequences; the paper's semantic-design principle relies on the tight genomic co-localization of functionally related genes in bacterial operons. A natural and significant extension is to ask whether a foundation model trained on eukaryotic regulatory sequences can exploit analogous co-occurrence patterns in cis-regulatory modules (enhancer clusters, TAD boundaries) to design novel transcription-factor binding sites or gene regulatory elements. The SynGenome construction pipeline could be adapted: instead of gene-adjacent prompts, one would use cell-type-specific chromatin accessibility loci as prompt contexts, directing the model to generate functionally related regulatory elements for that cell type.

Added value to my researchWhile my core domain is geospatial rather than genomic, the design principle — use contextual co-occurrence to guide generation of functionally related new instances — is directly translatable to context-conditioned spatial data augmentation. For example, conditioning a spatiotemporal generative model on the "neighborhood context" of a healthcare facility (surrounding POIs, road connectivity, demographic mix) to synthesize realistic synthetic mobility or disease-burden records for data-sparse regions — a pressing issue for my 2SFCA accessibility modeling in low-data settings.

③ Use SynGenome's domain co-occurrence network as a substrate for machine-learning-guided CRISPR enzyme engineering

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that SynGenome's prompt–response association network reveals functional domain co-occurrences that match known genomic colocalization and can illuminate DUFs. A targeted extension: mine the SynGenome network specifically for novel Cas enzyme variants by seeding prompts from known Cas9 PAM-recognition domains and generating chimeric Cas proteins from the response sequences. The paper's bioinformatic filtering pipeline already produces candidate sequences with AlphaFold 3 structural predictions; pairing those with scalable PAM-specificity machine learning classifiers would create a closed-loop discovery pipeline for custom CRISPR tools with minimal experimental cost.

Analogously, I can apply SHAP feature attribution at the spatial context level to explain which neighborhood features most drive predicted healthcare accessibility gaps — grounding XAI explanations in the same "context-as-signal" logic that makes semantic design work.

④ Apply backpropagation through the genomic LM to steer semantic design toward optimized multi-objective functions

Extending this paperThe current semantic design approach is a one-shot prompt-and-filter pipeline: Evo generates sequences, and the authors filter by heuristic bioinformatic criteria (predicted structure, size, repeat content) and then test experimentally. The paper does not yet incorporate differentiable feedback from downstream predictors back into sequence generation. An extension: treat the PaCRISPR score, AlphaFold 3 pLDDT, and an experimental activity surrogate as a multi-objective reward, and use language model feedback propagation to iteratively refine the prompt or steer the generation distribution toward higher-scoring sequences — dramatically reducing the experimental validation burden relative to the current random-sampling-and-filter approach.

Added value to my researchThe parallel in my work is optimization of EV charging station placement under efficiency-equity constraints: instead of exhaustive spatial search, one could train a surrogate LM over spatial configurations and backpropagate gradients through an accessibility-equity objective — effectively turning facility-location optimization into a differentiable text-completion problem over urban topology. This cross-domain analogy opens a methodological bridge between genomic generative AI and geospatial optimization.

Cross-ref (corpus): Optimizing generative AI by backpropagating language model feedback — 10.1038/s41586-025-08661-4

⑤ Build a protein sequence design layer on top of SynGenome for targeted molecular function

Extending this paperSynGenome provides over 120 billion base pairs of synthetic DNA, but its utility for directed protein engineering is currently limited to database search and semantic discovery rather than structure-conditioned design. A valuable extension of the paper: combine SynGenome's context-enriched sequences with a deep-learning protein sequence design head that can accept a target backbone structure as input and output a sequence predicted to fold into that structure while preserving the contextual functional signal of the SynGenome prompt. This would create a two-stage pipeline: Evo generates functionally enriched candidates, then the design head refines them toward a desired fold — merging the paper's sequence-diversity with structure-based precision.

Added value to my researchMy agenda around multi-source data fusion and geospatial foundation models parallels this two-stage logic: a geospatial Evo equivalent could generate region-level context embeddings from heterogeneous data streams (mobile phone, remote sensing, POIs), and a downstream task head (e.g., disease risk predictor or accessibility gap estimator) could be trained on those embeddings with minimal labeled data — a foundation-model fine-tuning pipeline for health geography analogous to the SynGenome + design head architecture.
