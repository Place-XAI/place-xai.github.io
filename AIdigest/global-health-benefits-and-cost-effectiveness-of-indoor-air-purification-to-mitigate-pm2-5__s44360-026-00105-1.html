Global health benefits and cost-effectiveness of indoor air purification to mitigate PM2.5 from wildfire smoke

Dongjia Han · Yongxuan Guo · John S. Ji · Jianghao Wang · Bin Zhao Published 2026-04-07 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00105-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Indoor air purifiers could avert 60.8 million disability-adjusted life years (DALYs) attributable to wildfire PM2.5 and 2.2 billion DALYs from all-source PM2.5 globally over 2003–2021. However, a stark income-driven equity divide emerges: interventions are cost-effective in high-income countries but face severe affordability constraints in low- and middle-income countries, calling for targeted subsidies and international support to make indoor air purification an equitable global mitigation strategy.

Why It Matters

  • First global, subnational health-and-cost assessment of indoor air purification. Prior work was single-country or regional; this study spans 3,586 subnational administrative units across the globe and 19 years (2003–2021), establishing a truly planetary benchmark for an indoor mitigation strategy.
  • Quantifies an overlooked indoor exposure pathway. People spend most of their time indoors, yet wildfire-PM2.5 risk assessments have focused on outdoor concentrations. This framework explicitly models the indoor-outdoor concentration relationship and air-exchange rates to derive actionable indoor targets.
  • Reveals the global equity paradox of a cheap-looking intervention. While portable air purifiers appear affordable in wealthy nations, the cost-per-DALY-averted relative to GDP shows severe unaffordability in LMIC, reframing the debate from technology to financing and distributional justice.
  • Provides a roadmap for proportionate action. By mapping cost-effectiveness under WHO guideline (5 µg/m³) and interim (15 µg/m³) targets simultaneously, and by quantifying the cost-reduction needed for LMICs to reach cost-effectiveness thresholds, the paper directly informs subsidy design and international climate-health finance.
  • Timed for escalating wildfire risk. As climate change intensifies wildfire frequency and severity, this analysis gives policymakers a pre-emptive evidence base rather than a retrospective one.

Core Methods

  • Indoor exposure modelling: outdoor PM2.5 (MERRA-2 and FMI fire) is converted to indoor concentrations using the globally modelled air exchange rate (AER) and a building infiltration model. Clean Air Delivery Rate (CADR) of air purifiers drives the simulated indoor PM2.5 reduction.
  • Concentration–response functions: both Integrated Exposure-Response (IER) and MR-BRT (meta-regression — Bayesian, regularised, trimmed) functions are applied to link indoor PM2.5 levels to cause-specific DALY rates, with IER as primary and MR-BRT as sensitivity check.
  • Disease burden quantification (DALYs): avoidable DALYs are computed as the difference in burden with and without air purifier intervention under two WHO indoor PM2.5 targets — the guideline value (5 µg/m³) and the interim target (15 µg/m³) — applied to both fire-PM2.5 and all-source PM2.5.
  • Cost-effectiveness analysis: the cost per avoidable DALY (CostAD) is estimated using market-based air-purifier cost data, annualised ownership costs, and population weights. CostAD is compared to local GDP per capita thresholds (WHO-recommended cost-effectiveness benchmarks) to classify regions as cost-effective or not.
  • Monte Carlo uncertainty quantification: 1,000,000 iterations propagate uncertainty in concentration–response parameters, AER, and cost estimates to produce 95% confidence intervals for all outputs.
  • Spatio-temporal stratification: results are disaggregated by continent, World Bank income group, and four sub-periods (2003–2006, 2007–2011, 2012–2016, 2017–2021) to reveal trends and geographic heterogeneity.

① Replace static subnational aggregates with mobility-adjusted personal exposure

Extending this paperThe paper assigns fire-PM2.5 burden at the subnational administrative-unit level using a population-weighted average, implicitly assuming people stay within their unit throughout the day. A direct extension: integrate GPS-based mobility flows to compute activity-location-specific exposure — when a person commutes from a low-fire-burden suburb into a high-burden downtown or vice versa, the static unit estimate misleads. Linking the paper's indoor/outdoor infiltration model with fine-grained mobility trajectories would reveal whether administrative-unit DALYs over- or under-count true exposure for commuters and mobile workers, and whether the cost-effectiveness verdict changes for mobile populations in border zones.

② Apply spatially varying cost-effectiveness thresholds via geographically weighted models

Extending this paperThe paper adopts global GDP-per-capita thresholds (1× and 3× GDP) as uniform cost-effectiveness benchmarks across all subnational units within a country, but local purchasing power, household income distribution, and health system capacity vary enormously at subnational scale. A meaningful extension: replace the national GDP benchmark with subnational disposable income or healthcare expenditure surfaces, and apply geographically weighted regression (GWR) to model how the CostAD/threshold ratio varies spatially within countries — particularly revealing in large, internally heterogeneous nations (China, India, Brazil, USA) where the paper's national-income-group classification conceals vast within-country gradients.

The resulting locally calibrated cost maps would feed into targeted subsidy design at scales actionable by local governments — precisely the policy translation my GeoAI-for-public-health agenda aims at.

④ Extend the indoor-outdoor model to compound wildfire–heat exposure

Extending this paperThe paper treats wildfire-PM2.5 as a single-hazard problem, yet during wildfires extreme heat co-occurs and drives competing behaviours: opening windows for cooling increases PM2.5 infiltration; closing windows to run a purifier increases heat stress. A direct extension of the paper's air-exchange-rate model: incorporate temperature-dependent window-opening behaviour to create a compound heat × fire-PM2.5 indoor exposure model, where the CADR benefit of purifiers is weighted by the probability of indoor-outdoor air exchange at different heat-stress levels. This would reveal where purifier effectiveness is undermined by simultaneous heat and where the health benefit estimate needs to be discounted.
