Large-scale transport simulation by deep learning

Jie Pan Published 2021-05-14 DOI 10.1038/s43588-021-00080-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A neural-network + non-equilibrium Green's function framework (NN-NEGF) extends quantum electronic transport simulation to experimentally realistic material sizes. By replacing the expensive first-principles step with a trained neural-network interatomic potential and a separate transport neural network, the method computed ~200,000 electronic conductance values for potassium nanowires — a feat impossible with conventional NEGF — while faithfully reproducing experimental conductance histograms. This demonstrates that deep learning can breach the scale barrier that has long confined quantum transport modeling to simplified toy systems.

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the scale wall of quantum transport. The non-equilibrium Green's function (NEGF) method is regarded as a gold standard for electronic transport but is so computationally costly that it is limited to small, idealized structures. NN-NEGF shows for the first time that conductance can be computed at experimentally realizable nanowire sizes and in bulk (200,000 samples).
  • Two-tier deep-learning strategy generalizes the pipeline. Training a neural-network potential for geometry generation and a separate transport NN for conductance prediction is a conceptually clean decomposition that can, in principle, be adapted to any material system where local structural descriptors govern transport.
  • Opens thermoelectric and nanoelectronic design to ML-accelerated screening. Electronic transport governs energy conversion efficiency in thermoelectric devices; large-scale conductance prediction enables high-throughput screening that was previously out of reach.
  • Validates a physics-informed locality assumption. The core insight — that electronic transport is primarily sensitive to local atomic environments (captured by the SOAP descriptor) — is a non-trivial physical claim that NN-NEGF supports experimentally, providing a theoretical foundation for further extensions.

Core Methods

  • Neural-network interatomic potential (NN-IP): a deep neural network trained to reproduce the potential energy surface of K atoms, enabling efficient molecular dynamics (MD) simulation of large nanowire structures that would be too expensive for density functional theory (DFT) alone.
  • First-principles NEGF on small structures: for small geometry samples, exact conductance is computed using DFT-based NEGF, providing high-fidelity labels for training the transport NN.
  • Transport neural network: a second NN is trained on (local atomic descriptor → conductance) pairs extracted from small-structure NEGF calculations. At inference time, it predicts conductance for large MD-generated structures without any further quantum calculations.
  • SOAP local descriptors: the smooth overlap of atomic positions encodes the local chemical environment of each atom, operationalizing the locality assumption that electronic transport properties depend primarily on nearby atomic disorder and defects.
  • Conductance histogram construction: ~200,000 predictions are aggregated into a conductance histogram, directly comparable to experimental break-junction measurements — closing the gap between simulation and experiment.

① Replace the two-stage NN-NEGF pipeline with a unified graph neural network

Extending this paperThe NN-NEGF framework uses two decoupled networks: one for atomic potentials (geometry generation) and another for transport (conductance prediction). This decoupling sacrifices end-to-end consistency — errors in the geometry network propagate uncorrected into the transport network. A direct extension of the paper: replace both stages with a single equivariant graph neural network that maps the atomic graph directly to conductance, trained jointly on geometry + transport labels. The "M3GNet" universal interatomic potential (corpus) already graphs the periodic table; appending a transport head to such a foundation model could unify the two NN-NEGF stages into one transferable operator.

Added value to my researchThe same graph-based architecture is the backbone of my physics-informed graph learning agenda for urban systems. Seeing how equivariant graph layers encode local atomic symmetry in materials directly informs how to encode spatial symmetry and isotropy into graph layers for urban street networks — a principled design principle transferable across scales.

② Learn the DFT Hamiltonian rather than the conductance directly, enabling deeper physical fidelity

Extending this paperNN-NEGF trains on conductance values as scalar labels, which discards structural information about how quantum interference arises from the electronic Hamiltonian. A conceptually deeper extension: train a deep-learning model to predict the DFT Hamiltonian matrix H(structure) directly — as demonstrated in the DeepH and DeepH-E3 papers (corpus) — and then feed that predicted Hamiltonian into NEGF. This restores the full Hamiltonian pathway, making conductance a derived rather than directly regressed quantity, which dramatically improves generalizability to unseen perturbations (disorder, defects, junctions).

Added value to my researchThis teaches a methodological lesson for spatial modeling: instead of regressing outputs (accessibility scores, health outcomes) directly, predict the structural operator (the mobility kernel or spatial weight matrix) and derive outcomes from it. This is the physics-informed spirit applied to geospatial prediction — model the mechanism, not just the response.

Cross-ref (corpus): Deep-learning density functional theory Hamiltonian for efficient ab initio electronic-structure calculation — 10.1038/s43588-022-00265-6

③ Extend NN-NEGF to active learning to close the training-data bottleneck

Extending this paperThe paper's NN-NEGF relies on a fixed corpus of expensive first-principles NEGF calculations for training. For new materials, this corpus must be rebuilt from scratch. A natural extension: couple the transport NN with an uncertainty-quantification (UQ) module so that, during the large-scale MD run, structures with high predicted uncertainty are automatically queued for NEGF re-calculation and added back to the training set — iteratively shrinking the first-principles burden while expanding material coverage. The corpus paper on uncertainty-driven active learning for interatomic potentials (Nat. Comput. Sci. 2023) provides exactly this UQ-driven loop, here applicable to the transport NN.

Added value to my researchActive learning with spatial uncertainty is equally useful for my GeoAI pipelines: when fine-tuning geospatial foundation models on sparse labeled health or mobility data, a UQ-driven active sampler would identify which geographic areas most reduce model uncertainty, targeting expensive ground-truth collection (field surveys, telehealth audits) most efficiently.

Cross-ref (corpus): Uncertainty-driven dynamics for active learning of interatomic potentials — 10.1038/s43588-023-00406-5

④ Lift the locality assumption by learning effective coarse-grained dynamics for multiscale transport

Extending this paperNN-NEGF's transport NN rests on a locality hypothesis: conductance depends only on the local SOAP environment. While valid for single-channel nanowires, this breaks down in devices where long-range quantum coherence, back-scattering from distant defects, or electrode geometry matter. A principled extension: instead of a local NN, learn the effective coarse-grained dynamics of the electron density matrix across length scales, as the "multiscale simulations by learning effective dynamics" corpus paper demonstrates for complex physical systems. This would make NN-NEGF applicable to multi-terminal devices and realistic field-effect transistors.

Added value to my researchThe coarse-graining idea maps cleanly onto spatiotemporal kernel density estimation across urban scales: instead of modeling individual trips or facilities, learn the effective population-mobility kernel at successively coarser spatial resolutions, then reconstruct fine-scale patterns from the learned dynamics — a multiscale GeoAI approach to urban analytics.

Cross-ref (corpus): Multiscale simulations of complex systems by learning their effective dynamics — 10.1038/s42256-022-00464-w
