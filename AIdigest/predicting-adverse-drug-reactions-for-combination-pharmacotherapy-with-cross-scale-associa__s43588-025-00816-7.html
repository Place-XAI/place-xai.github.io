Predicting adverse drug reactions for combination pharmacotherapy with cross-scale associative learning via attention modules

Boyang Li · Yifan Qi · Bo Li · Xiaoqiong Li Published 2025-06-30 DOI 10.1038/s43588-025-00816-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

OrganADR is an associative learning-enhanced deep learning model that predicts adverse drug reactions (ADRs) at the organ level for emerging combination pharmacotherapies. By fusing molecular-level drug features, biomedical knowledge-graph information and an organ-level ADR association matrix through GNN, GCN and attention modules, it achieves state-of-the-art performance across 15 organs (ROC-AUC 85.93% on task 2), while providing interpretable insights into the key protein–protein interaction networks that mechanistically underpin each ADR — a tool directly applicable to clinical precision medicine and drug safety management.

★

Why It Matters

  • Addresses a critical clinical gap. 3.6–15.6% of hospital admissions worldwide are related to ADRs; accurate prediction for emerging drug combinations (cold-start) — where no clinical records yet exist — is an unresolved challenge with direct patient-safety implications.
  • Introduces organ-level perspective. Existing models treat each ADR independently; OrganADR explicitly models the co-occurrence associations of ADRs across 15 organs via a symmetrically normalised association matrix, capturing biological reality (e.g., immune-related events co-affecting intestine, liver, and nervous system).
  • Multi-scale, interpretable architecture. The model integrates molecular fingerprints, biomedical KG paths, and organ-level ADR co-occurrences into a single end-to-end framework with three interpretable outputs: organ-level predictions, key protein identification, and key PPI network extraction.
  • Rigorous cold-start evaluation. Tested on two independent datasets (Dataset D and Dataset R) across two tasks, three KGs and 15 organs — demonstrating robustness, not just benchmark overfitting.
  • Open science. Code (GitHub + Zenodo), preprocessed datasets (Zenodo) and source data are all publicly available, enabling reproducible clinical pharmacovigilance research.

Core Methods

  • Molecular feature encoding: each drug is encoded as a 1,024-dimensional vector by concatenating RDKit Descriptor (with trainable self-attention), MACCS Fingerprint (with trainable self-attention), RDKit path-based fingerprint, and Morgan fingerprint (radius=2).
  • Flow-based GNN on biomedical KG: propagates drug features through the KG via a 3-hop flow GNN with drug-dependent attention weights on each relation type, identifying key protein paths linking drug pairs.
  • Organ-level ADR association matrix: a 15×15 symmetrically normalised conditional-probability matrix computed from training-set co-occurrences of ADRs across organ pairs; used as the GCN adjacency to explicitly model ADR correlations.
  • GCN module: latent organ-level labels (from a linear multiclassifier on drug-pair embeddings) are fed into a GCN with the association matrix as edge weights; the GCN updates label embeddings to propagate organ-level information.
  • Attention fusion module: combines molecular-level embeddings (GNN output) and organ-level embeddings (GCN output) via element-wise attention scoring, producing a concatenated representation for final multilabel prediction.
  • Cold-start evaluation: drugs split 8:1:1 into disjoint subsets; task 1 — both drugs unseen in training; task 2 — one emerging, one existing. Evaluated by ROC-AUC, PR-AUC, accuracy, precision, recall, F1, and Hamming loss.
  • Interpretability: drug-dependent attention weights on KG paths identify key PPI networks; organ-level association matrix provides organ-perspective reasoning; ablation and causal analysis confirm the contribution of each module.
  • Baselines: compared against EmerGNN, HIN-DDI, KG-DDI, CSMDDI (deep learning) and RandomForest, KNeighbors, DecisionTree, GaussianNB (classical ML). OrganADR surpasses all on both tasks across three KGs.

① Replace the static ADR association matrix with a learned, drug-pair-adaptive graph structure

Extending this paperOrganADR’s organ-level association matrix M is precomputed from the training set and then frozen — a static population-level prior that cannot adapt when a specific drug pair produces unusual cross-organ ADR co-occurrence patterns not seen in training. A direct extension is to make M jointly learnable via differentiable graph structure learning (e.g., attention-based adjacency conditioned on the drug-pair embedding), so that the association matrix becomes a function of the specific pair being evaluated rather than a fixed constant. This would also allow OrganADR to generalise to new organs or clinical taxonomies expanding beyond the current 15.

Added value to my researchDynamic graph structure learning is the analogue in my spatial accessibility work of the bandwidth-selection problem in MGWR: the spatial interaction matrix (who visits which facility) is typically fixed or cross-validated, but it could instead be jointly learned from observed mobility flows. The same differentiable-adjacency mechanism could let me build a demand-adaptive, learnable spatial interaction graph for 2SFCA-type models where the gravity decay function is conditioned on local context (deprivation, transport mode, time of day) rather than globally assumed.

② Lift the KG-membership bottleneck with foundation-model molecular representations

Extending this paperOrganADR currently relies on RDKit-computed fingerprints that require 2D SDF files, limiting the model to 639 drugs present in all four databases simultaneously. The paper explicitly acknowledges this as its main limitation, citing future directions involving large language models in molecular representation. Extending it: replace or augment fingerprint encoders with pretrained chemical language model embeddings (e.g., ChemBERTa, MolBERT, or similar) so that the model can handle drugs absent from the KG. The GCN and attention modules remain intact, receiving richer initial drug embeddings, directly tackling broader cold-start coverage.

③ Add a spatial epidemiology layer: mapping organ-level ADR risk onto patient populations

Extending this paperOrganADR produces ADR probabilities for a drug pair but abstracts away the patient — their age, comorbidities, geographic access to monitoring, or socioeconomic context. A powerful extension is to couple OrganADR’s predictions with population-level pharmacovigilance data (e.g., national dispensing records) to model which communities face elevated ADR exposure based on their local prescribing patterns and care-access constraints, making the model actionable for public health surveillance rather than only individual drug approval workflows.

Added value to my researchThis extension sits at the intersection of my health geography work and real-world evidence. My 2SFCA spatial accessibility framework could quantify geographic disparities in access to ADR monitoring and pharmacovigilance — which patients, by virtue of living far from pharmacists or GPs, are least likely to have an ADR caught early. OrganADR’s predictions combined with my spatial accessibility scores would generate a geographically targeted drug safety alert: high predicted organ-ADR risk + low local monitoring access = priority intervention zone.

④ Use identified PPI networks as physics-informed structural priors in graph learning

Extending this paperOrganADR identifies key PPI networks (e.g., CYP3A4-centred binding/upregulation paths for hydrocortisone+cisapride) but treats them purely qualitatively — they illustrate mechanism but do not feed back into training. A direct extension: use the attention-weighted PPI paths as structural priors in a physics-informed GNN, so that known biological constraints (protein essentiality, pathway conservation, enzyme inhibition hierarchies) act as soft regularisers during training. This would improve data efficiency in the low-data regime of emerging drug combinations with few historical ADRs.

Added value to my researchPhysics-informed graph learning is a central theme of my research agenda. The exact design pattern — taking domain-known structural constraints and encoding them as soft losses or graph priors in a GNN — applies directly to my spatiotemporal health prediction work: spatial autocorrelation, Tobler’s first law, and population gravity could be encoded as physics-like priors in graph-based disease spread models, improving prediction in data-sparse regions. OrganADR demonstrates the pattern concretely in the biomedical domain, providing a methodological blueprint.

⑤ Embed OrganADR within a calibrated, explainable clinical decision-support workflow

Extending this paperOrganADR demonstrates interpretability at the research level (attention weights, PPI networks, causal analyses) but stops short of a clinical deployment framework: how does a clinician query it, how does it integrate with an electronic medical record system, and what confidence level is required before surfacing an ADR alert? Extending the paper: pair OrganADR’s predictions with calibrated uncertainty estimates (conformal prediction or Bayesian dropout) and embed the output in a human-in-the-loop workflow that presents organ-level ADR risk scores alongside the identified PPI mechanism, enabling evidence-graded decisions rather than binary alerts.
