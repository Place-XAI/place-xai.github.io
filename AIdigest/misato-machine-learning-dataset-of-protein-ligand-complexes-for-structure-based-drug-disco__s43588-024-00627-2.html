MISATO: machine learning dataset of protein–ligand complexes for structure-based drug discovery

Till Siebenmorgen · Filipe Menezes · Sabrina Benassou · Erinc Merdivan · Kieran Didi · André Santos Dias Mourão · Radosław Kitel · Pietro Liò · Stefan Kesselheim · Marie Piraud · Fabian J. Core Conclusion

MISATO is an open, quantum-mechanically curated dataset of ~19,443 protein–ligand complexes built from PDBbind (release 2022), augmented with semi-empirical quantum chemical (QM) properties and molecular dynamics (MD) traces totalling over 170 µs across 16,972 complexes. By incorporating QM-derived atomic charges and MD-derived conformational flexibility into graph neural network (GNN) baseline models, MISATO raises binding-affinity prediction from a Spearman correlation of 0.50 to 0.64 — demonstrating that explicit quantum-mechanical and dynamic features are essential stepping-stones toward next-generation structure-based drug discovery AI.

Why It Matters

  • Fills a critical data gap in structural biology AI. Existing protein–ligand datasets provide static crystallographic snapshots; MISATO adds two missing dimensions — quantum chemical electronic structure and conformational dynamics — making it the largest MD-augmented protein–ligand resource to date (170 µs across 16,972 complexes).
  • Systematic curation removes crystallographic noise. Semi-empirical QM protocols identified and corrected geometry errors, protonation states, and incorrect atom assignments in ~20% (3,930) of the PDBbind structures — a reproducible workflow any lab can apply to future releases.
  • Ships as production-ready ML infrastructure. Two HDF5 files (QM + MD), PyTorch/ATOM3D data loaders, GPU container images, baseline GNN models, and a carefully curated affinity benchmark (127 ligands, 5 protein families) drastically lower the on-ramp for AI drug discovery researchers.
  • Experimentally validated dynamics. RMSF from MD correlates with X-ray B factors at r = 0.59 across all structures; NMR hetNOE spectroscopy on influenza PB2 polymerase independently confirms the adaptability model's predictions — grounding simulation outputs in real biology.
  • Community-extensible by design. MD checkpoint files allow continuation beyond 10 ns; ATOM3D compatibility ensures integration with the broader ML-for-molecules ecosystem; Zenodo archiving guarantees long-term reproducibility.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Primary source
PDBbind 2022 — 19,443 experimentally determined protein–ligand complex structures with binding affinity annotations, originally from the Protein Data Bank (PDB).
QM dataset
All 19,443 ligands processed with the ULYSSES semi-empirical library (GFN2-xTB, AM1, PM6). 3,930 structures (~20%) required substantial correction. Properties include: electron affinity, chemical hardness, electronegativity, ionization potentials, static log P, polarizabilities, partial charges (AM1, AM1-BCC, CM1A-LBCC, Mulliken), Fukui indices, orbital hybridizations, and tight-binding electronic densities.
MD dataset
16,972 complexes × 10 ns each in TIP3P explicit water (Amber20, ff14SB + GAFF2 force fields), accumulating >170 µs total. 8 ns stored per complex (100 snapshots; first 2 ns discarded as equilibration). Properties: RMSD, RMSF, MMGBSA binding affinity, buried surface area, centre-of-mass ligand–receptor distance.
Affinity benchmark
Curated holdout of 127 ligands across 5 protein families; affinities from the same publication per set, high dynamic range, no confounding cofactors — designed as a reliable gold-standard validation set.
Validation data
Experimental B factors from PDB crystal structures; hetNOE NMR spectra of influenza PB2 cap-binding domain measured at 298 K (AV600 spectrometer); DFT and experimental oxidation potentials for photocatalysts, catechins, and thiaflavans from CCCBDB.
Access
Publicly available on Zenodo (zenodo.org/records/7711953); code at github.com/t7morgen/misato-dataset; open-access (CC BY 4.0).

Core Methods

  • Semi-empirical QM curation (ULYSSES): GFN2-xTB, AM1, and PM6 applied in vacuum and implicit solvent (ALPB). Consistency pipeline: wavefunction convergence checks, HOMO–LUMO gap monitoring, octet-rule violation detection, D4 polarizability vs. partial-charge outlier plots, and geometry optimization to fix distorted heteroatom skeletons.
  • Classical molecular dynamics (Amber20): complexes solvated in octahedral TIP3P boxes (12 Å padding); neutralized with Na⁺/Cl⁻; minimized (1,000-step steepest descent + conjugate gradient) and heated to 300 K over 16 ps; 10 ns NVT production; GAFF2 ligand force field, ff14SB protein force field; AM1-BCC charges via antechamber.
  • GNN baseline models (ATOM3D code base): (1) QM model: graph convolutional network (Kipf–Welling) for simultaneous prediction of electron affinity (r = 0.75) and chemical hardness (r = 0.77); (2) Adaptability model: node-regression GCN predicting per-atom mean displacement (r = 0.66, top-100 accuracy 0.42); (3) Binding affinity model: pairwise relative-affinity GNN with QM charges + MD adaptability features (Spearman r = 0.64 vs. 0.50 baseline). All models trained on 4 × NVIDIA A100 GPUs.
  • Binding affinity benchmark construction: 30% sequence-similarity clustering to prevent data leakage; UniProt-based cluster pairing for relative affinity learning; five sets with ≥15 entries and high dynamic range from consistent experimental methods.
  • Multi-modal experimental validation: B-factor correlation (PDB crystal structures vs. MD RMSF, r = 0.59); NMR hetNOE on influenza PB2 (r = 0.63 vs. predicted adaptability); Koopmans ionization potentials validated against DFT and experimental oxidation potentials across three molecule families.

① Upgrade MISATO baselines with molecular contrastive pre-training

Extending this paperMISATO's three GNN baselines are trained from scratch under fully supervised objectives on modest sample counts (127-ligand affinity benchmark, ~17K adaptability labels). The paper itself demonstrates that MISATO QM features improve correlation over no-feature baselines, but the encoder is not pre-trained. A direct extension: treat MISATO's 19,443 QM-annotated ligand graphs as a contrastive pre-training corpus, pairing different MD conformers or protonation variants of the same molecule as "views," then fine-tune on the held-out affinity benchmark. The existing benchmark would measure exactly how much the representation gains — making the already-released benchmark more useful and directly testing the claim that MISATO data encodes transferable molecular knowledge.

Added value to my researchThe contrastive workflow mirrors my geospatial foundation model fine-tuning agenda: pre-train a general encoder on abundant unlabelled observations (here: MD snapshots; in my work: remote-sensing patches or mobile-phone traces) then adapt to low-data downstream tasks (binding affinity; in my work: health-outcome prediction). MISATO also delivers a concrete open-science template — FAIR HDF5, containerized loaders, Zenodo DOI — I can replicate for spatiotemporal health datasets to enable community benchmarking of GeoAI accessibility models.

② Apply spatiotemporal kernel density estimation to binding-pocket dynamics

Extending this paperMISATO currently summarises pocket dynamics via a scalar adaptability index — the mean absolute atomic displacement from the initial frame across 100 MD snapshots. This flattens the temporal structure and cannot distinguish transient from persistent flexibility. An extension: represent each complex's binding pocket as a spatiotemporal point cloud (atom position × simulation time) and apply kernel density estimation in 3D space × time to identify high-density configurational regions without thresholding. The result is a continuous flexibility "landscape" that directly locates cryptic pockets hinted at in Fig. 3 and quantifies how long each transient state persists — information currently absent from the 100-snapshot summary.

Added value to my researchSpatiotemporal KDE is one of my core geospatial methods, applied to GPS trajectories and infectious-disease event clustering. Seeing it potentially applied to molecular dynamics — with identical mathematical foundations — reveals a method-transfer opportunity: the adaptive bandwidth selection and edge-effect corrections I implement for urban mobility hotspots translate directly to 3D atomic density estimation. This cross-domain parallel also strengthens the reproducibility case for releasing GeoAI toolkits as generic spatiotemporal analysis libraries rather than domain-specific silos.

③ Attach explainability to MISATO GNNs for atom-level attribution of QM-driven affinity

Extending this paperThe affinity and adaptability GNN models achieve respectable correlations but provide no atom-level explanation of which quantum features drive the prediction. Extending MISATO: apply GNN attribution methods (GradCAM on node embeddings, integrated gradients, or attention-based subgraph masks) to the three baseline models, then aggregate node-level attribution scores across the 127-ligand benchmark to identify which QM descriptors — Fukui indices, partial charges, D4 polarizabilities — most consistently govern binding-pocket flexibility and relative affinity. Because MISATO's ground-truth QM labels are physically interpretable, attribution outputs can be validated against chemical intuition, providing a rare opportunity to test whether GNN explainability actually recovers known structure–activity relationships.

④ Joint multi-task learning across QM, adaptability, and affinity with physics-derived consistency constraints

Extending this paperMISATO provides three separate baseline models trained independently, even though the paper demonstrates that QM charges and MD adaptabilities improve the affinity model as features. A direct extension: a single multi-task GNN encoder with task-specific heads for all three objectives, trained with soft physics constraints — electroneutrality of partial charges, monotonic RMSF–flexibility ordering — as auxiliary losses. Joint training exploits the cross-task correlations already implicit in MISATO (the same ligand's charge distribution affects both QM hardness and MD dynamics) and reduces the labelled-data requirement for each task.

Added value to my researchMulti-task learning is my strategy for multi-source data fusion, simultaneously predicting spatial health outcomes from heterogeneous inputs (remote sensing, social media, POIs). MISATO's multi-property setup is structurally analogous: fusing QM signals, MD trajectories, and affinity labels mirrors fusing satellite imagery, mobility traces, and census data. The challenge of physics-derived equality constraints (electroneutrality, spatial coherence) directly parallels my work on Monte Carlo uncertainty propagation and the MGWR constraint that spatial regression coefficients must vary smoothly — providing a molecular test case for constraint-aware multi-task architectures I can then transfer to geospatial settings.
