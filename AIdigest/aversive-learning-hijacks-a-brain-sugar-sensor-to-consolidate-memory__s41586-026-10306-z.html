Aversive learning hijacks a brain sugar sensor to consolidate memory

Raquel Francés · Typhaine Comyn · Coraline Desnous · Francesca Bettoni · Alice Pavlowsky · Thomas Preat · Pierre-Yves Plaçais Published 2026-03-25 DOI 10.1038/s41586-026-10306-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Spaced repetition of aversive learning — independent of food cues — hijacks the brain's internal fructose sensor (Gr43a neurons) by placing it in a fasted-like state through a cholinergic disinhibition circuit. Post-training sugar intake then activates these neurons, which release the glycoprotein hormone thyrostimulin (Gpa2–Gpb5), triggering metabolic activation of mushroom-body neurons and long-term memory consolidation. The same mechanism also drives a marked increase in sucrose preference, providing a neural circuit basis for non-homeostatic hunger and emotional eating.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reframes the spacing effect as a metabolic gate. The well-known cognitive phenomenon — spaced practice beats massed practice — turns out to depend on a sugar-sensing AND logic gate: spacing unlocks fructose-sensor sensitivity; food intake is the second input that triggers consolidation. This moves the spacing effect from a purely cognitive to a metabolic-cognitive explanation.
  • Reveals non-homeostatic hunger. Spaced training makes fully satiated flies hungry for sugar through a brain-circuit mechanism, not a peripheral energy signal — establishing a cognitive origin of appetite that mirrors emotional or stress eating in humans.
  • Identifies thyrostimulin as a universal memory consolidation signal. The glycoprotein hormone Gpa2–Gpb5 (thyrostimulin) links nutrient sensing to mushroom-body metabolic activation in both appetitive and aversive learning — a shared hormonal consolidation switch across learning contexts.
  • Unifies reward and aversive memory pathways. Appetitive and aversive long-term memory share the same downstream circuit (Gr43a → thyrostimulin → MB α/β Lgr1), suggesting a common, evolutionarily conserved architecture that may generalize across species.
  • Opens a bypass to the spacing effect. Blocking Tk-mediated inhibition of Gr43a neurons allows LTM formation with only two training sessions instead of five — a pharmacological handle on the spacing requirement with implications for learning efficiency and memory disorders.

Core Methods

  • GAL4/UAS binary expression system: cell-type-specific transgene expression in Drosophila; combined with TARGET system (tub-GAL80ts) for adult-stage-restricted induction at 30.5 °C for 3 days.
  • Acute neuronal silencing (Shits): dominant-negative thermosensitive dynamin blocks neurotransmission at 33 °C, enabling time-resolved silencing of specific neuron populations post-training to define critical consolidation windows.
  • Neuron activation (TrpA1): heat-activated cation channel used to force firing of specific neurons (Gr43a, dFB, Janus) at defined times to test sufficiency.
  • RNAi knockdown: UAS-RNAi constructs (2 independent constructs per target for most genes) to chronically reduce expression of Gr43a, Gpb5, Gpa2, Lgr1, mAchR-B, ChAT, TkR99D; validated by RT-qPCR.
  • In vivo two-photon calcium imaging: GCaMP3/6s in Gr43a neurons; GCaMP6f in dFB neurons; live fly preparation with bath application of fructose (20–75 mM); motion-correction via Template Matching in Fiji.
  • FRET-based metabolic imaging: Pyronic sensor (pyruvate) and FLII12Pglu sensor (glucose) in mushroom-body neurons; pharmacological blocking with sodium azide (pyruvate consumption rate) or validamycin A (glucose consumption rate).
  • Connectome analysis (FlyWire): manual identification of FB.5/6 neurons in FAFB EM dataset; NeuronBridge matching to split-GAL4 line SS01491.
  • Single-cell transcriptomics: re-analysis of Davie et al. 2018 SCope dataset to identify co-expression of Gr43a, Crz, Gpb5, and Lgr1 in defined cell clusters.
  • CRISPR-Cas9 knock-in: Lgr1-3×HA reporter line generated to confirm Lgr1 expression pattern in α/β mushroom-body lobes.
  • Statistics: two-tailed t-tests, one-way ANOVA (Tukey pairwise), two-way ANOVA (Sidak); FlyPad data: Mann-Whitney U and Kruskal-Wallis tests (Dunn correction). GraphPad Prism v9.0.

① Translate the paper's AND-gate consolidation logic into a computational model of spaced repetition

Extending this paperThe paper establishes that LTM consolidation requires two simultaneous inputs — restored fructose-sensor sensitivity AND carbohydrate intake — forming a biological AND gate. This circuit logic is described qualitatively, but no mathematical or computational model is provided. A direct extension would formalize it as a threshold-gated reinforcement learning rule: the spacing interval sets a nutrient-sensor "readiness" variable that must exceed a threshold, and post-training reward magnitude (sugar dose) determines consolidation probability. This would connect the paper's neurobiology to the well-studied distributional reinforcement learning framework, where reward magnitude modulates learning efficiency.

Added value to my researchThis bridges to GeoAI via spatiotemporal learning systems: the AND-gate metaphor maps naturally onto dual-condition activation functions in graph neural networks, where two spatially distributed inputs (e.g., healthcare supply and realized demand) must both exceed local thresholds before a node "consolidates" into a stable service area. My R2SFCA accessibility models could adopt gating logic to model the joint condition of supply proximity AND demand density before a facility is counted as accessible.

② Map the experience-dependent disinhibition circuit onto multi-timescale learning architectures

Extending this paperThe paper identifies a three-layer circuit: spaced training → FB.5/6 cholinergic inhibition → dFB silencing → Gr43a disinhibition → thyrostimulin release → MB metabolic activation. This cascade operates across timescales (minutes to hours) and involves both fast (calcium transient) and slow (hormone diffusion) signals. An extension of the paper would characterize the temporal dynamics of each step quantitatively — using the in vivo imaging platform already established — and compare them to the timescale separations seen in multi-timescale reinforcement learning models of the brain, potentially identifying the biological substrate of slow vs. fast learning systems in flies.

Added value to my researchMulti-timescale signal propagation is a key challenge in spatiotemporal GeoAI. The circuit decomposition approach — identifying which layer operates at which timescale — inspires a hierarchical spatiotemporal graph architecture where short-range, fast signals (daily mobility) and long-range, slow signals (seasonal or structural patterns) are separated into distinct propagation pathways, improving prediction of public health outcomes across temporal scales.

④ Test whether the sugar-sensing consolidation gate is modulated by chronic metabolic state (obesity, diabetes)

Extending this paperThe paper studies flies under controlled satiation, but the Gr43a disinhibition mechanism should be sensitive to chronic metabolic state: obese or diabetic animals have chronically elevated blood glucose and altered gut–brain nutrient signalling. A straightforward extension would test whether diet-induced metabolic dysregulation in flies (high-sugar or high-fat diets) impairs the spacing-dependent restoration of Gr43a sensitivity, and whether thyrostimulin release is attenuated. This would link the paper's fundamental mechanism to epidemiologically relevant conditions and potentially explain cognitive impairment associated with metabolic disease.

⑤ Derive a biologically grounded spacing law and test it as a scheduling principle for adaptive learning systems

Extending this paperThe paper shows that spaced training with 15 min inter-trial intervals in flies resets Gr43a sensitivity within ~6 hours, defining a biological consolidation window. Extending this: by varying inter-trial intervals and post-training food access windows systematically, one could derive a biological spacing function — analogous to the Ebbinghaus forgetting curve — that maps training schedule to consolidation probability via Gr43a sensitivity recovery. This would transform the paper's mechanistic finding into a predictive model with normative implications for optimal study schedules in education and rehabilitation.

Added value to my researchThe notion that a system requires a minimum "reset interval" between stimuli to consolidate learning is directly analogous to temporal autocorrelation in spatiotemporal models: my spatiotemporal kernel density models implicitly assume a decay constant for spatial influence, but rarely model the time window needed between successive data inputs for stable pattern consolidation. The paper's "AND gate + reset window" architecture motivates explicit temporal gating in spatiotemporal GNNs, where node states are only updated after a minimum inter-event interval — improving stability of mobility-based public-health predictions derived from GPS or mobile-phone data.
