A machine learning-based multiscale model to predict bone formation in scaffolds

Chi Wu · Ali Entezari · Keke Zheng · Jianguang Fang · Hala Zreiqat · Grant P. Steven · Michael V. Swain · Qing Li Published 2021-08-20 DOI 10.1038/s43588-021-00115-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A three-neural-network pipeline replaces the computationally prohibitive conventional multilevel finite element (FE2) homogenization at the microscopic scale, reducing prediction time for 12-month bone ingrowth from over 150 hours to 300 seconds — a >1,800-fold speedup. The ML model achieves a linear regression of R = 0.997 against FE2 ground truth and successfully predicts species-specific bone remodeling in a sheep tibia scaffold by inverse identification of Wolff's law parameters from longitudinal in vivo X-ray images, validating at a withheld 12-month time point.

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the computational bottleneck in multiscale tissue engineering. Conventional FE2 requires individual micro-FE analyses across all 2,333 representative volume elements (RVEs) at each monthly time step — the ML surrogate converts this into a one-time offline training plus instant inference, making scaffold optimization at clinically relevant scales feasible.
  • Closes the loop between simulation and in vivo data. By coupling the surrogate with inverse identification (optimizing 5,000 candidate remodeling parameter sets via neural network NN-3 and a genetic algorithm), the authors extract animal-specific bone remodeling constants that match longitudinal X-ray data — a rare example of ML-assisted model calibration in biomechanics.
  • Predicts what animal experiments cannot measure. The in silico pipeline generates full 3D bone density distributions and virtual X-ray images across micro and macro scales simultaneously, providing spatial resolution unattainable by in vivo imaging alone.
  • Opens the path to subject-specific scaffold design. Because the trained networks encode scaffold geometry and loading conditions, the framework can be re-applied to new patient geometries derived from CT, directly supporting personalized implant engineering.

Core Methods

  • FE2 multilevel framework (baseline): macroscopic FE model treats scaffold RVEs as homogenized solid media; micro-FE analyses run asymptotic homogenization on each RVE to derive effective elastic tensors and strain energy densities (SEDs), iterated monthly per Wolff's law bone remodeling equations.
  • Three-neural-network surrogate pipeline: NN-1 (20 neurons, single hidden layer, sigmoid activation) maps RVE bone density → homogenized elastic tensor (9 independent orthotropic components, R = 0.997); NN-2 (2,522 neurons per output, 21 independent unit SED components) maps RVE bone density → unit SED field; NN-3 (10 neurons) maps 5 remodeling parameters → Pearson correlation coefficients P1 and P2.
  • Sequential data generation: initial 40,000 random RVE samples for first training round; ML model then generates 55,992 samples from physically plausible bone formation trajectories; second training incorporates both batches to improve distribution coverage and regression values.
  • Inverse identification: 5,000 candidate Wolff's law parameter sets (R1, R2, R3, K, s) evaluated via the ML pipeline and correlated with in vivo X-ray signal densities; a genetic algorithm maximizes P1 + P2 using NN-3 as a differentiable objective function surrogate.
  • Virtual X-ray generation: Abaqus plugin converts predicted bone density distributions to 512 × 512 pixel virtual X-ray images at each time point; ROI-level signal densities compared with in vivo X-rays via Pearson correlation coefficients.
  • Bone remodeling rule (Wolff's law): SED-driven adaptive density update: bone apposition if SED/density exceeds K(1+s), resorption below K(1−s), lazy zone in between; density updated monthly via Euler forward integration.

① Replace sequential data generation with uncertainty-driven active learning

Extending this paperThe paper's sequential data generation strategy (first 40,000 random RVE samples, then 55,992 trajectory-informed samples) is a hand-crafted two-stage heuristic. The second-stage samples improve regression from R = 0.932 to R = 0.972, but the boundary between informative and redundant samples is chosen by trial and error. A principled extension: replace this with an uncertainty-driven active learning loop where an ensemble of NNs measures predictive disagreement across the RVE bone density space and queries only those FE analyses that reduce uncertainty most. This would require far fewer than 95,992 training samples while likely achieving comparable or better accuracy — and generalizes the approach to new scaffold geometries without manual re-tuning of the two-stage scheme.

Added value to my researchUncertainty-driven sample selection maps directly to my work on spatiotemporal kernel density estimation with adaptive bandwidth: instead of querying FE analyses, an analogous active scheme selects new mobile-phone measurement locations to fill spatial uncertainty gaps in service-area delineation, reducing survey costs while maintaining R2SFCA catchment precision. The same ensemble-disagreement criterion also applies to my multi-source data fusion pipelines (GPS + social media + remote sensing) to identify where additional data would most reduce predictive uncertainty in health-outcome models.

② Upgrade NN-3's inverse identification to a physics-informed graph neural network

Extending this paperNN-3 is a shallow fully connected network that maps five scalar Wolff's law parameters to two Pearson correlation coefficients. It can only capture global correlations and discards all spatial structure in the bone density field. A direct extension: replace NN-3 with a physics-infused heterogeneous graph neural network where nodes are RVEs, edges encode mechanical connectivity (adjacent RVEs share boundary tractions), and the Wolff's law differential equation is embedded as a soft physical constraint on node updates. This would allow the inverse identification to leverage the full 3D spatial pattern of bone formation rather than eight ROI-level signal densities, increasing the information content of each in vivo X-ray data point and improving validation Pearson coefficients beyond the current P = 0.642 at month 12.

③ Extend the surrogate from single scaffold to a library of scaffold architectures via transfer learning

Extending this paperThe paper trains three NNs specifically for a single orthogonal-configuration scaffold with fixed strut diameter (500 µm) and pore size (1 mm). Re-training from scratch for a different scaffold architecture (honeycomb, gyroid, gradient porosity) would require a new full FE dataset. An extension: pre-train NN-1 and NN-2 on a diverse library of scaffold RVE geometries using a graph representation of the microstructure, then fine-tune on a small number of FE samples for each new design. This converts the paper's single-case surrogate into a reusable foundation model for scaffold mechanics, dramatically reducing the data cost of exploring design spaces and supporting the personalized implant engineering the authors envision.

The key methodological insight transfers: encode structural priors (scaffold topology / road network topology) in the pre-training phase so downstream task fine-tuning requires only small labeled datasets — reducing the data burden for under-resourced healthcare systems or cities without comprehensive open data.

④ Add explainability: which RVE regions drive bone remodeling predictions?

Extending this paperNN-1 and NN-2 are black-box fully connected networks — the paper validates accuracy (R = 0.997) but never interrogates which input features (which of the 1,261 bone density variables per RVE) most influence elastic tensor or SED predictions. An extension using gradient-based saliency maps or integrated gradients would identify the spatial loci within RVEs whose bone density most determines macroscopic stiffness and remodeling rate. This could reveal whether bone at strut junctions, pore surfaces, or scaffold core drives mechanics — informing scaffold design rules that are currently derived only from intuition or full parameter sweeps. A causal-perspective lens (avoiding dataset bias from the specific sheep's loading conditions) would further strengthen generalizability of the identified relationships.

⑤ Port the in silico pipeline to epidemiological spatiotemporal prediction

Extending this paperThe paper's architecture — (i) a surrogate that replaces expensive forward simulations, (ii) a sequential data generation strategy that samples from plausible trajectories, and (iii) an inverse identification module that calibrates model parameters against sparse observational signals — is generic. An extension maps this to epidemiological modeling: replace RVEs with spatial census units, FE2 homogenization with spatial SIR/SEIR transmission equations, Wolff's law with disease remodeling dynamics, and in vivo X-rays with administrative case counts.
