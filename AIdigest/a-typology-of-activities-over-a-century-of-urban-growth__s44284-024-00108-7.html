A typology of activities over a century of urban growth

Julie Gravier · Marc Barthelemy Published 2024-08-09 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00108-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Mining a new geohistorical database of ~1 million directory entries for Paris over almost a century (1829–1907), the authors show that urban activities sort into three dynamical classes by how they scale with population: linear for everyday-need activities (food stores, clothing, healthcare practitioners), sublinear for public services (legal, administrative, educational), and superlinear for the city's specific or transient features (fads, specialization, timely needs). These trajectories are highly sensitive to historical shocks — large-scale public works (Haussmann) and political conflicts leave clear signatures in the activity dynamics.

Why It Matters

  • Extends urban scaling from a snapshot to a century-long trajectory. Most scaling work is cross-sectional across many present-day cities; here a single city is followed through time, testing whether scaling relations actually hold as a city grows — a much stronger and rarely available test.
  • Builds a typology, not just an exponent. By classifying activities as linear / sublinear / superlinear, the work turns an abstract scaling exponent into an interpretable functional taxonomy of what a growing city needs at each stage.
  • Recovers history from data. The activity dynamics are shown to register concrete events — Haussmann's public works, sieges and political upheaval — demonstrating that economic micro-data can serve as a quantitative historiographic instrument.
  • Releases reusable open infrastructure. The ~1M-entry NAICS-coded directory dataset and full reproducibility code are deposited openly (Zenodo, Nakala), a durable resource for urban science, economic history, and digital humanities.

Core Methods

  • Geohistorical database construction: transcribe, geocode and harmonize ~1M directory entries into a consistent NAICS-inspired activity taxonomy across 1829–1907.
  • Urban scaling analysis: fit the relation between activity counts Na and population, estimating the scaling exponent β per category to label it linear (β≈1), sublinear (β<1) or superlinear (β>1).
  • Dynamical typology: group activities into three classes by their growth dynamics and their β, mapping each class to a functional role (everyday needs / public services / city-specific features).
  • Rank clocks: use rank-clock visualizations (Fig. 3) to track how the relative rank of each activity category rotates over time, exposing turnover and persistence.
  • Perturbation detection: read deviations in the trajectories against known historical shocks (Haussmann public works, political conflict) to show the dynamics' sensitivity to events.

① From a single city to a cross-city panel of activity-scaling regimes

Extending this paperThe typology is established on one city (Paris) over one century — powerful but n = 1 in the cross-sectional sense. A direct extension: assemble comparable directory/census panels for several cities and test whether the linear/sublinear/superlinear assignment of each activity is universal or path-dependent, turning the paper's typology into a falsifiable cross-city law. Long-horizon multi-city scaling work in the corpus (railways over 190 years) shows this is feasible.

Added value to my researchA validated activity-by-density typology gives me a prior for healthcare-supply scaling: if "health-care practitioners" scale linearly with population, my 2SFCA supply terms can be normalized against an expected scaling baseline, flagging genuinely under-served districts versus those merely small.

Cross-ref (corpus): Railways and urban scaling in England and Wales over 190 years of development — 10.1038/s44284-025-00351-6

② Add a spatiotemporal layer: where inside the city does each regime concentrate?

Extending this paperThe analysis aggregates activities to city/district level and to time, but the geohistorical entries are geocoded — so the superlinear "city-specific" activities could be mapped as a moving spatial field and tracked with spatiotemporal kernel density / hot-spot methods. This would reveal whether superlinear activities cluster (and migrate) while linear everyday-need activities stay diffuse, sharpening the mechanism behind the exponents.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my spatiotemporal KDE and MGWR toolkit: a century-long, finely geocoded amenity dataset is an ideal stress-test for measuring local, time-varying scaling relationships (locally varying β via GWR) rather than one global exponent.

Cross-ref (corpus): A universal scaling law of intra-urban inequality — 10.1038/s41467-026-73015-1

③ Forecast activity trajectories with an interpretable spatiotemporal model

Extending this paperThe paper describes trajectories and their shocks but does not build a predictive model. Extending it: train an interpretable time-series / graph model on the ~1M-entry series to forecast each category's count and, crucially, to attribute deviations to covariates (public-works periods, conflict). The rank clocks become a target the model must reproduce, converting historiographic narrative into testable prediction.

Added value to my researchThe shock-attribution framing maps onto my explainable-ML for spatial prediction agenda: SHAP/feature-attribution over a century of amenity dynamics is a clean benchmark for whether XAI recovers known causal events — a validation rarely possible with modern data.

Cross-ref (corpus): Explaining the prevalence, scaling and variance of urban phenomena — 10.1038/s41562-016-0012

④ Link the superlinear "specialization" class to economic complexity

Extending this paperThe paper labels superlinear activities as "specialization / fads / timely needs" but stops at the dynamical class. Extending it: connect this class to economic-complexity and diversification theory — do superlinear activities mark the city's "complex" frontier, and do they co-locate following relatedness rules? This would give the typology an economic-mechanism backbone instead of a phenomenological one.

Added value to my researchFor my urban-agglomeration and regional-collaboration work, a century-long complexity trajectory offers a way to study how specialized service clusters emerge and network — directly informing job-housing and regional-collaboration network models with a historical baseline.

⑤ Couple activity scaling with demographic dynamics into a generative urban model

Extending this paperThe paper treats population as an exogenous denominator. Extending it: build a coupled generative model in which district population and activity counts co-evolve, so the observed scaling exponents emerge from demographic flows plus a placement process — reproducing both Fig. 1 trajectories and the rank clocks. Corpus work on demography-driven universal urban patterns provides the mechanism.

Added value to my researchA demographically grounded generative model is the substrate for my agent-based and physics-informed graph-learning work: encode scaling/conservation priors so a model calibrated on historical Paris can be transferred to forecast amenity provision in fast-growing contemporary cities — a reusable healthy-cities planning tool.
