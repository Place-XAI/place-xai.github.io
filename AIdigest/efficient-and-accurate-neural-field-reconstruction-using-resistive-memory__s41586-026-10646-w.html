Efficient and accurate neural-field reconstruction using resistive memory

Yifei Yu · Xinyuan Zhang · Shaocong Wang · Woyu Zhang · Xiuzhe Wu · Yangu He · Jichang Yang · Yue Zhang · Ning Lin · Bo Wang · Xi Chen · Songqi Wang · Xiaoshan Wu · Shihao Han · Yi Li · Meng Xu · Hegan Chen · Wenkui Zhang · Jingyi Chen · Xumeng Zhang · Xiaojuan Qi · Dashan Shang · Qi Liu · Zhongrui Wang · Kwang-Ting Cheng · Ming Liu Published 2026-06-10 DOI 10.1038/s41586-026-10646-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A software–hardware co-optimization framework couples implicit neural-field representations (compressed via low-rank decomposition and structured pruning) with a resistive-memory computing-in-memory chip to reconstruct complex signals from sparse inputs. On a 40-nm 256 Kb resistive-memory macro, the system achieves up to 32.3× energy-efficiency gain and up to 38.8× parallelism gain over digital baselines across 3D CT sparse reconstruction, novel view synthesis, and dynamic-scene novel view synthesis — all without compromising reconstruction quality. This work establishes resistive memory as a practical substrate for efficient and robust medical AI and 3D vision at the edge.

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the von Neumann bottleneck for AI inference. By co-designing the neural field representation with a resistive-memory chip, the system eliminates the energy-hungry data-movement cycle that dominates conventional digital hardware — a fundamental architectural advance rather than incremental engineering.
  • Orders-of-magnitude efficiency with no quality penalty. 23.5–32.3× energy gains and 6.2–38.8× parallelism gains are demonstrated simultaneously across three distinct reconstruction tasks (CT, NeRF, dynamic NeRF), showing the gains are not task-specific.
  • Enables sparse-input reconstruction for resource-constrained settings. Medical imaging devices, AR/VR headsets, and embodied robots all operate under power and bandwidth constraints; this framework directly targets those regimes.
  • Provides a complete, reproducible co-design methodology. Hardware-aware quantization (HAQ) circuit, Gaussian encoder exploiting intrinsic memristor stochasticity, and open-source simulation code (GitHub) make the approach transferable to other neural-field tasks.

Core Methods

  • Neural field implicit representation: signals (CT volumes, radiance fields) are represented implicitly as neural networks (multi-layer perceptrons with Gaussian positional encoding) rather than explicit voxel grids — enabling compact, differentiable signal storage.
  • Low-rank decomposition and structured pruning: MLP weight matrices are factorized via low-rank decomposition; progressive structured pruning removes entire neurons/channels during training, making the compressed network hardware-friendly with no unstructured sparsity.
  • Hardware-aware quantization (HAQ): a write-verify-style quantization circuit maps low-rank MLP weights precisely onto resistive-memory conductance states, closing the software–hardware accuracy gap created by device variation and noise.
  • Gaussian encoder on resistive memory: the positional encoding layer exploits the intrinsic stochastic conductance fluctuations of resistive memory to generate Gaussian random features efficiently, turning a hardware imperfection into a computational asset.
  • Computing-in-memory (CIM) MLP engine: matrix–vector multiplications of the MLP inference are performed in-situ within the resistive-memory array, avoiding DRAM accesses and the associated energy/latency overhead (von Neumann bottleneck).
  • Hardware-aware population-based training: a co-optimization loop jointly tunes software (MLP weights, pruning schedule) and hardware (resistive-memory programming parameters) via evolutionary algorithms and hardware simulation (Extended Data Fig. 4).

The reconstructed field from sparse sensors could then be fed into downstream analytics at near-zero energy cost.
