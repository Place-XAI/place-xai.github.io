Envisioning better benchmarks for machine learning PDE solvers

Johannes Brandstetter Published 2024-12-13 DOI 10.1038/s42256-024-00962-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Machine learning–based PDE solvers have been evaluated against weak numerical baselines and with a pervasive reporting bias favouring positive results, producing an overly optimistic view of the field. The path forward lies not in incremental academic comparisons but in defining open, rigorous benchmark challenges — akin to an "ImageNet for PDEs" or a "CASP competition for PDE modelling" — that genuinely expose where machine learning adds unique value over classical numerical methods.

① Build the "ImageNet for PDEs" as a living benchmark that rewards generalization, not just accuracy

Extending this paperThe paper argues convincingly that an ImageNet-style benchmark is missing for PDEs, but stops short of specifying what it should contain. A concrete extension: design a multi-PDE benchmark suite (Navier–Stokes, heat equation, wave equation, Darcy flow, across varied geometries and resolutions) with a held-out test distribution explicitly shifted from training — so that models must generalize rather than overfit to specific solver configurations. The benchmark would quantify not only error but also speed, stability under long rollouts, and uncertainty calibration, directly addressing the three layers of criteria the paper enumerates.

Added value to my researchGeospatial surrogate modelling faces the exact same benchmark vacuum: spatiotemporal prediction tasks (urban heat, pollution dispersion, flood inundation) lack standardized evaluation suites. Designing a GeoAI benchmark inspired by this paper's CASP analogy — with fixed held-out spatial contexts, explicit distribution shift, and multiple metrics — would elevate reproducibility in my explainable spatiotemporal ML work and make cross-method comparisons credible.

② Operationalize the "weak baseline" critique with a formal baseline-strength metric

Extending this paperThe paper qualitatively identifies "weak baselines" as a structural problem but offers no operational criterion for detecting them automatically during peer review. An extension: define a baseline-strength score — e.g., fraction of hyperparameter grid explored for the numerical comparator, whether the classical solver is run at the same computational budget as the ML model — and train a lightweight meta-classifier on the McGreivy & Hakim corpus to flag papers that likely overclaim. This would convert the paper's call for rigor into an actionable, automatable review tool.

Added value to my researchIn GeoAI, the analogous problem is spatial autocorrelation leakage: models are evaluated on random splits that do not respect spatial structure, making results non-transferable. Applying the paper's logic, I can formalize a spatial-leakage severity score that flags evaluation protocols in spatial ML papers — directly strengthening the reproducibility and open-science agenda in my geospatial methods work.

③ Apply transfer-operator learning to bridge the benchmark fragmentation the paper diagnoses

Extending this paperThe paper attributes benchmark fragmentation to the enormous diversity of PDE families and solver requirements. A direct response: develop transfer operator learning frameworks that learn a single backbone operator across multiple PDE families (sharing representations across Navier–Stokes, heat, wave equations) and evaluate it on a diverse benchmark — directly testing whether the paper's hypothesis holds that "machine learning might not disrupt each subproblem at every scale." Transfer learning would reduce the number of specialist models while making benchmark comparisons across PDE types tractable.
