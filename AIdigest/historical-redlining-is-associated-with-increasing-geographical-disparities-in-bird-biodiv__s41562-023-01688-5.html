Historical redlining is associated with increasing geographical disparities in bird biodiversity sampling in the United States

Diego Ellis-Soto · Melissa Chapman · Dexter H. Locke Published 2023-09-07 DOI 10.1038/s41562-023-01688-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Historically redlined (HOLC grade D) urban neighbourhoods across 195 US cities remain the most undersampled areas for bird biodiversity today: sampling density odds are 74% lower and survey completeness is ~7 percentage points lower than formerly A-graded areas. This knowledge gap has grown by 35.6% over the past 20 years, driven by the explosive growth of citizen-science platforms (chiefly eBird) whose volunteer demographics mirror the socioeconomic inequities originally encoded in 1930s mortgage maps — meaning that digital biodiversity data is actively reproducing, not erasing, historical environmental injustice.

Why It Matters

  • Connects a 90-year-old housing policy to today's biodiversity knowledge base. The paper quantifies for the first time how 1930s race-based mortgage zoning shapes which urban neighbourhoods appear — and are absent — in the world's largest bird database (GBIF/eBird) today.
  • Knowledge gaps can entrench conservation inequity. Undersampled areas appear species-poor on any distribution map, which risks directing conservation funding and restoration efforts away from already-disadvantaged communities — creating a feedback loop of disinvestment.
  • The digital data revolution is making things worse, not better. Citizen-science data volumes grew enormously from 2000–2020, but the sampling disparity between HOLC A and D areas increased rather than converged — counterintuitive and policy-urgent.
  • Provides a coldspots map to guide targeted action. The supplementary dataset identifies specific HOLC polygons across 195 cities where survey completeness is critically low, turning the analysis into an operational targeting tool for NGOs and grassroots organizations.
  • Proposes biodiversity sampling density as a new EPA environmental-justice indicator. The Justice40 Initiative already channels climate funding to disadvantaged communities; adding a biodiversity-sampling metric could channel scientific infrastructure investment to the same neighbourhoods.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

HOLC polygons
9,851 HOLC-defined neighbourhood polygons across 195 US cities, sourced from the Mapping Inequality Project (University of Richmond, dsl.richmond.edu). Polygons retain the original A/B/C/D grading system from the 1930s Home Owners' Loan Corporation.
Bird records
12,297,506 georeferenced bird occurrence records (class Aves) queried from the Global Biodiversity Information Facility (GBIF) (gbif.org), including the eBird and iNaturalist databases, covering records from 1932 to October 2022. After removing empty polygons, 8,909 polygons were retained.
Temporal scope
Records span 1932–2022; temporal trend analysis focuses on 2000–2020 to capture the digital citizen-science era.
Vegetation (NDVI)
Composite mean NDVI derived from Landsat 8 surface reflectance on Google Earth Engine (earthengine.google.com), 5-year mean 2014–2019, per HOLC polygon.
Protected open space
Protected Areas Database of the United States (PAD-US), downloaded from the USGS (usgs.gov); used to calculate percent open-space coverage per HOLC polygon.
Climate
Mean annual temperature and precipitation from CHELSA Climatology (chelsa-climate.org), monthly station averages 1979–2013 at 1-km² resolution.
Population
Census block-group population from the 2013–2017 American Community Survey (ACS), areal-interpolated to each HOLC polygon.
Zenodo deposit
All analysis data and code available at zenodo.org/doi/10.5281/zenodo.8052525; GBIF derived dataset at doi.org/10.15468/dd.ha9ksv.

Core Methods

  • Sampling density calculation: bird records per km² per HOLC polygon; log-transformed to meet model assumptions. Separately computed for eBird, iNaturalist, and "other" (museum/academic) data sources.
  • Survey completeness: estimated per HOLC polygon using the KnowBR package (v2.0) — the proportion of expected species richness captured by observed species richness, derived from species accumulation curves.
  • Biodiversity coldspot identification: polygons flagged as coldspots if species-accumulation-curve slope >0.3, completeness <50%, and record-to-species ratio <3 (KnowBR parameter recommendations).
  • Mixed-effects models (3 models): (1) binary GLMM (sampled or not), (2) linear LMM for log-sampling density, (3) linear LMM for completeness — all fit with lme4 in R, with HOLC grade as fixed effect, MSA as random intercept, HOLC grade as random slope per MSA; NDVI, open space (%), and population density as fixed covariates (z-score standardized); model selection via AIC; Type I and II ANOVA for robustness.
  • Temporal trend analysis: generalized additive model (GAM) predicting yearly sampling density as a function of HOLC grade × year interaction, capturing whether the disparity expands or contracts over 2000–2020.
  • Areal interpolation: Census block-group population weighted by proportional overlap with HOLC polygons (sf package v1.0.12 and tigris v1.5 in R v4.1.1).

① Quantify the downstream impact of sampling bias on species distribution models and conservation funding flows

Extending this paperThis paper documents where bird data is absent but does not trace the consequence: which bird species have their ranges systematically under-estimated in redlined areas, and whether federal conservation grants (e.g., US Fish & Wildlife grants to metro areas) are correlated with the sampling-density gradient rather than true habitat quality? A direct extension would couple the coldspots map with species distribution models (SDMs) fitted separately on HOLC-A-rich vs. HOLC-D-rich data splits, quantifying the bias in predicted richness and range boundaries. The paper's own GAM framework could be repurposed: instead of predicting sampling density over time, predict SDM uncertainty as a function of cumulative sampling deficit per polygon. The multidecadal urbanization legacy paper in our corpus shows that historical urban-development trajectories create lasting biodiversity debts — a closely parallel logic that could frame the SDM-bias analysis.

The paper's mixed-effects decomposition (HOLC grade + NDVI + open space + population density) maps directly onto a bias-corrected 2SFCA where the catchment weights adjust for the observed volunteered-data density gradient rather than assuming uniform coverage.

② Model the socio-spatial determinants of citizen-science participation with explainable GeoAI

Extending this paperThe paper controls for NDVI, open space, and population density but leaves the human participation mechanism as a black box: who are the eBird volunteers in each MSA, why do they avoid certain neighbourhoods, and which neighbourhood-level features (not just HOLC grade) best predict sampling density? ③ Extend the redlining-biodiversity link to climate risk compounding: a multi-hazard environmental justice audit

Extending this paperThis paper shows one environmental inequality leg (bird data scarcity) of redlining's legacy. The corpus contains two redlining-climate papers covering heat exposure and climate risk across HOLC grades. A powerful extension is a compound exposure analysis: for the same 195-city, 9,851-polygon dataset, combine this paper's bird coldspot indicator with urban heat island intensity and climate risk scores per polygon, and test whether the triple compound (least sampled + hottest + most climate-exposed) disproportionately falls in D-graded areas. The paper's mixed-effects framework generalizes directly: replace sampling density with heat anomaly or flood risk as the outcome, with identical controls. This would quantify whether biodiversity sampling gaps and physical climate risks reinforce each other spatially — making the case for a unified EPA EJScreen indicator far stronger.

④ Disentangle historical policy legacy from contemporary income inequality using a regression-discontinuity design

Extending this paperThe paper's mixed-effects model treats HOLC grade as a fixed effect alongside contemporary covariates (NDVI, population density), but cannot cleanly separate the causal effect of 1930s grading from present-day income sorting that occurred because of redlining. The corpus contains a paper showing that contemporary income inequality outweighs historic redlining in shaping intra-urban heat disparities in LA — a finding that could equally apply to bird sampling. A spatial regression discontinuity design (RDD) exploiting the sharp HOLC grade boundaries (where adjacent A/B vs. C/D polygons are near-identical in ecology but differ in historical grading) would isolate the legacy effect of the grade label itself from correlated socioeconomic endogenous sorting. This is precisely the boundary design used in the climate-risk companion paper and would give this paper's causal interpretation a firmer footing.

Added value to my researchSpatial RDD at administrative boundaries is a method I could apply in healthcare accessibility: exploiting county or district boundaries where facility placement policy shifted discontinuously — holding landscape ecology constant while varying the governance regime — to identify causal effects of policy on spatial access patterns, using MGWR to model the gradient of effect near the boundary.

⑤ Design optimized park-and-community-science deployment strategies to fill biodiversity coldspots equitably

Extending this paperThe paper identifies coldspots but leaves the "how to fix it" question largely to qualitative strategies (grassroots organizations, STEM programmes). A quantitative extension: treating each coldspot polygon as a "service gap" and the addition of a citizen-science facilitation event or a new park bird-monitoring station as a "facility," a p-median or maximum-coverage location model could solve for the optimal set of interventions to maximally close the sampling deficit within a fixed budget. This is analogous to the 15-minute city facility-redistribution framework in our corpus. The corpus also contains a paper showing that parks with variable features in multiple US cities drive avian diversity — directly informing which park attributes to prioritize when designing new monitoring sites in coldspot zones.
