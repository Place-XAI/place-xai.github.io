Well-being from nature exposure depends on socio-environmental contexts in Paraguay

Violeta Berdejo-Espinola · Richard A. Fuller · Renee Zahnow Published 2024-05-01 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00058-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The well-being benefit of urban nature is not universal — it depends on socio-environmental context. In a survey of 1,119 residents of metropolitan Asunción, Paraguay, a stronger connection to nature was positively associated with life satisfaction across the board. But everyday greenness near the home behaved oppositely by setting: in formal neighbourhoods it tracked higher well-being (as in the Global North), whereas in informal settlements higher residential greenness was associated with lower life satisfaction. Intentional, motivated nature-seeking — rather than passive proximity — is what benefits the urban poor.

★

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the "greener is always better" assumption. Most nature-and-health evidence comes from the Global North; this study shows the same proximate greenness can carry opposite well-being signs depending on who lives there and how — a direct caution against transplanting Global-North dose-response curves to Global-South cities.
  • Separates exposure type from exposure context. It disentangles passive residential greenness from active nature connection / nature-seeking, showing the two are not interchangeable and can point in different directions for the same person.
  • Centres informal settlements. By contrasting formal vs. informal parts of one metropolitan area, it surfaces an environmental-justice nuance — in deprived settings, nearby vegetation may signal neglect, hazard, or insecurity rather than amenity.
  • Reframes green-space policy for equity. Investing in greenery without addressing the socio-environmental conditions that make it usable and safe may not yield the expected mental-health returns for the urban poor.

Core Methods

  • Survey instrument: structured questionnaire capturing well-being/life satisfaction, the Nature Relatedness Scale, nature-exposure behaviour (context and intentionality), and sociodemographics.
  • Remote-sensing greenness linkage: compute residential greenness from ESA WorldCover 10 m land cover around each respondent's home and merge with survey records.
  • Stepwise regression model selection: a stepwise procedure (Fig. 1) builds the well-being models from candidate predictors of nature exposure, connection, and context.
  • Interaction / moderation analysis: test greenness × settlement type interactions (Fig. 2) to show how the greenness–well-being relationship flips between formal and informal settings.
  • Context stratification: contrast formal-neighbourhood vs. informal-settlement residents to interpret divergent effect directions.

① Replace the binary formal/informal moderator with a learned deprivation surface

Extending this paperThe paper's pivotal moderator is a binary formal-vs-informal label drawn for the study area (Fig. 3). That dichotomy hides gradients — two informal settlements can differ enormously in tenure security, services, and hazard. A direct extension: replace the binary with a continuous, remotely-sensed socio-environmental deprivation index (built-form texture, roof material, canopy fragmentation, flood exposure) and re-estimate the greenness × context interaction along that gradient, testing where exactly the well-being sign of greenness flips.

Added value to my researchThis is a multi-source data-fusion task squarely in my wheelhouse: fusing satellite imagery, POIs and survey records into a deprivation surface, then using it as a spatially varying coefficient in MGWR so that nature's effect is allowed to vary continuously across the city rather than by a single dummy.

Cross-ref (corpus): Disproportionate flood exposure for slum populations of the Global South — 10.1038/s44284-025-00273-3

② Turn the survey snapshot into a dose–response curve with explainable ML

Extending this paperThe paper establishes a direction (greenness helps in formal areas, hurts in informal ones) but not a dose–response shape. Extending it: fit a flexible, monotonicity-free learner (gradient boosting + SHAP) on greenness, nature relatedness, and context, and recover the per-context response curves — does the informal-area penalty saturate, reverse, or have a threshold? This converts the paper's interaction term into an interpretable curve planners can act on.

Added value to my researchThis operationalizes my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction line: SHAP interaction values would attribute exactly how much of the greenness effect is conditioned on settlement context, giving a transparent, auditable alternative to the stepwise regression.

Cross-ref (corpus): A generalized relationship between dose of greenness and mental health response — 10.1038/s44284-025-00285-z

③ Add accessibility: it may be usable parks, not nearby pixels, that matter

Extending this paperThe paper measures greenness in pixels around the home — a supply-side proximity measure that ignores whether residents can actually reach and use safe, maintained green space. Extending it: complement WorldCover greenness with a network-based accessibility metric to usable public parks, and test whether park accessibility (not raw greenness) is what carries the well-being benefit for informal-settlement residents.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my 2SFCA/R2SFCA framework to green-space provision: treat usable parks as "supply", residents as "demand", and compute context-stratified accessibility — reframing urban-nature equity as a service-area delineation problem.

Cross-ref (corpus): Unequal access to social, environmental and health amenities in US urban parks — 10.1038/s44284-024-00153-2

④ Stress-test transferability: does the Asunción flip generalize across Global-South cities?

Extending this paperThe result is a single-metro case (Asunción). Its boldest claim — that greenness benefits depend on socio-environmental context — begs a multi-city test. Extending it: replicate the greenness × informality interaction across a panel of Global-South cities using harmonized WorldCover greenness and settlement layers, and quantify how much of the effect heterogeneity is between-city versus within-city.

Added value to my researchThis feeds my urban-agglomeration / regional-collaboration work and reproducibility agenda: a FAIR, multi-city pipeline (open greenness raster + open survey schema) that lets the Asunción finding be falsified or confirmed elsewhere, with spatial entropy summarizing inequality of exposure.

Cross-ref (corpus): Contrasting inequality in human exposure to greenspace between cities of Global North and Global South — 10.1038/s41467-022-32258-4

⑤ Embed the result in a 15-minute-city nature layer for healthy-city planning

Extending this paperThe paper implies that passive greenness is the wrong lever for the urban poor, while motivated nature-seeking helps. Extending it toward planning: encode this as a design rule in a proximity-based ("15-minute") nature layer — prioritising safe, attractive, intentionally-visitable green destinations over raw vegetation cover in informal areas, and simulate the well-being payoff of different siting strategies.
