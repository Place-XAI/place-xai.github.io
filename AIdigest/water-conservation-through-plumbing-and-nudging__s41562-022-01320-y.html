Water conservation through plumbing and nudging

Sumit Agarwal · Eduardo Araral · Mingxuan Fan · Yu Qin · Huanhuan Zheng Published 2022-03-31 DOI 10.1038/s41562-022-01320-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A nationwide plumbing upgrade programme in Singapore (Home Improvement Programme) causally reduced residential water consumption by 3.5%, a persistent effect sustained over a decade and robust across all demographic subgroups — driven by infrastructure change, not behaviour change. In sharp contrast, a nationwide peer-comparison nudging programme produced no net reduction in water use, as boomerang effects among low-usage households cancelled savings from high-usage households. Efficiency improvements also enhanced the efficacy of other conservation policies and buffered water use against extreme heat and air pollution, while the increase in housing value resulting from the upgrade exceeded households' private costs.

★

Why It Matters

  • Largest causal evaluation of plumbing efficiency to date. Drawing on 98.2 million monthly billing observations from 1.5 million households over nine years, this is the most statistically powered causal study of residential plumbing efficiency in the literature, resolving selection-bias problems that plagued prior before-and-after comparisons.
  • Separates hardware from behaviour — definitively. The 3.5% saving is substantially smaller than engineering estimates, revealing a real-world behavioural rebound that neither disappears nor worsens over ten years — providing a honest, durable baseline for policy design.
  • Exposes a structural failure of national-scale nudging. Peer-comparison messaging that works in small-scale RCTs produces a null effect — or worse — when scaled nationwide, because boomerang effects from the majority of below-average consumers erase gains from above-average consumers.
  • Infrastructure interacts with other policies. Households with new plumbing respond 0.69% more strongly to peer comparison and are better buffered against heat- and pollution-driven water demand spikes, showing that efficiency investments are a platform multiplier.
  • Reframes the cost–benefit calculus. Although water-bill savings are modest, housing value appreciation covers household costs — an externality that planners often ignore when evaluating green infrastructure subsidies.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Primary dataset
Anonymized monthly water billing records from PUB, Singapore's National Water Agency — 1,506,296 unique accounts (all public HDB housing) from January 2011 to December 2019 (9 years). Baseline analytical sample: 98,291,320 observations from 1,503,350 accounts in 10,188 HDB blocks, after trimming the top/bottom 1% per flat type. Access: onsite at NUS under non-disclosure agreement on request to PUB.
Block-level housing
Year of construction, HIP status, public rental status — from the HDB public database (services2.hdb.gov.sg). Private rental status scraped from SRX.com.sg (2019–2021).
Demographics
Administrative records for 2.8 million adult Singaporeans in 2011; aggregated to block level (family size, gender, ethnicity, age cohorts). Individual-level matching to accounts not possible due to anonymization.
Weather & air quality
Daily weather by station from Meteorological Service Singapore (weather.gov.sg); historical 24-hour PSI readings by monitor from the National Environment Agency (haze.gov.sg). Block-level indicators derived via inverse distance weighting across all stations within 10 km, 2011–2019.
Resale transactions
178,185 HDB resale flat transactions (2011–2019) from the HDB public database, used for the housing-value cost–benefit analysis. A sub-sample of 18,160 transactions within ±24 months of HIP completion used for the discontinuity test.
Nudging programme
Nationwide redesign of utility bills from August 2016; all 1.5 million accounts received one of four norm-based messages on their monthly bill. Nudging analysis uses 96,786,609 observations (slightly fewer because message type depends on the prior month's reading, dropping first account observations).
Code & ancillary data
Stata code and non-PUB datasets (housing characteristics, demographics, weather, resale transactions) are openly available on GitHub: github.com/fmsgp/DataCode-HIP. Singapore base map from data.gov.sg.

Core Methods

  • Staggered difference-in-differences (DiD): the core causal identification strategy, comparing treated HIP blocks with non-HIP blocks before and after upgrade completion. Staggered timing (projects completed in different years 2009–2019) allows within-programme comparisons. Two-way clustering of standard errors by block and year–month.
  • Event-study analysis (equation 3): estimates annual treatment effects up to 9 years before and 10 years after HIP completion to test the parallel-trends assumption and trace the long-run dynamic; validates persistence of the effect over a decade.
  • Heterogeneous treatment effects (equation 2): subgroup analysis across HIP cohort, flat type, flat age, ownership, water-demand quartile, family size, gender, ethnicity and age cohort (quartile cuts).
  • Interaction models (equations 4, 5): test whether plumbing efficiency amplifies the effect of (a) peer comparison and price-increase policies, and (b) extreme environmental conditions (top-10% temperature, top-10% rainfall, PSI > 100).
  • Fixed-effects model for nudging (equation 6): quasi-experimental evaluation of nationwide peer-comparison bill redesign, controlling for household fixed effects, month and year–month fixed effects, weather, drought and price-increase shocks.
  • Regression discontinuity (equation 10): tests for a consumption discontinuity at the national and neighbourhood norm thresholds on the monthly bill to isolate the causal norm effect, finds no credible discontinuity.
  • Bayesian model comparison: Bayes factor (BF = 0.00013) confirms the null nudging model over the alternative, supplementing frequentist results.
  • Cost–benefit analysis: estimates private savings in water bills versus cost of upgrade; uses a hedonic housing-value model on 178,185 resale transactions to compute the HIP premium (S$12,320 per flat).

① Transfer the staggered DiD framework to urban greenspace and heat exposure across cities

Extending this paperThe paper's causal engine — staggered DiD with 10-year panel data from a natural experiment — is locked to a single city and a single infrastructure type. A direct extension is to replicate the same quasi-experimental design for urban heat mitigation interventions (green roofs, shade trees, cool pavements) that have similarly phased, block-level rollouts and long-run outcomes. This would also test the paper's finding that plumbing efficiency buffers heat-driven demand spikes: does urban cooling achieve the same buffering through a different mechanism?

Added value to my researchThe spatiotemporal design maps cleanly onto my spatiotemporal kernel density and MGWR/GWR toolkit: treatment timing varies across space (like a moving treatment surface), and the block-level heat-water interaction mirrors the kind of spatially heterogeneous policy effect I routinely estimate. Combining staggered DiD with geographically weighted regression could reveal where the plumbing-heat interaction is strongest — a spatial heterogeneity result the original paper does not provide.

② Use explainable ML to learn what predicts the boomerang nudging effect before deployment

Extending this paperThe paper's most actionable finding is that nationwide nudging fails because low-usage households increase consumption upon receiving "below average" messages — the boomerang effect. The paper characterises this ex post using decile splits and regression. A prospective extension: train an explainable gradient-boosted model on pre-treatment covariates (flat type, family size, baseline consumption decile, demographics, weather exposure) to predict which households will exhibit a boomerang effect before the campaign launches, enabling precision targeting. SHAP/LIME decompositions would reveal the demographic and geographic drivers of boomerang susceptibility.

Added value to my researchThis is my explainable ML agenda applied to a clean causal experiment rather than an observational study: the treatment assignment is quasi-random (nationwide bill redesign), so the ML model is predicting heterogeneous treatment effects rather than confounded outcomes. The resulting SHAP values would serve as spatially granular policy scores directly analogous to the R2SFCA demand weights I compute for healthcare facility access.

Cross-ref (corpus): Large and persistent effects of green energy defaults in the household and business sectors — 10.1038/s41562-021-01070-3

③ Link water-consumption data to fine-scale socioeconomic inequality measures for an equity audit

Extending this paperThe paper shows that HIP effects are consistent across flat types (a proxy for income) — a reassuring equity finding. However, it does not ask whether access to the plumbing upgrade itself was equitably distributed, nor whether post-HIP water-bill savings translate into meaningful welfare gains for the lowest-income public renters (who showed only a 1% effect versus 3.5% overall). An extension would pair the paper's billing panel with household income data and use the paper's quasi-experimental design to estimate distributional welfare effects — who saves the most in absolute and relative terms, and does HIP narrow or widen the water-cost burden across the income distribution?

Added value to my researchEquity audits of urban infrastructure are central to my healthy-cities and 15-minute-neighbourhood work. The method mirrors my 2SFCA equity analysis for healthcare access: decompose an aggregate efficiency gain into its distributional implications across groups. A water-equity score analogous to a spatial accessibility score could be mapped at block level, feeding housing-price and public-health models.

Cross-ref (corpus): Urban inequality, the housing crisis and deteriorating water access in US cities — 10.1038/s44284-024-00180-z

④ Quantify the energy–water nexus interaction: does efficient plumbing also reduce electricity demand?

Extending this paperThe paper focuses entirely on water. Yet heating water for showers and washing is a major driver of residential electricity use, and Singapore's tropical context means air-conditioning interacts with bathing behaviour. An extension linked to the paper's natural experiment: obtain electricity billing records from the same PUB accounts and run a parallel staggered DiD to estimate the co-benefit of plumbing upgrades on electricity consumption. The paper's finding that heat drives water use (Fig. 5) already hints at an energy nexus; this would close the loop.

Added value to my researchMulti-utility data fusion (water + electricity + demographics + weather) is exactly the kind of multi-source data fusion problem I work on. The linked billing panel would be a clean, anonymized dataset for testing physics-informed or cross-modal graph learning, where water and electricity consumption nodes are coupled via household behaviour and the physical laws of hot-water heating.

Cross-ref (corpus): Electrical appliances moderate households' water demand response to heat — 10.1038/s41467-018-07833-3

⑤ Port the quasi-experimental design to cross-city water governance comparisons

Extending this paperThe paper is confined to Singapore's single-city, centrally managed HDB system — a deliberate methodological choice that maximises internal validity. The natural next step is external validity: does a plumbing-efficiency programme generate the same 3.5% saving, with the same demographic heterogeneity, in cities with different housing types, water prices and governance systems? Replication using data from cities that have implemented comparable retrofit programmes (e.g., water utility rebate programmes in Australia, the US or China) would test whether the null nudging result and the persistent efficiency effect are generalizable laws or Singapore-specific artefacts.
