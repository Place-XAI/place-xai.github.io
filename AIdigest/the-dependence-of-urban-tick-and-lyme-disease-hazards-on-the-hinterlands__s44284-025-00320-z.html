The dependence of urban tick and Lyme disease hazards on the hinterlands

Sara L. Gandy · Jessica L. Hall · Grace Plahe · Kirsty Watkinson · David Johnson · Richard J. Birtles · Lucy Gilbert Published 2025-09-23 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00320-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 16 UK cities, urban greenspaces carry two- to threefold lower tick and Lyme disease hazards than nearby hinterland woodlands. Crucially, the variation in a city's tick and Lyme disease hazard is driven by the tick abundance and landcover (woodland and built-up) of its rural hinterland (5–10 km out), not by the city's own internal landcover or even urban–hinterland connectivity. Cities and their surroundings behave as a coupled meta-ecosystem, so urban eco-epidemiology depends on the rural landscape beyond the city limits.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reframes urban disease risk as a regional, not local, problem. Disease-mitigation strategies usually assume within-city factors are the prime drivers; this work shows the surrounding hinterland landcover is the stronger predictor — a result with direct implications for where greenspace and health interventions are targeted.
  • Quantifies a reassuring but actionable gradient. Recreation in urban parks is substantially safer (2–3× lower hazard) than in rural woodlands, yet the high footfall of city parks means even low per-area hazard can matter at the population level.
  • Operationalizes the meta-ecosystem concept for cities. It is the first application of the rural–urban meta-ecosystem framing to terrestrial disease ecology, giving urban science a transferable lens for coupled socio-ecological systems.
  • Calls for cross-boundary governance. Because hazard drivers sit in a different administrative district from the affected residents, the paper argues for collaboration between urban and rural governance bodies — a concrete policy lever.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Field sampling
162 sites across 16 UK small cities (population 50,000–100,000): five urban greenspaces (0–5 km from centre) and five hinterland woodlands (5–10 km) per city (Burnley had six of each). Surveyed at least once, with tick-positive cities re-surveyed, giving 221 site visits in 2022–2023 (May–August).
Tick measurements
Standard blanket-drag transects (1 m × 1 m over 10 m), 15 transects per visit (12–13 at 19 visits). Analyses used ~3,254 transects total (1,624 hinterland + 1,630 urban for the tick model) and 109 urban / 112 hinterland site visits for the Lyme model.
Pathogen testing
Each nymph tested for the Borrelia burgdorferi s.l. complex by DNA extraction and real-time PCR (23S rDNA, later ospA assay; n = 282 cross-checked, identical results).
Landcover & connectivity
UKCEH Land Cover Map 2021 (Land Parcels, GB) — % woodland (broadleaved + coniferous) and % built-up cover for urban (0–5 km) and hinterland (5–10 km) rings; least-cost-path connectivity (deer cost surface) computed with the grainscape R package.
Confounders
City latitude, population size (ONS), annual cumulative rainfall (Met Office Climate Averages 1991–2020), survey year, month, and ground-vegetation density (sward-stick index).
Comparison data
Previously published urban hazard values for five southern-England cities (Bath, Bristol, London, Salisbury, Southampton) mapped for context but excluded from statistics.

Analysis datasets and statistical code at Code Ocean 10.24433/CO.6388252.v1; Source Data for Fig. 2 provided as CSV.

Core Methods

  • Standardized field protocol: blanket-drag transects for questing Ixodes ricinus nymph density; PCR on each nymph for B. burgdorferi s.l. to derive density of infected nymphs (the "environmental disease hazard").
  • Binomial GLMMs: because counts were strongly zero-inflated, hazards were modeled as presence/absence — nymph presence at the transect level (tick hazard index) and infected-nymph presence at the site-visit level (Lyme hazard index), with site, city and survey month as random intercepts.
  • Hypothesis-driven model suite: separate models test (1) urban vs. hinterland, (2) effect of hinterland nymph density, (3) hinterland landcover, (4) within-city landcover, and (5) urban–hinterland connectivity.
  • Landcover extraction: % woodland and % built-up computed in concentric rings (0–5 km city, 5–10 km hinterland) from the UKCEH 2021 map using R sf and raster.
  • Connectivity via least-cost paths: deer-movement cost surface (built-up = 1000, woodland = 1) and grainscape network paths, normalized by woodland patch size.
  • Model selection & checks: VIF collinearity screening (drop > 4), AICc dredge selection (MuMIn), DHARMa diagnostics, likelihood-ratio tests; analyses in R 4.2.2.

Expanded Ideas — this paper's extensibility & added value to my research

Each idea stays anchored to this paper: first how the paper itself could be extended (drawing on our corpus), then the added value for my research lines (migis.net — GeoAI, 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility, explainable & physics-informed graph learning, multi-source data fusion).

① From a 5–10 km ring to a learned hinterland kernel

Extending this paperThe paper fixes "hinterland" as a single 5–10 km ring and "city" as 0–5 km — a coarse, hand-chosen geometry. A direct extension: replace the binary rings with a distance-weighted landcover kernel whose decay shape (and effective range) is estimated per host type, so the model learns how far woodland/built-up signal actually propagates into the city rather than assuming it. This would also resolve why ticks (deer-driven, long range) but not Lyme pathogens (small-mammal/bird-driven, short range) respond to hinterland landcover.

Added value to my researchThis is exactly the distance-decay estimation problem at the heart of my 2SFCA/R2SFCA work: a host-specific learned kernel maps cleanly onto learned catchment functions for healthcare service areas, replacing assumed Gaussian/power decay with empirically fitted decay.

Cross-ref (corpus): Mitigating urban heat island through neighboring rural land cover — 10.1038/s44284-024-00091-z

② Turn the meta-ecosystem GLMM into an explainable spatial-prediction model

Extending this paperThe paper's GLMM is interpretable but linear and binomial, and it leaves much between-city variance unexplained. Extending it: fit an explainable ML ensemble (gradient boosting + SHAP) on the same predictors plus richer hinterland descriptors (woodland fragmentation, patch size, edge density), then use SHAP interaction values to test whether hinterland woodland × built-up effects are truly additive — directly probing the paper's claim that the two are independent.

Added value to my researchThis slots straight into my explainable machine learning for spatial prediction line: a hazard surface for cities not surveyed, with feature attributions that planners can audit — the same recipe I use for accessibility and housing-price prediction.

Cross-ref (corpus): Urbanization and scrub typhus expansion in temperate settings — 10.1038/s44284-025-00262-6

③ Couple hazard supply with population demand for an exposure-equity metric

Extending this paperThe paper measures per-area hazard (density of infected nymphs) but explicitly flags that high footfall in city parks could make a low-hazard greenspace a high-population-level risk. Extending it: weight each greenspace's hazard by visitor flows (mobile-phone or social-media-derived footfall) to convert ecological hazard into realized human exposure, and ask whether exposure is equitably distributed across neighbourhoods.

Added value to my researchThis is a 2SFCA-style supply×demand coupling applied to disease exposure rather than service access, and a natural use of my multi-source data fusion (GPS trajectories, mobile-phone records, social media) to turn a hazard map into an equity map.

Cross-ref (corpus): Unequal access to social, environmental and health amenities in US urban parks — 10.1038/s44284-024-00153-2

④ Model the meta-ecosystem as a physics-informed host–tick graph

Extending this paperThe paper infers but cannot observe the dispersal mechanism (deer-mediated transport vs. urbanization enveloping established tick patches). Extending it: build a physics-informed graph where nodes are woodland/greenspace patches and edges carry deer least-cost movement, then impose a source–sink conservation prior so the network simulates tick seeding into cities — letting the two competing mechanisms be disentangled with the connectivity data already collected.

Added value to my researchThis is my physics-informed graph learning agenda applied to disease ecology: conservation/flow priors on a spatial graph, with agent-based or SIR dynamics on top — reusing the network-community-detection and ABM toolkit I already deploy for job-housing and mobility networks.

Cross-ref (corpus): Biodiversity-rich recreational areas near cities as a nature-based mental health solution — 10.1038/s44284-025-00251-9

⑤ Scale from 16 surveyed cities to a national hazard map via remote sensing

Extending this paperThe paper's hazard values exist only for 16 (plus 5 published) cities, leaving most of the UK blank. Extending it: use the validated hinterland-landcover relationship as labels to fine-tune a geospatial foundation model on remote-sensing imagery (canopy, fragmentation, NDVI), producing a continuous, transferable tick/Lyme hazard surface for every UK city — and testing whether the central-England "low-hazard belt" the paper describes holds nationwide.

Added value to my researchThis is a concrete instance of my fine-tuning geospatial foundation models and FAIR/open-science workflow: sparse field labels + open remote sensing → a reproducible national prediction, exactly the multi-source fusion pattern I pursue for urban-health surfaces.
