A causal perspective on dataset bias in machine learning for medical imaging

Charles Jones · Daniel C. Castro · Fabio De Sousa Ribeiro · Ozan Oktay · Melissa McCradden · Ben Glocker Published 2024-02-15 DOI 10.1038/s42256-024-00797-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Dataset bias in medical imaging ML is not monolithic: it arises from three distinct causal mechanisms — prevalence disparities, presentation disparities, and annotation disparities — that are observationally indistinguishable yet require fundamentally different mitigation strategies. No single bias-mitigation method or metric can address all mechanisms; every intervention makes implicit causal and ethical assumptions that must be made explicit. The authors provide a practical three-step framework — identify the causal nature of the bias, evaluate which pathways are fair or unfair, then select methods and metrics accordingly — to support the development of safe and equitable predictive models.

Core Methods

  • Causal graphical modeling (directed acyclic graphs): all bias mechanisms are encoded as DAGs; Pearl's causal hierarchy (association, intervention, counterfactual) and the d-separation criterion are used to derive conditional independence relationships implied by each structure.
  • Anticausal classification framing: medical image analysis is modeled as predicting the cause (disease Z) from its effect (image X), generalizing the standard anticausal assumption to allow noisy labels (Y ← Z) and embedding it in a selection-node framework for sampling biases.
  • Three-family bias taxonomy: prevalence disparities (A → disease prevalence), presentation disparities (A → disease appearance), and annotation disparities (A → label generation) are formally derived from the conditions under which the attribute-independence criterion (Y ⊥ XA | XZ) is violated.
  • Causal transportability theory (Pearl & Bareinboim): invoked to assess when a model trained on a biased dataset can legitimately be deployed in an unbiased setting and when ERM or specific mitigation strategies are theoretically valid or futile.
  • Fair vs. unfair pathway analysis: distinguishes causal paths encoding legitimate biological differences (fair) from those encoding historical inequities (unfair), formalizing the notion of a "hypothetical unbiased deployment setting" as the target of all mitigation.
  • Three-step practical framework (Table 1): (1) identify causal nature of bias from the DAG; (2) determine ethical status of each pathway; (3) select methods and metrics that exploit fair paths and suppress unfair ones.
  • ERM analysis: derives conditions under which standard empirical risk minimization is appropriate vs. theoretically unsound for each disparity family, directly connecting training paradigm choices to causal assumptions.

① Operationalize the three-step framework with automated causal DAG discovery

Extending this paperThe paper's three-step framework (identify, evaluate, mitigate) currently relies on domain experts manually constructing DAGs — a bottleneck that limits its reach to settings with abundant clinical expertise. A direct extension: apply automated causal discovery (constraint-based PC algorithm or score-based GES) to the dataset's metadata variables (acquisition site, patient demographics, imaging protocol, label source) to semi-automatically surface candidate bias mechanisms, then present these candidate DAGs to domain experts for ethical pathway classification. This converts the paper's qualitative audit into a computationally supported pipeline deployable before any model is trained.

Added value to my researchIn my telehealth and digital-divide research, the same DAG-auditing pipeline can distinguish whether a spatial selection gap (e.g., urban vs. rural patients who adopt telehealth) is a prevalence disparity (structural access barriers) or a presentation disparity (disease severity at first contact differs by geography). Automated causal discovery applied to multi-source data (mobile phone records, census, EHR) could reveal which confounders drive apparent spatial health inequalities — disciplining my MGWR model-selection choices.

② Extend the annotation-disparity model to spatially heterogeneous labeling quality

Extending this paperThe paper's Fig. 6b annotation-disparity vignette shows that NLP auto-labeling introduces bias when report quality varies by language. A natural extension maps spatial geography as the annotation-quality variable: radiology reporting standards, pathologist expertise, and EHR completeness all vary systematically by hospital geography and healthcare system. Building a formal DAG where geographic site mediates annotation quality — and carefully distinguishing it from a genuine presentation disparity caused by site-specific imaging equipment — extends the paper's framework to multi-site federated learning at scale, which is becoming the norm in medical AI development.

Added value to my researchWhen fusing multi-source health data (insurance claims, EHR, remote sensing of facility locations), spatial heterogeneity in data quality is an annotation disparity in the causal sense. Encoding it explicitly in a DAG — rather than treating spatial variation as random noise — enables more principled imputation and uncertainty quantification in spatial health models, directly improving the reliability of my accessibility and health-outcome predictions across heterogeneous urban and rural settings.

③ Couple the fair/unfair pathway decomposition with RL-based adaptive debiasing

Extending this paperThe paper articulates why adversarial training fails for presentation disparities (it strips task-relevant sensitive information) and can work for prevalence disparities — but leaves practitioners without a method that adaptively selects its debiasing strategy based on the detected causal structure. Extending it: reformulate bias mitigation as a reinforcement learning problem where the agent receives the audited DAG as state and selects among resampling, adversarial training, decoupled classifiers, and label-noise correction, receiving reward based on causal transportability metrics on a held-out unbiased reference set. The paper's taxonomy provides the structured action space; transportability theory provides the reward signal.

Added value to my researchThe RL-over-causal-structure formulation applies directly to spatial accessibility modeling: different spatial data-quality gaps (missing rural facilities vs. biased survey sampling vs. inconsistent geocoding) are analogous to the three disparity families. An RL agent trained on the causal structure of each gap could automatically select the right imputation strategy — an upgrade over my current Monte Carlo missing-data approaches that treat all gaps as equivalent.

④ Build counterfactual generative models grounded in the paper's causal DAG interventions

Extending this paperThe Outlook identifies counterfactual generative models (deep structural causal models, DECAF) as a promising direction for debiased synthetic data, but leaves this as a research call. A concrete extension: for each of the three disparity types, derive the minimal interventional distribution needed to remove the unfair causal pathway, then train a diffusion-based or VAE image generator constrained to sample from that interventional distribution — do(race), do(site), do(labeling_policy). The paper's DAG taxonomy provides the intervention targets and the causal identification conditions; the three-step framework specifies which pathways to intervene on and how to verify that the resulting samples are aligned with the hypothetical unbiased deployment distribution.

This enriches my facility-location optimization and 2SFCA research with causally grounded scenario planning: instead of asking "what is accessible now?" ⑤ Re-derive the causal disparity taxonomy for spatially indexed health data in GeoAI

Extending this paperThe three disparity families were developed for medical imaging but their structure is general. A systematic extension: formally re-derive prevalence, presentation, and annotation disparities for spatially indexed observational health data. Prevalence disparities become spatial access gradients (disease prevalence co-varies with distance to care via healthcare-access selection nodes); presentation disparities become survey-instrument or protocol heterogeneity across geographic strata; annotation disparities become differential ICD coding quality across hospital systems with varying documentation cultures. Publishing this spatial re-derivation with DAGs for each would extend the paper's reach into public-health GIS and build a principled bridge between causal medical AI and spatial epidemiology.

Added value to my researchClassifying which of my spatial health datasets suffer from prevalence, presentation, or annotation disparities — using this paper's three-step framework — would discipline my model-selection choices: MGWR/GWR for fair spatial heterogeneity, propensity-score reweighting for unfair prevalence disparities in facility-access datasets, and label-noise models for annotation disparities in EHR-derived outcomes. It also provides a principled argument for why explainable ML (SHAP/LIME) feature attributions can be misleading when underlying data contain unfair causal pathways, motivating causal-attribution methods over post-hoc explanations in my GeoAI work.
