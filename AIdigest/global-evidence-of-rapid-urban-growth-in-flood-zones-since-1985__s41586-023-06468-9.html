Global evidence of rapid urban growth in flood zones since 1985

Jun Rentschler · Paolo Avner · Mattia Marconcini · Rui Su · Emanuele Strano · Michalis Vousdoukas · Stéphane Hallegatte Published 2023-10-04 DOI 10.1038/s41586-023-06468-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Since 1985, human settlements worldwide — from villages to megacities — have continuously and rapidly expanded into present-day flood zones. In many regions, growth in high-hazard flood areas is outpacing growth in safe areas; in East Asia, high-hazard settlements grew 60% faster than flood-safe ones. Rather than adapting, many countries are actively amplifying their own flood exposure through locally determined urbanization decisions, making disaster losses a product of governance failure as much as climate change.

Why It Matters

  • Shifts the disaster narrative from climate determinism to governance accountability. The study shows that locally driven urbanization — not just climate change — is the primary engine of rising flood losses, restoring agency to local and national authorities.
  • First systematic global quantification of annual settlement growth inside flood hazard zones. Using annual satellite-derived data from 1985 to 2015, the paper tracks built-up area expansion at every hazard level across virtually all countries, producing the most comprehensive evidence base to date.
  • Documents a dangerous divergence across nations. Some countries are reducing flood exposure while others amplify it — the cross-national heterogeneity is policy-relevant for international risk frameworks (Sendai, Paris Agreement).
  • Quantifies the scale of the problem. By 2015, global high-hazard settlements covered 144,600 km², of which 76,400 km² (more than half) were newly built since 1985 — concrete numbers that anchor infrastructure investment and insurance decisions.
  • Provides open, reproducible resources. The WSF-Evo settlement dataset, coastal flood maps (Zenodo), and full source code (Zenodo) are publicly available, enabling follow-on work on adaptation policy modeling.

Core Methods

  • Spatial overlay analysis: annual WSF-Evo settlement layers (1985–2015) are intersected with multi-hazard flood maps to assign each built-up pixel a flood hazard class for each year, yielding a 31-year panel of settlement extent by hazard level.
  • Settlement growth decomposition: year-on-year growth rates of built-up area are computed separately within each hazard class (none, low, moderate, high, very high), enabling direct comparison of how fast development is occurring in safe vs. flood-exposed zones.
  • Divergence index (IDC score): a composite index contrasting relative settlement growth in high-hazard zones against flood-safe zones, used to rank and compare countries systematically.
  • Multi-hazard integration: fluvial, pluvial, and coastal flood hazards are treated jointly, allowing the analysis to capture the full spectrum of flood risk rather than a single hazard type.
  • Sensitivity analysis: results are tested across multiple inundation depth thresholds and different flood return periods (Extended Data Fig. 4) to demonstrate robustness to hazard parameterization choices.
  • Subnational disaggregation: region-level and admin-1-level decomposition allows identification of geographic hotspots and income-group heterogeneity in exposure trajectories.
