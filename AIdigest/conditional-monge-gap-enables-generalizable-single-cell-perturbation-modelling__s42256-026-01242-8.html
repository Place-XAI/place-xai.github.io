Conditional Monge Gap enables generalizable single-cell perturbation modelling

Alice Driessen · Dhruva Abhijit Rajwade · Benedek Harsanyi · Marianna Rapsomaniki · Jannis Born Published 2026-06-01 DOI 10.1038/s42256-026-01242-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The Conditional Monge Gap (CMonge) introduces a mathematically grounded, parameter-efficient conditional optimal transport framework that learns a single global model across hundreds of drug perturbation conditions. Unlike prior OT methods that train one model per condition, CMonge conditions on arbitrary covariates (drug identity, dose, combinations) and achieves performance comparable to — and sometimes exceeding — condition-specific state-of-the-art baselines. Critically, by scaling to hundreds of drugs, CMonge generalizes to entirely unseen compounds using only molecular structure (SMILES), opening a practical route to virtual drug screening and repurposing without the combinatorial cost of experimental assays.

★

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the one-model-per-condition bottleneck. Existing neural OT methods require a separate model for each drug–dose pair. CMonge trains a single global model across all 748 conditions on SciPlex (187 drugs × 4 doses), achieving comparable accuracy at drastically lower computational cost.
  • Unlocks out-of-sample (OOS) prediction for unseen drugs. This is the practically relevant scenario for drug repurposing: given only a novel compound's SMILES representation, CMonge can predict transcriptional responses without any measured perturbation data for that compound.
  • Better captures cellular heterogeneity. While most methods predict mean gene expression changes, CMonge explicitly models the full distribution of single-cell responses, outperforming baselines on Wasserstein distance and MMD — crucial for detecting rare, potentially drug-resistant cell states.
  • Highly parameter-efficient relative to foundation models. CMonge achieves strong results with as few as 23,000 parameters in its OT module, compared to 100M+ parameters in single-cell foundation models — yet still competes with or outperforms them on perturbation prediction benchmarks.
  • Biologically interpretable outputs. Pathway enrichment analysis of CMonge's predicted gene expression profiles closely mirrors ground-truth enriched pathways (e.g., MAPK signalling for MEK inhibitor trametinib), validating the biological meaningfulness of the transport maps.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

SciPlex3 dataset
Single-cell RNA sequencing profiles from three human cancer cell lines (A549, K562, MCF7) exposed to 188 compounds (187 drugs + control) at four doses (10, 100, 1,000, 10,000 nM). Total: 762,039 single-cell measurements, including 17,565 control cells; average ~4,032 observations per drug–dose condition. Only the top 1,000 highly variable genes were used; evaluation focused on the top 50 differentially expressed marker genes.
4i dataset
Iterative indirect immunofluorescence imaging (4i) data measuring 40 proteins in 97,748 cells (10,995 controls) from two melanoma tumour lines (WM989 and WM983b) treated with one of 35 cancer therapies (27 single drugs + 6 combinatorial + 2 excluded). Preprocessed data yields 48 features (cell marker expression + shape); approximately 2,500 cells per condition.
Drug embeddings
RDKit molecular fingerprints (194 features; atom type, bond type, formal charge, atomic mass, hydrogen count) extracted from SMILES representations; and Mode-of-Action (MoA) embeddings derived from pairwise Wasserstein distances between target cell populations, reduced to 10 dimensions via multidimensional scaling (SMACOF algorithm).
Data availability
Both datasets (preprocessed SciPlex3 and 4i) are publicly available via ETH Research Collection at ETH DOI. Code is available at GitHub (MIT license) and installable as cmonge from PyPI.

Core Methods

  • Conditional Monge Gap (CMonge): extends the unconditional Monge Gap regularizer by minimizing a conditional Sinkhorn divergence + Monge Gap loss jointly across all K conditions simultaneously. The transport map Tθ(ci, si) = si + vi is a residual (shift) prediction conditioned on context ci and source cell si.
  • Architecture: multi-layer perceptron (MLP) transport map with GELU activations; drug encoder Wdrug and drug–dose encoder Wdose produce a concatenated context vector. For combinatorial treatments, a DeepSets layer with average pooling handles permutation-invariant multi-drug conditioning.
  • Drug encodings: two strategies compared — RDKit fingerprints (194 structural features; requires only compound SMILES, no experimental data) and MoA embeddings (10-dimensional multidimensional scaling from pairwise Wasserstein distances; requires observed cell data per drug).
  • Autoencoder for SciPlex: a vanilla autoencoder pre-trained on SciPlex gene expression maps 1,000-dimensional gene space to a 50-dimensional latent space; OT learning occurs in latent space, predictions decoded back to gene space for evaluation.
  • Evaluation metrics: coefficient of determination R2 of feature means for top 50 differentially expressed genes; entropy-regularized Wasserstein distance; maximum mean discrepancy (MMD). All metrics bootstrapped over 9 independent evaluations per condition by randomly sampling control cells.
  • Benchmarking: compared against CellOT (ICNN-based OT), scGen (autoencoder), and chemCPA (state-of-the-art conditional perturbation model with 1.37M parameters). Both in-sample (IS) and out-of-sample (OOS) settings tested; OOS uses leave-one-drug-out and 21-fold cross-validation at scale.
  • Pathway analysis: enrichment of gene expression predictions assessed via Fisher's exact test (one-tailed) with Benjamini–Hochberg FDR correction against a panel of 653 pathways (n = 9 drugs validated).

① Extend CMonge's conditional OT to spatiotemporal health-service demand modelling

Extending this paperCMonge learns how a source distribution (unperturbed cells) transforms into a target distribution (perturbed cells) conditional on a drug context. An underexplored extension of the paper's framework is to replace drug contexts with spatial and temporal covariates — conditioning the transport map on location, season, or demographic strata. The paper already notes that "categorical covariates (e.g., cell type) or continuous covariates (e.g., time) could be easily incorporated." A direct extension would apply CMonge to longitudinal scRNA-seq time-series data, using time as a continuous covariate and validating OOS generalization to held-out time points, as the paper does for unseen drugs.

Added value to my researchThe CMonge formulation maps directly onto a core challenge in my spatial accessibility work: modelling how a healthcare demand distribution (patient population) transforms into a utilization distribution (actual clinic visits) conditional on covariates such as transportation mode, distance, insurance type, or season. The Sinkhorn divergence + Monge Gap loss would let me learn a demand-conditioned 2SFCA catchment without assuming a fixed Gaussian decay kernel — replacing the ad hoc distance-decay function in R2SFCA with a learned, covariate-conditioned OT map. The RDKit analogue here would be travel-time isochrones encoded as structural features; the MoA analogue would be learned accessibility embeddings derived from observed utilization flows.

② Replace fixed RDKit/MoA embeddings with a geospatial foundation model encoder for place-based contexts

Extending this paperA central finding of the paper is that the quality of the context embedding determines out-of-sample generalization: MoA embeddings (derived from observed data) consistently outperform RDKit fingerprints (derived from structure alone) when training data are scarce. The gap narrows only when the training set is scaled to hundreds of conditions. This is precisely the representation learning challenge addressed by single-cell foundation models — yet the paper shows CMonge's lightweight OT head is a drop-in replacement for the perturbation prediction head. A direct extension would evaluate whether replacing RDKit with embeddings from a molecule foundation model (e.g., trained on hundreds of millions of compounds) closes the MoA gap even at small-data regimes, as hypothesized in the discussion section.

Added value to my researchThis maps onto my agenda of fine-tuning geospatial foundation models for downstream tasks. Just as CMonge uses a fixed drug encoder head and a separate OT map, I could freeze a pre-trained geospatial encoder (e.g., on remote sensing or POI data) and attach a lightweight conditional OT head to predict how spatial distributions of health outcomes shift across neighborhoods or policy interventions — enabling OOS generalization to new cities or new policy contexts with minimal retraining.

③ Apply CMonge's OOS evaluation framework to out-of-distribution spatial generalization in GeoAI

Extending this paperThe paper's rigorous OOS protocol — leave-one-drug-out, 21-fold cross-validation at scale, bootstrapped evaluation per condition — provides a transferable evaluation template for any domain where conditions are discrete and new ones emerge over time. The paper explicitly validates that performance correlates with structural similarity between the unseen drug and nearest training drugs (Supplementary Fig. 3). An extension would systematically study how CMonge's OOS performance degrades as the "distance" from training conditions increases, formalizing a generalizability radius concept analogous to coverage in spatial statistics.

Added value to my researchIn GeoAI, the analogous OOS challenge is spatial transferability: a model trained on cities in one region should generalize to cities in a new region with different density profiles, infrastructure, or demographics. The paper's leave-one-out OOS protocol, translated to a leave-one-city-out or leave-one-region-out benchmark, would give my spatiotemporal models a principled evaluation harness for geographic generalization — directly relevant to assessing explainable ML models for health geography across cities with heterogeneous data availability.

④ Use CMonge's DeepSets combinatorial layer to model compound spatial interventions

Extending this paperCMonge handles combinatorial drug treatments (multiple drugs per condition) via a DeepSets layer with average pooling, which is permutation-invariant and naturally extends to higher-order drug combinations. The paper notes this is "relatively simple" and that "more expressive permutation-invariant architectures such as attention pooling could improve the modelling of combinatorial effects." A direct extension of the paper: replace the average pooling with multi-head self-attention across drug embeddings, learning interaction terms between drugs (synergy/antagonism) rather than treating the combination as a mean of single-drug effects — building on the paper's own finding that the current approach may not capture "non-additive effects."

Added value to my researchIn urban accessibility, spatial interventions are frequently combinatorial: a neighborhood improvement might simultaneously add a clinic, extend a bus line, and open a pharmacy. The DeepSets formulation — permutation-invariant aggregation of intervention embeddings — provides a theoretically grounded way to model joint multi-facility accessibility without enumerating all intervention combinations. Applied to my 2SFCA framework, this would enable a single global model for multi-intervention accessibility that generalizes to unseen intervention portfolios, directly analogous to CMonge's OOS drug generalization.

⑤ Leverage CMonge's distributional heterogeneity focus for equity analysis in spatial health systems

Extending this paperA key differentiator of CMonge over mean-prediction baselines (such as chemCPA) is that it explicitly models the full distribution of cellular responses, not just mean gene expression changes. The paper quantifies this advantage via Wasserstein distance and MMD, showing CMonge "widely outperforms other conditional models in capturing heterogeneity in cell populations" — particularly important for identifying rare, potentially drug-resistant cell states. A natural extension would use CMonge to characterize how drug-induced heterogeneity varies across different patient cell lines, quantifying inter-patient variability as a conditional distribution and enabling personalized prediction of therapy resistance.

Added value to my researchThe shift from mean-prediction to full distributional modelling is an exact parallel to a core challenge in my health geography work: standard 2SFCA reports mean accessibility per zone, masking intra-zone distributional inequity. Applying CMonge's conditional OT approach to model the distribution of travel-time-to-care across individuals within a catchment — conditioned on income, mobility mode, and age — would yield a distributional accessibility metric sensitive to within-zone heterogeneity. The Wasserstein distance used by CMonge is directly applicable as a spatial equity measure comparing accessibility distributions across demographic groups, connecting optimal transport theory to spatial justice in health facility allocation.
