Impacts of remote work on vehicle miles traveled and transit ridership in the USA

Yunhan Zheng · Shenhao Wang · Lun Liu · Jim Aloisi · Jinhua Zhao Published 2024-04-09 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00057-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across the USA from April 2020 to October 2022, remote work cuts driving and transit ridership asymmetrically. Relative to prepandemic baselines, a 1% drop in onsite workers corresponds to a 0.99% reduction in state-level vehicle miles traveled (VMT) but a far steeper 2.26% drop in metropolitan transit ridership. A 10% decline in onsite workers would yield a 191.8 Mt (10%) annual cut in transportation CO₂ — yet also a US$3.7 billion (26.7%) annual loss in transit fare revenue across the contiguous USA. Remote work is environmentally beneficial but fiscally corrosive for public transit.

Why It Matters

  • Quantifies a structural, post-pandemic shift, not a transient one. Remote work is treated as a persistent feature of urban life; the study measures its marginal effect on mobility over a 31-month window rather than a one-off lockdown shock.
  • Exposes the transit–environment tension. The same behavior that delivers large CO₂ savings simultaneously starves public transit of fare revenue — a direct, quantified policy trade-off between decarbonization and transit solvency.
  • Separates two travel modes that usually get lumped together. Driving falls roughly proportionally with onsite work, but transit falls more than twice as fast — implying remote work erodes transit's commute backbone disproportionately.
  • Gives planners spatially explicit levers. Effects are mapped by state (CO₂) and by MSA (fare revenue), so impacts can be tied to specific jurisdictions and budgets.
  • Reproducible and open. Compiled datasets and analysis code are released on GitHub.

Core Methods

  • Recovery-rate framing: express VMT, transit ridership and onsite work as recovery rates relative to prepandemic baselines, making heterogeneous geographies comparable on a common scale.
  • Elasticity estimation: regress recovery of VMT (state) and transit ridership (MSA) on recovery of onsite workers to obtain the headline −0.99% / −2.26% per 1% marginal effects.
  • Temporal dynamics: estimate how the effect of onsite-worker recovery on VMT and transit ridership evolves over the 31-month window (Fig. 2), not just on average.
  • Scenario / counterfactual analysis: translate a hypothetical 10% reduction in onsite workers into annual CO₂ and fare-revenue changes.
  • Impact mapping: compute the marginal effect by state (on-road CO₂) and by MSA (transit fare revenue) to expose spatial heterogeneity (Fig. 3).

① Replace state/MSA elasticities with a spatially varying remote-work coefficient surface

Extending this paperThe paper reports aggregate elasticities (−0.99% VMT, −2.26% transit) and then maps marginal effects by state/MSA, but the underlying response is treated as a near-global coefficient. A direct extension: estimate the remote-work-to-VMT/transit relationship with MGWR/GWR so the elasticity itself becomes a continuous surface, revealing where transit is most fiscally exposed and where VMT is stickiest — sharpening the paper's own heterogeneity maps into a non-stationary model.
