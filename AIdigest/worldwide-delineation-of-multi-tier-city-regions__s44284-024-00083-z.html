Worldwide delineation of multi-tier city–regions

Andrea Cattaneo · Serkan Girgin · Rolf de By · Theresa McMenomy · Andrew Nelson · Sara Vaz Published 2024-06-26 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00083-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

This study delivers the first systematic global delineation of city–regions, classifying 30,079 urban centers across 213 countries/territories into four tiers (town, small, intermediate, large city) and mapping their travel-time catchments. It identifies 1,403 primary city–regions at a 3 h cutoff, rising to 4,210 at 1 h. Crucially, 3.2 billion people can reach multiple urban tiers within 1 h (4.7 billion within 3 h), and among people closest to towns or small cities twice as many have easier access to intermediate than to large cities — overturning the primate-city focus and underscoring the connective role of intermediate cities.

★

Why It Matters

  • Replaces case studies with a global system view. City–region research has overwhelmingly relied on case studies of major cities; this is the first exhaustive, worldwide delineation spanning small towns to megacities, closing a documented North–South and small–large-city evidence gap.
  • Moves beyond the single-center assumption. Earlier FUA/URCA approaches link each location to one urban center. This work lets a location belong to up to four overlapping catchments, capturing how people use a town for groceries, an intermediate city for work, and a large city for specialized services.
  • Elevates intermediate and small cities as policy targets. Because most people do not need frequent access to the highly specialized services only large cities provide, intermediate and small cities engage twice or more of the surrounding population — arguing for investment in these nodes and in tertiary roads rather than primate cities.
  • Ships an open, reusable spatial dataset. The City–Region System Toolbox (Zenodo, multiple travel-time cutoffs and four population datasets) is published so any country can generate its own city–region population distributions — research as infrastructure for regional planning, food systems, health/education access, and resource management.

Core Methods

  • Tiered urban classification: population thresholds map urban centers to four activity tiers, where higher tiers are assumed to provide all activities of lower tiers (a global operationalization of central place theory and Parr's C-zone/S-zone framing).
  • Travel-time catchments: a least-cost path algorithm over a global cost surface, plus a custom minimum cumulative weighted distance method (eight-connectivity on a sphere, with rolling at the date line and poles), produces minimum-travel-time sheds per tier.
  • Multi-center patches: overlapping catchments are merged into >100,000 unique patches via a variable-bit encoding; a four-digit code (digits 0–4) records which tier provides each activity level (e.g. 2230, 4444).
  • Primary vs secondary city–regions: patches sharing the same highest-tier center define a city–region; centers are classified as primary, secondary (satellite or nested) by overlap logic, counted separately per tier to avoid double counting.
  • Sensitivity & robustness: three alternative travel-time cutoffs (1/2/3 h) and four population datasets (GHS-POP, GPW, LandScan, WorldPop); results reported as robust. Spatial processing in Go/PHP/Python; maps in QGIS 3.22 and R.

Expanded Ideas — this paper's extensibility & added value to my research

Each idea stays anchored to this paper: first how the paper itself could be extended (drawing on our corpus), then the added value for my research lines (migis.net — GeoAI, 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility, health geography, explainable & physics-informed graph learning, facility-location optimization).

① Convert physical-proximity catchments into demand-aware service accessibility

Extending this paperThe delineation is purely supply-side and physical: a location "has access" if a tier is reachable within a travel-time cutoff, regardless of capacity, crowding, or whether people actually go there. A direct extension: replace the binary "closest-center" catchment with a competition-weighted, multi-tier 2SFCA/R2SFCA layer so each patch's accessibility is discounted by supply capacity and demand pressure across its (up to four) reference centers — turning the global patch dataset into a realized-accessibility surface rather than a reachability map.

Added value to my researchThis paper hands me a ready-made, globally consistent nested catchment hierarchy to seed multi-tier R2SFCA for primary care, mental health and telehealth: the town/small/intermediate/large tiers map naturally onto primary→specialized care levels, and the 30 arc-sec patches become my service-area units worldwide.

② Validate and reweight catchments with revealed mobility flows

Extending this paperThe paper's "people use a town for groceries, an intermediate city for work" hierarchy is an assumption from central place theory, never validated against observed behavior. Extending it: ingest mobile-phone / GPS / visitation data to test whether realized trips respect the assumed tier hierarchy, and reweight catchment membership by empirical visitation probability — directly addressing the paper's own caveat that activity provision varies within a tier across contexts.

Added value to my researchThis is exactly my multi-source fusion line: I can fit learned distance-decay functions from flows to replace the paper's hard cutoffs, and use visitation asymmetries to expose service-access disparities for ageing or low-mobility populations within city–regions.

Cross-ref (corpus): Visitation patterns reveal service access disparities for ageing populations in the USA — 10.1038/s41562-025-02285-4

③ Make catchments dynamic with transport-investment and rail scenarios

Extending this paperThe travel-time surface is a static 2020 snapshot; yet the paper repeatedly argues that infrastructure quality (not just geography) drives the Ethiopia-vs-France contrast. Extending it: turn the cost grid into a scenario engine — add tertiary roads, public-transport, or high-speed rail edges and recompute which tiers each patch can reach, quantifying how many people are pulled into a higher-tier city–region per dollar of investment.

Added value to my researchThis couples to my urban-agglomeration / regional-collaboration and EV-charging efficiency-vs-equity work: I can run Monte Carlo infrastructure scenarios over the patch graph and optimize edge additions for an equity objective (lifting the worst-served rural patches) rather than mean accessibility.

Cross-ref (corpus): Public transport accessibility in villages in and around major Chinese cities — 10.1038/s44284-025-00277-z

④ Treat the city–region system as a graph and learn its organizing rules

Extending this paperThe paper describes diverse organizational patterns (single vs multi-tier, primary vs secondary, polycentric) but stops at descriptive statistics. Extending it: cast urban centers as nodes and catchment overlaps as edges to build a global multi-tier city–region graph, then apply community detection and explainable ML to predict a country's city–region structure from geography, income and infrastructure — operationalizing the polycentricity the discussion only gestures at.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my network-community-detection + explainable-ML agenda: I can use MGWR/GWR and SHAP-style attribution to quantify how local non-stationarity (terrain, road density) shapes tier connectivity, and feed the graph into job-housing / regional-collaboration network analyses.

Cross-ref (corpus): Underlying rules of evolutionary urban systems in Africa — 10.1038/s44284-025-00208-y

⑤ Stress-test the equity implications: who is left behind, and is connection always good?

Extending this paperThe paper flags countries (Madagascar, South Sudan, Zimbabwe) where >50% of people are >2 h from any small city, but does not analyze distributional outcomes of being in a multi-tier city–region. Extending it: link patch-level tier access to health/education/pollution outcomes and ask whether stronger high-tier connectivity (e.g. via rail) genuinely improves welfare or instead widens urban–rural disparities by draining services upward.

Added value to my researchThis anchors my health-geography and digital-divide lines: the global patch dataset becomes a controlled frame to test telehealth substitution where physical access to intermediate/large cities is poor, and to build physics-informed models of access-vs-outcome that are honest about the equity downside of connectivity.
