Air connectivity boosts urban attractiveness for global firms

Ambra Amico · Fabio Duarte · Wen-Chi Liao · Siqi Zheng Published 2026-01-07 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00361-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A city's position in the global air network is a powerful driver of where multinational corporations (MNCs) place foreign subsidiaries. By merging 7.5 million firms with 400,000 international flight routes (1993–2023), the study shows that fewer layovers predict more subsidiaries in destination cities, that face-to-face-reliant sectors (e.g., knowledge-based services) respond most strongly to air connectivity, and that eigenvector centrality — a city's embeddedness in the whole network — is the most robust predictor of subsidiary location. Physical connectivity still matters for coordinating global firms.

★

Why It Matters

  • Physical proximity still rules in a "digital" economy. Despite videoconferencing, the paper shows that reducing layovers — the friction of getting people physically together — measurably reshapes the global map of corporate investment.
  • It identifies which network property matters. Not raw flight count or direct routes, but eigenvector centrality (embeddedness in the whole network) best predicts subsidiary location — a precise, transferable lever for cities and airport authorities.
  • Sectoral heterogeneity gives targeted policy. Because face-to-face-intensive sectors react most strongly, an airport investment is effectively an industrial-policy instrument for attracting knowledge-based services and FDI.
  • Massive, unusual data fusion. Linking 7.5M firm records to a three-decade flight-route network is a rare evidence base for a question usually argued from theory or small samples.

Core Methods

  • Air-network construction: build a global air-connectivity network from OpenFlights routes (1993–2023), with cities as nodes and routes as edges; derive layover structure and city-level centralities.
  • Centrality analysis: compute multiple node-importance measures and test which predicts subsidiary location best — eigenvector centrality emerges as the most robust.
  • Firm–network data fusion: merge 7.5M Orbis firm records to destination cities, linking subsidiary presence to that city's network position and layover accessibility.
  • Econometric estimation (PCR, Fig. 2): regression-style modeling relating reductions in layovers / higher connectivity to greater subsidiary presence, controlling for city attributes.
  • Sectoral heterogeneity (Fig. 3): split effects by industry to show that face-to-face-reliant sectors (knowledge-based services) respond more strongly than interaction-light sectors.

① From correlational centrality to a causal, dynamic network treatment

Extending this paperThe paper links static eigenvector centrality to subsidiary counts, but its 30-year route panel (1993–2023) is largely untapped for causal identification. A direct extension: exploit new route openings as natural experiments on a temporal network, estimating how a shock to a city's centrality propagates into subsequent subsidiary formation — turning the paper's cross-sectional predictor into a dynamic, identified effect with spillovers to neighboring nodes.

② Reduce the connectivity–firm relationship to an urban scaling law

Extending this paperThe paper documents that connectivity predicts firm presence but stops short of a closed-form regularity. Extending it: test whether subsidiary count scales with centrality via a power law with a (super-/sub-linear) exponent, and whether that exponent is stable across decades and continents — converting the heterogeneous regressions into a single transferable "connectivity-elasticity" constant that strengthens the universal claim.

③ From layover proximity to a learned firm-location gravity model

Extending this paperThe paper's accessibility proxy is essentially layover count / centrality — an a priori friction. Extending it with a learned gravity/flow layer over the air network would let the data infer the true distance-decay between origin headquarters and destination cities, and predict which specific city a firm chooses, not just aggregate counts — a behaviorally grounded firm-location model.
