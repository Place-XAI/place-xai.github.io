Land subsidence risk to infrastructure in US metropolises

Leonard O. Ohenhen · Guang Zhai · Jonathan Lucy · Susanna Werth · Grace Carlson · Mohammad Khorrami · Florence Onyike · Nitheshnirmal Sadhasivam · Ashutosh Tiwari · Khosro Ghobadi-Far · Sonam F. Sherpa · Jui-Chi Lee · Sonia Zehsaz · Manoochehr Shirzaei Published 2025-05-08 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00240-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using Sentinel-1 InSAR at ~28 m resolution (2015–2021), the study maps vertical land motion for the 28 most populous US cities and finds that at least 20% of every city's area is sinking — mostly from groundwater extraction — affecting ~34 million people. Subsidence is not just a coastal problem: fast-sinking inland cities (Houston, Dallas, Fort Worth) are among the worst. Crucially, infrastructure damage is driven by differential land motion (uneven settlement), not subsidence magnitude alone, placing more than 29,000 buildings in high or very-high damage-risk zones.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reframes subsidence as an inland, nationwide hazard. The dominant "sinking cities" narrative is coastal; this work shows 25 of 28 major US cities are sinking, including landlocked Texas and Midwest metros previously lacking spatially resolved data.
  • Isolates the actual damage mechanism. The key insight is that differential settlement (angular distortion) — not absolute sinking — threatens structures; 31.9% of high-hazard and 51.8% of very-high-hazard zones actually occur in uplifting areas, overturning a common assumption.
  • Connects hazard to people and assets. ~33.8 million residents (10% of the US population) live on sinking land; 5.6 million buildings were assessed, with >29,000 in high/very-high risk — quantifying exposure planners can act on.
  • Names the anthropogenic lever. A strong correlation (ρ = 0.87, R² = 76%) between confined-aquifer groundwater decline and non-GIA subsidence points to groundwater management as the most actionable mitigation pathway.
  • Delivers open, high-resolution datasets. City-level VLM, angular-distortion and risk layers are released publicly, giving local agencies the granular maps needed for land-use zoning and retrofitting.

Core Methods

  • Multitemporal InSAR (WabInSAR v5.6): wavelet-based filtering of Sentinel-1 interferograms (700-day temporal, 500 m perpendicular baselines), 12×2 multilooking to ~28 m, coherence/amplitude-dispersion pixel selection, 2D minimum-cost-flow unwrapping, and reweighted least-squares time-series estimation.
  • LOS-to-vertical projection & global referencing: plane removal for horizontal motion, projection by local incidence angle (VLM = LOS / cos θ), affine alignment to the IGS14 frame via a global VLM model; GNSS cross-validation.
  • Area-weighted exposure analysis: subsidence weighted by sinking area (not simple mean) to avoid hotspot bias; census-block population binned into five subsidence-rate severity classes.
  • Angular-distortion hazard (β): differential settlement between adjacent 28 m pixels over a 25-year projection, classified low/medium/high/very-high using geotechnical thresholds.
  • Risk matrix: combines angular-distortion hazard with Microsoft building densities (low/medium/high) into five risk classes (VL–VH) per city.
  • Groundwater attribution: Theil–Sen regression of well-level trends; lagged Pearson correlation between detrended VLM and groundwater time series; copula modeling (Gaussian/t/Clayton/Frank/Gumbel, MCMC, BIC selection) to estimate conditional probability of subsidence given groundwater decline.

① Couple the subsidence layer with relative-sea-level and flood-exposure models

Extending this paperThe paper measures vertical land motion but flags — without quantifying — that subsidence compounds flood risk in its coastal cities (Houston, New York, Boston). A direct extension: fuse its ~28 m VLM rates with relative-sea-level-rise and pluvial/fluvial flood models to produce compound flood-plus-subsidence exposure maps, separating the land-motion contribution from the ocean contribution the way coastal-subsidence studies already do.

Added value to my researchThis compound-hazard layer becomes a vulnerability covariate in my health-geography accessibility work: facilities and care pathways sitting on differentially settling, flood-prone land can be flagged as resilience-critical, integrating physical hazard into 2SFCA service-area delineation.

② Push from grid-pixel hazard to per-structure, function-aware risk

Extending this paperThe authors explicitly note that they compute angular distortion at the 28 m pixel scale and lack building-resolution footprints and geotechnical metadata. Extending it: replace coarse density bins with building-level footprints carrying function and height attributes, computing β and deflection ratios per structure so risk reflects what each building is (hospital, school, warehouse) and how it is built.

Added value to my researchFunction-aware building maps let me weight subsidence risk by health-relevant facilities (clinics, pharmacies, elder-care), feeding a fusion of remote sensing and POI data that is squarely in my multi-source data-fusion agenda.

③ Replace the static copula with a physics-informed groundwater–subsidence operator

Extending this paperThe paper models the groundwater–subsidence link with per-city copulas and a single regression slope, and concedes that one slope cannot generalize across aquifers. Extending it: encode the effective-stress / poroelastic relationship as a physics-informed graph operator over wells and InSAR pixels, so aquifer connectivity and diffusivity are learned constraints — enabling transfer to the cities where groundwater data are missing (15 of 28).

Added value to my researchThis is a textbook target for my physics-informed graph learning direction: a spatiotemporal GNN that respects a conservation prior and extrapolates a measured driver field into unobserved areas — the same machinery I want for spatial prediction under sparse sensing.

④ Build a temporal, monitoring-aware infrastructure-risk index

Extending this paperThe risk matrix here is essentially a snapshot projected linearly over 25 years. Extending it: make the index temporally dynamic and monitoring-aware — update structure vulnerability as new Sentinel-1 passes arrive and as inspection/structural-health data accrue, mirroring InSAR-enhanced geo-hazard assessment for long-span bridges.

⑤ Overlay subsidence risk with social vulnerability to expose equity gaps

Extending this paperThe paper quantifies who lives on sinking land but stops short of asking which communities bear disproportionate, latent infrastructure risk. Extending it: intersect the high/very-high-risk zones with social-vulnerability and housing data to test whether subsidence risk — like flood exposure in vulnerable settlements — concentrates in marginalized neighborhoods, adding a justice dimension to the Engage step of its framework.
