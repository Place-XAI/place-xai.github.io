Genomic evidence consistent with antagonistic pleiotropy may help explain the evolutionary maintenance of same-sex sexual behaviour in humans

Brendan P. Zietsch · Morgan J. Sidari · Abdel Abdellaoui · Robert Maier · Niklas Långström · Shengru Guo · Gary W. Beecham · Eden R. Martin · Alan R. Sanders · Karin J. H. Verweij Published 2021-08-23 DOI 10.1038/s41562-021-01168-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using genome-wide data from 358,426 individuals, this study shows that genetic variants associated with same-sex sexual behaviour (SSB) are positively correlated with number of opposite-sex partners in individuals who only engage in opposite-sex behaviour — consistent with antagonistic pleiotropy. Evolutionary simulations confirm that this mating advantage in non-SSB individuals is sufficient to help maintain SSB-associated alleles in the population despite any fitness cost of SSB itself, offering a plausible genomic resolution to a longstanding Darwinian paradox.

★

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a Darwinian paradox with direct genomic evidence. Same-sex sexual behaviour is heritable yet apparently fitness-reducing — this study moves beyond twin studies to provide the first direct genome-wide evidence that SSB-associated alleles confer a countervailing mating advantage.
  • Scale unprecedented for this question. The GWAS of opposite-sex partner count uses 358,426 OSB individuals from UK Biobank, and the SSB GWAS draws on a meta-analytic sample of 477,522 — far exceeding prior studies of sexual behaviour genetics.
  • Polygenic validation in an independent cohort. Polygenic scores built on UK Biobank SSB effects replicate the predicted mating-advantage association in the independent Add Health sample (N = 4,414), with additional evidence linking SSB polygenic scores to interviewer-rated physical attractiveness.
  • Implicates specific personality pathways. Genomic structural equation modelling shows that openness to experience and risk-taking propensity jointly partially mediate the genetic correlation, pointing toward behavioural mechanisms of the mating advantage.
  • Ethical framework and transparency. The study is pre-registered (OSF), acknowledges historical and societal constraints on sexual behaviour data, and explicitly frames SSB as a normal part of human diversity rather than a disorder.

Core Methods

  • GWAS of opposite-sex partner count: linear mixed models (BOLT-LMM) in UK Biobank, adjusting for sex, birth year, 10 genetic PCs, genotype platform, and relatedness; locus identification via FUMA with LD pruning (r² < 0.6 and r² < 0.1).
  • SNP-based heritability and per-chromosome heritability: LD score regression (LDSC) on HapMap 3 SNPs; per-chromosome heritability tested against expectation from chromosome length to assess polygenicity.
  • Genetic correlation (cross-trait LD score regression): genome-wide genetic covariance estimated between SSB and opposite-sex partner count in OSB individuals, and between SSB and other personality traits; sex-stratified and sex-combined.
  • Polygenic score analysis (LDpred): polygenic scores for SSB constructed in the independent Add Health sample using LDpred (LD-informed posterior weights); associations with partner count and interviewer-rated attractiveness assessed via OLS regression with standard controls.
  • Genomic structural equation modelling (Genomic SEM): openness to experience and risk-taking propensity modelled as partial mediators of the SSB–partner-count genetic correlation; residual correlation estimated after partialling out personality traits.
  • Evolutionary computer simulations: empirically calibrated agent-based simulations of allele frequency change over 60 generations, comparing scenarios with and without antagonistic pleiotropy, to evaluate whether the observed mating advantage could maintain SSB prevalence.
  • Sensitivity analyses: stricter SSB phenotype definitions (bisexual vs. homosexual) tested to check robustness; cross-sex genetic correlations reported in Supplementary.

① Geographically stratified replication: does the antagonistic-pleiotropy signal vary by neighbourhood deprivation or urbanicity?

Extending this paperThe authors explicitly acknowledge that their UK and US samples are culturally specific and subject to societal regulation of sexual behaviour that differs by region and socioeconomic context. The paper notes UK Biobank is biased toward less-deprived, older participants. A direct extension: use the geographic coordinates of UK Biobank assessment centres and participant postcodes to test whether the genetic correlation between SSB and opposite-sex partner count varies by local deprivation index, urban–rural gradient, or regional liberalism — capturing how the social environment modulates the expression of SSB-associated alleles into mating behaviour.

Added value to my researchThis maps squarely onto my health geography agenda: the UK Biobank's approximate postcodes, combined with the Index of Multiple Deprivation and rural–urban classification, constitute a spatial dataset amenable to MGWR or GWR modelling of spatially varying genetic effect-environment interactions — exactly the kind of multi-source data fusion (health records, genetics, area-level indices) I work with for primary care and mental health accessibility.

② Environmental mediation via early-life urbanicity: can menarche timing link SSB-associated alleles to developmental phenotypes?

Extending this paperThe paper finds that openness to experience and risk-taking only partly mediate the SSB–partner-count genetic correlation, leaving most of the genetic covariance unexplained. A life-history theory extension: test whether genetic variants associated with SSB also relate to pubertal timing (an established mediator of adult personality and reproductive behaviour), and whether any such association is moderated by early-life urbanicity. This would extend the paper's Genomic SEM framework by adding a developmental mediator that bridges the genetic signal to observable adolescent biology.

Added value to my researchThis connects to my interest in spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and health-outcome mapping: pubertal-timing data linked to residential history across urban–rural gradients creates a natural use case for spatiotemporal exposure modelling — the kind of multi-source fusion (GPS trajectories, census geography, health records) I routinely apply to urban health questions.

Cross-ref (corpus): Pathways from early-life urbanicity to adult neurobehavioral traits via menarche timing — 10.1038/s44284-025-00352-5

③ Disentangle genes from environment at population scale: a 50-million-individual replication of heritability partitioning

Extending this paperThe paper uses LD score regression to estimate SNP heritability at ~8–25% for SSB — but openly acknowledges that common SNPs capture only a fraction of total heritability (pedigree-based estimate: 32%), and that environmental confounding (neighbourhood, socioeconomic context, historical regulation) is hard to separate from genetic effects in UK Biobank. A direct extension: apply the approximate-pedigree heritability and gene–environment interaction framework of large EHR datasets — with approximate geographical coordinates as a spatial confounder — to disentangle how much of the residual heritability reflects shared environment versus rare/gene-environment-interacting variants.

Added value to my researchPedigree-approximate spatial designs mirror my interest in geospatial methods for causal inference: treating residential geography as a natural-experiment instrument (like Mendelian randomization but in space) is central to my spatial epidemiology work — translating this to genetic-environmental partitioning would be a methodological bridge between health geography and genomics.

Cross-ref (corpus): Dissecting heritability, environmental risk, and air pollution causal effects using >50 million individuals in MarketScan — 10.1038/s41467-024-49566-6

④ From personality traits to ambient environment: does regional temperature moderate the SSB–attractiveness genetic signal?

Extending this paperThe paper shows that SSB polygenic scores associate (tentatively) with physical attractiveness in Add Health and that openness/risk-taking partly mediate the SSB–partner-count genetic correlation. Both personality and attractiveness have been shown to vary geographically with ambient temperature. A multi-level extension: use cross-trait GWAS to test whether the genetic components of the SSB–attractiveness association show gene-by-environment (G×E) interaction with regional ambient temperature, leveraging the Add Health geocodes and the well-validated temperature–personality surface from corpus literature.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of spatiotemporal analysis to health behaviour: mapping genetic effect sizes onto climate surfaces using spatial interpolation or MGWR creates a new modality of environmental health geography where the "exposure" is not a pollutant but an ambient climatic cue — extending my environmental GeoAI toolkit.

Cross-ref (corpus): Regional ambient temperature is associated with human personality — 10.1038/s41562-017-0240-0

⑤ Polygenic scores as spatial predictors: machine-learning integration of genomic and place-based features for health stratification

Extending this paperThe paper's polygenic score for SSB predicts mating success and attractiveness in an independent cohort — but the variance explained is small (as expected for a polygenic trait in a small replication sample). A GeoAI extension: combine the paper's polygenic scores with neighbourhood-level features (housing density, green space, walkability, social deprivation) in an explainable machine-learning framework, testing whether area-level context amplifies or suppresses the polygenic signal for health-relevant outcomes (mental health, partner count, attractiveness). This turns a purely genomic predictor into a geo-aware, context-sensitive health score.

Added value to my researchThis is exactly my explainable ML for spatial prediction agenda: SHAP-attributed ensemble models that decompose the relative importance of genetic vs. place-based features for health outcomes would produce interpretable, policy-relevant maps of where genomic risk is amplified by neighbourhood disadvantage — directly applicable to my primary care and mental health accessibility work.
