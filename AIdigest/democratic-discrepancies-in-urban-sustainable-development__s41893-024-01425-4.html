Democratic discrepancies in urban sustainable development

David Kaufmann · Michael Wicki · Stefan Wittwer · Jake Stephan Published 2024-09-17 DOI 10.1038/s41893-024-01425-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across eight European cities, residents broadly support urban sustainable development (USD) — 72.61% average acceptance — but a systematic democratic gap exists between what residents want and what USD policy plans prioritize. While plans focus on biodiversity, public transport, and urban green spaces, residents consistently rank cost of living, public health, and poverty reduction highest. Securing basic human needs is not merely desirable but democratically demanded and is the prerequisite for deeper, long-term ecological sustainability transformations.

Why It Matters

  • First large-scale empirical test of the USD legitimacy gap. Prior work theorized a mismatch between technocratic planners and residents; this study quantifies it across 5,800 residents and 166 USD policy plans in 8 diverse European cities, moving from speculation to evidence.
  • Exposes the hidden cost of ecological-first planning. Plans that over-prioritize biodiversity and green spaces while under-prioritizing cost of living and public health may undermine the democratic legitimacy that ambitious sustainability transitions require.
  • Stable, cross-city finding. The democratic discrepancy holds across cities varying by GDP, ecological vulnerability, national context, and framing of sustainability — making it a robust stylized fact rather than a local anomaly.
  • Actionable design principle. The finding that framing (triangle vs. nested SD concept) has minimal effect on preferences empowers planners: they cannot reframe residents into supporting different priorities, so they must genuinely integrate residents' everyday socio-economic concerns into USD plans.
  • Open science infrastructure. All survey data, coding of 166 USD plans, and R replication code are publicly archived at OSF, enabling independent reanalysis and cross-national extension.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Survey data
5,800 residents across 8 European cities (Antwerp, Frankfurt, Helsinki, Lisbon, Manchester, Marseille, Milan, Valencia). Conducted September–November 2022 via IPSOS online panels. Quotas on age, gender, and income decile (soft quota). Manchester: 900 respondents; all other cities: 700 each.
Policy plan corpus
166 USD policy plans collected and thematically coded from official city and departmental websites. Originally reviewed 219 plans; 166 retained after exclusion criteria (plans must reference USD, SD, or sustainability; authored by city government; not purely evaluative). Analysis used MAXQDA for thematic coding.
City selection
8 cities systematically selected from all 86 Western/Northern European cities >350,000 inhabitants, stratified by ecological vulnerability (heatwave/drought/flood index) and financial capacity (metropolitan GDP per capita). Mean vulnerability index of selected cities: 4.31 (dataset mean: 4.12); mean GDP per capita: 38,928 (dataset mean: 40,281).
Survey instrument
Three-component design: (1) framing experiment (triangle vs. nested SD concept, random assignment, n = 2,900 per frame); (2) ranking of 17 USD policy issues; (3) choice experiment with 6 randomly selected issues per proposal, 3 pairs per respondent — 34,800 total observations. Median completion time: 19.9 min.
Replication data
Full survey questionnaire, all 166 coded USD plans, and R analysis code publicly available at OSF: Y9HMP and OSF: CZ3AK. Pre-registered before data collection.

Core Methods

  • Systematic case selection: stratified sampling of 8 cities from 86 eligible European cities along two axes (ecological vulnerability × financial capacity / GDP), with pairwise city matching within each quadrant to control for context effects.
  • Survey experiment — framing: randomized control trial design assigning respondents to one of two SD conceptualizations (triangle vs. nested doughnut), creating two equal subsamples of 2,900 to test framing effects on preferences.
  • Ranking experiment: respondents rank-ordered all 17 identified USD policy issues; results reported as mean rank ± s.e.; two-sample t-tests compare the two framing groups.
  • Discrete choice experiment (DCE): respondents rated pairs of randomly composed USD policy proposals (6 of 17 issues each); Average Marginal Effects (AMEs) estimated from a regression to identify which specific issues increase or decrease plan acceptance.
  • Thematic coding of policy plans: 166 USD plans qualitatively coded in MAXQDA using an iterative, city-specific glossary; absolute and relative (per page) frequencies of 17 USD policy issues computed. No AI tools used in coding.
  • Robustness checks: forced-choice DCE variant; cross-city stability tests; temporal robustness controlling for plan introduction date (Supplementary Figs. 1–8, Tables 1–5).

① Map the democratic gap spatially within each city

Extending this paperThe paper aggregates preferences at the city level and compares them with citywide USD plans. A direct extension would geo-locate each of the 5,800 survey respondents to their neighborhood and apply geographically weighted regression (GWR/MGWR) to reveal whether the democratic discrepancy varies by intra-city location — for instance, whether residents in lower-income neighborhoods show stronger preferences for cost-of-living and public health than those in wealthier districts. Layering in neighborhood-level deprivation indices would test whether the gap is driven by spatial inequality within cities, providing planners with a sub-city targeting map for USD co-design.

Added value to my researchThis directly bridges the paper's preference data to my MGWR/GWR toolkit. Modeling spatially varying democratic discrepancies — rather than a single city-level gap — is a natural application of my spatiotemporal kernel density and MGWR methods, and would produce an "equity-in-sustainability-planning" layer compatible with my healthcare and public-facility accessibility studies.

② Predict the democratic gap from city-level structural indicators using explainable ML

Extending this paperThe paper documents the gap across 8 cities and offers qualitative explanations (technocratic rationalities, powerful interests, time lags). Extending it to the full 86-city sample — or across European cities globally — using explainable machine learning (e.g., XGBoost + SHAP) to predict the magnitude of democratic discrepancy from structural city-level features (GDP, governance quality, participatory planning culture, urban density, deprivation index, political ideology) would convert a descriptive finding into a predictive model. SHAP values would reveal which city characteristics most strongly amplify or dampen the gap, directly informing international urban governance reform.

③ Build a USD preference—accessibility mismatch index linking supply gaps to democratic demand

Extending this paperThe paper reveals that residents demand public health and cost-of-living relief — but does not ask whether these preferences are correlated with actual spatial deficits in healthcare facilities, affordable housing, or transit access. Extending it: link the survey's preference rankings to spatial accessibility metrics (2SFCA for primary care, walking-distance food-environment scores, transit GTFS analysis) at the neighborhood level, and test whether residents in areas with worse measured access demand those services most urgently. This would ground the democratic discrepancy in a measurable spatial gap, turning the paper's normative finding into a diagnostic planning instrument.

Added value to my researchThis directly connects the paper's revealed preferences to my 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility framework. Treating residents' democratically expressed demands as the "need" input to a supply-demand accessibility model creates a demand-weighted accessibility metric that goes beyond population-count need proxies — a methodological contribution that redefines the "need" side of my spatial accessibility models using survey-revealed preferences.

④ Apply NLP and machine learning to automate and scale the USD plan coding

Extending this paperThe paper's most labor-intensive step — manually coding 166 USD plans across eight cities using MAXQDA — is the bottleneck preventing scale-up. A direct extension would develop a fine-tuned LLM-based text classifier (potentially using a geospatial foundation model or a domain-adapted BERT variant) that learns the iterative glossary developed in this paper and applies it automatically to hundreds or thousands of USD plans across Europe or globally. This would allow near-real-time monitoring of how city USD plans respond to changes in public preferences over time — converting a one-shot snapshot into a longitudinal observatory.

Added value to my researchFine-tuning geospatial or domain-specific foundation models for structured information extraction from planning documents is central to my open-science/FAIR data agenda. The structured output (issue-frequency per plan) would form a new multi-city urban governance dataset that is directly compatible with my multi-source data fusion pipelines — and the classifier itself would be a reusable resource for urban policy analytics at scale.

⑤ Design a participatory USD co-production platform that closes the feedback loop in real time

Extending this paperThe paper concludes that USD plans should be co-designed with residents to close the democratic gap but does not operationalize how. A logical extension: design and evaluate a digital participatory platform that (a) presents residents with choice experiments drawn from the paper's validated 17-issue framework, (b) aggregates preferences in real time with spatial stratification, and (c) automatically generates a "democratic discrepancy score" by comparing the live preference distribution against the current USD plan issue frequencies. This would turn the paper's static analysis into a continuous democratic feedback loop — and testing it in a longitudinal randomized trial would directly address the paper's limitation regarding implementation gaps.
