Subsistence styles shape human social learning strategies

Luke Glowacki · Lucas Molleman Published 2017-04-28 DOI 10.1038/s41562-017-0098 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Social learning propensities are not species-universal but are strongly shaped by everyday subsistence ecology. In a single-ethnicity group (Nyangatom, Ethiopia), interdependent pastoralists and urban dwellers rely far more on social information than independent horticulturalists, who prioritize individual payoff feedback. The findings show that shifts in subsistence style — including urbanization — can rapidly reshape reliance on social learning and may thereby alter a society’s capacity to adapt to novel environments through cumulative culture.

★

Why It Matters

  • Challenges the species-universality assumption. The dominant view treats social learning as a fixed human universal; this paper provides experimental evidence within a single ethnicity that subsistence ecology alone drives population-level variation in learning strategy — without any genetic or broad cultural confounds.
  • Urbanization as a cognitive lever. Urban dwellers in the study display learning profiles similar to interdependent pastoralists and very different from horticulturalists, implying that the global wave of urbanization may be systematically restructuring the cognitive strategies societies deploy, with consequences for collective adaptation.
  • Explains population-level cognitive variation through environment, not genetics. All three groups share the same ethnicity and recent common ancestry; the effect is therefore environmental (subsistence style), providing a clean natural experiment for causal inference.
  • Implication for adaptive capacity under change. Higher social learning accelerates cultural transmission but may also amplify conformism and reduce exploration; societies undergoing subsistence transitions face hidden changes in their collective problem-solving architecture.

Core Methods

  • Natural quasi-experiment: three subsistence-diverged sub-populations of a single ethnicity (Nyangatom) isolate the effect of subsistence ecology on learning strategy, controlling for genetic and broad cultural confounds.
  • Incentivized decision-making task: a noisy binary choice between two options (expected payoffs 38 vs. 30 points) with stochastic noise (s.d. = 12), making the higher option correct only 67% of the time and creating genuine uncertainty that motivates use of social information.
  • Social vs. individual information choice: at the start of each period, participants chose to observe either (a) the decisions of three demonstrators in the prior period, or (b) their own prior payoff — making learning strategy directly observable and incentive-compatible.
  • Ingroup / outgroup manipulation: demonstrators were either co-ethnic Nyangatom (ingroup) or historically antagonistic Daasanach (outgroup), allowing a test of social identity as a moderator of social learning.
  • Statistical analysis: logistic GLMM with participant as random effect; Tukey-corrected pairwise comparisons across the three subsistence groups; conformist bias analyzed by regressing choice probability on the count of demonstrators choosing the same option.

① Map social learning propensity onto urban form: operationalize interdependence spatially

Extending this paperThe paper identifies “interdependence” as the causal mechanism but operationalizes it as a self-reported categorical label. A direct extension would quantify interdependence geometrically: compute daily mobility network overlap, co-location frequency, and shared resource-access patterns for each group using GPS traces, and test whether those spatial interdependence metrics fully mediate the learning-strategy difference. This would move the paper’s key explanatory concept from an ethnographic category to a measurable, spatially grounded quantity.

② Test whether urban scaling amplifies social learning at the city level

Extending this paperThe paper shows urban dwellers rely more on social information, but within one small town. An urban-scaling extension would ask: does social learning propensity scale super-linearly with city population size, as other social phenomena do? Sampling participants from cities of varying sizes across the same or multiple countries, and regressing social information request rates against log city population, would test whether this paper’s urban-pastoralist parity generalizes to a graded population-size effect — a prediction derivable from the paper’s ecological argument and testable with the same experimental design.

③ Cross-cultural replication across urban–rural gradients using mobile-phone and remote-sensing data

Extending this paperThe sample is restricted to one ethnic group in one region of Ethiopia — a limitation the authors acknowledge. A direct replication extension would deploy the same LIONESS task across 10–20 cities spanning different continents and subsistence-to-urban transition histories, using mobile-phone call-detail records or anonymized mobility data to independently characterize each population’s actual interdependence level. This would decouple the self-reported subsistence label from the underlying social structure the paper claims drives the effect.

⑤ Build a spatial predictor of adaptive capacity: where does urban form most amplify social learning?

Extending this paperThe paper establishes that interdependence drives social learning and urban life is one form of interdependence. An explicit spatial extension would ask: within a city, which neighborhoods (dense mixed-use areas vs. low-density residential) generate the highest experienced interdependence, and does measured social learning propensity track the within-city gradient? This would transform the paper’s between-group comparison into a within-city accessibility and land-use question, linking cognitive strategy to urban morphology at fine spatial resolution using the same experimental paradigm administered in multiple neighborhoods.
