A community-codesigned LLM-powered chatbot for primary care: a randomized controlled trial

Sairan Li · Yanzeng Li · Shuya Zhou · Xinge Tao · Changjie Yu · Muzi Shen · Wangyue Chen · En Meng · Boyou Wu · Qirui Huang · Frances S. Mair · Jinchao Zhang · Jie Zhou · Lei Zou · Shasha Han Published 2026-01-19 DOI 10.1038/s44360-025-00021-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Pairing an LLM-powered primary-care chatbot (P&P Care, built on GPT-4o mini) with short, community-codesigned e-learning on how to talk to AI significantly raised patients' objective health awareness (2.95 ± 1.22 vs 2.34 ± 1.02 consultation-only; P < 0.001) in a pragmatic RCT of 2,113 urban and rural participants across 11 Chinese provinces. A dual-track role-play codesign framework — where community stakeholders and researchers simulated each other's perspectives — closed AI-literacy and connectivity gaps and let the chatbot outperform usual care and telemedicine on consultation quality, offering a scalable route to deploy LLMs in resource-limited settings.

★

Why It Matters

  • Moves patient-facing LLMs out of well-resourced hospitals. Most clinical-LLM deployments are confined to tertiary hospitals; this is a pragmatic RCT explicitly designed for rural and resource-limited communities that conventional telemedicine bypasses.
  • Identifies "AI health literacy" as the real bottleneck. The gain came not from a better model but from teaching users how to interact — a ≤30-second multimedia e-learning layer that primed richer, longer, more productive consultations (1,495.6 vs 1,221.9 words; P < 0.001).
  • Quantifies who benefits most. Older adults (≥40 y) and patients needing multidisciplinary (multiple ICD-10 domain) care gained disproportionately, challenging the stereotype of low digital adaptability in older users — while rural participants gained least, flagging a residual digital divide.
  • Closes the perception–knowledge gap. E-learning narrowed the "health awareness gap" between self-reported and objective understanding (0.83 vs 1.37; P < 0.001), a clinically meaningful recalibration of over/under-confidence in self-care.
  • Codesign as a transferable method. The dual-track role-play framework — and the engineering for ≤100 kbps, 60%-packet-loss rural networks — is a replicable recipe, not a one-off chatbot.

Core Methods

  • Dual-track role-play codesign: community stakeholders and researchers reciprocally simulate each other's roles across four phases (contextual understanding → cocreation → testing & refinement → implementation & evolution) to mitigate tokenism and power imbalance.
  • Pragmatic, double-blinded RCT: 1:1 individual-level randomization (computer-generated, allocation-concealed) to e-learning-plus vs consultation-only; primary outcome = objective awareness of health needs; CONSORT-AI reporting; sample size re-estimated at interim (500 → 2,000).
  • Chatbot engineering: bidirectional (proactive) dialogue with inquiry + conclusion stages via RLHF; Chain-of-Thought for differential diagnoses & test suggestions; layered safety (Llama2-Guard) and hallucination (SelfCheckGPT) verification pipeline.
  • Literacy-bridging e-learning: ≤30-second modular video tutorials (initiation, closure, time management, content autonomy) chosen by community voting; non-linear navigation; speech-to-text for low-literacy users.
  • Connectivity hardening: tiered service backbone, offline caching, adaptive latency handling, bandwidth-prioritized text; ablation under 60% packet loss / 1,000 ms latency / 100 kbps reaching 98.43% completion.
  • Evaluation & statistics: blinded expert + lay panels scoring six rubrics (awareness, attention, listenability, conciseness, integrity, empathy) on 5-point Likert; Fleiss' κ > 0.80; two-sample t-tests / Mann–Whitney U; Benjamini–Hochberg correction; subgroup analyses by age, setting, sex, ICD-10 domains; R 4.3.0.

① Geolocate the trial: from 11 provinces to a spatial-accessibility map of who the chatbot reaches

Extending this paperThe RCT samples 11 provinces but treats geography as a coarse rural/urban + east/west covariate; the striking rural under-performance (awareness gain 0.53 vs urban 0.73) and network-dropout patterns are never mapped to where care deserts and connectivity gaps actually are. Extending it: couple participant locations with a 2SFCA/R2SFCA supply layer of physicians and broadband to model the chatbot as a virtual supply that fills measurable accessibility voids, predicting which townships would gain most before deployment.

Added value to my researchThis directly feeds my telehealth-and-digital-divide line: a chatbot becomes a tunable "supply" term in R2SFCA service-area delineation, letting me quantify how much an AI clinic shrinks spatial inequity in primary-care access versus building a physical clinic.

② Explain the rural–urban gain gap with explainable ML instead of stratified t-tests

Extending this paperThe paper attributes diminished rural gains to "engagement variations" but offers no attribution model. Added value to my researchThis is a clean testbed for my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction agenda: a place-aware attribution of telehealth efficacy that distinguishes individual literacy from structural (network/dialect) determinants — exactly the human–space coupling I model.

③ Optimize e-learning + connectivity rollout as an efficiency-vs-equity facility-location problem

Extending this paperThe trial is a one-shot snapshot; it cannot say how AI-health literacy and the self-vs-objective awareness gap would spread if the chatbot scaled community-wide. Extending it: simulate uptake with an SIR-style / agent-based diffusion on a population-facility network seeded by the observed subgroup effects (older adults as super-spreaders of literacy, rural friction as low contact rate) to forecast equilibrium coverage and residual inequity.
