Deep learning large-scale drug discovery and repurposing

Min Yu · Weiming Li · Yunru Yu · Yu Zhao · Lizhi Xiao · Volker M. Lauschke · Yiyu Cheng · Xingcai Zhang · Yi Wang Published 2024-08-21 DOI 10.1038/s43588-024-00679-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

MitoReID, a deep learning re-identification model built on an Inflated 3D ResNet backbone, classifies 477 FDA-approved drugs into 38 mechanism-of-action (MOA) categories by reading time-resolved mitochondrial phenotype sequences — achieving 76.32% Rank-1 accuracy and 65.92% mAP. The model correctly predicts MOAs for six unseen drugs and identifies epicatechin in tea as a cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2) inhibitor, a finding subsequently validated in vitro via four independent biochemical assays. This approach provides an automated, cost-effective, high-throughput alternative to biochemical assays for large-scale drug repurposing.

Why It Matters

  • Turns organelle dynamics into a drug-class fingerprint. Rather than expensive biochemical assays, the paper shows that the temporal trajectory of mitochondrial morphology and membrane potential is sufficiently informative to decode drug MOA at throughput impossible with existing methods.
  • Bridges video-understanding AI and cell biology. The re-identification (ReID) framework — borrowed from person re-identification in computer vision — is repurposed to match unknown drug phenotypes to known drug identities, opening a transferable paradigm for image-based drug profiling that requires no explicit feature engineering.
  • Ships a large, open benchmark dataset. 570,096 single-cell images across 1,068 FDA-approved drugs at 16 timepoints (>1.2 TB raw) are deposited openly on Zenodo, constituting a new community resource for phenotypic profiling research.

  • Cost and scalability advantage. GPU-accelerated inference and automated image acquisition eliminate labor-intensive manual feature extraction of classical cell-painting pipelines, making MOA screening scalable to tens of thousands of compounds.

Core Methods

  • High-content live-cell imaging pipeline: H9c2-MtEGFP cells in 96-well plates; ImageXpress Micro Confocal (Molecular Devices); widefield mode; 60× objective; 60 min at 4-min intervals; three-channel time-lapse (Hoechst / MtEGFP / TMRM). Photo-damage from repeated laser stimuli creates a physiologically relevant oxidative-stress model that amplifies drug-induced mitochondrial dynamics.
  • Cell and mitochondria segmentation: nuclear segmentation with Cellpose; mitochondria segmented with Image Pro Plus v6.0 (IPP) to extract 53 morphological features per timepoint as a classical-ML baseline (accuracy <16%).
  • Re-identification (ReID) framework: MitoReID casts MOA prediction as a person-ReID problem — FDA-approved drugs with known MOAs form the "gallery," unknown compounds are the "query." Feature similarity is measured by cosine distance; the top-ranked gallery drug's MOA is predicted for each query sample.
  • Inflated 3D ResNet-50 backbone (I3D ResNet): 2D ResNet-50 extended to 3D to process spatiotemporal sequences (input: 3×16×112×112). Spatial downsampling removed from stages 3–4 to preserve spatial features; output feature matrix: 2,048×4×4×4 (4× parameters vs. standard I3D ResNet-50).
  • Multi-loss metric learning: TriHard loss (online batch-hard mining), center loss, and softmax loss with label smoothing; drugs predicted as DMSO during training excluded from loss to handle non-mitochondria-targeting compounds.
  • Self-supervised pretraining: each of the 1,069 compounds/DMSO treated as an independent identity class; pretraining on this task improves Rank-1 by ~35% compared to training from scratch.
  • Biochemical validation of epicatechin COX-2 inhibition: four-assay validation chain: COX-2 inhibition screening kit (IC50 = 143.9 nM), molecular docking (AutoDock4.2.6; −9.1 kcal/mol), cellular thermal shift assay (CETSA), and surface plasmon resonance (KD = 5.87×10−7 M).

① Inject molecular structure knowledge into MitoReID via graph-based pretraining

Extending this paperMitoReID's self-supervised pretraining treats each drug as an atomic identity class — it has no knowledge that chemically similar drugs (e.g., two ACE inhibitors) should produce similar mitochondrial trajectories. A direct extension: initialize the backbone's feature space with molecular graph contrastive embeddings as a prior, so the image-sequence encoder starts from a chemically meaningful latent space. This would reduce the ~35% pretraining gain currently needed and improve generalization to novel chemical scaffolds, including the 38 natural compounds currently lacking established MOA evidence.

Added value to my researchThis structure-informed initialization strategy mirrors how I use physics-based priors in graph learning: baking domain knowledge (chemical structure graphs) into the feature space parallels baking spatial-physics priors (e.g., decay laws, gravity constraints) into GNN layers. The approach directly informs how I fine-tune geospatial foundation models — providing a template for injecting known relational structure before supervised training on scarce labeled spatial data.

② Extend MitoReID's gallery-matching to zero-shot cell-type transfer

Extending this paperMitoReID is currently trained and evaluated on a single rat cardiac myocyte cell line (H9c2-MtEGFP). Its gallery is fixed; if the same drug produces different mitochondrial dynamics in hepatocytes vs. cardiomyocytes, the gallery is no longer informative for cross-tissue prediction. Extending this paper: incorporate cell-type-conditioned feature adapters from foundation-model transfer learning for drug response prediction, allowing the gallery to be queried across cell types without retraining. This would make the platform applicable to organ-specific toxicity screening beyond cardiac cells.

Added value to my researchThe zero-shot transfer problem here is structurally analogous to my work on spatial transferability: can a model trained in one spatial context (e.g., accessibility scoring in one city) predict in a new, unseen context without full retraining? Foundation-model adapter techniques for cell types directly translate to fine-tuning geospatial foundation models for new urban regions or new health outcome definitions — informing my multi-source data fusion and domain-adaptation pipeline.

③ Augment cosine-distance gallery retrieval with protein–ligand affinity re-ranking

Extending this paperMitoReID's inference ranks gallery drugs by cosine distance in phenotype feature space. This ranking has no awareness of why two drugs have similar phenotypes — shared molecular targets, shared off-targets, or coincidental pathway overlap are indistinguishable. A concrete extension: augment cosine retrieval with a GNN-based protein–ligand affinity re-ranking score, so that drugs with both phenotypically similar mitochondrial trajectories and structurally compatible target binding rank highest. This would reduce false positives from off-target phenotypic mimicry and produce mechanistically interpretable MOA predictions.

Added value to my researchThis two-stage retrieval (phenotype similarity retrieval followed by mechanistic re-ranking) is directly relevant to my explainable ML agenda: a first-stage model retrieves candidates by pattern similarity; a second-stage mechanistic model re-ranks for interpretability. The drug context provides a clean, well-labeled benchmark for developing and validating such two-stage explainability pipelines, applicable to spatial health outcome prediction.

Cross-ref (corpus): Learning characteristics of graph neural networks predicting protein–ligand affinities — 10.1038/s42256-023-00756-9

④ Apply 3D Grad-CAM to localize which spatiotemporal mitochondrial regions drive MOA classification

Extending this paperMitoReID's I3D ResNet learns which spatiotemporal patches of the mitochondrial image sequence are most informative for MOA classification, but the paper does not visualize these attention patterns. A natural extension: apply 3D Grad-CAM or class activation mapping across time and spatial position to reveal whether MOA signals concentrate in early vs. late timepoints, in the TMRM (membrane potential) vs. MtEGFP (morphology) channel, or in specific sub-cellular regions (e.g., perinuclear mitochondrial network). This would generate testable biological hypotheses about temporal drug-action kinetics currently invisible in the model.

⑤ Transfer the ReID phenotype-matching paradigm to neighborhood health outcome profiling

Extending this paperMitoReID's core innovation is that temporal phenotype sequences can identify unknown drug instances by matching them to a labeled gallery — without predefined class labels at inference time. This paper could be extended by applying the same gallery-matching framework to any longitudinal multi-channel biological readout (e.g., gene expression or metabolite time courses from public datasets such as JUMP-CP), positioning MitoReID as a general temporal phenotype-to-identity retrieval engine rather than a single-purpose drug tool.

Added value to my researchThe ReID paradigm — matching unknown instances to a labeled gallery via learned embeddings, without retraining per query — is directly transferable to spatial health surveillance. Neighborhoods or census tracts have multivariate temporal profiles from mobile phone, remote sensing, and POI data, analogous to cells with mitochondrial image sequences. A "neighborhood ReID" approach could identify areas with phenotype profiles matching known high-risk health-outcome zones, enabling prospective surveillance without requiring labeled training data for every outcome type. This would complement my 2SFCA accessibility work by adding a demand-side phenotypic matching layer to identify underserved populations from behavioral and environmental signals.
