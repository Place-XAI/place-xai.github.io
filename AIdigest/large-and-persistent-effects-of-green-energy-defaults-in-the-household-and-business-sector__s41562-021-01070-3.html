Large and persistent effects of green energy defaults in the household and business sectors

Ulf Liebe · Jennifer Gewinner · Andreas Diekmann Published 2021-03-11 DOI 10.1038/s41562-021-01070-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

In two large-scale field studies across Switzerland — covering over 200,000 households and 8,000 enterprises — presenting renewable electricity as the standard (default) option led approximately 80% of customers to stay with green energy, a large effect that remained stable over at least four years. The default worked equally powerfully in the business sector, electricity consumption had only a weak effect on acceptance, and no moral licensing (compensatory over-consumption) was detected. Non-monetary choice-architecture nudges can therefore be a highly effective and durable climate-policy tool for both households and firms.

★

Why It Matters

  • Demonstrates scale and durability. Prior green-default research focused on new customers or short windows; this study covers existing customers and tracks behaviour for up to six years, proving the effect is not just a novelty artifact.
  • Extends the default mechanism to the business sector. Entrepreneurs are expected to be more price-sensitive and homo oeconomicus-like, yet the default effect was equally large for SMEs and even large enterprises (87% uptake despite an average annual surcharge of CHF 2,301).
  • Refutes the moral-licensing concern. A common fear is that opting into green energy makes people feel licensed to consume more; the panel data across six years found no such compensatory rebound, strengthening the policy case.
  • Quantifies real welfare benefits. The authors calculate that an 80% default effect in Germany alone would avoid ~45 million tonnes of CO₂ per year, worth roughly €1,240 million in social-cost-of-carbon terms — making the cost-benefit arithmetic compelling.
  • Gender and attitudes complement the default. Women and those with higher environmental concern were slightly more likely to accept the default, and 76% of a national survey supported green defaults as policy, bolstering democratic legitimacy.

Core Methods

  • Natural / quasi-natural field experiment: the green default was introduced by each supplier on a set calendar date, affecting all customers simultaneously — creating a clean before–after comparison without experimenter demand effects or Hawthorne effects.
  • Fixed-effects panel regression: binary outcome (renewable vs. conventional) modelled with time-trend fixed effects to estimate the year-2 default effect; reported as log-odds coefficients with 95% CIs for both suppliers and both sectors.
  • Binary logit models: used to estimate predicted probabilities of green-default acceptance as a function of prior electricity consumption (kWh in year 1), controlling for the lowest and highest 5% of consumers.
  • Chi-squared tests: used to assess the association between gender and green-default acceptance in household and SME samples of supplier B (where salutation data were available).
  • Mediation analysis (bootstrapped): based on Buis (2010), 3,000 bootstrap replications decompose the gender gap in the survey data into a direct effect and an indirect effect mediated through environmental concern.
  • Welfare / CO₂ calculation: back-of-envelope multiplication of default-acceptance rate × national household electricity demand × CO₂ displacement factor (IRENA) × social cost of carbon (€50/tonne), extrapolated to Germany as a policy illustration.

① Spatialize the green-default effect: does urban–rural context moderate uptake?

Extending this paperThe paper treats the two Swiss supplier areas as a convenient urban/rural contrast but never formally models how spatial context — neighbourhood income, building density, distance to city centre, or local social norms — moderates default acceptance. Extending it: map each customer's postal zone, merge with national census data, and refit the panel models with a geographically weighted regression (GWR) or multi-level framework, testing whether the default is more or less powerful in high-density cores versus suburban and rural peripheries. This would produce the first spatially-resolved map of nudge effectiveness — directly informing which municipalities benefit most from mandating green defaults.

Added value to my researchThis is a natural application of MGWR/GWR to a non-spatial policy outcome: the regression coefficient on "default introduction" becomes the spatially varying estimand. The same pipeline could be used to assess spatial heterogeneity in EV-charging adoption or telehealth uptake, two of my core empirical targets.

② Combine green defaults with carbon pricing to trace synergistic or substitution effects

Extending this paperThe paper consciously focuses on a non-monetary nudge and shows it dominates the weak price effect. But the two instruments are never tested together. A natural extension: construct a panel dataset spanning jurisdictions that combine a green-default mandate with a carbon price, and estimate the interaction term — does a high carbon price amplify the default (complementarity) or make it redundant (substitution)? The paper's own fixed-effects regression framework scales directly to this cross-country design.

Added value to my researchThis connects to my interest in equity effects of policy combinations: carbon prices are regressive, while defaults are income-neutral. A spatial-panel analysis could quantify whether stacking the two instruments narrows or widens the green-energy adoption gap across income deciles — informing combined climate-and-equity policy in cities.

Cross-ref (corpus): Carbon pricing drives critical transition to green growth — 10.1038/s41467-025-56540-3

③ Replace binary logit with an explainable ML model to uncover heterogeneous treatment effects

Extending this paperThe paper uses binary logit models with a handful of covariates (consumption, gender, education, environmental concern). A large sample of 230,000+ households is well-powered for gradient-boosted trees or causal forests, which can recover heterogeneous treatment effects across the joint distribution of covariates without imposing linearity. SHAP values would then identify which customer segments drive the small but significant consumption–acceptance gradient, and whether the gender gap varies by consumption tier — questions the paper raises but cannot fully answer with logit marginal effects.

Added value to my researchHeterogeneous-treatment-effect estimation with SHAP-explained ML is directly applicable to my 2SFCA accessibility work: rather than a single average access score, one can recover the conditional distribution of accessibility gains from a facility-location intervention, stratified by income, age, or urban form — the same conceptual challenge as identifying who benefits from a green default.

④ Model contagion and social-tipping dynamics across the electricity network

Extending this paperThe paper treats each customer as independent, but green-energy adoption may spread through social networks: a household that opts in may influence neighbours and colleagues, and the paper's own finding that women and more environmentally concerned individuals accept the default suggests the existence of identifiable seed nodes. Extending it: reconstruct the underlying social or spatial network (neighbourhood adjacency, workplace co-location), fit a threshold contagion model, and test whether the temporal stability of the default is partly driven by network reinforcement — or whether the default would fade without such reinforcement if social identities are divisive.

Added value to my researchThis maps onto my network community detection methods: identifying clusters of households that move together in energy choices could inform targeting strategies for spatial interventions (e.g., prioritizing community-scale solar or EV infrastructure in tightly connected blocks), bridging behavioural nudge science and spatial planning.

Cross-ref (corpus): Group identities can undermine social tipping after intervention — 10.1038/s41562-022-01440-5

⑤ Link rooftop solar adoption to default-acceptance dynamics in a unified city-scale energy model

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that demand-side green defaults shift 80% of customers to renewable electricity procurement; a parallel question is whether the same customers would accept a supply-side default — i.e., automatic enrolment in community rooftop solar schemes. Extending the green-default framework to solar photovoltaic adoption would test whether the opt-out mechanism transfers across energy technologies and whether it interacts with local solar irradiance or building morphology — data now available at city scale from remote sensing.

Added value to my researchIntegrating green-default acceptance rates into a city-scale facility-siting model — akin to my EV charging-station optimization — would allow joint optimization of where to site rooftop solar and which default tariff structure to offer so that uptake, equity, and grid stability are co-optimized. This bridges behavioural economics and spatial GeoAI in a novel way.
