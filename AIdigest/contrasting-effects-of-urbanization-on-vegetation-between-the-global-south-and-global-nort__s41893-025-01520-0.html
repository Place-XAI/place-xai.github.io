Contrasting effects of urbanization on vegetation between the Global South and Global North

Jiuyi Chen · Bo Qiu · TC Chakraborty · Xin Miao · Yipeng Cao · Lingfeng Li · Siwen Zhao · Yueyang Ni · Xiaohui Tian · Yun Qian · Weidong Guo Published 2025-03-03 DOI 10.1038/s41893-025-01520-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 4,718 cities worldwide (2000–2019), positive indirect urbanization effects (vegetation growth enhancement) offset 56.85% of the negative direct effects (vegetation cover loss). This offsetting is far greater in Global North cities (79.13%) than Global South cities (38.01%), driven by socioeconomic disparities. Projections to 2040 show this divide will persist: roughly 60% of Global North cities but only 30% of Global South cities will become greener — a fundamental equity challenge for sustainable urban development.

Why It Matters

  • First global quantification of the direct–indirect urbanization split. Prior work showed divergent urban vegetation trends but could not attribute changes to vegetation cover loss versus growth enhancement. This framework cleanly decomposes both effects across nearly 5,000 cities simultaneously.

Core Methods

  • Direct–indirect decomposition framework: the paper decomposes urban pixel EVI into contributions from vegetated vs. impervious surfaces using a linear mixture model. "Direct impact" (Idirect) is the EVI loss attributable purely to land-cover conversion (increasing β). "Indirect impact" (Iindirect) is the residual between observed EVI and the hypothetical EVI that would result from climate change and cover loss alone — capturing vegetation growth enhancement driven by urban environmental change and human management.
  • Adjacent substitution for macroclimate separation: the macroclimate factor γ (background vegetation growth) is estimated from surrounding rural natural-vegetation pixels (excluding croplands and elevation outliers), using size-scaled buffer distances to avoid boundary contamination from urbanization. This separates the "climate-only" trajectory from the urbanization-driven signal.
  • Partial correlation analysis: Pearson and Spearman partial correlation coefficients quantify the relative contributions of climate (MAT, MAP, aridity) and socioeconomic (GDP per capita, population density, growth rates, β2000) factors to indirect effects (ωi) and offsetting coefficient (η), controlling for urbanization rate Δβ.
  • Boosted regression tree (BRT) machine learning: BRT models (tree complexity = 10, learning rate = 0.005, bag fraction = 0.6; training r = 0.90, CV r = 0.75, R² = 0.79) learn the relationship between indirect effects and nine predictors (MAT, AI, βt0, Δβ, GDP, GRGDP, POP, GRPOP) for future projection under SSP scenarios.
  • CMIP6 multi-model ensemble: LAI outputs from 9 CMIP6 earth system models are converted to EVI via a calibrated quadratic regression (R² = 0.591), then ensemble-averaged to represent the macroclimate factor (γ') for 2020–2040 projections.
  • Statistical tests: two-sided Student's t-tests (threshold P < 0.01) and Wilcoxon non-parametric tests for GN vs. GS comparisons; 95% confidence intervals for trend lines.

① Decompose the within-city "greening gap" using subcity-scale spatial analysis

Extending this paperThe paper quantifies direct and indirect urbanization effects at the city-average scale, explicitly noting this as a limitation. The GS cities' lower offsetting coefficient (38%) may mask an even more severe intra-city disparity: wealthy urban cores may be greening while peri-urban slums brown. Extending the framework subcity-scale (e.g., 500 m pixels or census-tract-equivalent zones) and combining it with income or population-density layers would reveal whether "global urban greening inequality" reported in the companion corpus paper is driven by between-city or within-city dynamics — and whether the SDG 11 greenspace equity gap is primarily an intra-city or an inter-city problem.

Added value to my researchThis connects directly to my spatial accessibility and health geography work. Subcity decomposition of vegetation loss vs. growth — analogous to how 2SFCA catchment delineation isolates supply from demand — would allow me to model how urban-greening disparities translate into differential health exposure (heat stress, air quality) and to validate my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation methods against a remote-sensing ground truth. The MODIS/GISA data pipeline in GEE pairs naturally with my multi-source data fusion agenda.

② Replace the boosted regression tree with an explainable GeoAI model to spatialize future greening drivers

Extending this paperThe paper's BRT model (CV r = 0.75, R² = 0.79) predicts indirect effects (ωi) from nine city-level predictors. While effective, BRT offers limited spatial interpretation — it cannot reveal how the importance of GDP vs. climate vs. population growth shifts geographically. An extension using geographically weighted or spatially embedded gradient boosting (e.g., MGWR-style feature attribution or SHAP with spatial clustering) on the same predictor set would reveal whether socioeconomic leverage on urban greening is spatially stationary or varies by development region. This would sharpen the policy recommendations: a Southeast Asian city and a West African city might need different greening investment strategies even at the same HDI level.

Added value to my researchThis is precisely the GeoAI + explainable ML agenda I pursue: SHAP attribution in spatially non-stationary models is methodologically central to my work. Applying it to the paper's 4,718-city dataset would produce a transferable "greening-driver atlas" and serve as a compelling application of MGWR/geographically weighted feature importance — extending my existing toolkit from health accessibility into urban ecology.

③ Integrate urban heat island feedbacks to model the vegetation–cooling–health loop

Extending this paperThe paper's indirect urbanization effects include the UHI-driven extension of the growing season as a driver of vegetation growth enhancement — yet the paper does not close the feedback loop. Cities that green more should cool more, which itself modifies the indirect signal in subsequent years. Extending the framework with a coupled vegetation–temperature feedback module (leveraging MODIS LST alongside EVI) would capture this dynamic non-linearity, particularly relevant for Global South tropical cities where the cooling potential of urban vegetation depends strongly on humidity — a dimension the corpus paper on dense canopies and humid cities addresses directly.

If I can quantify which neighborhoods lack both vegetation cooling and healthcare access (a compound vulnerability index), the result would be a novel geospatial indicator for climate-health equity — linking my 2SFCA accessibility methods to this paper's remote sensing pipeline.

④ Use the paper's Global South–North divergence to design targeted urban greening facility-location optimization

Extending this paperThe paper's most actionable finding is that Global South cities invest less in urban greening infrastructure, resulting in lower indirect effects. But it does not prescribe where within Global South cities new green infrastructure should be placed to maximize the offsetting coefficient. Extending the paper with a facility-location optimization layer — treating new parks, street trees, and green corridors as "facilities" to be sited so as to maximize the increase in ωi subject to budget constraints — would turn a descriptive finding into a prescriptive planning tool. The paper's existing BRT model provides the response function (how ωi varies with local conditions), and the optimization can be formulated as a spatial integer program at the subcity scale.

⑤ Build a transferable vegetation-dynamics foundation model pre-trained on the paper's 4,718-city time series

Extending this paperThe paper produces a rich longitudinal dataset: 4,718 cities × 20 years × multiple variables (EVI, β, climate, socioeconomics).
