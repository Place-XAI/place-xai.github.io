Enzyme specificity prediction using cross-attention graph neural networks

Haiyang Cui · Yufeng Su · Tanner J. Dean · Tianhao Yu · Zhengyi Zhang · Jian Peng · Diwakar Shukla · Huimin Zhao Published 2025-10-08 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09697-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

EZSpecificity, a cross-attention-empowered SE(3)-equivariant graph neural network, predicts enzyme substrate specificity from both sequence and 3D structural information — trained on the purpose-built ESIBank database of enzyme–substrate interactions. Validated against eight halogenases and 78 substrates, EZSpecificity achieved 91.7% accuracy in identifying the single reactive substrate per enzyme, versus 58.3% for the prior state-of-the-art, and generalizes across seven proof-of-concept protein families — positioning it as a general-purpose tool to decode the substrate specificity of the millions of enzymes currently lacking annotation.

Why It Matters

  • Fills a vast annotation gap. Millions of known enzymes still lack reliable substrate specificity information; EZSpecificity provides a scalable computational route to annotating this dark matter of biochemistry.
  • Dual-modality design outperforms single-modality baselines. By encoding both protein sequence (via pre-trained language models) and 3D active-site structure (via an SE(3)-equivariant GNN) and linking them through cross-attention, the model captures complementary information that neither modality alone can provide.
  • Experimental wet-lab validation at scale. Rather than benchmarking only in silico, the study tests 78 real substrates against eight halogenases — providing direct evidence of practical utility for enzyme engineering and synthetic biology.
  • Broad applicability across biology and medicine. Predicting enzyme–substrate specificity underpins drug discovery, biosynthesis pathway design, industrial biocatalysis, and understanding microbial metabolism — the model's generality amplifies its impact across all of these.
  • Open data and code. The ESIBank dataset (Google Drive) and EZSpecificity source code (Zenodo) are released, enabling community reuse and benchmarking.

Core Methods

  • SE(3)-equivariant graph neural network: the enzyme active site is represented as a 3D molecular graph; an E(n)-equivariant GNN (following Satorras et al., 2021) processes it while preserving rotational and translational symmetry — crucial for capturing true 3D binding geometry.
  • Substrate molecular graph encoding: substrates are featurized as molecular graphs using RDKit/Open Babel; graph-level representations are extracted and projected into a shared latent space with the enzyme encoder.
  • Cross-attention mechanism: enzyme and substrate representations interact via cross-attention (inspired by Vaswani et al., 2017), allowing each modality to query the other — learning which enzyme residues are important for which substrate functional groups.
  • Pre-trained protein language model fine-tuning: sequence embeddings from a pre-trained protein language model (ESM-2 / evolutionary-scale model, Lin et al., Science 2023) are fine-tuned with parameter-efficient adapters (following Ding et al., Nat. Mach. Intell. 2023), contributing a complementary sequence-level signal.
  • Comprehensive ESIBank training: trained on the purpose-built ESIBank database; evaluated on a held-out unknown-substrate/enzyme database and seven protein family benchmarks.
  • Docking-score comparison baseline: AutoDock Vina used as a representative physics-based baseline to contextualize EZSpecificity's advantage (29% vs. 20% top-5% recall for metabolite-enzyme prediction; 91.7% vs. 58.3% for halogenase substrate identification).
  • Biosynthetic gene cluster (BGC) retrieval: enzyme retrieval within two BGCs (clavulanic acid and albonoursin) as a pathway-scale generalization test.

① Elevate EZSpecificity's molecular graph encoder to a contrastive pre-training paradigm

Extending this paperEZSpecificity learns substrate representations from labeled enzyme–substrate pairs in a supervised fashion. A direct extension: pre-train the substrate molecular graph encoder using contrastive self-supervised learning on the vast pool of unlabeled small molecules (from ChEMBL, PubChem, etc.), then fine-tune on ESIBank. This would address the class-imbalance and data-scarcity challenge the paper acknowledges, since ESIBank coverage is sparser for rare enzyme families. Contrastive molecular graph pre-training has already been shown to generalize effectively across molecular property tasks.

Added value to my researchThe contrastive graph pre-training framework is directly transferable to geospatial contexts: I can pre-train a spatiotemporal graph encoder on unlabeled mobility or POI graph data (urban regions as nodes, flows as edges), then fine-tune for downstream GeoAI tasks such as spatial health outcome prediction or facility accessibility modeling — the same self-supervised strategy, a different graph domain.

② Replace ESIBank's static protein structures with a dynamic ensemble representation

Extending this paperEZSpecificity uses AlphaFold-predicted 3D structures (enriched with AlphaFill ligands/cofactors) as fixed inputs. However, enzyme active sites exhibit conformational flexibility that governs their specificity — a single predicted structure misses this dynamics. A direct extension: incorporate multiple structural conformations generated by molecular dynamics or AlphaFold 3 co-prediction, feeding an ensemble of active-site graphs into the SE(3)-equivariant encoder. The cross-attention mechanism can then learn substrate-specific conformational preferences across the ensemble. AlphaFold 3's biomolecular interaction structure predictions (cited in the paper, doi:10.1038/s41586-024-07487-w) provide a natural source of interaction-aware structures.

Added value to my researchThe principle of replacing a single static representation with a dynamic ensemble maps to my spatiotemporal work: instead of a fixed spatial weight matrix in MGWR/GWR, I can encode temporal snapshots of spatial relationships as an ensemble graph, letting a cross-attention module learn which time slice is most predictive for each health outcome. This operationalizes the spatiotemporal non-stationarity I currently handle with bandwidth tuning.

③ Convert EZSpecificity's predictions into explainable active-site attribution maps

Extending this paperEZSpecificity reports prediction accuracy but does not expose which active-site residues or substrate functional groups drive its specificity calls. A direct extension: add gradient-based attribution (GradCAM or attention-rollout over the cross-attention weights) to produce per-residue and per-atom importance scores. These maps would let biochemists understand why the model predicts a given substrate as reactive — enabling rational enzyme engineering guided by predicted specificity determinants, rather than black-box screening. The MISATO protein–ligand dataset (with quantum-mechanical energy labels per atom) provides a ready benchmark for testing whether the attributions align with known binding-energy hotspots.

Added value to my researchThis is the core of my explainable GeoAI agenda: the cross-attention attribution framework is directly applicable to spatial prediction models where I need to explain which spatial features and which neighboring locations drive a health or accessibility outcome. An attention-weight-based spatial explainability module — analogous to the residue-attribution map here — would be a principled replacement for post-hoc SHAP analysis I currently use.

④ Scale EZSpecificity to pathway-level multi-enzyme specificity prediction via graph-of-graphs

Extending this paperThe paper applies EZSpecificity enzyme-by-enzyme; its BGC evaluation (clavulanic acid, albonoursin) hints at pathway-scale deployment but treats each enzyme independently. A direct extension: represent a biosynthetic pathway as a graph-of-graphs — nodes are individual enzyme graphs, edges encode metabolite hand-off between reaction steps — and learn joint specificity constraints propagated along the pathway. The GNN protein refinement framework (which models residue–residue graphs with message passing) provides a template for this hierarchical graph architecture. This would enable prediction of the entire substrate flux through a multi-enzyme pathway, not just single-step specificity.

Added value to my researchThe graph-of-graphs paradigm maps directly to my physics-informed graph learning work: a multi-scale urban graph where neighborhoods are nodes within city-graphs, which are in turn nodes in a regional network. Pathway-level flow constraints in biochemistry mirror my spatiotemporal flow conservation constraints in mobility/accessibility modeling. Implementing the hierarchical message-passing from this extension would advance my GeoAI architecture design.

⑤ Build a multimodal enzyme foundation model fine-tuned from EZSpecificity's dual-encoder

Extending this paperEZSpecificity combines a protein language model (sequence) with a structural GNN (3D graph) through cross-attention — effectively a dual-encoder multimodal architecture. A direct extension: treat this architecture as a pre-trained foundation model for enzyme biology, fine-tuning it for additional tasks beyond substrate specificity — e.g., predicting promiscuity scores, reaction type, optimal pH/temperature, or directed evolution fitness.
