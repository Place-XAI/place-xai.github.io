A symbolic Neanderthal accumulation of large herbivore crania

Core Conclusion

Excavations at Cueva Des-Cubierta (central Spain) reveal that Neanderthals repeatedly and deliberately brought the crania of large herbivores — bison, aurochs, red deer, and rhinoceros — into a cave over an extended period spanning the depth of Level 3, associating them with small hearths and percussion tools. The near-total absence of post-cranial elements, maxillae, and mandibles — combined with experimental butchering evidence — shows the heads were pre-processed outside and their crania brought in for a second round of working (likely brain extraction), but the sustained, recurrent nature of the behaviour across generations indicates a symbolic or ritual intent beyond subsistence, extending our understanding of Neanderthal cognitive and cultural complexity.

★

Why It Matters

  • Pushes the boundary of Neanderthal cognition. The site provides one of the strongest documented cases of non-subsistence symbolic behaviour by Neanderthals — a category previously considered scarce or contested in the archaeological record.
  • Demonstrates cultural transmission across generations. The consistency of the assemblage throughout the full ~2 m depth of Level 3 indicates the behaviour was recurrent over years, decades, or centuries, implying intergenerational transmission of a cultural practice.
  • Provides a robust multi-method taphonomic argument. Taxonomic, anatomical, taphonomic, experimental butchering, spatial, palynological, and radiometric analyses converge on a single interpretation, setting a methodological standard for inferring intent from faunal assemblages.
  • Offers a new type of Neanderthal site. Unlike known Neanderthal hunting camps, hyaena dens, or burial sites, Cueva Des-Cubierta appears to be exclusively symbolic in function — a previously unrecognised site type in the Middle Palaeolithic record.
  • Opens comparative frameworks. Ethnographic parallels with modern hunter-gatherer skull caches and hunting shrines provide interpretive scaffolding that can be tested systematically against other Mousterian assemblages worldwide.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Site
Cueva Des-Cubierta, Lozoya Valley, Madrid Region, Spain (coordinates 40°55′23″N, 3°48′29″W; altitude 1,112 m). Discovered in 2009; excavations ongoing since 2009 (trenching since 2015).
Stratigraphic unit
Level 3 of the cave infill — approx. 27 m² spatial extent, reaching ~2 m in depth. Biochronologically placed in MIS 4 or the first half of MIS 3.
Faunal assemblage
Total of 2,265 faunal remains over 2 cm recovered from Level 3; 1,616 identified taxonomically. Dominated by large ungulates: bovines (Bison priscus, Bos primigenius), cervids (Cervus elaphus, Capreolus capreolus), steppe rhinoceroses (Stephanorhinus hemitoechus), horses (Equus ferus).
Crania count
35 large mammal crania identified: 28 bovine (Bison priscus 14, Bos primigenius 3, Bos/Bison 11), 5 red deer (all male, with unshed antlers), 2 steppe rhinoceros.
Lithic industry
1,421 anvils, hammerstones, cores, flakes, and shaped tools recovered from Level 3, all Mousterian. An additional 734 lithic elements from Level 2. Raw materials predominantly local (quartz, gneiss).
Charcoal
338 charcoal fragments recovered; 34.0% of total archaeological record affected by fire.
Dating methods
Radiocarbon dating (AMS, Oxford Radiocarbon Accelerator Unit; ultrafiltration method) on charcoal and charred bones. U/Th dating of underlying speleothems S1 and S2 (CENIEH Uranium Series Laboratory and Jaume Almera Institute). One radiocarbon date obtained (OxA-31346, Level 2 charcoal); speleothem U/Th provides maximum age for Level 3.
Spatial tools
Leica total station (TCRP 1205 R400) for 3D coordinate recording; AutoCAD 2021 and QGIS v.3.22 for spatial analysis and visualization.
Human remains
Immature Neanderthal mandible and six deciduous teeth from Level 2 (individual aged 3–5 years by modern standards); μCT-scanned at CENIEH (Phoenix v|tome|x s scanner, GE).
Data deposit
Archaeological and palaeontological remains deposited in the Museo Arqueológico y Paleontológico de la Comunidad de Madrid, Alcalá de Henares, Spain. Accession codes in Supplementary Table 10.

Core Methods

  • Taphonomic analysis: macroscopic and microscopic (binocular up to ×40; digital microscope up to ×200) examination of all bone surfaces for cut marks, percussion marks, carnivore tooth marks, thermal alteration, and post-depositional weathering. Modified frequency indices: NISP, MNE, MAU, %MAU, MNI.
  • Taxonomic and anatomical identification: identification against reference collections and bibliographic sources; comparative anatomy atlases; in-house modern carnivore collections (Smithsonian, British Museum, etc.); Mitutoyo digital caliper measurements to 0.05 mm.
  • Experimental butchering: three experienced butchers processed three cow heads using stone-tool replicas (quartz, quartzite, porphyry) to generate a taphonomic reference for the type and location of modifications expected at each processing stage.
  • Spatial analysis with GIS: all objects recorded by 3D coordinates (Leica total station, ED50 datum); AutoCAD 2021 and QGIS v.3.22 used to map the distribution of crania, thermoaltered materials, charcoal, and lithics across and through Level 3 for temporal-stratigraphic ordering.
  • Pollen analysis: chemical extraction (acid/alkali) and heavy-liquid flotation of sediment samples; counts along 42 slide rows; arboreal/shrub/herb categorization; TILIA software for representation and statistics.
  • Radiometric dating: AMS radiocarbon (ultrafiltration, OxCal v.4.4, IntCal20 calibration) and U/Th isotope dilution on speleothems to bracket the age of Level 3.
  • μCT scanning: high-resolution computed tomography of human mandible (voxel 0.025 mm) and deciduous teeth (voxel 0.019 mm); virtual reconstruction with Mimics v.18 (Materialise).

① Spatial clustering of crania and hearths as a proxy for ritualized place-making

Extending this paperThe paper maps cranium positions and thermoaltered clusters in AutoCAD/QGIS, but the spatial analysis remains descriptive. A direct extension: apply kernel density estimation (KDE) or spatial scan statistics to test whether crania cluster non-randomly relative to identified combustion points — operationalizing the "ritual association with fire" hypothesis as a falsifiable spatial test. Time-stratified KDE through Level 3's vertical sequence would reveal whether the spatial pattern of cranium–hearth co-location intensified or drifted across the deposit's formation history.

② Formalizing cultural transmission: agent-based modelling of recurrent skull caching

Extending this paperThe paper argues that the sustained character of the behaviour across Level 3 implies intergenerational cultural transmission, but this remains a verbal inference. An extension: build a simple agent-based model (ABM) in which Neanderthal agents can learn the cranium-cache behaviour by social imitation with a given fidelity parameter, and test what transmission fidelity and group-size regimes are consistent with a ~2 m of undisturbed stratigraphic consistency. The model would convert the paper's qualitative claim into a quantitative likelihood. This is directly comparable to what quantitative ethnographic meta-analyses do for modern forager behaviour.

Added value to my researchAgent-based modelling is already part of my toolkit (SIR epidemiology, urban mobility). Calibrating an ABM to stratigraphic accumulation data introduces a novel temporal depth dimension — the "stratigraphy as time series" idea — that could generalize to modelling the slow accumulation of urban infrastructure or incremental neighborhood change over decades.

③ From experimental butchering to explainable machine learning for taphonomic signature classification

Extending this paperThe paper's experimental butchering generates qualitative reference data for distinguishing processing stages by bone-element representation. Extending this: compile a training dataset of %MAU skeletal profiles from multiple Mousterian sites (kill sites, hyaena dens, hunting camps, this putative symbolic site) and train a classification model (random forest or gradient boosting) to distinguish site types from bone-element frequencies alone. SHAP values would reveal which skeletal elements are most diagnostic — operationalizing taphonomic theory as an explainable classifier, enabling rapid screening of new assemblages.

④ Mapping ritual landscape connectivity: network analysis of Mousterian symbolic sites

Extending this paperCueva Des-Cubierta is contextualised against a handful of comparator sites (Grotte du Prince, Le Regourdou, Teshik-Tash, Grotte de Chauvet). A structured extension: treat known Mousterian sites with possible symbolic cranium deposits as nodes in a spatial network, with edges weighted by geographic proximity, cultural period overlap, and similarity of the assemblage profile (taxa represented, type of modification). Community detection would reveal whether symbolic cranium practices propagated along geographic corridors — testing the cultural-diffusion versus independent-invention hypotheses against archaeological geography.

⑤ Multi-proxy data fusion for reconstructing past human landscape use intensity

Extending this paperThe paper integrates palynology, charcoal, lithics, fauna, spatial coordinates, and radiometric dates — each a separate modality. The next step is to treat these as heterogeneous multi-source inputs to a joint model that reconstructs human occupational intensity as a continuous latent variable through the deposit. A hierarchical Bayesian model or a multi-modal fusion network could combine pollen principal components, charcoal density, cranium count per 10 cm spit, and lithic density into a single occupational-intensity signal, with uncertainty quantified from the dating constraints. This directly parallels multi-source data fusion in remote sensing.
