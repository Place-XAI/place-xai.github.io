Direct and indirect loss of natural area from urban expansion

Jasper van Vliet Published 2019-07-29 DOI 10.1038/s41893-019-0340-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Urban expansion between 1992 and 2015 converted 38.0 million hectares of land globally — mostly former cropland — but its most damaging effect on nature is indirect: by displacing cropland production, urban growth triggers new agricultural clearing of forests and shrublands elsewhere. Indirect forest losses (17.8–32.4 Mha) are 5–10 times larger than direct ones (3.3 Mha), amplified by a productivity leverage effect — urban areas displace highly productive cropland, requiring even more new, less-productive farmland to compensate. Steering urban development toward less-fertile land and promoting densification could substantially reduce these cascading global land losses.

Why It Matters

  • Reframes the urban expansion–deforestation narrative. Urban land was long considered a minor cause of forest loss; this study shows its indirect footprint via cropland displacement is 5–10 times larger than the direct footprint — fundamentally shifting policy priorities.
  • Quantifies a hidden leverage mechanism at global scale. Cities tend to absorb highly productive farmland; replacement cropland in remote regions is far less productive, so more hectares must be cleared — a measurable leverage factor of 1.34–2.39.
  • Covers 23 years and ten world regions with empirical data. By combining ESA CCI land cover maps with global crop-yield datasets for 1992–2015, it provides the first observation-based (not modelled) estimate of this indirect effect.

Reclassified into six aggregate classes (cropland, forest, urban land, shrubland, grassland, other). Used to derive land cover change for every pixel between 1992 and 2015.

Core Methods

  • Land cover change analysis: overlay ESA CCI land cover maps for 1992 and 2015 pixel-by-pixel; classify transitions (forest/shrubland → urban; cropland → urban; forest/shrubland → cropland) and compute net change areas per world region and globally.
  • Representative productivity calculation: for each ~300 m pixel, compute an area-weighted average yield of wheat, maize, and rice (from Monfreda + Mueller datasets at 5 arcmin, resampled via nearest-neighbour) multiplied by the multi-cropping factor — yielding a comparable productivity metric across heterogeneous croplands.
  • Cropland displacement under two bounding assumptions: (1) within-region displacement, using average productivity of new cropland in the same region; (2) across-all-regions displacement, using global average productivity of new cropland — the two assumptions bound the feasible range of outcomes.
  • Leverage factor quantification: ratio of average productivity of cropland converted to urban land versus average productivity of new replacement cropland. Values >1 indicate that more hectares of new cropland must be cleared than were lost to urbanisation.
  • Sensitivity analysis: repeat core calculations using (a) actual vs. potential yields to isolate the land-management component; (b) three major cereals vs. 16 crop types expressed in caloric value — producing a grid of eight bounding scenarios reported in Fig. 3.
  • Indirect loss estimation: multiply displaced cropland area by the regional fraction of new cropland that converts forest or shrubland, aggregated globally.

① Replace region-level productivity averages with pixel-level spatiotemporal modelling

Extending this paperThe paper's core displacement calculation uses regional-average productivity for new cropland, introducing wide uncertainty bounds. A direct extension would replace these regional averages with spatially explicit, pixel-level productivity estimates for each year — leveraging annual ESA CCI land cover and global yield datasets to track changing productivity surfaces over time. This would narrow the 17.8–32.4 Mha indirect-forest-loss range to a tighter, geographically traceable estimate and allow causal attribution to specific bilateral displacement corridors (e.g., Chinese urban expansion → Brazilian soy deforestation).

Added value to my researchThis spatiotemporal productivitysurface approach maps directly onto my expertise in spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and multi-source data fusion. Integrating remote-sensing crop-yield proxies (NDVI, EVI time series) with trade-flow data to model displacement corridors is an instance of the kind of spatially heterogeneous regression I apply via MGWR/GWR — and would generate labelled examples for training geospatial foundation models on land-use change tasks.

② Extend the leverage model to urban–biodiversity impacts via vertebrate-habitat overlap

Extending this paperThe paper stops at forest and shrubland area loss, but the ecological harm depends critically on which forests are cleared. An extension would overlay the paper's indirect-displacement footprint with species-range maps for terrestrial vertebrates, converting abstract hectare losses into a habitat-threat score per species. Given that indirect losses are spatially correlated with high-biodiversity tropical forests (Amazon, Congo, Southeast Asia), this would likely show that the biodiversity footprint of urban expansion is even more disproportionate than the raw area figures suggest.

Added value to my researchThis creates a GeoAI pipeline connecting urban growth models to ecological risk maps — a spatial accessibility problem in reverse: instead of measuring human access to services, measuring wildlife access to undisturbed habitat as a function of human land decisions. The spatial-accessibility and 2SFCA distance-decay methods I use for healthcare can be adapted to model species-range fragmentation from indirect land-use cascades.

③ Quantify the indirect land cascade under SSP urban-growth scenarios

Extending this paperThe paper is historical (1992–2015); the obvious forward extension is to apply the same indirect-loss accounting to future urban land projections under the Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs). By combining the paper's leverage-factor framework with SSP-consistent global urban land simulations, one could produce scenario-conditioned bounds on indirect forest loss through 2100 — directly comparing compact-city (SSP1) versus sprawl (SSP3/5) trajectories. The paper's own discussion notes that future losses could far exceed prior simulation results, making this a high-priority calibration task.

Added value to my researchSSP-conditioned indirect-loss projections would be a natural input to agent-based models of urban expansion that I use — agents (households, developers) responding to land prices and policy constraints generate the spatial expansion pattern that then propagates through the leverage mechanism. Coupling ABM with this paper's indirect-loss accounting creates a policy simulation tool linking local urban decisions to global forest outcomes.

④ Add pasture displacement to close the full indirect land-loss budget

Extending this paperThe paper explicitly acknowledges that grassland conversion is omitted because managed pasture cannot be distinguished from natural grassland in the ESA CCI data. Closing this gap requires fusing ESA CCI with livestock density datasets (e.g., FAO GLEAM, GLW3) to classify grassland pixels by management intensity. This would allow the study to account for pasture displacement — urban expansion displacing pastoralism, which then expands into forests, especially in the Amazon. The paper's own sensitivity analysis suggests this is a non-trivial additional loss, particularly for Sub-Saharan Africa and Latin America.

Added value to my researchThis is a methodological challenge in multi-source data fusion — exactly the domain I work in. Combining remote-sensing land cover with livestock tabular data and trade-flow proxies to produce a joint cropland-plus-pasture displacement model would exercise the same data-integration skills I apply to mobile-phone + social-media + POI fusion, and could contribute a new training dataset for geospatial foundation models focused on agricultural land systems.

⑤ Build an explainable ML model to predict indirect-loss leverage from city attributes

Extending this paperThe paper computes leverage factors for ten macro-regions, but the actual leverage at the city level is hidden: a city in southeastern China sitting on paddy rice land has a very different leverage factor from one expanding into arid scrubland. An extension would train an explainable gradient-boosted model (or SHAP-attributed random forest) on city-level attributes — population growth rate, surrounding cropland productivity, trade exposure, governance index — to predict which individual cities will generate the highest indirect forest losses. This turns the paper's retrospective regional average into a prospective, spatially targeted policy tool.

Added value to my researchThis directly exercises my agenda of explainable machine learning for spatial prediction. The leverage factor becomes a continuous spatial target variable; SHAP values attribute each city's predicted indirect loss to interpretable features (soil quality, trade connectivity, planning regime). The resulting model is also a template for my healthcare and urban-analytics work: the same SHAP-based attribution pipeline can explain which city characteristics most drive health accessibility gaps, unifying the methodological toolkit across domains.
