A latent measure of cultural racism and its association with US mortality and life expectancy

Tomi Akinyemiju · Oyomoare L. Osazuwa-Peters · Tyson H. Brown · Jude Ramos · Shaun Jones · Lauren E. Wilson · Nancy Krieger Published 2025-08-26 DOI 10.1038/s41562-025-02290-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

This study develops and validates the first theory-informed, multi-indicator Cultural Racism Factor (CRF) — a latent measure derived from nine state-level indicators spanning ideology and cultural expression. Each unit increase in the CRF was associated with ~136 additional all-cause deaths per 100,000 and a one-year decline in life expectancy across all 50 US states and DC, with consistent harm to both Black and white adults. The CRF uniquely advances systemic racism science by capturing the ideological and cultural substrate that upholds structural and interpersonal racism, and is made publicly available for reuse.

★

Why It Matters

  • First empirically validated, multi-dimensional measure of cultural racism. Prior work relied on single indicators or self-report surveys vulnerable to social desirability bias; the CRF combines self-report and non-self-report data within a confirmatory factor analysis framework.
  • Quantifies a previously unmeasured driver of health inequity. The CRF explains substantial variance in all-cause, cardiovascular, and diabetes mortality beyond established structural racism indices, demonstrating incremental and discriminant validity.
  • Universal harm to both Black and white adults. The CRF degrades health for all adults — while Black adults bear a higher burden — mirroring the "universal harm" pattern documented for structural sexism and extending it to racism.
  • Maps cultural racism spatially across the US. The geographic distribution — highest CRF in southeastern, southwestern, and north-central states — reveals contemporary patterns not fully explained by historical Jim Crow or Confederate affiliations.
  • Open data resource for the scientific community. All nine CRF indicators and the resulting factor scores are deposited on Zenodo, enabling replication and extension to additional health outcomes and geographies.

Core Methods

  • Confirmatory factor analysis (CFA): four CFA models tested on nine indicators; final one-factor model (Model 3) retained seven indicators with two pairs of correlated errors, achieving CFI = 0.96, TLI = 0.94, RMSEA = 0.09. Factor loadings range from 0.37 (implicit bias) to 0.90 (lack of white Medicaid support).
  • Psychometric validity battery: external validity (Wilcoxon rank-sum by Jim Crow / Confederacy status); convergent validity (correlation with Price et al. anti-Black racism index, r = 0.66); incremental validity (variance explained above established structural racism indices); discriminant validity (correlations vs. three structural racism measures; test against theoretically unrelated aviation incident rate).
  • Construct reliability testing: average inter-item correlation (0.31), McDonald's omega (0.89, 95% CI: 0.83–0.93), test–retest reliability (r = 0.83 comparing 2004 vs. 2017 CFA using four available indicators).
  • Random-effects linear regression: CRF (continuous and tertile-dichotomized) regressed on age-adjusted mortality rates and life expectancy for NH Black and NH white adults; models adjusted for demographics (log population, region, year), socioeconomics (income inequality, poverty), and % NH Black.
  • Effect measure modification: interaction terms tested for race, geographic region (Northeast / Midwest / South / West), and population density; stratified models estimated where interaction was statistically significant (P < 0.05).
  • Sensitivity analyses: alternative police-citizen indicators (police killings of unarmed Black Americans); inclusion vs. exclusion of Confederate symbols with pre-2017 removal dates; per-indicator associations with all-cause mortality. All analyses in R 4.2.2 (lavaan, nlme, ggeffects, ggplot2).

① Disaggregate the CRF to county or census-tract level using spatial downscaling

Extending this paperThe paper's CRF operates at the state level, driven mainly by data availability. A direct extension: use multilevel regression and post-stratification (MrP) together with high-resolution covariates — residential segregation indices, historical redlining boundaries, demographic composition from Census block groups — to downscale the seven CRF indicators to the county or census-tract level. The paper already applies MrP for racial resentment, Medicaid support, and anti-immigrant sentiments; the same machinery could be applied to generate sub-state CRF estimates that could reveal within-state spatial heterogeneity, which the current state-level analysis cannot detect. Corpus research on residential segregation patterns provides a ready-made covariate layer for this downscaling task.

Added value to my researchFine-grained CRF estimates at census-tract resolution would become a social determinants layer in my spatial accessibility models. Specifically, I could weight 2SFCA catchment scores by tract-level CRF to produce "racism-adjusted accessibility" — quantifying how cultural racism reduces the effective healthcare reach for Black residents even when geographic proximity to facilities is nominally equal. This directly extends my R2SFCA and health geography work toward an equity-aware accessibility metric grounded in validated social-determinants science.

② Extend CRF predictive validity to environmental health disparities via PM2.5 exposure

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates CRF associations with cardiovascular and diabetes mortality, noting that cultural racism operates partly through exposures to harmful environmental conditions. A logical extension: test whether state-level CRF scores moderate the racial gap in PM2.5-attributable cardiovascular mortality. The corpus already contains a paper quantifying racial/ethnic disparities in PM2.5 cardiovascular mortality across US states; linking it to the CRF would test whether states with high cultural racism amplify the environmental health disadvantage for Black residents — providing a second pathway (cultural ideology → differential exposure → differential mortality) beyond the psychosocial pathway the paper primarily discusses. This could also leverage the CRF's Zenodo deposit directly.

③ Incorporate temporal dynamics: CRF trajectory models post-2020 racial reckoning

Extending this paperThe paper is explicitly cross-sectional, using 2017 CRF indicators against 2018–2021 health outcomes. The authors themselves flag this limitation and call for temporal analyses to examine change since "the upsurge of anti-racist advocacy following the murder of George Floyd, and current anti-DEI backlash." A direct extension: reconstruct CRF trajectories from 2010 onward (exploiting the paper's 2004 test–retest framework) using the four temporally consistent indicators (Confederate symbols, implicit bias, anti-immigrant sentiment, racial resentment), then link annual CRF estimates to year-matched mortality data to estimate the temporal dose–response between cultural racism exposure and health outcomes. Structural break tests could detect whether the 2020 Black Lives Matter period produced a measurable shift.

Treating the annual CRF as a spatiotemporal surface (state × year) and identifying recession and intensification clusters would convert the CRF from a static predictor into a dynamic exposure surface, enabling time-lagged regression analogous to how I model lag effects in disease surveillance. This connects my spatiotemporal analysis toolkit to a policy-relevant racism-and-health question.

④ Use the CRF as a spatial confounder layer in geographically weighted regression of healthcare access

Extending this paperThe paper finds that the CRF remains significant after adjusting for income inequality and poverty, suggesting it captures a uniquely cultural dimension of harm. However, associations are estimated via random-effects models assuming spatially homogeneous effects. A direct methodological extension: re-estimate CRF–mortality associations using MGWR (Multiscale Geographically Weighted Regression), which allows the CRF effect size itself to vary spatially. This would reveal whether the mortality toll of cultural racism is spatially stationary (as implicitly assumed) or whether its local impact amplifies in particular regions — for example, whether the South's higher CRF translates into a disproportionately larger health penalty per CRF unit than the Northeast.

Added value to my researchMGWR is one of my core spatial methods. Integrating the CRF as a predictor in MGWR-based health models would yield spatially varying "racism multipliers" on mortality rates — coefficients I could map and interpret with SHAP-style attribution to identify which CRF dimension (ideology vs. expression) dominates locally. This bridges my explainable ML work with a theory-grounded social-determinants variable, enabling a richer narrative than models that include only structural covariates. It would also serve as a methodological demonstration of how latent racism constructs can be incorporated into GWR-family models.

⑤ Link state-level CRF to surveillance infrastructure density as an additional cultural-racism indicator

Extending this paperThe paper deliberately excluded police brutality metrics (killings of unarmed Black Americans) from the final CRF because they loaded poorly and may better belong under structural racism. However, surveillance camera density directed at non-white neighborhoods — a corpus paper documents this across ten US cities — represents a distinct expression of cultural racism as social control: it reflects the prevailing ideology that non-white residents are threats. A future CRF iteration could incorporate city-level surveillance intensity, aggregated to the state level, as a tenth CRF indicator in a revised CFA model, potentially tightening AVE and improving the measure's breadth without the misclassification concerns of police-killing rates.

Added value to my researchSurveillance camera placement is spatial data amenable to my GeoAI methods. If I can estimate city-wide surveillance density from Google Street View imagery (as the corpus paper does), I can fuse this layer with the CRF to build a multi-modal social-control index — one component of which (surveillance) is directly observable at fine resolution from remote sensing data. This would connect my remote sensing and multi-source data-fusion capabilities to the racism-and-health literature, potentially enabling sub-state CRF estimates from observable physical infrastructure rather than relying solely on surveys and administrative records.
