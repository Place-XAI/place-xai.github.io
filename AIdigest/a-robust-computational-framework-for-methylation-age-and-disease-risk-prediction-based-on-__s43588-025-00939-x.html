A robust computational framework for methylation age and disease-risk prediction based on pairwise learning

Yu Zhang · Yichen Yao · Yuanhao Tang · Yuan Cheng · Yinghui Xu · Ying He · Yuan Qi · Li Jin Published 2026-01-13 DOI 10.1038/s43588-025-00939-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

MAPLE, a Siamese-network framework for DNA methylation analysis, resolves the longstanding batch-effect problem of epigenetic clocks by predicting relative age or disease-risk differences between sample pairs rather than absolute values. Across 31 benchmark tests spanning multiple sequencing platforms, preprocessing pipelines and tissue types, MAPLE achieves a median absolute error of 1.6 years for epigenetic age — outperforming all five competing clocks. It further achieves mean AUROCs of 0.97 for disease identification and 0.85 for pre-disease detection in cardiovascular disease and type 2 diabetes cohorts, demonstrating its potential for privacy-preserving clinical methylation-based risk assessment.

Why It Matters

  • Solves the generalizability crisis of epigenetic clocks. Existing clocks (HorvathAge, HannumAge, PhenoAge, AltumAge, cAge) degrade sharply when applied to platforms or cohorts outside their training data; MAPLE's pairwise design encodes data into a unified latent space that is insensitive to these technical confounders.
  • Converts a high-dimensional, low-sample-size problem into a tractable one. Pairwise construction of training samples grows the effective training set quadratically, directly attacking the over-fitting risk endemic to genome-wide methylation arrays.
  • Extends epigenetic clocks to clinical disease risk. Beyond age estimation, MAPLE reliably distinguishes pre-disease states (insulin resistance, prediabetes, coronary artery ectasia, subclinical atherosclerosis) from controls — a clinically meaningful capability not demonstrated by earlier clocks.
  • Respects privacy constraints of large cohort studies. By training against publicly released Cox-model risk scores rather than raw patient data, MAPLE circumvents the data-sharing barriers that limit most advanced prediction frameworks.
  • Ships reproducible, open-source software. MAPLE code and documentation are available on GitHub, and all training/test datasets are publicly accessible via NCBI GEO and the EWAS Data Hub — enabling immediate replication and extension.

Core Methods

  • Pairwise (Siamese) learning: two DNAm profiles are simultaneously fed through a weight-shared MLP encoder; the decoder predicts the age difference (age task) or risk-score difference (disease task) between the pair, encoding data into a unified latent space free of dataset-specific offsets.
  • Two-step training: (1) the Siamese encoder is trained with Huber loss (age) or epsilon-insensitive SVR loss (disease) on pairwise differences; (2) a downstream age predictor (regression) or disease classifier (cross-entropy softmax) is fine-tuned on the frozen encoder outputs.
  • Batch-effect quantification: PCA + Principal Variance Component Analysis (PVCA) confirms that batch variance drops from 0.88 to 0.53 after MAPLE encoding, while age-explained variance rises from <0.01 to 0.42.
  • Data augmentation for disease imbalance: minority disease samples are augmented by randomly masking 20% of beta values and imputing with per-CpG cohort means; ablation confirms this outperforms SMOTE and SVMSMOTE.
  • Interpretability via Integrated Gradients (IG): top-2,000 CpG sites ranked by IG scores are used for GO enrichment analysis; robustness confirmed across SHAP, DeepLIFT and Feature Ablation.
  • Disease risk scoring at inference: risk scores are computed as normalized Euclidean distances from test embeddings to healthy and disease anchor centroids in latent space — avoiding the need to share raw patient data.
  • Benchmarking: 31 benchmark tests (24 blood + 7 tissue types) against HorvathAge, HannumAge, PhenoAge, AltumAge and cAge; Cox-LASSO models for CVD/T2D baselines. Evaluation via MAE, PCC, AUROC and AUPRC (1,000-bootstrap CI).
  • Hardware: trained on NVIDIA A100 GPU (12.92 h age; 26.85 h CVD; 22.86 h T2D); inference for 1,000 profiles takes ~3 min.

③ Integrate MAPLE with a foundation model to separate genetically determined from environmentally induced methylation change

Extending this paperThe Discussion explicitly proposes pairing MAPLE with sequence-based models (MethylGPT) to disentangle genetic and environmental determinants of DNAm. A concrete extension: use a pre-trained genomic foundation model to generate predicted DNAm profiles from DNA sequence alone, then train MAPLE on the residuals (empirical minus predicted methylation). The pairwise encoder would then encode only the environmentally modulated component — yielding epigenetic age and disease-risk scores that are genuinely independent of genetic architecture. This directly addresses the current limitation that MAPLE cannot separate inherited from acquired epigenetic variation.
