A whole-slide foundation model for digital pathology from real-world data

Core Conclusion

Prov-GigaPath, a whole-slide pathology foundation model pretrained on 1.3 billion image tiles from 171,189 real-world clinical slides across 28 cancer centres, achieves state-of-the-art performance on 25 out of 26 digital pathology tasks — including cancer subtyping, mutation prediction, and zero-shot vision–language tasks — demonstrating that real-world data scale and ultra-large-context modelling of entire gigapixel slides are the decisive factors for advancing computational pathology.

★

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the tile-subsampling bottleneck. Prior models could only process a small fraction of a gigapixel slide; GigaPath's LongNet-based architecture handles the full sequence of up to 70,000 tiles, capturing slide-level context that was previously inaccessible.
  • Real-world clinical data at unprecedented scale. The 1.3-billion-tile Prov-Path dataset from Providence Health Network is more than 5× larger than the widely used TCGA benchmark, enabling better generalization to out-of-distribution clinical samples.
  • Broad clinical utility demonstrated. A single pretrained model covers 9 cancer subtyping tasks and 17 pathomics tasks (mutation prediction, TMB), outperforming specialized models on nearly all of them.
  • First zero-shot gene-mutation prediction via vision–language alignment. By pairing slides with their clinical pathology reports, Prov-GigaPath enables zero-shot mutation prediction — a capability not previously demonstrated in pathology VL models.
  • Fully open-weight for clinical AI research. Model weights and code are publicly released, directly accelerating the development of foundation models in resource-constrained clinical settings worldwide.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Primary dataset
Prov-Path — proprietary real-world clinical dataset from the Providence Health Network (28 cancer centres, USA). Contains 1,384,860,229 image tiles from 171,189 H&E-stained and immunohistochemistry whole-slide images, sourced from biopsies and resections of more than 30,000 patients covering 31 major tissue types. Data collected before March 2023.
Public benchmark
TCGA (The Cancer Genome Atlas) — approximately 30,000 slides and 208 million image tiles; used for benchmarking generalizability. TCGA-LUAD subset (whole slides + labels) available via NIH Genomic Data Commons at portal.gdc.cancer.gov.
De-identified subset
A small de-identified patient subset from Prov-Path is released at zenodo.10909616 and zenodo.10909922 to support model replication.
Vision–language pairs
17,383 WSI–pathology report pairs constructed from Prov-Path; reports cleaned using GPT-3.5 (OpenAI); text embeddings computed with text-embedding-ada-002.
Tissue resolution
All slides standardized to 0.5 µm per pixel (20× magnification); tiles cropped to 256 × 256 pixels using pyvips; tissue segmentation via Otsu thresholding.
Benchmark tasks
26 tasks total: 9 cancer subtyping tasks (NSCLC, BRCA, RCC, COADREAD, HB, DIFG, OVT, CNS, EGC) and 17 pathomics tasks (18-biomarker pan-cancer mutation prediction, 5-gene LUAD mutation, pan-cancer 5-gene mutation, TMB prediction); evaluated on both Providence and TCGA data using 10-fold cross-validation.
Compute
Preprocessing on a cluster of up to 200 nodes (32 CPU cores, 256 GB RAM each), ~157 hours. Slide-encoder pretraining: 16 nodes × 4 × 80 GB A100 GPUs, ~2 days (3,072 A100 GPU hours).

Core Methods

  • Two-stage pretraining (GigaPath): (1) tile-level self-supervised learning with DINOv2 using a Vision Transformer (ViT) backbone; (2) slide-level self-supervised learning with a masked autoencoder built on LongNet (dilated self-attention) to handle sequences of up to 70,000 tiles without quadratic cost.
  • Whole-slide serialization: WSI tiles are arranged in row-major order to form a long token sequence; coordinate discretization uses a grid (grid size 256, 1,000 rows/columns) to encode spatial position within the slide.
  • Downstream fine-tuning: the pretrained tile encoder is frozen; only the LongNet slide encoder is fine-tuned per task; a shallow ABMIL (attention-based multiple instance learning) layer aggregates contextualized tile embeddings for classification.
  • Vision–language alignment: CLIP-based contrastive learning aligns the slide encoder (Prov-GigaPath) with PubMedBERT on 17,383 WSI–report pairs; zero-shot classification is performed by matching slide embeddings against text prompts for cancer types or gene names.
  • Benchmarking: 26 prediction tasks evaluated with 10-fold cross-validation; AUROC and AUPRC as primary metrics; statistical significance tested via one-sided Wilcoxon test; competing models include HIPT, CtransPath, REMEDIS, MI-Zero, BiomedCLIP, and PLIP.
  • Report denoising: real-world pathology reports were cleaned using in-context learning with GPT-3.5, removing patient/institution identifiers while preserving cancer-relevant content.

① Spatially-aware fine-tuning: geography of real-world clinical data as an explicit model input

Extending this paperProv-GigaPath's pretraining dataset comes from 28 cancer centres across a single US health network, yet the paper treats all slides as exchangeable regardless of where they were collected. A direct extension would incorporate geographic metadata (centre location, regional cancer incidence rates, environmental exposures) as conditioning variables during fine-tuning — allowing the model to learn which slide-level features are universal versus site-specific. This would explicitly address the distribution shift the paper acknowledges when applying Providence-trained models to TCGA data from different institutions.

② From institution-level access disparities to a geospatial access model for digital pathology AI

Extending this paperThe paper's central motivation — that real-world clinical slides differ dramatically from curated benchmarks — is fundamentally a data-access equity problem. Cancer centres contributing to Prov-Path are concentrated in specific geographies; patients in rural or low-resource areas are systematically underrepresented in the 30,000-patient training set. Extending this paper: map the spatial distribution of contributing slides against cancer incidence and population density to quantify representation gaps by geography, then use targeted data collection or synthetic augmentation (e.g., stain transfer) to close them — directly improving model fairness for underserved regions.

This would pair with my telehealth/digital divide work to map AI-readiness of health systems spatially.

③ Scaling laws in pathology: how does slide-level performance scale with data volume and model size?

Extending this paperThe paper hints at scaling laws — GigaPath pretrained on Prov-Path (1.3B tiles) beats GigaPath pretrained on TCGA (208M tiles), and a smaller 23M-parameter model still outperforms competitors — but stops short of fitting a formal power-law relationship between pretraining tokens, model parameters, and downstream AUROC. A rigorous scaling-law study with multiple model sizes (23M, 300M, ViT-L) and data fractions would determine the returns to data and compute, enabling rational resource allocation for the next generation of pathology foundation models. The companion paper on a pathology foundation model for cancer diagnosis and prognosis (UNI, Nature 2024) provides an independent data point for cross-model scaling comparison.

Understanding how performance scales with pretraining data volume in pathology provides a transferable methodological blueprint — specifically, whether "quality beats quantity" thresholds exist that are relevant to scarce geospatial labelled data.

④ Multimodal extension: integrating EHR tabular data and clinical notes alongside whole-slide images

Extending this paperProv-GigaPath's vision–language extension aligns slides with free-text pathology reports — a critical first step — but the Providence dataset also contains structured clinical records (cancer staging, demographic data, genomic mutation profiles). A natural and tractable extension: add a tabular encoder (e.g., a transformer over structured EHR fields) as a third modality alongside the visual slide encoder and the PubMedBERT text encoder, and pretrain with a three-way contrastive objective. This would allow the model to leverage the same data hierarchy already present in Prov-Path without requiring new data collection.

The challenge of aligning tabular clinical data with unstructured pathology images is structurally identical to aligning GPS trajectories with satellite imagery for urban activity inference — the contrastive alignment architecture of this paper is a reusable template. ⑤ Explainability: spatially grounded attention maps as interpretable clinical biomarkers

Extending this paperProv-GigaPath generates slide-level embeddings through LongNet's dilated self-attention, but the paper does not extensively analyse which spatial regions of the slide drive predictions. The attention weights in the ABMIL aggregation layer are a natural interpretability handle: visualizing which tile positions receive high attention for a given mutation prediction would (1) validate biological plausibility against known histological correlates, (2) generate candidate spatial biomarkers from morphology alone, and (3) help clinicians understand model decisions — a prerequisite for regulatory approval. The generative AI copilot for pathology paper (PathChat, Nature 2024) points toward human-in-the-loop interpretable AI as the next frontier.

My experience with explainable ML (SHAP, MGWR spatial attribution) translates directly — I can apply similar post-hoc spatial attribution frameworks to pathology foundation models, linking tile-level attention to slide coordinates to produce spatially explicit clinical explanations. This could also inform my physics-informed graph learning by identifying which spatial scales carry predictive signal.
