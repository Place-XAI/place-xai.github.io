Multimodal cell maps as a foundation for structural and functional genomics

Leah V. Schaffer · Mengzhou Hu · Gege Qian · Kyung-Mee Moon · Abantika Pal · Neelesh Soni · Andrew P. Latham · Laura Pontano Vaites · Dorothy Tsai · Nicole M. Core Conclusion

By jointly measuring biophysical interactions (AP–MS) and immunofluorescence images for over 5,100 proteins in human U2OS cells and fusing them through self-supervised multimodal deep learning, a global map of 275 subcellular assemblies spanning four orders of magnitude (10−8–10−5 m) was constructed and validated. The map assigns new functions to 975 proteins, resolves 111 novel heterodimeric structures, implicates 21 recurrently mutated assemblies in paediatric cancer, and ships as an open interactive portal plus a reusable Python toolkit — providing a reference platform for structural and functional cell biology.

Why It Matters

  • First proteome-wide, multiscale reference cell map. Covering more than 5,100 proteins across scales from nanometres to micrometres, this is the most comprehensive map of human subcellular organization to date, filling a 20-year gap since genome sequencing.
  • Multimodal integration beats any single modality. Integrating imaging and biochemical data identified 104 assemblies that neither modality alone could resolve, demonstrating that multimodal self-supervised learning substantively extends biological discovery.
  • Systematic validation by an orthogonal third modality. Whole-cell size-exclusion chromatography–MS independently corroborated 89 assemblies (43% of those with >5 proteins), providing rigorous quality control absent from most omics studies.
  • Assigns function to hundreds of unknown proteins. 138 previously uncharacterized proteins receive functional candidates through guilt-by-association, with C18orf21 (RNase MRP) and DPP9 (interferon signalling) experimentally confirmed.
  • Translates to cancer genomics. Aggregating mutations at the assembly level — rather than the gene level — amplifies signal: 21 mutated assemblies and 102 validated new cancer proteins were identified in 772 paediatric tumours across 18 types.
  • Platform contribution with open tools. The MuSIC portal (musicmaps.ai/u2os-cellmap) and the Cell Mapping Toolkit (GitHub) enable the community to build maps for any cell type or disease state.

Core Methods

  • Multimodal feature extraction: AP–MS interaction networks embedded with node2vec (1,024-dim vectors); immunofluorescence images embedded with DenseNet-121 pre-trained on protein IF images (1,024-dim vectors per protein).
  • Self-supervised multimodal autoencoder: modality-specific encoders compress each 1,024-dim input to 128 dims; a joint encoder fuses both into a single L2-normalized 128-dim latent z. Training jointly minimizes reconstruction loss (MSE per modality) and triplet (contrastive) loss to separate dissimilar proteins and pull similar ones together, without any supervised labels.
  • Pan-resolution hierarchical community detection: pairwise cosine similarities in the shared embedding define 10 proximity networks (top 0.2–10% pairs); HiDeF (Hierarchical community Decoding Framework) finds communities at multiple resolutions simultaneously, yielding a directed acyclic graph of 275 assemblies from nanometres to micrometres.
  • LLM-guided annotation (GPT-4): the GSAI pipeline uses a chain-of-thought prompt to query GPT-4 for assembly names, confidence scores, and referenced analysis essays; a citation module validates references via PubMed. Median GPT-4 confidence of 0.92 for known assemblies and 0.85 for newly discovered ones.
  • SEC–MS validation: whole-cell SEC of U2OS lysates into 40 fractions (3 replicates); Pearson co-elution correlations within assemblies compared to null via Wilcoxon rank-sum test (BH-corrected FDR).
  • AlphaFold-Multimer structure prediction: all protein pairs within small assemblies (<10 proteins; 1,666 pairs) folded by AlphaFold-Multimer; structures scored by weighted ipTM+pTM; 111 high-confidence novel heterodimers identified.
  • Integrative structural modelling (IMP): Rag–Ragulator 11-mer assembled by combining cryo-EM template (PDB: 6WJ2), AlphaFold single predictions, and pairwise AlphaFold-Multimer restraints; sampling by Monte Carlo replica-exchange Gibbs.
  • Assembly-level cancer mutation analysis (HiSig): L1-regularized linear regression of somatic mutation counts against assembly protein membership across 772 paediatric tumours; empirical P-values from 10,000 random permutations.

① Extend the self-supervised co-embedding to additional omics modalities for a truly universal cell atlas

Extending this paperThe paper fuses exactly two modalities (AP–MS interactions and immunofluorescence images). Its self-supervised autoencoder architecture is modality-agnostic: each input feeds its own encoder before joint fusion. A direct extension would add proximity-labelling (BioID/TurboID), cryo-electron tomography maps, or single-cell transcriptomics as third and fourth encoders within the same multimodal structured embedding framework. The paper's own Discussion explicitly anticipates this, noting that "expanded cell maps incorporating new modalities ... as well as time-dependent measurements" are a natural next step. Adding temporally resolved omics (cell-cycle phases) would convert the current static snapshot into a dynamic trajectory map, allowing detection of assemblies that rewire across biological states.

Added value to my researchThe same encoder-fusion pattern maps onto my multi-source data fusion agenda (mobile-phone records + remote sensing + social media). Just as the paper merges protein-level graph features with spatial image features into one latent space, I can co-embed GPS trajectory graphs with satellite imagery and POI networks to learn a unified place representation — a geospatial analogue of the cell map that could anchor spatiotemporal accessibility models without requiring handcrafted feature engineering across heterogeneous sources.

② Replace fixed graph topology in node2vec with a physics-informed graph neural network that encodes structural priors

Extending this paperThe paper represents AP–MS interaction data with node2vec, a random-walk-based embedding that treats all edges equally and ignores known biochemical constraints (stoichiometry, binding affinity, evolutionary conservation). A direct extension: replace node2vec with a message-passing GNN that encodes edge weights derived from CompPASS scores and AlphaFold-predicted interface confidences as physical priors, effectively building a physics-informed graph embedding of the interactome. This would improve embedding quality specifically for small, low-degree assemblies that node2vec's fixed-length walks represent poorly — exactly the regime where the paper concedes it currently misses structures.

Added value to my researchMy physics-informed graph learning agenda operates in the same design space: replace generic graph embeddings with architectures that respect domain laws. The paper's node2vec → physics-informed GNN upgrade for protein networks is a direct template for replacing unconstrained GNNs in urban mobility graphs with models that embed conservation laws (flow balance, capacity constraints) as inductive biases — a blueprint transferable across biological and spatial network domains.

③ Use the paper's assembly-level mutation aggregation logic as a spatial cluster analysis template for disease geography

Extending this paperThe paper's most striking cancer finding is methodological: aggregating rare mutations at the assembly level — using HiSig's L1-regularized linear model against hierarchical protein membership — boosts signal from noise, revealing 21 mutated assemblies where gene-by-gene scanning found only 6 significant genes. This "aggregate over known structure" principle is general. An extension would apply it to multi-ancestry paediatric cohorts or adult TCGA data to ask whether assembly-level mutational signatures cluster by tumour microenvironment composition, linking the cell map directly to spatial transcriptomics. The paper explicitly notes the majority of its 102 cancer proteins were missed by prior adult-cancer gene lists.

Added value to my researchThe hierarchical aggregation logic is directly analogous to my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and areal-unit analyses for health geography. Instead of protein assemblies, substitute spatial health units (census tracts, neighbourhoods) and instead of mutation frequencies, use disease incidence rates: the HiSig-style regularized linear model then identifies which neighbourhood-level clusters — defined by a known spatial hierarchy — carry excess disease burden beyond what individual locations reveal. This bridges cell-level assembly thinking and area-level health geography into a unified multi-resolution signal-amplification framework.

④ Apply the paper's LLM-assisted annotation pipeline to interpret geospatial cluster outputs

Extending this paperThe paper's GSAI pipeline uses GPT-4 with chain-of-thought and one-shot prompting to name and functionally interpret gene-set assemblies, achieving median confidence 0.92 for known assemblies. A direct extension is to fine-tune this pipeline on cell-type-specific maps: the paper notes that 155 out of 258 assemblies are cell-type-specific between U2OS and HEK293, meaning LLM interpretation of cell-type divergence could automate comparative proteome annotation at scale — a bottleneck the paper currently addresses only through manual "Annotation Jamborees."

Added value to my researchGeoAI models — whether clustering urban neighbourhoods by mobility patterns or grouping census tracts by health indicator profiles — routinely produce cluster outputs that require expert interpretation. The GSAI pattern (structured prompt + confidence scoring + citation retrieval) is directly adoptable: give GPT-4 a cluster's top spatiotemporal feature profile and ask it to name the urban typology and hypothesize drivers. This would automate the interpretive bottleneck in my explainable machine learning for spatial prediction, converting model-output clusters into human-readable policy narratives with literature backing — essentially an LLM co-pilot for GeoAI results.

⑤ Build a transferable cell-map solver that generalizes across cell types via transfer learning with foundation models

Extending this paperThe current U2OS cell map is built entirely from scratch for one cell type. Extending it to additional cell types requires re-running the full AP–MS and IF data collection pipeline — expensive in time and reagents. A smarter extension: pre-train the multimodal autoencoder on the existing U2OS data as a foundation encoder, then fine-tune on smaller paired datasets from HEK293, iPSC, or disease-state cell lines, using the paper's own HEK293 BioPlex 3.0 data as a starting transfer target. The paper's own data show that 103 assemblies are conserved across U2OS and HEK293, providing a natural "anchor" set for contrastive transfer that aligns shared components while allowing cell-type-specific assemblies to diverge.

Added value to my researchTransfer learning for geospatial foundation models is a central theme of my current research agenda. The cell map's two-stage strategy — self-supervised pre-training on rich data, contrastive fine-tuning on sparse data — maps exactly onto my approach of pre-training a geospatial encoder on high-coverage cities then fine-tuning on data-scarce regions for accessibility prediction. The cell map paper makes the transfer task concrete: conserved assemblies are like spatially invariant features (road network topology, population density gradients) while cell-type-specific assemblies are like place-specific cultural or policy contexts that require local adaptation. This biological analogy sharpens the conceptual design of my fine-tuning protocols for geospatial foundation models.
