A deep learning approach for rational ligand generation with toxicity control via reactive building blocks

Pengyong Li · Kaihao Zhang · Tianxiao Liu · Ruiqiang Lu · Yangyang Chen · Xiaojun Yao · Lin Gao · Xiangxiang Zeng Published 2024-11-08 DOI 10.1038/s43588-024-00718-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

DeepBlock is a deep learning framework for de novo ligand design that decomposes molecule generation into two steps — building-block sequence generation and rule-based reconstruction — inspired by DNA-encoded compound library chemistry. It outperforms existing methods on affinity, synthetic accessibility, and drug-likeness, and, when coupled with simulated annealing or Bayesian optimization, successfully generates ligands with reduced toxicity while preserving target binding affinity. The approach works directly from protein sequence, removing the need for 3D structural information.

★

Why It Matters

  • Bridges the synthesizability gap. Most generative drug-design models optimize affinity alone, producing molecules that are difficult or impossible to synthesize. DeepBlock's block-based strategy directly encodes synthetic feasibility — Retro* success rates substantially exceed those of competing methods.
  • Toxicity as a first-class objective. The field has largely ignored toxicity during generation. DeepBlock demonstrates that simultaneous toxicity and affinity optimization is achievable within a single framework, a critical step toward clinically viable candidates.
  • Protein-sequence-only inputs. Structural information (3D crystal structures) is unavailable for most novel targets. DeepBlock's contribution-perception module allows it to identify binding-site residues from sequence alone, greatly expanding applicability.
  • Large-scale validated dictionary. A block vocabulary of 10,701 reactive fragments, extracted from 2.3 million ChEMBL molecules, combined with pre-training, extends the explorable chemical space beyond what small protein–ligand pair datasets allow alone.

Core Methods

  • BRICS-based molecular fragmentation: molecules are decomposed into reactive building blocks using BRICS retrosynthetic rules; a custom graph-search serialization algorithm converts the fragment graph into a unique, reversible block sequence with ~99.997% reconstruction fidelity on ChEMBL.
  • Block Generative Network (BGNet): a conditional variational autoencoder (CVAE) using GRU-based block encoder/decoder, a bag-of-words auxiliary loss to prevent latent variable collapse, and a target contribution perception module that weights protein residue features by predicted binding relevance.
  • ESM-2 protein language model integration: a transformer-based protein language model pre-trained on large-scale sequence data provides rich residue-level features without requiring 3D structural information.
  • Binding contribution prediction: a multilayer perceptron predicts residue-level contribution coefficients (inversely correlated with distance to binding pocket center); Pearson r = 0.68 (p < 0.001) on the ABL2 test case.
  • Simulated annealing (SATMO) and Bayesian optimization (BOTMO): two property-control strategies that use BGNet as a candidate generator and optimize a composite energy function balancing toxicity (TOXRIC LD50 predictor trained via AutoML/TPOT) and drug-likeness (QED) while preserving docking affinity.
  • Benchmark evaluation: 100 molecules generated per test protein (100 proteins); compared against LiGAN, 3D-SBDD, Pocket2Mol, and TargetDiff on Vina score, QED, SAscore, Retro* success rate, Tanimoto diversity, fragment similarity, and scaffold similarity.

① Replace fixed block dictionary with out-of-distribution generalization

Extending this paperDeepBlock's major acknowledged limitation is that it can only generate blocks from its 10,701-entry dictionary extracted from ChEMBL 31 — novel chemical entities outside this vocabulary are inaccessible. The paper suggests de novo block generation as future work but does not pursue it. A direct extension: pair DeepBlock's fragment graph with a molecular out-of-distribution (OOD) detector that flags generated SMILES near the dictionary boundary and triggers a secondary atom-level generative model to synthesize novel blocks on demand. This hybrid would preserve DeepBlock's structural consistency guarantee while unlocking genuinely novel chemical space.

Added value to my researchThe same OOD-awareness challenge appears in geospatial foundation model fine-tuning: a model trained on POI distributions in dense cities may generate nonsensical accessibility predictions when applied to sparse rural geographies. DeepBlock's block-boundary detection logic translates directly into a domain-shift detector for spatial prediction models — alerting when a GeoAI model is extrapolating beyond its training distribution, directly supporting open-science reproducibility and data-fusion robustness in my work.

② Extend the binding contribution perception module into a spatial attention explainability layer

Extending this paperDeepBlock's contribution-perception network produces per-residue attention weights that loosely correlate with binding-pocket geometry (Pearson r = 0.68). These weights are used internally to improve generation but are not systematically mined as an interpretable explanation of which residues matter and why. A natural extension: train a post-hoc explainer that maps DeepBlock's attention weights to known pharmacophore patterns — validating that the model has learned chemically meaningful representations rather than spurious correlations. This would transform an auxiliary training signal into a structured interpretability output usable by medicinal chemists.

Added value to my researchThis is structurally identical to the explainable ML agenda in my GeoAI work: spatial attention weights in graph neural networks applied to urban accessibility or public-health prediction often correlate with known geographic confounders (population density, road hierarchy) but are rarely verified against domain knowledge. DeepBlock's residue-contribution validation protocol — comparing predicted attention against a ground-truth proximity metric — is a transferable template for validating spatial attention in GeoAI models, relevant to my explainable ML for spatial prediction research line.

③ Integrate 3D geometric structure into the block-generation step

Extending this paperDeepBlock generates 2D SMILES/block sequences and only later invokes ETKDG to generate 3D conformers for docking evaluation. The paper acknowledges this and proposes integrating approaches like LiGAN for 3D generation as future work. A concrete extension: replace BGNet's GRU decoder with a geometric graph neural network that operates on the 3D pocket geometry when structural information is available, and falls back to ESM-2 sequence features when it is not — giving a hybrid that exploits structure opportunistically without requiring it. Learning on topological surface and geometric graph features from the protein pocket directly would improve affinity estimation accuracy.

Added value to my researchThe pattern of having a model that gracefully degrades from rich structured inputs to sequence-only inputs when 3D geometry is unavailable mirrors a key challenge in spatiotemporal analysis: some geographic problems have dense sensor coverage (GPS, remote sensing) while others have only tabular socioeconomic proxies. DeepBlock's dual-mode architecture — structured-input path vs. sequence-only fallback — is a design template for physics-informed graph learning with heterogeneous data availability, directly relevant to my spatiotemporal kernel density and agent-based modeling work.

④ Replace AutoML toxicity surrogate with a multi-task graph neural network trained on richer bioactivity data

Extending this paperDeepBlock's toxicity predictor is a single-task regressor (MACCS fingerprints + TPOT AutoML, R² = 0.60) trained on one mouse LD50 sub-dataset from TOXRIC. This is a relatively weak surrogate: it ignores structural context beyond 2D fingerprints, covers only one toxicity endpoint, and may not generalize across chemical scaffolds. A direct extension: replace it with a multi-task graph neural network trained jointly on multiple TOXRIC endpoints (hepatotoxicity, cardiotoxicity, etc.) and pre-trained on ChEMBL bioactivity assays — the same chemical space DeepBlock already exploits. The richer surrogate would make the SA/BO optimization more realistic and its trade-offs more nuanced.

Added value to my researchThe same surrogate-model quality problem appears in environmental health geography: predicting disease risk at fine spatial resolution often relies on single-exposure surrogate models (e.g., a PM2.5 spatial interpolator used as a health exposure proxy). DeepBlock's critique of single-task surrogates and the multi-task solution directly supports a multi-outcome spatial health risk framework — aligning with my health geography and telehealth/digital divide research, where multiple health outcomes should be modeled jointly to avoid Simpson's paradox-style confounding.

⑤ Apply DeepBlock's two-stage decompose-then-optimize paradigm to spatial facility siting

Extending this paperDeepBlock's core innovation is decomposing the molecule generation problem into two sequentially tractable subproblems: (1) generate a sequence of chemically compatible fragments, then (2) reconstruct a valid whole molecule from them. This divide-and-conquer strategy avoids the combinatorial explosion of directly generating full molecules. The approach could be extended to optimize entire lead series simultaneously — decomposing multiple scaffolds in parallel with shared block representations — enabling portfolio-level drug design rather than single-molecule optimization, which would be a significant advance for multi-target drug discovery.

Added value to my researchThe decompose-then-optimize paradigm translates directly to spatial facility location: rather than jointly optimizing the placement of all healthcare facilities in a city (NP-hard), first decompose the city into coverage sub-regions (analogous to building blocks), then assign facilities to sub-regions under budget constraints. This is the algorithmic core of my EV charging-station efficiency-vs-equity optimization and my 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility analysis: DeepBlock's two-stage approach provides a formal template for hierarchical spatial optimization that scales to large cities, directly enriching my urban analytics and public-facility accessibility research.
