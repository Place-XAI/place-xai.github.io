Uncertainty-driven dynamics for active learning of interatomic potentials

Maksim Kulichenko · Kipton Barros · Nicholas Lubbers · Ying Wai Li · Richard Messerly · Sergei Tretiak · Justin S. Smith · Benjamin Nebgen Published 2023-03-06 DOI 10.1038/s43588-023-00406-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

UDD-AL (uncertainty-driven dynamics for active learning) accelerates the construction of machine learning interatomic potentials by modifying the molecular dynamics potential energy surface to steer simulations toward high-uncertainty, under-represented configurations. Applied to glycine conformational sampling and acetylacetone proton-transfer reactions, UDD-AL achieves broad, balanced chemical-space coverage at low temperature — sampling both near-equilibrium and high-energy transition states — without the random distortions that afflict high-temperature MD, and without manually defined collective variables as required by metadynamics.

★

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a core bottleneck in ML potential development. High-quality interatomic potentials require diverse training data spanning transition states and rare conformers — configurations that standard low-temperature MD almost never visits. UDD-AL finds them efficiently without expensive, high-temperature runs.
  • Eliminates the need for manual collective variable selection. Unlike metadynamics, UDD-AL uses the ensemble uncertainty itself as the guiding "collective variable," removing domain-expert bias and making the method broadly applicable without system-specific tuning.
  • Balanced low- and high-energy coverage. High-temperature MD samples high-energy states but neglects low-energy regions; UDD-AL at 350 K achieves coverage comparable to 600–1,000 K MD while preserving near-equilibrium structural distributions — critical for temperature-sensitive and metastable systems.
  • Negligible computational overhead. Because ensemble uncertainties and forces are already computed at every MD step in query-by-committee AL, adding the bias potential costs essentially nothing extra, making the method immediately deployable in existing AL pipelines.
  • Enables reactive pathway sampling without bond-breaking knowledge. In the acetylacetone test, UDD drives 90 proton-transfer events at 350 K compared to zero in unbiased MD — demonstrating that uncertainty-driven exploration can reveal reactive chemistry that would otherwise require much higher temperatures or handcrafted reaction coordinates.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Training data
125 near-equilibrium glycine geometries as initial training set, sampled from a 5-ps MD trajectory at 350 K using the pre-trained ANI-1x potential. Each AL iteration adds 16 new DFT-computed conformations; the final training set comprises 1,280 + 125 = 1,405 glycine conformations.
Test data
50,000 glycine structures from a 50-ns MD simulation at 400 K using the ANI-1ccx potential (0.5-fs time step), used to benchmark model accuracy across the conformational space.
Quantum chemistry
Reference energies and forces computed at the ωB97X-D/cc-pVTZ level of theory using PSI4 code. This DFT level of theory provides high-fidelity quantum reference data for new conformations added at each AL iteration.
Acetylacetone test
Pre-trained ANI-1x ensemble (trained at the wB97x DFT level). No new DFT data generated for this system; 0.5-ns trajectory analyses with 350 K MD, 350 K UDD, and 620 K MD simulations. The proton-transfer barrier is 4.7 kcal mol−1 (DFT) vs. 6.3 kcal mol−1 (ANI-1x overestimate).
ML model
ANI-type neural network potentials: architecture 768:32:16:8:1, three hidden layers, Gaussian activations, eightfold cross-validation ensemble (NM = 8). Atomic environment vector: 32 radial parameters (cutoff 4.6 Å), 8 radial + 8 angular parameters (cutoff 3.5 Å), 768-element descriptor.

All generated data at Zenodo (doi:10.5281/zenodo.7526389). Source code at GitHub/lanl/Active-Learning-Framework and Zenodo (doi:10.5281/zenodo.7522399).

Core Methods

  • Query-by-committee (QBC) active learning: an ensemble of NM = 8 ANI neural network potentials, trained on different cross-validation splits, provides an uncertainty estimate as the variance of ensemble energy predictions; configurations exceeding an uncertainty threshold ρ are selected for DFT labeling and added to the training set.
  • Uncertainty-driven dynamics (UDD): the bias potential Ebias(σE2) = A[exp(−σE2 / NM NA B2) − 1] is added to the physical energy surface. High-uncertainty configurations are energetically favored; forces derived via the chain rule steer dynamics toward under-sampled regions automatically, without any collective variable definition.
  • UMAP dimensionality reduction: 672-dimensional atomic environment vector activations from the first layer of a pre-trained ANI-1x model are projected to 2D using UMAP for visualization of glycine conformational space coverage across sampling strategies.
  • Langevin thermostat MD: all simulations use a Langevin thermostat (friction coefficient 0.01 a.u.), interfaced with the Atomic Simulation Environment (ASE). Each AL iteration runs 16 independent 200-ps MD simulations (1-fs time step, 200,000 steps limit).
  • Comparison baselines: four strategies compared quantitatively — 350 K MD-AL, 600 K MD-AL, 1,000 K MD-AL, and 350 K UDD-AL — via energy-distribution histograms, RMSE on the held-out test set, UMAP coverage maps, and bond-length distribution analysis.

① Transfer UDD-AL's uncertainty-steered dynamics to geospatial active learning for spatial prediction

Extending this paperUDD-AL's central insight — that biasing a dynamical sampler toward high-uncertainty regions of the prediction space accelerates data collection far more efficiently than random or high-"temperature" sampling — is entirely domain-agnostic. The paper demonstrates this for molecular configuration space, but the same principle applies to any iterative data acquisition problem where an ensemble of models provides uncertainty estimates. A direct extension would be to replace molecular dynamics with a spatial random walk on a geographic graph, where the "temperature" analogue is geographic exploration radius, and the bias potential steers sensing resources (e.g., survey sites, sensor deployments) toward regions of high ensemble disagreement in a spatial prediction model. This would generalize UDD-AL from atomic to geographic space while keeping the mathematical structure of equation (4) intact.

This would convert UDD-AL's two fixed hyperparameters into a self-tuning system, removing the only significant manual step remaining in the pipeline. ③ Extend UDD-AL from ensemble variance to calibrated conformal prediction intervals

Extending this paperUDD-AL uses ensemble variance (σE2) as its uncertainty signal. Ensemble variance is a practical proxy but is known to be poorly calibrated: it can underestimate uncertainty in interpolation and overestimate it in extrapolation. A rigorous extension would replace the ensemble variance bias with conformal prediction intervals or deep kernel uncertainty estimates that provide coverage guarantees. This would make the "high-uncertainty regions" targeted by the dynamics provably correct — i.e., the regions where the trained potential genuinely cannot be trusted — rather than heuristic estimates. The drug-discovery active learning paper in the corpus benchmarks several such calibrated uncertainty methods in a closely related iterative labeling context.

Added value to my researchCalibrated uncertainty is equally critical for explainable spatial ML: when my ensemble of gradient boosting or random forest models predicts healthcare access scores, knowing whether the ensemble variance reflects genuine spatial data gaps or model artifacts matters for policy use. Importing conformal prediction into the spatial ensemble framework would provide statistically valid confidence intervals on accessibility maps — a key step toward trustworthy, publicly communicable GeoAI outputs.

⑤ Apply UDD-AL's "rare event" logic to discover spatial tipping points in health accessibility systems

Extending this paperThe paper's most striking demonstration is that UDD steers molecular dynamics into chemically rare transition states — configurations almost never visited by unbiased low-temperature sampling but critical for reactive chemistry. This "rare event" logic is conceptually transferable to any complex system where important states are seldom visited under normal dynamics. For spatial health systems, the analogous rare events are accessibility tipping points: combinations of facility closure, population shift, or transport disruption that cause sudden large drops in healthcare access for vulnerable populations. Extending UDD-AL: define a spatial-accessibility ensemble (e.g., multiple 2SFCA-based models with different demand estimates) and use ensemble disagreement as a bias to steer Monte Carlo simulation of policy counterfactuals toward high-uncertainty, high-impact combinations of spatial changes.
