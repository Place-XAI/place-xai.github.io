Observed evaporative cooling of urban trees and lawns during heatwaves

Tao Fang · Weiting Hu · Chunhua Yan · Chao Zhang · Bei Wang · Muhammad Hayat · Guo Yu Qiu Published 2025-12-09 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00353-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Urban lawns and trees cool cities through opposite water-use strategies during heatwaves. Lawns behave as spenders: high canopy stomatal conductance lets them surge evapotranspiration (+37.65%) for strong cooling (7.05 °C m−2 per day), but they rapidly deplete surface water. Trees behave as savers: they close stomata (−35.06%) and tap deeper soil moisture, holding transpiration nearly steady (1.77 → 1.66 mm per day) for a lower but more stable cooling of 3.5 °C m−2 per day. The implication: lawn-versus-tree greening should be matched to local water availability, not chosen by cooling power alone.

  • Resolves a recurring greening debate with mechanism, not just correlation. Instead of asking "do trees or grass cool more," the paper links the cooling difference to the underlying stomatal regulation and water-use strategy, explaining why the answer flips during heat stress.
  • Reframes cooling as a water-budget trade-off. Lawns deliver fast cooling but burn through surface water; trees deliver durable cooling by drawing on deep soil moisture — a critical distinction for water-scarce cities choosing greening portfolios.

  • Directly actionable for adaptation planning. The findings argue for climate- and water-availability-aware vegetation design — favouring trees where irrigation is constrained and lawns only where water can sustain peak demand.

Core Methods

  • Long-term flux & sap-flow observation: measure ET of lawns and TR of trees continuously over 2014–2023, then isolate responses across 54 identified heatwave events.
  • Cooling-effect quantification: convert observed evapotranspiration into a daily cooling effect (°C m−2 per day) to compare lawn and tree on a common energetic basis.
  • Stomatal-regulation analysis: estimate canopy stomatal conductance (G) and its sensitivity (m) to VPD, characterizing the contrasting "spender" (lawn) vs. "saver" (tree) regulation.
  • Stable-isotope water tracing: use soil and stem-water δ18O/δ2H to attribute the proportional depth of water uptake, evidencing tree access to deeper soil moisture.
  • Structural driver modeling: apply Partial Least Squares Path Modeling (PLS-PM) to link climate, soil water content and precipitation to ET/TR before, during and after heatwaves.
