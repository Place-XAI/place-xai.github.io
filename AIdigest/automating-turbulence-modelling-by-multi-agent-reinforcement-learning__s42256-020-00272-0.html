Nature Machine Intelligence Reinforcement Learning Turbulence Modelling Large-Eddy Simulation Fluid Dynamics

Automating turbulence modelling by multi-agent reinforcement learning

Guido Novati · Hugues Lascombes de Laroussilhe · Petros Koumoutsakos Published 2021-01-04 DOI 10.1038/s42256-020-00272-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) can automate the discovery of subgrid-scale closure models for large-eddy simulations of turbulent flows without any supervised training on DNS data. The learned policy (πLL) matches or surpasses classical Smagorinsky-based models in reproducing the energy spectrum of direct numerical simulations and, critically, generalizes across unseen Reynolds numbers and grid resolutions well beyond the training regime. This establishes MARL as a principled alternative to both hand-crafted physics models and supervised learning for computational fluid dynamics.

★

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the supervised-learning bottleneck in CFD. All prior data-driven turbulence models relied on supervised learning requiring expensive filtered DNS targets and suffering from compounding a priori/a posteriori errors. MARL bypasses this by optimizing directly against high-level flow statistics.
  • Generalization is the key advance. Classical SGS models require manual tuning per flow regime. The MARL policy, trained on Reynolds numbers Reλ ∈ {65–163}, remains stable and accurate up to Reλ = 205 — a regime never seen during training.
  • Grid-resolution transfer at no extra training cost. The policy trained on 323 grids transfers to 643 and 1283 grids by simply scaling the number of agents — no retraining needed.
  • Physically meaningful beyond the reward objective. Even though MARL only optimizes energy spectra, the learned model correctly recovers kinetic energy, integral length scales, and dissipation rates — quantities never included in training.
  • Entirely open-source and reproducible. Flow solver (CubismUP_3D), RL library (smarties), and training code are all publicly available on GitHub at github.com/cselab/MARL_LES.

Core Methods

  • Multi-agent RL (MARL) setup: Nagents = 43 = 64 agents dispersed uniformly in the LES domain, each observing a 28-dimensional state (5 invariants of the velocity gradient + 6 invariants of the Hessian, non-dimensionalized, plus global energy spectrum modes up to Nyquist frequency and dissipation rates). All agents share a single policy network with ∼6,211 parameters.
  • Policy network: feed-forward NN with 2 hidden layers of 64 units each, tanh activations, skip connections; outputs mean and standard deviation of a Gaussian policy over the local Smagorinsky dissipation coefficient Cs2.
  • ReF-ER (Remember and Forget Experience Replay): off-policy algorithm combining experience replay (V-RACER) with a KL-divergence trust-region constraint that rejects samples with importance weights outside [1/C, C] and adds a penalty term to attract the policy back toward past behavior. Critical for stabilizing MARL training where correlated agent actions create confounding feedback.
  • Reward functions: two variants — rG (local Germano-identity error) and rLL (global regularized log-likelihood of LES energy spectrum vs. DNS statistics). The rLL reward produces the best-performing model.
  • Baselines: Standard Smagorinsky Model (SSM, fixed Cs) and Dynamic Smagorinsky Model (DSM, spatiotemporally adaptive Cs via Germano identity) — both widely used industrial-standard LES closure models.
  • Generalization evaluation: models evaluated on held-out Reλ ∈ {60, 82, 111, 151, 190, 205} (all unseen during training) and on grids 643, 1283 with proportionally scaled agent counts.

① Replace the independent-agent architecture with a graph-communicating policy for spatially heterogeneous flows

Extending this paperThe current MARL framework places agents on a uniform grid with no explicit inter-agent communication — each agent acts only from its local state and global spectral statistics, which works for statistically homogeneous isotropic turbulence. For wall-bounded or geometrically complex flows (one of the paper's own stated open challenges), the optimal closure at any grid point depends on neighboring conditions. A direct extension: model the agent network as a dynamic spatial graph where each agent aggregates messages from spatial neighbors before selecting its local Cs2. A GNN policy would let agents implicitly learn spatial correlations of subgrid-scale stresses — potentially critical for wall-layer modelling. The cross-resolution transferability result already hints that the learned policy encodes scale-invariant spatial patterns; explicit graph structure would make this inductive bias architectural and interpretable.

Added value to my researchMy physics-informed graph learning agenda maps directly here: the same GNN-on-spatial-graph formulation I apply to urban accessibility networks (nodes as neighborhoods, edges as travel links) is applicable to the LES grid where nodes are computational cells. Encoding physics priors (energy conservation, Navier–Stokes constraints) as soft losses in the GNN training mirrors my approach of baking spatial autocorrelation and scaling laws into graph-based spatiotemporal prediction. The inter-agent coordination mechanism also directly informs my agent-based modeling of service access dynamics, providing a principled RL recipe for spatially distributed agents that must learn cooperative policies.

② Train turbulence closures from experimentally measurable statistics, bypassing the DNS reward entirely

Extending this paperThe paper's reward is computed from DNS energy spectra — data four orders of magnitude more expensive than LES to generate. The authors explicitly flag as an open direction training from experimental measurements (wall shear stresses, velocity statistics from PIV/LDV). Extending this: define a reward using macroscopic statistics observable from real sensors (drag coefficients, integral dissipation, coarse velocity profiles) and learn a closure without ever running a DNS. The key algorithmic challenge is dealing with partial, noisy, and temporally sparse observations — a natural fit for combining MARL with Bayesian reward inference or inverse RL to estimate latent DNS-equivalent statistics from coarse measurements. This would make MARL-LES applicable to turbulent flows too complex or expensive for DNS (geophysical flows, combustion).

The inverse problem of inferring a reward from observable proxies is structurally identical to calibrating a 2SFCA accessibility model from revealed-preference data (actual healthcare utilization rates vs. predicted access scores), suggesting a unified methodological framework.

③ Transfer the MARL closure framework to spatial accessibility modelling — RL-discovered floating catchment policies

Extending this paperThe paper's core insight is that the hand-crafted Smagorinsky constant Cs can be replaced by a locally adaptive policy that maximizes a high-level objective without supervision. This maps precisely onto the 2SFCA family of accessibility models: the fixed catchment radius and distance-decay function in 2SFCA play the same role as Cs in SSM — they are manually chosen constants governing how service supply "diffuses" to demand. A direct extension: formulate a MARL problem where agents correspond to healthcare facilities, each observing local demand density and travel-time patterns, and output a catchment size and decay function maximizing an equity reward (e.g., minimizing the spatial Gini coefficient of access scores). The ReF-ER trust-region mechanism — crucial in MARL-LES to prevent correlated agent actions from destabilizing training — would be equally important here, as overlapping catchments create the same confounding-feedback structure between facilities.

④ Benchmark ReF-ER stability guarantees across PDE closure problems to establish when trust-region ER is necessary

Extending this paperReF-ER is the methodological engine that makes MARL stable in the LES setting: it constrains policy drift by rejecting experiences with importance weights outside a trust region and adding a KL-divergence penalty. This addresses the fundamental challenge of compounding errors in any autoregressive model trained off-policy. The paper demonstrates ReF-ER in one setting; an important extension would be a systematic meta-study across PDE closure problems (shallow-water equations, advection-diffusion, Boussinesq) comparing ReF-ER vs. simpler ER strategies (PPO, SAC, DDPG) to identify when the trust-region constraint is necessary vs. when standard ER suffices. This would produce actionable algorithmic guidelines for the scientific ML community adopting RL-based model discovery.

Added value to my researchLong-horizon stability under compounding errors is a shared challenge in my Monte Carlo SIR epidemic models where small parameter errors accumulate over multi-week forecasting horizons, and in my RL-based resource allocation formulations. The ReF-ER trust-region mechanism offers a principled recipe for stable off-policy training in these settings — particularly for RL-augmented SIR models where facility allocation agents must learn from delayed, aggregated health outcomes over weeks, structurally analogous to turbulence closure learning from temporally averaged energy spectra.

⑤ Formalize the "side-channel diagnostic" of Fig. 6 as a physical coherence benchmark for scientific ML models

Extending this paperOne of the paper's most compelling results (Fig. 6) is that πLL, trained only on energy spectra, also correctly recovers kinetic energy, integral length scales, and dissipation rates — physical quantities never included in the reward. The authors interpret this as evidence of physical soundness beyond the optimization target. This constitutes a general diagnostic: after training any ML closure on one physical objective, evaluate it on a suite of related but untrained physical quantities. Extending the paper: systematize this test as a "physical coherence score" analogous to probabilistic calibration, and use it to rank competing closure models across PDE families — giving the community a physics-grounded model selection criterion that complements standard held-out test loss.
