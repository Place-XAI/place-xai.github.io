Large language model diagnostic assistance for physicians in a lower-middle-income country: a randomized controlled trial

Ihsan Ayyub Qazi · Ayesha Ali · Asad Ullah Khawaja · Muhammad Junaid Akhtar · Ali Zafar Sheikh · Muhammad Hamad Alizai Published 2026-02-06 DOI 10.1038/s44360-025-00007-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

In a single-blind RCT of 60 licensed physicians in Pakistan, those given access to GPT-4o after structured 20-hour AI-literacy training scored 71.4% versus 42.6% for the conventional-resources-only group — an adjusted improvement of 27.5 percentage points (95% CI 22.8–32.2; P < 0.001) with no increase in time per case. While GPT-4o alone outperformed physicians, 31.4% of physician-plus-LLM responses exceeded the median AI-only score, demonstrating human-AI complementarity. The findings suggest that AI-literacy training is the critical enabler for LLM-assisted diagnostics in resource-limited settings.

Why It Matters

  • First rigorous RCT of LLM-assisted diagnostics in an LMIC. Most AI-in-medicine trials have been conducted in high-income settings; this establishes a generalizable evidence base for the 4.7 billion people living in lower-middle-income countries where diagnostic error rates are significantly higher.
  • Reverses the "no-benefit" finding of prior untrained-physician trials. A recent U.S. study found no benefit from GPT-4 access without AI-literacy training. This RCT isolates the training effect as the pivotal variable — a crucial practical finding for deployment policy.
  • Identifies a prior-experience paradox. Physicians with less prior LLM exposure improved more than frequent users (+46.4 pp vs. +25.4 pp), implying suboptimal habitual usage undermines performance — raising important questions about the design of AI-literacy curricula.
  • Demonstrates human-AI complementarity beyond simple benchmarking. In nearly one-third of cases, the augmented physician outperformed the AI acting alone, showing that human judgment adds measurable value that pure automation cannot replicate.
  • Immediately actionable for health systems. The 20-hour curriculum, GPT-4o subscription, and outcome rubric are reproducible — providing a low-cost, scalable pathway for other LMICs to reduce diagnostic errors.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Study design
Single-blind randomized controlled trial; registered at ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT06774612); approved by the LUMS Institutional Review Board.
Participants
60 licensed physicians recruited from multiple medical institutions in Pakistan between 10 January 2025 and 17 May 2025; 58 completed the study (2 withdrew consent). Median years of practice: 8.5 years (IQR 3.3–12.0). 35 participants (60%) virtual, 23 (40%) in-person.
Intervention
GPT-4o (ChatGPT Plus; OpenAI) as a commercial LLM chatbot accessed alongside conventional resources (PubMed, Google Search without AI features); intervention group had LLM access, control group did not. Interactions were recorded via study accounts.
Clinical vignettes
6 expert-developed clinical vignettes of moderate diagnostic complexity derived from real patient cases and modified for research. Each presented in randomized order via Kobotoolbox secure survey platform. A total of 342 cases completed (172 LLM group, 170 control).
AI-literacy training
20-hour structured curriculum covering LLM capabilities, prompt engineering, hallucination risks, and critical output evaluation. All 58 participants completed training before randomization.
Scoring rubric
Expert-validated rubric developed by a panel of 3 licensed physicians. Inter-rater reliability: Krippendorff's α = 0.85; internal consistency: Cronbach's α = 0.80. Each case scored by 3 blinded graders; mean score used.
LLM-alone benchmark
GPT-4o prompted 3 times per case by study team (zero-shot) for a total of 18 LLM-only observations. Mean score: 82.9% (SD 10.4%).
Data availability
Raw participant scores and Python analysis scripts available via figshare at 10.6084/m9.figshare.30121300. Source data provided with the paper. Clinical vignettes not released publicly to preserve assessment integrity.

Core Methods

  • Randomized controlled trial (RCT) design: single-blind, two-arm parallel-group RCT. Participants randomized to (i) GPT-4o plus conventional resources or (ii) conventional resources only after all completing the same AI-literacy training. A browser extension blocked Google AI Overviews for the control group.
  • 20-hour AI-literacy curriculum: pre-randomization structured training covering LLM capabilities, appropriate use, prompt engineering, hallucination risks, and critical evaluation of AI outputs — designed to create a precise isolating comparison.
  • Structured diagnostic reasoning assessment: participants submitted top-3 differential diagnoses, supporting and opposing evidence for each, a final diagnosis, and up to 3 next steps using a standardized template. The rubric captures both diagnostic accuracy and reasoning quality.
  • Linear mixed-effects models: primary and secondary outcomes modeled as continuous variables with random effects for participants (to capture within-physician correlation) and for cases (to control for case-difficulty variability). Holm-Bonferroni correction maintained family-wise error rate at α = 0.05.
  • Intent-to-treat analysis: all 342 completed cases analyzed at case level with cases clustered by participant. Sensitivity analysis included both complete and incomplete cases.
  • Subgroup analyses: stratified by years of clinical practice (median split at 8.5 years), self-reported LLM experience (less-than-monthly vs. monthly or more), and gender.
  • LLM-only arm: post-hoc exploratory comparison using a zero-shot prompt applied 3 times per vignette; LLM responses graded with identical blinded rubric alongside human responses.
  • Reliability validation: Krippendorff's α for inter-rater reliability; Cronbach's α for instrument internal consistency.

① Map the spatial distribution of AI-literacy training gaps across Pakistan to prioritize rollout

Extending this paperThis RCT recruited physicians from multiple institutions in Pakistan but treats the country as a uniform LMIC context. A direct extension: geocode participant institutions and hospitals, link to district-level diagnostic error proxies (e.g., avoidable in-hospital mortality, referral rates), and identify priority training zones where the marginal benefit of LLM access would be highest. The 20-hour curriculum is a discrete, scalable intervention that can be spatially optimized — analogous to facility-location problems.

Added value to my researchThis bridges clinical-AI evaluation to my healthcare accessibility and 2SFCA work: instead of mapping physical travel time to clinics, I can model AI-competence access — which physicians (by district) have received training, what patient populations they serve, and where the LLM-benefit gap is largest. Mobile phone data (a tool I already use) could provide catchment-area delineation for physician-to-population mapping in data-scarce LMICs.

② Explain the 27.5 pp improvement spatially and demographically using explainable ML

Extending this paperThe paper's subgroup analyses (experience, LLM exposure, gender) reveal heterogeneous treatment effects but are restricted to three binary stratifiers via mixed-effects models. An extension: collect richer physician-level covariates (hospital tier, specialty, internet quality, district GDP) and apply SHAP-attributed gradient boosting to decompose the 27.5 pp effect into contributions from individual features. This would transform the RCT's aggregate finding into a precision-training model — telling training programmes exactly which physician profiles benefit most.

Added value to my researchMy agenda of explainable ML for spatial prediction maps directly: I can treat physician location as a spatial covariate and use MGWR to test whether the LLM benefit varies across space (urban vs. peri-urban hospitals) — intersecting XAI with health geography at a level the original RCT design cannot reach.

Cross-ref (corpus): The landscape of AI implementation in US hospitals — 10.1038/s44360-025-00016-7

③ Build a multi-agent triage layer on top of the paper's single-LLM setup

Extending this paperThe paper uses a single GPT-4o instance accessed by individual physicians. The "prior-experience paradox" (less-experienced LLM users improved more) and the 11.5 pp gap between LLM-alone and physician-plus-LLM suggest that the interface between physician and model matters enormously. An extension: replace the bare chatbot with a multi-agent framework — one agent drafts a differential, a second critiques it against clinical guidelines, a third flags hallucinations — then present the synthesized output to the physician. This extends the paper's intervention from a tool to a structured reasoning scaffold.

Added value to my researchMulti-agent reasoning frameworks are a natural complement to my interest in fine-tuning geospatial foundation models: the same critic-validator pattern can be applied to geospatial LLM outputs — one agent generates spatial predictions, another checks physical plausibility constraints. This paper's RCT design offers a ready template for evaluating such agent architectures with human-in-the-loop studies.

Cross-ref (corpus): A multi-agent framework combining large language models with medical flowcharts for self-triage — 10.1038/s44360-026-00112-2

④ Extend the AI-literacy curriculum to community-facing primary care chatbots in LMICs

Extending this paperThis paper trains and evaluates physicians; but in LMICs, first contact often occurs with community health workers or patients themselves using consumer chatbots. An extension: take the paper's validated 20-hour curriculum, condense it into a patient-facing health-literacy module, and test whether community health workers armed with a codesigned LLM chatbot can triage effectively — a step downstream on the care pathway that the paper's infrastructure already supports.

Added value to my researchMy work on telehealth and digital divide is directly implicated: community-level AI-literacy is a spatial access variable — rural areas have lower digital infrastructure, limiting chatbot reliability. My 2SFCA framework could model effective LLM access (connectivity × training availability × language support) as a composite spatial accessibility score, identifying communities where AI-mediated care would fail even if the technology were deployed.

Cross-ref (corpus): A community-codesigned LLM-powered chatbot for primary care: a randomized controlled trial — 10.1038/s44360-025-00021-w

⑤ Quantify and monitor LLM clinical decision support safety via hallucination auditing in African/LMIC deployments

Extending this paperThe paper frames hallucinations as a risk managed by AI-literacy training but does not audit which of the LLM's outputs in the trial were hallucinations, nor measure how often physicians caught versus acted on them. An extension: apply the blinded rubric retroactively to the LLM-generated outputs (already recorded in study accounts) to classify hallucination incidence, severity, and physician detection rate by case type — building a hallucination taxonomy for LMIC clinical contexts that could inform safe-deployment standards.

Added value to my researchFrom a data science and reproducibility perspective, LLM hallucination auditing is closely related to my interest in open science and data quality. For geospatial AI, hallucinated spatial facts (wrong coordinates, fabricated POI locations) are an analogue problem: developing hallucination detection pipelines for clinical LLMs would transfer to evaluating geospatial LLM outputs, which I need for foundation model fine-tuning and quality control in FAIR data pipelines.
