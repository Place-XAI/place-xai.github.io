Uncovering the hidden role of peri-urban vegetation in modulating urban precipitation

Rui Shao · Jiaqi Li · Weiwei Shao · Yicheng Wang · Dawen Yang · Guangheng Ni · Yuting Yang · Zhiyong Yang · Jiahong Liu · Yong Zhao · Jianhua Wang · Hao Wang Published 2026-03-30 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00416-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Vegetation change in the peri-urban ring surrounding a city quietly steers that city's rainfall. Across 1,029 cities worldwide, vegetation-driven shifts in peri-urban evapotranspiration (ET) are carried by atmospheric moisture transport into the urban core, where they account for only 1.9% of annual urban precipitation but a disproportionate 18.3% of its long-term increase. This spatially coupled land–atmosphere mechanism strengthens in cities with more surrounding vegetation, wind-aligned greening, and lower background humidity — arguing that peri-urban ecosystems belong inside urban climate-resilience planning.

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the binary urban/non-urban framing. Most urban-climate work treats the city as a sealed box; this paper formalizes the peri-urban belt as an active hydroclimatic actor and quantifies its remote effect on rainfall inside the city.
  • Small mean, big trend. A contribution of only 1.9% of annual precipitation but 18.3% of its long-term increase shows that overlooked, slow-moving land processes can dominate the direction of change even when they look negligible in the average.
  • Identifies who is most exposed. Coupling intensifies under wind-aligned greening and low background humidity, giving planners a screening rule for which cities' water budgets are most sensitive to land-use decisions beyond their administrative borders.
  • Reframes green infrastructure as cross-boundary. Greening or clearing land around a city — not just inside it — changes the city's precipitation, implying that climate-resilience governance must extend past municipal lines.

Core Methods

  • City–peri-urban pairing: select 1,029 cities and delineate each one's surrounding peri-urban ring from GUB boundaries (Extended Data Fig. 2a).
  • Statistical coupling: a linear panel regression confirms the association between peri-urban LAI change (LAIperi) and urban precipitation change (Pur) — observed β = 0.21 (n = 22,638 site-years), with a 1,000-run placebo permutation test (empirical p = 0.0010) ruling out random coincidence.
  • Process-based ET attribution: the PT-JPLim model runs a real-vegetation vs. fixed-vegetation scenario pair to isolate the vegetation-change-induced ET signal (ΔETperi; Extended Data Fig. 3).
  • Physical moisture routing: each peri-urban source grid's ΔET is multiplied by the UTrack moisture-transport fraction U to estimate its contribution to urban precipitation (ΔPur = ΔETperi × U; Extended Data Fig. 4), with overlap handling for adjacent cities (Fig. ED5).
  • Driver analysis: identify city-specific modulators of the coupling (surrounding vegetation abundance, wind-aligned greening, background humidity) via the relative precipitation ratios RPR and RPR-T, with significance tested across city groups (Extended Data Tables 1–2).

① Make the moisture-transport step a physics-informed graph operator

Extending this paperThe paper computes urban precipitation as ΔETperi × U, where U comes from a precomputed, climatological UTrack table applied grid-by-grid and city-by-city. This bakes in a static, lagged moisture climatology and cannot adapt to changing circulation. A direct extension: cast each city + peri-urban ring as a directed source→sink graph and learn a physics-informed graph operator whose edge weights respect mass conservation but are conditioned on time-varying wind and humidity — turning the fixed transport matrix into a trainable, transferable atmospheric router that generalizes across the 1,029 cities.

Added value to my researchThis is exactly my physics-informed graph-learning agenda: a conservation-constrained GNN over a spatial source–sink graph is the same machinery I want for routing demand to facilities (2SFCA flows) or pollutants/people across a region — peri-urban→urban moisture is a clean physical testbed for the operator.

② Replace the panel regression with explainable ML to map the coupling drivers

Extending this paperThe paper establishes the LAIperi–Pur link with a single linear panel coefficient (β ≈ 0.21) and then stratifies cities by a few hand-picked factors (vegetation abundance, wind alignment, humidity) to explain spatial variability. Extending it: fit a spatially explicit explainable-ML ensemble (e.g., gradient boosting + SHAP, or MGWR) over all candidate drivers — topography, aridity, sectoral GDP, land cover — to recover nonlinear, locally varying sensitivities and rank which conditions most amplify the coupling, beyond the linear average.

Added value to my researchThis maps onto my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction and MGWR work directly: the same SHAP-over-spatial-features pipeline I use for accessibility/health outcomes transfers to attributing a land–atmosphere coupling, and the wind-alignment factor is a nice non-stationarity case for GWR bandwidth selection.

Extending this paperThe analysis runs at the resolution of coarse global products (MSWEP, ERA5-Land, GLOBMAP LAI), which smears exactly the fine peri-urban gradient the paper cares about.
