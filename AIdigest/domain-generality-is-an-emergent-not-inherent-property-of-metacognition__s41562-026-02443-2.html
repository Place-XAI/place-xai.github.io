Domain generality is an emergent, not inherent, property of metacognition

Carolyn Baer · Simona Ghetti · Darko Odic Published 2026-05-05 DOI 10.1038/s41562-026-02443-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Confidence in memory versus perception is distinct yet commensurable. Across adults (n = 236) and children aged 4–7 (n = 263), metacognitive sensitivity and efficiency do not correlate across domains (different internal signals), but bias does correlate (a shared reporting step). Crucially, children from age six, like adults, can compare confidence across memory and perception without any accuracy cost — evidence of a shared internal scale — whereas 4–5-year-olds cannot. Domain-general confidence is therefore an emergent developmental achievement, not an inherent property of metacognition.

★

Why It Matters

  • Decomposes "confidence" into separable steps. By splitting metacognition into signal selection (sensitivity/efficiency), internal scaling (commensurability), and externalization (bias), the paper shows the same word hides processes that are domain-specific in some parts and domain-general in others.
  • Reconciles a contradictory literature. Past studies disagreed on whether confidence is one general faculty or many; the answer is "both, but at different stages" — explaining why correlations appear for bias yet vanish for sensitivity.
  • Pins a developmental transition to age six. The capacity to translate domain-specific certainty into a common abstract metric emerges in early childhood, not at birth — a concrete, testable milestone.
  • Practical caution for education. Because metacognitive sensitivity does not transfer across domains, training "good judgement" in one subject should not be assumed to generalize, and a child's metacognition in one task should not be read as a global trait.

Core Methods

  • Three-step confidence framework: treat each confidence judgement as signal selection → internal scaling → externalization, then probe which step is shared across domains.
  • Correlational (individual-differences) approach (Exp. 1): estimate metacognitive sensitivity (meta-d′), efficiency (MRatio) and bias per person per domain, then correlate across memory vs perception (Spearman/Pearson with Bayes factors).
  • Commensurability / forced-choice approach (Exp. 2): participants answer two questions, then pick which answer they are more confident in; Within-Domain vs Across-Domain trials test whether cross-domain comparison incurs an accuracy cost.
  • Statistical inference: repeated-measures ANOVAs (Confidence Choice × Comparison Type × age) plus matched Bayesian RM-ANOVAs in JASP, all two-tailed and preregistered, with robustness tests under relaxed exclusions.
  • Developmental contrast: compare younger (4–5) vs older (6–7) children and adults to locate the emergence of a shared internal confidence scale.

① Decompose "model confidence" the way the paper decomposes human confidence

Extending this paperThe paper's central move is to split confidence into signal sensitivity, an internal scaling step, and a reporting bias, then show that only some steps are domain-general. A direct extension: apply the very same meta-d′/MRatio/bias decomposition to machine confidence, asking whether a model's calibration error is a sensitivity failure (wrong signal) or a bias failure (right signal, miscalibrated report) — and whether bias, but not sensitivity, transfers across task domains, exactly as in humans.

Added value to my researchFor my explainable spatial-prediction models this gives a principled diagnostic: separate where the model's uncertainty signal is genuinely informative from systematic over/under-confidence, so calibration fixes target the right step rather than blanket temperature scaling.

Cross-ref (corpus): Learning to be uncertain before learning from data — 10.1038/s42256-026-01205-z

② Test whether confidence "domain generality" emerges across spatial domains

Extending this paperThe paper compares memory vs perception. The same commensurability paradigm could be extended to spatially grounded decisions — e.g., judging "is a clinic within reach?" vs "is this street safe?" — to ask whether people hold a common confidence scale across place-based judgements and at what age it consolidates. This would port the paper's emergence claim into environmental cognition.

Added value to my researchSubjective confidence in accessibility is a hidden variable in my health-geography work: how sure residents are that care is reachable shapes whether they seek it. A commensurable confidence metric lets me couple objective 2SFCA scores with felt access in one model.

Cross-ref (corpus): Global urbanicity is associated with brain and behaviour in young people — 10.1038/s41562-021-01204-7

③ From individual perceivers to demographically structured confidence

Extending this paperThe paper finds bias correlates across domains but reports a fairly homogeneous Vancouver sample. Extending it: collect the same sensitivity/efficiency/bias indices across demographically diverse populations to test whether the distinct-yet-commensurable structure is itself universal or culturally modulated — turning an individual-differences result into a population-level one.

Added value to my researchThis dovetails with my interest in demographic heterogeneity of perception: pairing measured perceptual confidence with streetscape ratings would let me separate "this place looks unsafe" from "I am unsure it looks unsafe," sharpening explainable models of perceived environment.

Cross-ref (corpus): Global urban visual perception varies across demographics and personalities — 10.1038/s44284-025-00330-x

④ Confidence calibration as a lever for digital-health and the divide

Extending this paperThe paper notes metacognitive bias is stable within a person across domains and tied to self-efficacy. Extending it into applied settings: test whether interventions that recalibrate confidence in one domain (e.g., a digital task) shift the shared bias parameter — using the paper's metrics as the outcome measure for an experimental manipulation rather than only an observational correlate.

Added value to my researchIn telehealth/digital-divide work, mis-calibrated confidence (over- or under-confidence in using digital services) is a plausible mediator of uptake. The meta-d′/bias toolkit gives me a quantitative way to measure that mediator alongside spatial access.

A natural extension is a reusable, packaged pipeline (containerized meta-d′/MRatio/bias estimation with built-in robustness checks) so others can re-run the distinct-yet-commensurable test on new domains and populations out of the box.
