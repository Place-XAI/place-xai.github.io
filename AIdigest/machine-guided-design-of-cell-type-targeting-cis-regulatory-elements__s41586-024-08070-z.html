Machine-guided design of cell-type-targeting cis-regulatory elements

Gosai, Sager J. · Castro, Rodrigo I. · Fuentes, Natalia · Butts, John C. · Mouri, Kousuke · Alasoadura, Michael · Kales, Susan · Nguyen, Thanh Thanh L. · Noche, Ramil R. · Rao, Arya S. · Joy, Mary T. · Sabeti, Pardis C. · Reilly, Steven K. · Tewhey, Ryan Published 2024-10-23 DOI 10.1038/s41586-024-08070-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A machine-learning platform called CODA, powered by a deep convolutional model (Malinois) trained on over 776,000 MPRA-measured sequences, can design synthetic cis-regulatory elements (CREs) that surpass all naturally occurring genomic sequences in driving cell-type-specific gene expression across three human cell lines. Synthetic CREs achieve specificity through a unique combination of on-target activating and off-target repressing transcription-factor motifs rarely co-deployed in the human genome. These synthetic sequences retain tissue-level specificity in vivo in zebrafish and mouse, establishing a generalizable framework for writing programmable regulatory code for gene therapy and biotechnology applications.

★

Why It Matters

  • Unlocks programmable gene-therapy delivery. The lack of robust cell-type-targeted regulatory elements is a bottleneck for gene therapies. CODA provides a principled route to synthetic, tissue-specific CREs without relying on elements that happened to evolve naturally.
  • Demonstrates that evolution does not find the optimum. Synthetic sequences consistently out-perform natural sequences (94.1% specificity rate vs. 40.6% for DHS-natural), showing that the regulatory sequence space contains far better CREs than exist in the human genome.
  • Closes the model-to-experiment loop at scale. By combining an accurate predictive model, three optimization algorithms, and massively parallel reporter assays (77,157 sequences tested), the study validates machine-generated biology at a scale previously impractical.
  • Reveals interpretable regulatory grammar. NMF decomposition into 12 functional programs and motif contribution scores make the model's decisions transparent — the study writes and reads regulatory code simultaneously.
  • Cross-species in vivo validation. Liver- and neuron-specific CREs designed from human cell-line models retain specificity in zebrafish and mice, confirming that in silico optimization generalizes to complex organisms.

Core Methods

  • Malinois (deep CNN): a convolutional neural network adapted from the Basset architecture, trained on 798,064 MPRA-measured sequences to predict cell-type-specific CRE activity from 200 bp DNA sequences alone. Bayesian hyperparameter optimization on Google Cloud Vertex AI (NVIDIA A100 GPU). Pearson r = 0.88–0.89 on held-out chromosomes.
  • CODA optimization platform: iterative loop of (predict → score via MinGap objective → update sequence) using three classes of algorithm — gradient-based (Fast SeqProp), probabilistic (Simulated Annealing), and evolutionary (AdaLead). Motif-penalization variant diversifies TF-motif usage.
  • Massively Parallel Reporter Assay (MPRA): 200 bp oligos synthesized by Twist Bioscience, cloned upstream of a minimal promoter driving GFP with unique 20 bp barcodes; transcriptional output quantified by Illumina sequencing (NovaSeq). 77,157 sequences tested across three cell types in triplicate.
  • Sequence interpretation — Sampled Integrated Gradients (SIG): nucleotide-resolution contribution scores computed via a modified Integrated Gradients approach using a uniform random background, providing hypothetical contribution scores for TF-MoDISco motif discovery.
  • Motif discovery and NMF decomposition: TF-MoDISco Lite identifies 66 motif patterns → 36 non-redundant core motifs (7–18 bp). Non-negative matrix factorization (k = 12) decomposes sequences into functional programs describing co-occurring TF vocabularies.
  • In silico tissue-specificity prediction: Enformer applied to synthetic CREs inserted into the mouse H11 locus to predict epigenetic signatures across 10 tissues, guiding in vivo candidate selection.
  • In vivo validation: transient and stable transgenic reporter assays in zebrafish embryos (Tol2-based GFP reporters) and mouse H11 safe-harbour locus (lacZ/GFP reporters via zygote microinjection); immunofluorescence and confocal imaging for cell-type colocalization.
  • RNA-seq + saturation mutagenesis MPRA: DESeq2-based differential expression (lacZ in mouse tissues); 600-variant saturation mutagenesis library to empirically validate Malinois contribution scores for the synN1 neuronal CRE.

① Replace CODA's fixed-objective optimization with a learned generative model

Extending this paperCODA iterates three optimization heuristics (Fast SeqProp, Simulated Annealing, AdaLead) that search sequence space one CRE at a time without learning from past designs. A direct extension of this paper would replace the inner loop with a conditional generative model — such as a variational autoencoder or a genomic language model fine-tuned on MPRA data — that directly samples high-specificity sequences in a single forward pass. The corpus paper on semantic de-novo gene design from a genomic language model (Semantic design of functional de novo genes, Nature 2025) demonstrates exactly this paradigm: a generative model conditioned on functional properties outputs novel, functional DNA sequences orders of magnitude faster than iterative search. Applied to CODA, this would make multi-objective CRE design (e.g., simultaneous specificity + minimal length + motif diversity) tractable without the combinatorial explosion of exhaustive search.

Added value to my researchThe same conditional-generation principle is directly relevant to my work on fine-tuning geospatial foundation models: just as a genomic language model is conditioned on cell-type activity labels to generate sequences, a geospatial foundation model can be conditioned on accessibility or health-outcome targets to generate synthetic spatial patterns for data augmentation or scenario planning — for instance, generating hypothetical urban configurations that maximize healthcare accessibility.

Cross-ref (corpus): Semantic design of functional de novo genes from a genomic language model — 10.1038/s41586-025-09749-7

② Transfer Malinois' explainability framework (SIG + NMF) to spatiotemporal feature attribution

Extending this paperMalinois' interpretability stack — Sampled Integrated Gradients for position-level attribution, TF-MoDISco for motif discovery, and NMF for program decomposition — provides a complete, multi-resolution explanation of why a sequence drives activity. This paper applies it to a 1D biological sequence; extending it to predict regulatory activity across a wider variety of cell types (as in "Multiomics and deep learning dissect regulatory syntax in human development," Nature 2026) would require the same framework to handle multi-scale, multi-omics inputs while retaining interpretability. A specific extension of this paper: apply the NMF-program decomposition to the extended Malinois trained on primary tissue chromatin states (not just transformed cell lines) to discover whether the 12 programs are stable or cell-context dependent.

Added value to my researchSIG + NMF is a transferable explainability recipe for my explainable ML for spatial prediction work. In MGWR/GWR models, regression coefficients vary spatially but provide no feature-interaction story. Replacing the linear spatial decomposition with NMF over SHAP/integrated-gradient attribution maps would yield spatially coherent "programs" — groups of co-varying features that jointly explain health outcomes — analogous to the TF programs discovered here.

Cross-ref (corpus): Multiomics and deep learning dissect regulatory syntax in human development — 10.1038/s41586-026-10326-9

③ Upgrade Malinois from cell-line MPRA to primary tissue epigenomics via a foundation model

Extending this paperMalinois is trained on episomal reporter data from three transformed cell lines — a controlled but artificial context. The paper itself acknowledges that translating from in vitro to in vivo requires careful bridging (it uses Enformer as an ad hoc bridge). A principled extension: replace the Enformer bridge with a foundation model of transcription across human cell types (as demonstrated by the corpus paper "A foundation model of transcription across human cell types," Nature 2025) fine-tuned on primary-tissue MPRA or STARR-seq data, then use CODA to design CREs optimized against this richer, primary-tissue-aware objective. This would close the in-vitro/in-vivo gap that required laborious zebrafish and mouse validation in the original study.

Added value to my researchThe concept of a pre-trained foundation model fine-tuned on domain-specific data maps directly onto my agenda of fine-tuning geospatial foundation models: a geospatial FM pre-trained on global satellite imagery and census data, fine-tuned on local health-facility utilization data, could predict healthcare demand with the same efficiency that the genomic FM predicts tissue-specific regulatory activity — moving from generic representation to task-specific, context-aware prediction.

Cross-ref (corpus): A foundation model of transcription across human cell types — 10.1038/s41586-024-08391-z

④ Benchmark CODA's predictive accuracy against an uncertainty-aware model for higher-confidence CRE nomination

Extending this paperMalinois provides point predictions with no uncertainty estimates; the paper relies on empirical MPRA to catch false positives among 77,000 candidates. An extension of this paper would integrate uncertainty quantification — Bayesian dropout, deep ensembles, or knowledge distillation into a calibrated student network — into Malinois so that only sequences with high predicted specificity and low epistemic uncertainty are nominated, reducing the experimental validation burden. The corpus paper "Evaluating deep learning for predicting epigenomic profiles" (Nature Machine Intelligence 2022) rigorously benchmarks the failure modes of genomic deep-learning models, providing a roadmap for where uncertainty is highest and most consequential.

Added value to my researchUncertainty-aware prediction is directly relevant to my spatial accessibility modeling: 2SFCA/R2SFCA models propagate distance-decay uncertainty into accessibility estimates silently. Integrating ensemble uncertainty quantification into spatial ML models would allow health-policy makers to distinguish confidently accessible areas from ones where the prediction is uncertain — a critical distinction for resource-allocation decisions.

Cross-ref (corpus): Evaluating deep learning for predicting epigenomic profiles — 10.1038/s42256-022-00570-9

⑤ Extend CODA's regulatory grammar discovery to multi-scale motif syntax using a graph neural network

Extending this paperCODA's NMF decomposes CREs into co-occurring TF programs but treats motifs as a bag-of-words — spatial arrangement within the 200 bp sequence (syntax) is inferred indirectly from contribution scores rather than explicitly modelled. A direct extension: replace the NMF program layer with a graph neural network where nodes are motif instances, edges encode pairwise distances and orientations, and graph-level embeddings predict specificity. This would let CODA learn higher-order syntactic rules (e.g., "ETS and CREB heterodimers must be within 15 bp") rather than co-occurrence frequencies, and would operate at a resolution the current framework cannot reach. The corpus paper on end-to-end genomic sequence analysis with deep learning (Nature Computational Science 2023) provides a software backbone for such graph-augmented sequence models.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my physics-informed graph learning agenda translated to a new domain: the GNN syntax model encodes a constraint (motif proximity rule) as a structural prior — analogous to encoding geographic contiguity or transportation-network topology as physics constraints in a spatial GNN. The graph-regularized sequence model here and the physics-informed spatial model for urban accessibility both exploit the principle that structured interactions, not just feature co-occurrence, drive outcomes.
