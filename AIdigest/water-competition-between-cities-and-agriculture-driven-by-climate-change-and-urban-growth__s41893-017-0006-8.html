Water competition between cities and agriculture driven by climate change and urban growth

Martina Flörke · Christof Schneider · Robert I. McDonald Published 2018-01-08 DOI 10.1038/s41893-017-0006-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

By 2050, more than 27% of the world's 482 largest cities — home to 233 million people — will face surface-water deficits driven by the twin forces of climate change and urban population growth. An additional 19% of cities dependent on inter-basin water transfers will experience acute conflicts with agriculture. Crucially, a 10% improvement in irrigation efficiency across agricultural watersheds could relieve the water deficit for 78% of those vulnerable cities, making agricultural water-use efficiency a globally viable urban climate-adaptation strategy.

★

Why It Matters

  • First global accounting of urban-agricultural water conflict. Previous global studies either ignored inter-basin water transfers or treated urban and agricultural demand in isolation; this study explicitly models the competition between sectors across 592 subbasins worldwide.
  • Quantifies cascading impacts beyond city borders. For every three urban residents protected by cross-basin water transfer, one person in a remote source watershed is put at risk — the total affected population could reach 1.46 billion under an environmental-flow scenario.
  • Translates a threat into a solution. The finding that agricultural efficiency gains of just 10% could rescue 236 million urban residents from projected water deficit provides a concrete, costed policy lever that urban and agricultural planners can act on jointly.
  • Integrates climate uncertainty rigorously. Using an ensemble of five General Circulation Models under RCP6.0 and SSP2 means the results carry explicit uncertainty bounds rather than point estimates, making them suitable for risk-informed governance.
  • Highlights groundwater over-exploitation. The Urban Groundwater Footprint (UGF) will more than double in 116 cities by 2050, signalling unsustainable reliance on aquifer storage that is currently invisible in most urban risk assessments.

Core Methods

  • Integrated global water modelling (WaterGAP3): coupled global hydrology model (river discharge, soil water balance, lake and reservoir routing) with sector-specific water-use models (domestic, manufacturing, thermoelectric, irrigation, livestock) at 5×5 arcmin resolution over 143,653 subbasins.
  • Urban surface-water deficit indicator: monthly water balance per subbasin comparing available surface water against urban demand; computed under two allocation scenarios — urban water supply at first priority (highest) vs. last priority (after industry and agriculture), plus an additional environmental flow requirement (EFR) constraint protecting 80% of natural daily flows.
  • City Water Map integration: embedding inter-basin transfer infrastructure (withdrawal-point locations and volumes) into the global model to avoid overestimating urban water stress in cities that import water from distant subbasins.
  • Urban Groundwater Footprint (UGF): dimensionless ratio of annual urban groundwater abstraction to estimated recharge for 138 regional aquifers; UGF ≥ 1 flags unsustainable abstraction; trend attributed to climate change vs. socioeconomic drivers.
  • Multi-GCM ensemble uncertainty: five bias-corrected GCMs under RCP6.0 propagate climate uncertainty through all results; median and range reported for all key metrics.
  • Agricultural efficiency scenario analysis: evaluated whether a 10% or 30% improvement in irrigation project efficiency (range 0.3–0.8) in subbasins with surface-water withdrawal points could eliminate urban deficits under the last-priority assumption.
  • Cascading impact quantification: population in source watersheds (beyond the city) affected by urban water transfers quantified to capture hidden, remote water-security impacts.

① Disaggregate the city-level deficit to neighbourhood equity — who bears the water shortage burden?

Extending this paperThe paper projects surface-water deficits at the city and subbasin scale but treats each city as a single unit for population exposure. A direct extension: combine the paper's subbasin-level deficit volumes with intra-city gridded population data and socioeconomic vulnerability layers to compute which neighbourhoods within each at-risk city will experience water rationing first. High-income areas may bypass shortages via private storage; low-income areas bear disproportionate risk. This extension would quantify the within-city inequality in water-security outcomes that the paper's aggregate numbers obscure — directly relevant to the urban equity debate now prominent in Nature Sustainability.

Added value to my researchThis translates my 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility toolkit from healthcare facilities to water-supply points: catchment areas around water withdrawal infrastructure, weighted by supply capacity and demand, can map intra-city water accessibility gaps in the same framework I use for healthcare access. The result is a neighbourhood-level water-accessibility index comparable across the 482 cities in this paper's dataset — a novel health-geography output with strong policy visibility (SDG 6.1).

③ Learn the urban–agricultural water allocation policy with a graph neural network

Extending this paperThe paper tests two extreme allocation rules (first priority and last priority) as fixed heuristics. Real water governance operates somewhere between these poles and is shaped by institutional, economic, and geographic factors that vary by basin. Extending the paper: represent the river network as a directed graph where nodes are subbasins and edge weights encode upstream–downstream flow dependencies, then train a graph neural network (GNN) to learn the optimal allocation split between urban and agricultural demand that minimizes total social cost, using the paper's demand projections as node features. The trained GNN would generalize to future scenario combinations not explicitly simulated, eliminating the need to re-run WaterGAP3 for every policy test.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my physics-informed graph learning agenda: the river network's mass-balance constraints (flow conservation at each node) act as the physical prior that regularizes the GNN, preventing implausible allocation solutions. The framework would produce an explainable, deployable solver for water-allocation decisions — connecting hydrological physics, GeoAI, and urban governance in one model.

④ Quantify how urban sprawl amplifies water demand and transfer distances

Extending this paperThe paper models future urban water demand via SSP2 population and GDP growth but holds the spatial form of cities constant. Urban sprawl — low-density horizontal expansion — simultaneously increases per-capita water demand (more lawn irrigation, longer pipe networks, higher leakage) and forces cities to extend withdrawal infrastructure ever farther from the urban core, enlarging the transfer distances and the number of people in remote source basins who are affected. Extending the paper: couple its deficit framework with a land-use change model that projects sprawl under SSP2, re-running the City Water Map routing for expanded footprints, and quantifying how compact vs. sprawling growth trajectories widen or narrow the surface-water deficit gap.

Added value to my researchMy urban-analytics work on 15-minute neighbourhoods and job-housing networks already frames urban form as a policy lever. Connecting spatial compactness indicators (density, land-use mix, network connectivity) to water-demand coefficients would let me show that smart-growth policies simultaneously improve pedestrian accessibility and water security — a multi-benefit urban-form argument with strong policy traction for sustainable city planning.

⑤ Build an explainable ML model to predict which cities will cross the deficit threshold first

Extending this paperThe paper identifies 130+ cities at risk of surface-water deficit by 2050 but does not explain which city-level and watershed-level predictors (population growth rate, aridity index, groundwater dependence, inter-basin transfer length, agricultural irrigation intensity) most strongly determine whether a city will cross the deficit threshold. Extending the paper: treat each city as an observation with predictors extracted from the paper's own data and ancillary GIS layers, train a gradient-boosted tree or random-forest ensemble to predict deficit class, and apply SHAP values to rank feature importance globally and by world region. The result would give planners a transparent, fast-running screening tool — no WaterGAP3 run required — for identifying cities at imminent risk that are not yet in the spotlight.
