Positive association between Internet use and mental health among adults aged ≥50 years in 23 countries

Yan Luo · Paul Siu Fai Yip · Qingpeng Zhang Published 2024-11-18 DOI 10.1038/s41562-024-02048-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Among 87,559 adults aged ≥50 years across 23 countries, Internet use was robustly associated with fewer depressive symptoms (pooled AME −0.09), higher life satisfaction (AME +0.07), and better self-reported health (AME +0.15), even after adjusting for 14 sociodemographic and health confounders. The protective association held across three genetic risk strata in the USA and England, and strengthened with higher frequency and longer cumulative duration of Internet use. Country-level variations suggest that precision digital-inclusion policies — tailored to demographic, socio-economic, and genetic profiles — can deliver scalable population-level mental health gains in both high- and middle-income settings.

Why It Matters

  • First cross-national, harmonized individual-participant data (IPD) study on this question. All prior evidence came from single-country studies; this two-stage IPD meta-analysis across 23 countries establishes the association as cross-nationally consistent, not a regional artifact.
  • Covers a broad spectrum of mental health, not just depression. By including depressive symptoms (illness indicator), life satisfaction (positive well-being), and self-reported health as three distinct outcomes, the study aligns with the WHO's staging model and shows Internet use benefits all three domains.
  • Integrates genetic risk (polygenic scores) — a rare feature in population Internet research. Positive associations were observed even among participants with high genetic vulnerability to depression in England and the USA, suggesting Internet use can partially offset heritable risk.
  • Dose-response and longitudinal dose effects documented. Both greater frequency and longer cumulative periods of Internet use correlate with better mental health, supporting a dose-response rather than a simple binary on/off association.
  • Directly actionable for policy in low- and middle-income settings. Internet prevalence ranged from 2.2% (China, 2011) to 84.8% (Denmark), signaling where scalable digital-health investments would have the highest marginal mental health return.

Core Methods

  • Two-stage IPD meta-analysis: Stage 1 — linear mixed models (LMMs) with person-specific random intercepts and years-since-baseline as timescale, estimated separately within each country. Stage 2 — random-effects meta-analysis with REML estimator to pool average marginal effects (AMEs) across countries.
  • Average marginal effects (AMEs): the primary effect metric, expressing the mean change in standardized outcome when switching from non-Internet use to Internet use, calculated via the marginaleffects R package.
  • Multiple imputation: missing data handled via multiple imputation by chained equations (mice), pooled over 10 imputed datasets using Rubin's rules.
  • Parametric g-formula: used to estimate the causal effect of sustained Internet use (dynamic treatment) on end-of-follow-up mental health, adjusting for time-varying confounders via the gfoRmula package.
  • Subgroup meta-analyses: IPD meta-analysis stratified by age, gender, marital status, social contact, education, household wealth, labour-force status, health behaviours, ADL disability, and polygenic risk score categories (low/intermediate/high).
  • Heterogeneity assessment: τ², I², H² statistics and Cochran's Q-test; meta-regression on seven country-level indicators (GDP per capita, Gini index, world happiness index, Hofstede's index, Internet use rate, digital skill levels, healthy life expectancy).
  • Sensitivity analyses (×5): complete cases, excluding England and Mexico, inverse-probability-weighting for attrition, exclusion of baseline depressed participants, and bidirectionality test with generalized estimating equations.

③ Internet use as a substitute for physical service accessibility: a joint 2SFCA model

Extending this paperThe paper finds that Internet use confers stronger mental health benefits among older adults who have less frequent social contact — suggesting that digital connectivity compensates for in-person service and social inaccessibility. Extending it: formally test the substitution hypothesis by pairing the cohort data with a spatial accessibility index (2SFCA-derived) for mental health services and primary care in each participant's residential area, then model the interaction term (Internet use × facility-access score) in the LMM. Negative interaction coefficients would confirm that digital access most benefits those with the lowest physical access — directly linking the paper's social-mechanism explanation to geography.

④ Cross-country heterogeneity as a geo-policy learning problem: meta-regression at the city level

Extending this paperThe paper's meta-regression (Fig. S6) uses seven country-level aggregates to explain between-country variation in AMEs. This is an important but coarse analysis: national Gini, GDP per capita, and happiness index miss the intra-country urban–rural and city-tier variation that may drive most of the heterogeneity. A fine-grained extension: replace national indicators with city-level analogs (city-level Gini, broadband penetration, elderly-care infrastructure, digital-skill training centre density) compiled from open urban data, and re-run the meta-regression at the city tier using the SHARE sub-national geographic identifiers. This would turn the paper's exploratory country-level finding into a spatially resolved policy model.

⑤ Encode the g-formula's causal logic into a physics-informed temporal graph network for policy simulation

Extending this paperThe parametric g-formula in Fig. 5 estimates what would happen if the population followed a sustained Internet-use treatment strategy — a causal counterfactual. However, it assumes parametric models for every time-varying confounder, which may miss nonlinear confounder dynamics. Extending the paper: re-implement the g-formula as a physics-informed temporal graph network where nodes are individuals, edges encode social and geographic proximity (from the cohort's social-network and residential data), and the graph dynamics are constrained by the paper's established dose-response effect sizes as soft physics constraints. This would allow the simulation to propagate Internet-use interventions through social networks — capturing contagion of adoption — rather than treating individuals as independent.
