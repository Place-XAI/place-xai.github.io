Impacts of nature-inclusive urban development on well-being and fairness perceptions

Shuo Gao · Wenjing Zhang · Sophus O. S. E. zu Ermgassen · Joseph Bull · Thomas Pienkowski · Xiang Ao · Renlu Qiao · E. J. Milner-Gulland Published 2026-04-07 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00425-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Even an ecologically successful nature-inclusive urban development (NIUD) can produce uneven social outcomes. In Qunli New Town (Harbin, China), former agriculturalists reported a sharp happiness decline (OR = 0.15, P < 0.001), while happiness gains were tied to perceived social (OR = 1.30) and aesthetic (OR = 1.53) value of green space rather than housing or infrastructure. Crucially, fairness perceptions diverged by settlement wave: original residents saw the transformation as economically fairer (OR = 1.93) but ecologically less fair (OR = 0.54) than newcomers — driven by their rural reference points and the loss of customary access to landscapes turned into exclusive "club goods."

★

Why It Matters

  • Documents green gentrification in the global south. Most gentrification evidence comes from market-driven processes in North America/Europe; this is rare empirical evidence of state-led nature-positive urbanization producing displacement-like effects in China.
  • Decouples ecological success from social equity. Qunli achieved measurable biodiversity recovery (bird species rose to 49; Songhua River fish species rebounded from <40 to >60), yet still generated unfair outcomes — a direct caution for Global Biodiversity Framework Target 12.
  • Reveals reference dependence in fairness judgments. The same intervention is read as fair or unfair depending on whether residents compare to their rural past or an urban baseline — consistent with prospect theory and largely invisible to standard income/wealth metrics.
  • Concrete policy lever. Calls for social impact assessments to differentiate settlement waves and to keep green space as a public good rather than a fenced "club good," with social safeguards resourced as rigorously as ecological mitigation.

Core Methods

  • Sequential mixed-methods design: exploratory qualitative phase (interviews) generates well-being/fairness themes that directly inform the quantitative survey instrument.
  • Thematic analysis: deductive coding guided by a multi-dimensional well-being framework (material / relational / subjective), with iterative co-author review for thematic coherence.
  • Retrospective comparative survey: ordinal (−3 to +3) agreement scales on perceived change in well-being elements and happiness, with reverse-coded items; piloted with 42 residents.
  • Ordinal logistic regression: three models via clm() in the R ordinal package (R 4.3.1) — happiness among original residents (N = 289), economic fairness (N = 789), ecological fairness (N = 959); results reported as odds ratios with 95% CIs.
  • Theoretical lenses: gentrification theory (economic, exclusionary, psychological displacement) and prospect-theory reference dependence to interpret divergent fairness perceptions.

① Geolocate the "club good" divide with spatial accessibility metrics

Extending this paperThe paper's striking finding — that new high-quality green space becomes a fenced "club good" for newcomers while resettlement sites get lower-quality plots — is documented qualitatively but never mapped. A direct extension: digitize Qunli's green spaces and resettlement vs. newcomer developments, then compute 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility to public vs. restricted green space per block, testing whether measured access inequality matches perceived ecological unfairness.

Added value to my researchThis gives my accessibility work a perception-validated target: pairing R2SFCA scores against survey-based fairness lets me calibrate distance-decay and "supply quality" weights against lived experience rather than assumed parameters — directly transferable to my public-facility and green-space access models.

② Scale the single case into a multi-city green-gentrification comparison

Extending this paperQunli is one state-led case; the paper itself calls its mechanism distinct from market-driven global-north gentrification. Extending it: build a comparative panel of nature-positive developments across Chinese and Western cities, harmonizing fairness/well-being indicators so the reference-dependence mechanism can be tested for transferability — exactly the contrast the discussion gestures at.

Added value to my researchA multi-city design is where my multi-source data fusion (remote sensing greenness, POIs, mobile-phone footfall, social media sentiment) earns its keep — letting me proxy displacement and green-space use at scale where surveys cannot reach, an open-science, FAIR comparison framework.

③ Model context-dependence of well-being with explainable ML

Extending this paperThe paper shows happiness depends on which changes residents perceive (aesthetic/social value matter; housing does not) and on group identity (former agriculturalist). Its ordinal logit assumes additive linear effects. Extending it: fit an explainable ML ensemble (e.g., gradient boosting + SHAP) to surface non-linear interactions and reference-point thresholds the GLM cannot, while keeping interpretability for policy.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction line: SHAP-style attribution of subjective well-being to socio-ecological features is the same machinery I use for health/accessibility prediction, here extended to perception outcomes and context moderators.

Cross-ref (corpus): Well-being from nature exposure depends on socio-environmental contexts in Paraguay — 10.1038/s44284-024-00058-0

④ Connect ecological displacement to measurable mental-health outcomes

Extending this paperThe paper links loss of customary landscape access to lower subjective well-being but stops at self-reported happiness. Extending it: pair the fairness/well-being survey with objective mental-health and activity signals (wearables, clinic visits, validated stress scales) to test whether perceived ecological unfairness translates into health burden — strengthening the "do no harm" argument.

Added value to my researchThis bridges to my mental-health accessibility and healthy-cities work: green-space access becomes both a service to be measured (2SFCA) and a determinant of mental-health demand, closing the loop between environmental justice and care-seeking.

Cross-ref (corpus): Biodiversity-rich recreational areas near cities as a nature-based mental health solution — 10.1038/s44284-025-00251-9

⑤ Turn the "differentiate settlement waves" call into a procedural-justice planning tool

Extending this paperThe paper's headline recommendation — social impact assessments should distinguish settlement waves and avoid exclusive designs — is a principle, not yet an instrument. Extending it: encode it as an agent-based / equity-aware optimization that allocates green-space investment to minimize cross-wave fairness gaps under a fixed budget, simulating "public good vs. club good" governance choices before they are built.

Added value to my researchThis maps onto my efficiency-vs-equity optimization (e.g., EV-charging siting) and agent-based modeling: a green-space allocator that explicitly trades aggregate access against between-group fairness gives planners the procedural-justice lever the paper argues is missing.
