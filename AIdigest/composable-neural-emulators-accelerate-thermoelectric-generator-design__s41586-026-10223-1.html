Composable neural emulators accelerate thermoelectric generator design

Li, Airan · Wu, Xinzhi · Wang, Longquan · Wu, Gang · Li, Jiankang · Hu, Zhao · Wang, Xinyuan · Mori, Takao Published 2026-04-15 DOI 10.1038/s41586-026-10223-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

TEGNet, a composable neural network emulator, predicts thermoelectric (TE) generator performance with greater than 99% accuracy while requiring only 0.01% of the computational time of commercial finite-element solvers (COMSOL). Its key innovation is composability: material-specific emulators can be flexibly combined to simulate segmented legs and n–p paired generators without retraining, enabling rapid exploration of diverse device architectures. Guided by TEGNet, experimentally fabricated generators achieved conversion efficiencies of 9.3% (MgAgSb/Bi₀.₄Sb₁.₆Te₃ segmented) and 8.7% (Mg₃Bi₁.₄Sb₀.₆–MgAgSb n–p paired), ranking competitively high in the literature.

Why It Matters

  • Orders-of-magnitude speedup with verified accuracy. COMSOL requires ~2,237 s per material simulation; TEGNet delivers the same output in 0.25 s. The full n–p paired design space (which took COMSOL more than 5,800 s) was mapped by TEGNet in just 9 s, with R² ≥ 0.9993 throughout.
  • Composability unlocks architectural diversity. Unlike monolithic surrogate models, TEGNet's unit-level emulators can be assembled at inference time — no retraining needed when new material combinations or three-segment designs are explored, exploiting the constant-current rule of TE circuitry.
  • Closes the gap between materials discovery and device-level realization. Thermoelectric research has focused heavily on maximizing the figure of merit zT; TEGNet demonstrates that device geometry and architecture optimized jointly with materials can push efficiency significantly beyond what zT alone predicts.
  • Open-source and reproducible. TEGNet is publicly available at github.com/airannims/TEGNet, enabling the TE community to train new material-specific emulators following the same workflow and compose them freely.
  • Experimental validation in the loop. The paper goes beyond simulation: fabricated devices confirm the TEGNet-guided designs, with efficiencies independently ranked among the highest reported for the respective material systems.

Core Methods

  • Multilayer perceptron (MLP) surrogate: TEGNet is a fully connected feedforward network with 6 inputs (leg dimensions + boundary conditions), three hidden layers of 128 neurons each (SiLU activation), and 2 outputs (V₀, Q₀). MSE loss; Adam optimizer with CosineAnnealingLR scheduler; 6,000 training epochs.
  • Sobol sequence sampling: inputs a, b, c, Tc, Th sampled via quasi-random Sobol sequences to ensure space-filling coverage within physically valid bounds; current I sampled uniformly up to a material-dependent maximum.
  • Composable emulator assembly: the constant-current rule of series-connected TE legs is exploited as a physical constraint — unit emulators are composed at inference time by iteratively satisfying energy conservation (Q₀_1 = Q₀_2 + I × V) to resolve interface temperatures, enabling segmented and n–p paired simulations without retraining.
  • Design space exploration: TEGNet is used in a forward-prediction sweep over discrete grids of geometric parameters (leg length ratios, area ratios, heights) to extract optimal configurations — replacing the repeated PDE-solve loops of COMSOL with near-instantaneous neural forward passes.
  • Parasitic effects incorporation: electrical contact resistivity ρc and thermal contact resistivity rc are incorporated at inference time (Extended Data Figs. 2–3) without retraining, by modifying boundary condition inputs — demonstrating the generality of the trained emulator to practical device effects.
  • Experimental validation: spark plasma sintering (SPS) for materials fabrication; contact resistance measured with resistance distribution instrument; generator efficiency measured with Mini-PEM system under vacuum, varying Th from 373 to 593 K.

① Replace the forward-sweep grid search with gradient-based optimization through the emulator

Extending this paperTEGNet's current design workflow is a brute-force grid scan over discrete parameter values of leg dimensions and area ratios. Because TEGNet is a differentiable neural network, the gradient of efficiency with respect to geometry can be computed analytically, enabling gradient-based continuous optimization — directly analogous to what has been demonstrated for nanoparticle heterostructures. Extending TEGNet to include gradient flow through the composed emulator would let designers follow the loss landscape rather than discretely sample it, reaching the optimal configuration with far fewer forward passes and enabling simultaneous co-optimization of geometry, material choice, and contact resistivity in a single objective.

Added value to my researchThe differentiable-surrogate paradigm maps cleanly onto my facility-location optimization work: replacing a non-differentiable combinatorial search (EV charging station placement, healthcare facility siting) with a continuous relaxation and gradient descent through a learned accessibility emulator. TEGNet's composability principle — that unit emulators obeying a conservation rule can be chained — is structurally analogous to my network accessibility models where cumulative catchment areas can be decomposed by zone and gradient-optimized jointly.

② Upgrade material-specific emulators to physics-infused graph neural networks

Extending this paperTEGNet uses a standard MLP that treats leg geometry as a flat feature vector, learning thermoelectric physics implicitly through training data. The next architectural step: replace each material-specific MLP with a physics-infused graph neural network (PI-GNN) that encodes the spatial structure of the TE leg as a graph of material nodes and interface edges, embedding known physics — Seebeck coefficient, thermal conductance, electrical resistance — as node/edge attributes. This would allow TEGNet to generalize to materials outside its training distribution, reduce sample requirements, and handle heterogeneous multi-material legs without recomposing from scratch. The corpus paper on physics-infused heterogeneous GNNs for nanomaterials demonstrates exactly this strategy for complex spatially structured materials.

TEGNet's constant-current composability rule is formally equivalent to flow conservation in a network — the same principle I use in graph-based accessibility models where cumulative demand must balance supply. Seeing this principle applied at the device physics scale reinforces the design pattern and provides a materials-science benchmark for testing transferability of physics-constrained GNNs across domains.

③ From single-material emulators to a universal TE property surrogate via transfer learning

Extending this paperTEGNet trains one MLP per material — if a new material arrives, a new training campaign (4 h data generation + ~79 s training) is needed. The paper acknowledges that for "similar" materials a rough estimate can be borrowed, but this is informal. Extending it: train a universal surrogate that conditions on material descriptors (zT curve, Seebeck coefficient, conductivity profiles) as additional inputs, then fine-tune on a small material-specific set. This mirrors the materials-science trend of universal interatomic potentials (M3GNet, MACE) that cover the periodic table from a single pre-trained backbone. With a universal emulator, TEGNet's screening of n–p pair combinations (currently limited by the need to pre-train all candidates) would extend to any material in a descriptors database.

A universal TE surrogate pre-trained on broad material diversity and fine-tuned on local material properties is structurally the same pipeline as a geospatial FM pre-trained on remote sensing and fine-tuned on city-specific accessibility patterns — cross-domain insight for my foundation model work.

④ Embed uncertainty quantification so TEGNet guides experiment with calibrated confidence

Extending this paperTEGNet currently produces point predictions — there is no estimate of predictive uncertainty attached to its V₀ and Q₀ outputs. In practice, when a designer scans thousands of configurations, not all predictions are equally reliable: extrapolation beyond the training temperature range, or geometries near the Sobol boundary, will carry higher uncertainty. Extending TEGNet with a Bayesian or deep ensemble uncertainty head would allow the design loop to be closed: the optimizer selects the next COMSOL simulation at the configuration where uncertainty is highest (Bayesian active learning), progressively building a dataset focused on the hardest regions of the design space. This directly addresses the paper's acknowledged limitation that TEGNet is not yet designed for extrapolation to entirely novel conditions.

Added value to my researchCalibrated uncertainty is a gap in my explainable ML for spatial prediction: my gradient-boosted ensembles and MGWR models produce feature attributions but not spatially varying confidence intervals. Adopting the active-learning loop from TEGNet — query COMSOL where uncertain, query field surveys where my model is uncertain — would make my data-collection campaigns for healthcare accessibility more efficient, directing measurement resources to the spatial zones where predictions are least reliable.

⑤ Generalize the composable-emulator principle to multi-scale urban energy systems

Extending this paperTEGNet's composability exploits one physical invariant: the constant current I shared by all TE legs in series. The same principle generalizes to any system where a conserved quantity couples sub-unit emulators. Extending beyond thermoelectrics: a building-scale energy model could compose room-level thermal emulators (coupled by heat flux conservation), a district heating network could compose pipe-segment emulators (coupled by mass flow), and a city-scale power grid could compose substation emulators (coupled by power balance). This would create a hierarchical, composable AI solver for urban energy infrastructure — precisely the kind of physics-constrained, multi-scale AI surrogate that is missing from current smart-city platforms.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal analysis of urban systems — mobility, public health, accessibility — operates at multiple nested scales (individual, neighborhood, city, region). TEGNet's composability principle is a template for building scale-composable GeoAI models: individual accessibility emulators (2SFCA catchment-zone scale) composed by conservation rules (total demand served = sum of facility supply) into city-scale models, which in turn compose into regional-scale health geography analyses. This is a concrete design pattern for multi-resolution GeoAI that my current models lack.
