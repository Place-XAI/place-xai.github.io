Modeled temperature, mortality impact and external benefits of cool roofs and rooftop photovoltaics in London

Charles H. Simpson · Oscar Brousse · Tim Taylor · James Grellier · Jonathon Taylor · Lora E. Fleming · Mike Davies · Clare Heaviside Published 2024-10-01 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00138-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using a 3D urban climate model (WRF BEP-BEM) of London's record-hot summer 2018, both interventions cool the city, but cool roofs win: they lower population-weighted air temperature by −0.8 °C (vs −0.3 °C for rooftop PV) and could have avoided 249 (32%) of the ~786 heat-attributable deaths (vs 96, 12% for RPV). Monetized via value of statistical life, the avoided-mortality benefit is £615 M for cool roofs and £237 M for RPV — a similar order of magnitude to the value of the electricity that full RPV would generate (9.5–20 TWh). The health externality is therefore a real, quantifiable part of the economic case for both technologies.

Why It Matters

  • Settles a real rooftop trade-off. Roof space is scarce; planners must choose between solar PV and reflective cool roofs. This paper gives a physically consistent, head-to-head comparison of their cooling and health effects under one model.
  • First monetized health externality for these roofs. It is the first study to translate solar/cool-roof-driven temperature reductions into avoided deaths and pounds, putting health benefits into the same ledger as energy revenue.
  • Health benefit rivals energy value. The avoided-mortality value (£237–615 M) is comparable to the consumer value of RPV electricity — meaning health externalities belong in any strategic cost–benefit case.
  • Realistic, validated modeling. RPV heat exchange (top and bottom of panel, temperature-dependent efficiency) is modeled in detail and the baseline is validated against 35 Met Office stations and 402 crowdsourced personal weather stations, addressing criticisms of earlier RPV studies.

Core Methods

  • 3D urban climate modeling: WRF v4.3 with the BEP-BEM building-effect + building-energy parameterization, resolving roof, wall and canyon energy fluxes (rather than a grid-average bulk scheme) so roof-level changes propagate physically into canopy air temperature.
  • Four scenarios: baseline, full rooftop-PV coverage, full cool-roof (high-albedo) coverage, and a nonurban (all-vegetated) contrast.
  • Detailed RPV energy balance: convective + radiative fluxes from top and bottom of panels, temperature-dependent conversion efficiency (19% at 25 °C, declining 0.5%/°C), feeding both heat emission and electricity output.
  • Machine-learning bias adjustment: random-forest regression predicts daily model bias from urban canopy parameters, calibrated to crowdsourced PWS, then applied consistently across scenarios.
  • Health impact assessment: exposure–response functions applied to population-weighted 2-day-lagged temperature → relative risk → attributable fraction → attributable deaths and YLL, differenced against baseline to get avoidable mortality.
  • Economic valuation & Monte Carlo: avoided deaths/YLL monetized via value of statistical life; 100,000-draw Monte Carlo propagates ERF and temperature uncertainty; electricity value bracketed by efficiency and price ranges.

① Surrogate the WRF model so the rooftop trade-off can be optimized, not just compared

Extending this paperThe study runs only four discrete, all-or-nothing scenarios because each WRF BEP-BEM run is expensive; the realistic RPV/cool-roof mix is then approximated by assuming linear scaling. A direct extension: train an explainable ML surrogate of the urban climate model that maps (per-LCZ albedo, RPV fraction, morphology, weather) → canopy temperature field, then search the continuous intervention space for the mix that maximizes avoided mortality plus energy value per pound — turning the paper's manual comparison into a solvable optimization.

Added value to my researchThis is a clean testbed for my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction agenda: SHAP/feature attribution on the surrogate reveals which morphological levers (roof fraction, canyon aspect ratio) actually buy cooling, feeding physics-informed spatial prediction rather than black-box emulation.

② Make the verdict equity-aware, not just population-weighted-average

Extending this paperThe paper reports a population-weighted mean benefit, but heat mortality concentrates in the over-75s and the spatial maps (Fig. 3) show uneven cooling. Extending it: overlay the avoided-death field with deprivation, age and care-home location to ask who gains from each rooftop strategy, and re-optimize the cool-roof/RPV allocation to close the heat-mortality gap rather than maximize the citywide total.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly onto my efficiency-vs-equity optimization (developed for EV charging) and my health-geography work: the rooftop intervention becomes a spatial supply-allocation problem solvable with the same equity-constrained framework I use for healthcare-facility access.

③ Generalize the single-city result with a physics-informed graph operator over many cities

Extending this paperThe authors are explicit that results depend on baseline albedo, built form and climate, so London's −0.8 °C will not transfer directly. Extending it: encode the paper's albedo–temperature physics (≈0.3 °C per 0.1 albedo) as a soft constraint inside a physics-informed graph neural operator trained across many cities' LCZ + morphology graphs, so the cool-roof/RPV cooling response can be predicted for unseen cities without a full WRF run each time.

④ Replace the static exposure–response function with a learned, behavior-aware one

Extending this paperThe mortality estimate assumes a static, outdoor-temperature ERF and ignores indoor temperature, acclimatization and air-conditioning uptake — limitations the authors flag. Extending it: fuse mobile-phone presence, indoor-temperature proxies and adaptive behavior into a spatiotemporal ERF learned from data, so the avoided-mortality field reflects where people actually are and how exposure shifts under adaptation.

Added value to my researchThis leverages my multi-source data fusion (mobile-phone, social media, remote sensing) and spatiotemporal modeling to turn a fixed epidemiological coefficient into a dynamic, place- and time-varying exposure surface — the same machinery I use for accessibility under temporal demand.

⑤ Co-optimize rooftop PV/cool roofs with green space as a cooling-service network

Extending this paperThe paper treats roofs in isolation and excludes rooftop vegetation, yet roof space competes with green roofs, AC units and trees. Extending it: embed cool roofs / RPV / green roofs in a single cooling-service supply network and solve for the portfolio that minimizes heat-related mortality across a city, accounting for water, energy and carbon co-benefits and constraints.
