Compact deep neural network models of the visual cortex

Cowley, Benjamin R. · Stan, Patricia L. · Pillow, Jonathan W. · Smith, Matthew A. Published 2026-02-25 DOI 10.1038/s41586-026-10150-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Large deep neural networks (60 million parameters) are not necessary to predict individual visual cortex neurons: compact models with 5,000× fewer parameters achieve comparable accuracy after knowledge distillation. The compression reveals a shared computational motif — early filters are common across neurons, but each neuron's selectivity arises through a distinct "consolidation" step — providing interpretable, testable circuit hypotheses for areas V1, V4, and IT.

Why It Matters

  • Shatters the "bigger is better" assumption in computational neuroscience. The work shows that individual V4 neurons are predictable with ~12,000 parameters — a finding that reframes the field's reliance on task-driven mega-networks as explanatory models.
  • Compression as a scientific lens, not just an engineering goal. By distilling a black-box 60 M-parameter model, the authors expose an interpretable architecture: shared early filters + individual consolidation. This turns model compression into a mechanistic discovery tool.
  • Generates experimentally testable circuit hypotheses. A dot-detecting model neuron analysis yields a concrete circuit proposal for V4 dot-selective neurons — bridging computational modeling and in-vivo electrophysiology.
  • Demonstrates generality across the visual hierarchy. Strong compression holds for V1 (most compressible), V4, and IT, implying a conserved representational economy across primate visual areas.
  • Fully open data and code. Neural recordings, stimulus images, model weights, and spike-sorting software are publicly released on GitHub (cowleygroup/V4_compact_models), lowering barriers for replication and extension.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Neural recordings
Electrophysiological recordings from macaque visual areas V1, V4, and IT (inferior temporal cortex) using chronically implanted electrode arrays. V4 training data: 44 recording sessions from 3 macaques (M1, M2, M3); test set: 4 held-out sessions yielding 219 neurons. V4 comparison datasets from Bashivan et al. (131 neurons) and Cadena et al. Stimulus images
Natural images presented during recording sessions: 1,000–3,000 images per session for training; 500,000 natural images explored for response-maximizing image identification. Stimuli from standard image databases (Adobe Stock used for validation). All processed responses and stimulus images released on GitHub.
Spike sorting
Custom automated pipeline (convolutional neural network-based classification) validated against manual spike sorting; software at smithlabvision/spikesort.
Model architectures
Deep ensemble model backbone: ResNet50 (60 M parameters, 25 ensemble members, 44 training sessions). Compact models: 5-layer CNNs with 100–200 filters/layer (~12,000 parameters). Additional architectures evaluated: ResNet50-robust, VGG19, CORnet-S, NASNetMobile, InceptionV3, EGGnet.
Knowledge distillation
Compact models trained via distillation on 12 million images with varied filter counts (1–200 filters/core layer); compressibility characterized by noise-corrected R² and the BrainScore benchmark.

Core Methods

  • Adaptive closed-loop experiment design: alternating cycles of neural data collection and model training ensure the deep ensemble model is trained on an increasingly informative image distribution, including out-of-distribution stimuli.
  • Deep ensemble modeling: 25-member ensemble of DNNs built on ResNet50 backbone with nonlinear skip-connection readout layers; session "stitching" via linear mappings links neurons across recording sessions without guaranteed neuron identity.
  • Knowledge distillation for compression: the large deep ensemble (teacher) is distilled into compact 5-layer CNNs (students) with systematically varied filter counts; noise-corrected R² (R²_ER metric) used as the fidelity criterion.
  • Compressibility analysis: filter count ablation traces the compression-accuracy trade-off; "arrows" denote the minimum filters needed to stay within 5% of the 200-filter performance ceiling. Applied to V1, V4, IT, and DNN internal units for comparison.
  • Mechanistic interpretability: filter ablation (dot-size invariance and dot-number invariance metrics), saliency heatmaps, and response-maximizing synthesized images (via gradient ascent) dissect individual compact model computations.
  • Experimental validation: compact model predictions validated in-vivo — response-maximizing images, saliency maps, and epsilon-perturbed images are presented to awake macaques and compared to predicted responses.
  • BrainScore benchmarking: compact models submitted to the public BrainScore leaderboard and found to achieve state-of-the-art performance on corresponding V4 and IT datasets.

① Apply the paper's compression-as-interpretability pipeline to geospatial foundation models

Extending this paperThis paper's core insight is that distilling a large black-box model into a compact one unlocks mechanistic interpretability without sacrificing predictive accuracy. The authors stop at visual neuroscience, but the pipeline — train a large teacher on diverse data, compress it into a 5-layer student, then dissect the student's filters — is domain-agnostic. A natural extension: apply the same compression-then-interpret loop to large task-driven vision models used in remote sensing or urban scene understanding, asking which spatial features are truly necessary for a given prediction task, and whether compact versions reveal interpretable spatial filters analogous to V4's corner-curvature detectors.

The Cowley et al. distillation framework gives me a principled recipe: distill into a compact spatial CNN, ablate filters by a "spatial invariance" metric (analogous to dot-size invariance), and recover the minimal set of spatial features driving predictions — turning my fine-tuned foundation model into an explainable geospatial predictor aligned with my XAI agenda.

② Use the "shared early filters + individual consolidation" motif to design interpretable spatial feature banks

Extending this paperThe paper's discovery that compact V4 models share near-identical early-layer filters but then diverge in their consolidation layers implies a two-stage architecture that separates generic spatial feature extraction from neuron-specific read-out. An extension of the paper would test whether explicitly constraining the architecture — enforcing weight-sharing across the first N layers during training (not just discovering it post-hoc via compression) — achieves the same compressibility and interpretability at lower computational cost, and whether this reveals an even cleaner mechanistic picture.

Added value to my researchThis two-stage design maps directly onto physics-informed graph learning for spatiotemporal prediction: a shared graph-convolutional backbone captures generic spatial topology (analogous to shared early filters), while region-specific readout heads encode local heterogeneity (analogous to the consolidation step). This architecture naturally decomposes a spatiotemporal model into an interpretable, transferable spatial prior and a local calibration stage — directly serving my goal of explainable ML for spatial prediction in healthcare and urban contexts.

③ Extend the closed-loop active learning design to spatiotemporal data collection

Extending this paperA key methodological contribution is the adaptive closed-loop experiment: the model flags uncertain predictions, and the next batch of stimuli is drawn from that uncertain region, improving data efficiency dramatically. The paper shows this works for a controlled lab setting with a fixed number of images. An extension: scale this active learning loop to naturalistic, open-ended stimulus sets and test whether model-guided image selection can reduce the number of neurons needed to train a generalizable model — connecting to the open question of how many recorded neurons suffice for a data-driven foundation model of V4.

Added value to my researchThe same closed-loop active learning logic applies to spatiotemporal sensor placement in my research: rather than deploying sensors at fixed locations, iteratively select new data collection sites where a predictive model of (e.g.) air quality or COVID spread is most uncertain — a spatially adaptive sampling strategy. ④ Formalize a "spatial compressibility index" for spatiotemporal predictors analogous to the paper's neural compressibility

Extending this paperThe paper introduces a principled compressibility measure: how many filters must the compact model have before its noise-corrected R² drops no more than 5% below the full model? This is applied to V1, V4, IT, and DNN units, revealing that V1 neurons are more compressible than IT neurons. An extension: test whether compressibility correlates with the intrinsic dimensionality of the neural population's response manifold, connecting the paper's empirical finding to information-theoretic or manifold-geometry frameworks — and testing whether compressibility predicts the neuron's functional role in the visual hierarchy.

⑤ Test whether compact neural models transfer across individuals, analogous to cross-city transfer in urban models

Extending this paperThe paper shows that the deep ensemble model trained on one macaque's V4 data does not transfer well out-of-the-box to another macaque — suggesting idiosyncratic tuning — yet the compact model architecture and its shared-filter motif appear consistent across monkeys. This asymmetry (population-level architecture transfers, individual neuron responses do not) is left largely unexplored. An extension: formally characterize the degree to which compact model weights (not just architectures) transfer across animals, and whether fine-tuning with only a small number of stimuli from the new animal recovers full accuracy — a few-shot adaptation test for neural predictive models.

Added value to my researchThis cross-individual transfer question maps precisely onto cross-city transfer in GeoAI: a spatial model trained on one city's POI/health/mobility data rarely generalizes to another. The paper's few-shot adaptation framing suggests a concrete strategy — pre-train a compact spatial model on a "source city," freeze the shared early layers (generic spatial filters), and fine-tune only the consolidation/readout layers with a small local dataset from a "target city." This few-shot spatial transfer framework directly supports my research on geospatial foundation model fine-tuning and reduces the data burden for under-resourced cities.
