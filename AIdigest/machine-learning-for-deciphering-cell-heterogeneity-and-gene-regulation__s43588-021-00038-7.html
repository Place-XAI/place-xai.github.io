Machine learning for deciphering cell heterogeneity and gene regulation

Michael Scherer · Florian Schmidt · Olga Lazareva · Jörn Walter · Jan Baumbach · Marcel H. Schulz · Markus List Published 2021-03-15 DOI 10.1038/s43588-021-00038-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Machine learning — from non-negative matrix factorization and regularized regression to emerging deep learning — now spans the full pipeline of computational epigenomics: deconvolving cell-type mixtures in bulk data, predicting gene expression from epigenomic features, and clustering or inferring regulatory networks at single-cell resolution. Classical methods remain competitive with deep learning on interpretability grounds, but the rapidly growing scale of single-cell data and multi-omics integration signal a tipping point where more expressive neural architectures will eventually outperform them — provided interpretability is preserved.

Core Methods

  • Cell-type deconvolution: reference-based (constrained projection, least trimmed squares, support vector regression) and reference-free (non-negative matrix factorization, NMF) decomposition of bulk DNA methylation matrices into cell-type proportions and latent methylation components (LMCs). Hybrid approaches add Bayesian priors or tensor decomposition for sample-specific profiles.
  • Gene-expression prediction from epigenomics: regularized linear regression (lasso, elastic net, ridge) with feature matrices linking regulatory elements to target genes via window-based, nearest-gene, correlation-based, or Hi-C-informed strategies. Tools include TEPIC, RACER, RABIT, JEME, FOCS, STITCHIT, Efilter, and DeepChrome (CNN).
  • Dimensionality reduction and single-cell clustering: weighted k-medoids, NMF, topic modeling, PCA, deep generative neural networks, and k-nearest neighbor applied to sparse scATAC-seq / scRNA-seq matrices to reveal cell types and differentiation states.
  • Multi-modal integration (scATAC + scRNA): self-organizing maps (SOMatic), canonical correlation analysis (MAESTRO), integrative NMF (LIGER), and autoencoder-based co-embedding (SCIM, UnionCom) to produce joint low-dimensional representations across data modalities.
  • Trajectory inference: pseudotime reconstruction from single-cell chromatin accessibility and transcriptomic data (STREAM, APEC + Monocle) to map differentiation paths and metastable branch points.
  • Regulatory network inference: chromatin accessibility co-accessibility maps (Cicero) to define cis-regulatory maps and link distal enhancers to target genes at single-cell resolution.

① Replace per-gene regression with a single foundation model trained genome-wide

Extending this paperThe review identifies regularized linear regression — trained separately per gene or per sample — as the workhorse of gene-expression prediction from epigenomics. A direct extension: replace the per-gene optimization cascade with a single pretrained transformer that learns global regulatory grammar across tens of millions of single-cell transcriptomes. Such a model would see gene co-regulation patterns invisible to any per-gene elastic net, addressing the review's own call for more expressive architectures once data scale justifies them.

Added value to my researchThe transfer-learning paradigm directly mirrors my agenda of fine-tuning geospatial foundation models for downstream spatial prediction tasks. Just as a Geneformer pretrained on 100 M+ cells transfers to unseen cell types, a spatiotemporal foundation model pretrained on massive mobility or remote-sensing corpora can transfer to sparse local health or accessibility prediction — sharing the same adapt-pretrained-weights methodology.

② Unify deconvolution and prediction with a partially shared multi-modal embedding

Extending this paperThe review treats cell-type deconvolution (Section 1) and gene-expression prediction (Section 2) as separate pipelines. Yet both require a joint representation of multiple epigenomic modalities. An extension: learn a partially shared embedding in which modality-specific encoders (methylation, ATAC, RNA) share latent axes for co-regulation while retaining private axes for modality-unique variation. This simultaneously deconvolves cell-type proportions and predicts expression in one forward pass, closing the methodological gap the review implicitly identifies between its two central tasks.

Added value to my researchPartially shared multi-modal embedding is structurally identical to multi-source data fusion in GeoAI: mobile phone records, social media, and remote sensing each have private signal but share latent spatiotemporal factors. The epigenomics formulation — disentangle shared regulatory axes from modality-specific noise — provides a clean mathematical template for spatial data fusion where not all modalities overlap in geography or time.

③ Benchmark deep learning interpretability against the review's classical-regression baseline

Extending this paperThe review notes that deep networks (DeepChrome, autoencoders) do not dramatically outperform classical regularized regression and are less interpretable. A direct follow-on: construct a rigorous benchmark evaluating not just prediction accuracy but also biological faithfulness of extracted features — do CNN attention maps recover known transcription factor binding motifs or known regulatory elements? Such an evaluation would quantitatively answer the review's open question about when deep learning earns its interpretability cost in epigenomics.

④ Replace NMF/PCA single-cell embedding with a hierarchical autoencoder

Extending this paperThe review's single-cell section lists numerous clustering methods but notes that sparse, noisy scATAC-seq data remains a central bottleneck. A concrete extension: replace the NMF or PCA dimensionality reduction step with a hierarchical autoencoder that progressively compresses sparse peak matrices into dense cell embeddings — capturing both coarse cell-type and fine sub-state structure in a single model. This directly addresses the review's open problem of extracting meaningful clusters from extremely sparse single-cell epigenomic data without sacrificing interpretability.

Added value to my researchHierarchical autoencoders for noisy, sparse count data map onto my use of sparse GPS trajectory or POI check-in data to embed urban activity patterns. The inductive bias — compress hierarchically, preserving coarse and fine structure — transfers to spatiotemporal activity embedding where grid cells are often empty at fine resolution but dense at the city-block level, mirroring sparse scATAC-seq peak matrices.

⑤ Apply training-data composition principles to close the generalization gap in epigenomic ML

Extending this paperThe review acknowledges that most gene-expression prediction models are trained on ENCODE/IHEC cell lines and may not generalize to primary patient tissue. A key extension: systematically study how the composition of the training corpus — ratio of cancer vs. normal, cell lines vs. primary tissue, tissue type diversity — controls whether a model discovers genuine regulatory rules or overfits to a particular experimental context. This extends the review's method survey into a principled training-data design science for epigenomics, directly testable with ENCODE's publicly available metadata.

Added value to my researchTraining-data composition is equally critical in my multi-source data fusion for spatial prediction: whether a model trained on dense urban mobile-phone traces generalizes to rural or data-sparse settings depends on how training geography is sampled. The same negative-set design principles — ensure positive examples are not trivially separable from negatives by confounders — apply when training spatiotemporal health or accessibility models across heterogeneous cities.
