Rooftop photovoltaic-powered electric vehicle charging for accelerated decarbonization

Linlin You · Rui Zhu · Paolo Santi · Carlo Ratti · A. T. D. Perera · Zihan Guo · Ziyi Huang · Shixiang Xing · Min Chen · Guonian Lü · Zheng Qin · Jinyue Yan Published 2026-06-15 DOI 10.1038/s41893-026-01854-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 40 global cities, rooftop photovoltaic (RPV) generation and EV charging demand follow an exponential scaling law when measured across urban microgrids of varying size. By 2050, RPV generation will roughly double per city (1.6–434.7 TWh yr−1) while charging demand rises 4–1,759-fold, yet RPV supply remains sufficient to meet demand in all cities. The system is simultaneously economically viable — projecting US$3,173 billion total revenue — and climatically beneficial, accumulating 11.9 Gt of carbon mitigation from 2025 to 2050.

Why It Matters

  • First global-scale spatiotemporal feasibility assessment. Prior RPV-EV studies were single-city or small-sample; this paper analyses 3.38 billion real charging records from 22,000 piles across 40 cities on six continents, making its conclusions transferable and policy-relevant at the planetary scale.
  • Uncovers a universal scaling law. The exponential relationship between microgrid size and daily RPV charging capacity holds consistently across all 40 cities and three charging strategies, providing planners with a predictive rule-of-thumb independent of city type.
  • Integrates supply-side and demand-side projections coherently. By coupling IEA EV adoption scenarios with Shared Socioeconomic Pathway (SSP) rooftop expansion projections, the paper resolves the generation–demand mismatch problem quantitatively rather than qualitatively.

Core Methods

  • Two-stage framework (top-down + bottom-up): Stage 1 (top-down) forecasts daily EVCD per charging pile and estimates RPV generation to derive spatiotemporal charging capacity across urban microgrids. Stage 2 (bottom-up) uses an integrated scenario framework (SSPs × IEA scenarios) to project annual capacity to 2050, enabling economic and carbon accounting.
  • Federated meta-learning (EVCD forecasting): A federated meta-learning architecture (asynchronous FedAvg variant) trains a shared charging-demand predictor across all 40 cities without sharing raw transaction data, handling heterogeneous and non-IID EV data from different operators and cities.
  • Urban microgrid tessellation: Cities are partitioned into hexagonal microgrids at 8 resolution levels (r = 2 km to 16 km), and the generation-to-demand ratio (charging capacity) is computed per microgrid per day under three strategies: Always-on ESS (AES), Minimal ESS (MES), and without ESS (woESS).
  • Exponential scaling law derivation: Across all resolution levels, the log-log relationship between the size metric φ (microgrid feature) and mean charging capacity ccm follows a consistent exponential scaling law, validated via two-sided Pearson correlation for n = 8 resolution-level data points per city.
  • Scenario-integrated projection (2025–2050): RPV generation projections combine Global Solar Atlas irradiance with SSP1–SSP5 rooftop area trajectories; charging demand projections combine IEA STEPS/APS EV fleet sizes with per-vehicle energy consumption. Net profit and carbon mitigation are derived as integrated outcomes.
  • Carbon mitigation accounting: Separates contributions from RPV generation (grid displacement), EV substitution for internal combustion engines (fossil fuel displacement), and energy storage system (ESS) emissions, giving a signed carbon balance across all 40 cities.

① Disaggregate the scaling law by urban morphology and socioeconomic strata to reveal equity dimensions

Extending this paperThe paper's exponential scaling law is established as a cross-city average but is silent on who benefits within a city. The 22,000 charging piles are not uniformly distributed: wealthier neighborhoods host denser infrastructure, and the paper's Extended Data Fig. 4 already shows "noticeably heterogeneous distributions." A direct extension: stratify the microgrid-level charging capacity by census income quintile or nighttime-light proxy for wealth, then test whether the scaling exponent varies systematically — revealing whether RPV-EV co-benefits reproduce or reverse spatial inequality. The corpus paper on deployable rooftop solar across Chinese cities (10.1038/s44284-025-00270-6) provides a comparable intra-city rooftop stratification approach.

Added value to my researchThis connects directly to my EV charging-station efficiency-vs-equity optimization agenda. The scaling law becomes a supply-side constraint I can feed into a spatial accessibility (2SFCA/R2SFCA) framework: each microgrid's RPV charging capacity is the supply S, EV owners in the catchment are the demand D, and the generation-to-demand ratio cc becomes the accessibility score. Stratifying by income reveals whether my optimal charging-station placement rules perpetuate or correct the RPV-EV equity gap.

② Replace the hexagonal tessellation with data-driven functional microgrids learned from mobility flows

Extending this paperThe paper defines urban microgrids by a fixed hexagonal grid (r = 2–16 km), which is computationally convenient but ignores the actual geography of EV owner mobility — a charging pile in a commercial district serves commuters from distant residential zones. A richer extension: derive microgrids from empirical EV trip origins and destinations (GPS or charging-record implied trips), so each microgrid is a mobility community rather than a geometric cell. The scaling law could then be re-tested on mobility-functional microgrids, likely yielding tighter correlations and different optimal ESS strategies.

Added value to my researchThis aligns with my spatiotemporal kernel density and network community detection methods. I could apply graph-based community detection on a bipartite (residential–charging-pile) trip network — exactly how I approach job-housing networks — to define functional energy catchments. The result is a GeoAI-ready microgrid definition for the next generation of RPV-EV feasibility models, replacing arbitrary geometry with inferred human-activity boundaries.

③ Add grid-stress and carbon-signal feedback to the charging strategies

Extending this paperThe paper's three charging strategies (AES, MES, woESS) optimize the generation-to-demand ratio locally but ignore how coordinated RPV charging at the city scale affects the broader electricity grid. Under the APS scenario, 6 cities face >100-fold demand growth; concentrated daytime charging from RPV peaks could destabilize local grids. Extending the paper with a cascading marginal emissions signal — shifting charging to hours when the marginal grid emission intensity is lowest — would reduce both grid risk and net carbon, particularly in cities where the grid is still coal-heavy. The cascading marginal emissions corpus paper (10.1038/s41467-025-64979-7) provides exactly this layer.

Added value to my researchGrid-aware charging scheduling is a spatiotemporal optimization problem I can frame as a physics-informed graph learning task: the city power grid is a graph, RPV generation and EV demand at each node are physics constraints, and the learning objective is to minimize carbon while respecting grid capacity. This adds a power-flow physics layer to my existing urban-graph learning pipeline.

Cross-ref (corpus): Cascading marginal emissions signals for green charging with growing electric vehicle adoption — 10.1038/s41467-025-64979-7

④ Quantify resilience of RPV-EV charging capacity under extreme weather and flooding risk

Extending this paperThe paper projects RPV generation assuming stable solar irradiance averages (Global Solar Atlas annual means) but does not account for episodic disruptions: tropical cyclones, flooding, or prolonged cloudy periods that can simultaneously suppress RPV generation and disable charging infrastructure. For coastal cities (LOA = Los Angeles, SZH = Shenzhen, MEL = Melbourne, SYD = Sydney — all in the top-5 for RPV and EV demand), climate-induced disruptions could undermine the sufficiency finding. Extending the paper with a climate-shock stress test — Monte Carlo sampling of climate scenarios from CMIP6 — would bound the resilience of the 2050 feasibility conclusion. The flooding-resilience EV infrastructure paper (10.1038/s41467-022-30848-w) provides the disruption modeling template.

Added value to my researchMonte Carlo simulation under climate risk is a method I already apply (SIR and agent-based modeling). Treating the RPV-EV system as a resilience optimization problem connects to my 3-objective (efficiency × equity × resilience) framework: optimal charging-station placement must now also account for flood inundation probability, yielding a climate-adapted siting strategy directly relevant to urban health infrastructure planning.

⑤ Extend from EV transport to community-scale green hydrogen storage, closing the multi-vector urban energy loop

Extending this paperThe paper shows that RPV generation will exceed EV charging demand in some cities under some SSPs (charging capacity cca > 1). Surplus RPV energy currently has no modeled use in the paper. A natural extension: model hydrogen electrolysis as a secondary sink for surplus RPV electricity during generation peaks, and hydrogen fuel cells as a grid-balancing source during demand peaks — closing the urban energy loop across transport and building sectors. This is exactly the architecture explored for urban residential communities in the corpus paper on green hydrogen (10.1038/s44284-024-00178-7), which uses rooftop PV paired with hydrogen-battery storage.

Added value to my researchMulti-vector energy modeling (electricity + hydrogen) is a spatial optimization problem with strong GeoAI dimensions: rooftop PV panels, electrolyzer siting, hydrogen storage tanks, and fuel cell dispatch points are all spatially variable, and their joint optimal placement depends on urban morphology, grid topology, and mobility patterns. I can frame this as a multi-layer spatial network optimization, combining explainable ML for demand forecasting with graph-learning for dispatch — extending my existing GeoAI toolkit into urban energy systems.
