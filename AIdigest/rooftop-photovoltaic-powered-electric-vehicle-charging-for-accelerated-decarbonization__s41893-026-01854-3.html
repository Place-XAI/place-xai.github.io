Rooftop photovoltaic-powered electric vehicle charging for accelerated decarbonization

Linlin You · Rui Zhu · Paolo Santi · Carlo Ratti · A. T. D. Perera · Zihan Guo · Ziyi Huang · Shixiang Xing · Min Chen · Guonian Lü · Zheng Qin · Jinyue Yan Published 2026-06-15 DOI 10.1038/s41893-026-01854-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 40 global cities, rooftop photovoltaic (RPV) generation is economically viable and sufficient to meet growing EV charging demand through 2050. Charging capacity across urban microgrids follows a consistent exponential scaling law with the generation-to-demand ratio. By 2050, RPV output per city will double (1.6–434.7 TWh yr−1), delivering a cumulative US$3,173.2 billion in revenue and 11.9 Gt of carbon mitigation from 2025 to 2050 — provided charging strategies adapt to the spatiotemporal mismatch between solar supply and EV demand.

Why It Matters

  • First planet-scale empirical foundation for RPV–EV coupling. The study analyses 3.38 billion real charging records from 22,000 charging piles across 40 diverse global cities — an unprecedented observational basis for solar-powered transport research.
  • Discovers a universal exponential scaling law. Under all three charging strategies tested, the generation-to-demand ratio of urban microgrids follows an exponential scaling relationship with microgrid size — a transferable planning rule independent of city type or climate zone.
  • Quantifies the 2025–2050 decarbonization pathway with dual scenario uncertainty. Projections integrate IEA EV-adoption scenarios (STEPS and APS) with five Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSP1–5) to rigorously bound annual charging capacity, revenue, and carbon savings.
  • Names the key bottleneck. The primary barrier is the spatiotemporal mismatch between solar generation peaks and EV charging demand — a solvable operational challenge, not a fundamental resource limit, pointing planners toward storage strategies rather than more rooftop area.
  • Open data and code shipped with publication. All historical EV charging data and simulated RPV outputs for 40 cities are publicly available on GitHub (SolarCityEV), enabling immediate reproducibility and community extension.

Core Methods

  • Federated meta-learning for EVCD forecasting (AFML): an asynchronous federated meta-learning mechanism predicts daily EV charging demand per station across all 40 cities, handling biased and isolated data across jurisdictions. Linear regression fits show R2 ≈ 1 with minimal bias across the 2023 observation period.
  • Hexagonal urban microgrid delineation: cities are partitioned into hexagonal cells at eight resolution levels (r = 2–16 km), capturing spatially heterogeneous distributions of charging stations and rooftop solar availability.
  • Exponential scaling law discovery: Pearson correlation analysis across eight resolution-aggregated data points (n = 8, df = 6) identifies a statistically significant exponential relationship between the generation-to-demand ratio (φ) and mean daily charging capacity (ccm) — with strong negative correlation (AES: R in [−0.993, −0.795]; MES: R in [−0.997, −0.904]) consistent across all strategies.
  • Three charging strategies: All-Electric Storage (AES), Minimum Electric Storage (MES), and Without Electric Storage System (woESS) represent distinct battery-buffering assumptions with different scaling behaviors.
  • SSP × IEA scenario integration: SSP1–5 (rooftop area expansion) crossed with IEA STEPS and APS (EV adoption) yields a 10-scenario matrix bounding annual RPV generation, charging capacity, net profit, and carbon mitigation per city from 2025 to 2050.
  • Carbon mitigation accounting: carbon savings attributed separately to RPV-displaced grid electricity and EV-displaced gasoline/diesel combustion, minus ESS manufacturing and operational carbon costs, yielding net mitigation per city per scenario.

&circledone; Inject spatial equity into the scaling law — who benefits from RPV-powered charging?

Extending this paperThe exponential scaling law characterizes average charging capacity across microgrid sizes but is silent on who has access to RPV-powered chargers. Lower-density microgrids may have abundant rooftop solar yet few charging piles, while dense urban cores may have demand but restricted rooftop area. A direct extension: compute a spatial Gini coefficient of RPV-charged kWh per capita across microgrids within each of the 40 cities, stratified by income quintile using census data, then ask whether the scaling law's efficiency gains also reduce or worsen within-city charging equity.

&circledtwo; Replace the per-station EVCD predictor with a spatiotemporal graph neural network

Extending this paperThe federated meta-learning model predicts daily EVCD per station in isolation — the federation handles cross-city data privacy but does not capture spatial dependencies between nearby charging stations (demand spillover when a station is full, local commute corridors, residential density gradients). Extending it: model all charging piles within a city as nodes in a dynamic spatial graph and learn demand propagation with a physics-informed GNN where node constraints encode RPV generation availability and grid capacity limits — turning a per-station forecaster into a city-graph demand forecaster whose predictions respect the paper's own scaling law as a soft constraint.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely in my physics-informed graph learning agenda: the paper's RPV generation constraint functions as a node-level supply prior, and the inter-station demand interactions are the graph edges. The 3.38 billion record open dataset gives an unusually rich training signal for spatiotemporal charging network dynamics, and the 40-city scope enables cross-city transfer learning for the GNN — directly feeding my work on physics-informed geospatial foundation models.

&circledthree; Extend the SSP × IEA scenario cross to account for rooftop land competition

Extending this paperThe paper projects rooftop area expansion under SSP1–5 purely for RPV, but urban rooftops are contested space: rooftop agriculture provides food security, green roofs manage stormwater and reduce urban heat, and cool roofs reflect solar radiation. A direct extension: integrate the paper's RPV microgrid model with competing rooftop land-use constraints and derive the Pareto frontier of rooftop allocation strategies for each SSP — showing how much EV-charging potential cities sacrifice for food and cooling co-benefits, and vice versa, across urbanization trajectories.
