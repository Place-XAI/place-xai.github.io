Association of mortality and combined oxidative capacity of ozone and nitrogen dioxide

Yue Niu · Renjie Chen · Francesco Sera · Aleš Urban · Ana Maria Vicedo-Cabrera · Yasushi Honda · Veronika Huber · Yuming Guo · Shilu Tong · Antonio Gasparrini · Haidong Kan · and 33 co-authors Published 2026-06-15 DOI 10.1038/s41893-026-01875-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The weighted combined oxidative capacity of O3 and NO2 (Oxwt) is a stronger mortality predictor than either pollutant alone: a 10-ppb increase over 2 days (LAG01) associates with +0.82% total, +1.09% cardiovascular, and +0.88% respiratory deaths across 380 cities in 23 countries or regions (1985–2020). The Oxwt index accounts for more attributable deaths than O3 or NO2 individually, yet fewer than their simple sum, confirming partial overlap — and positioning Oxwt as a parsimonious, actionable composite air-quality indicator for public health policy.

Why It Matters

  • First global two-stage time-series study on combined gaseous oxidative capacity. Prior work studied O3 and NO2 separately or in two-pollutant models that ignore their electrochemical relationship; this is the largest multi-country analysis of a single theoretically motivated composite index (Oxwt).
  • Resolves the confounded double-counting problem. By weighting each pollutant by its standard electrode potential, Oxwt quantifies synergy without arbitrarily summing or averaging — and the paper empirically confirms partial non-additivity.
  • Provides global variation and trend evidence. Associations vary by geographic region and study period, guiding where regulatory attention should focus and how tightening standards over time translates to fewer deaths.
  • Directly policy-actionable. An Oxwt-based standard could replace two separate O3 and NO2 thresholds, simplifying compliance monitoring while better capturing biological harm.
  • Open reproducible code. Full analysis code is archived on Zenodo (zenodo.20019197), supporting replication and adaptation to other city networks.

Core Methods

  • Oxwt index construction: combine daily O3 and NO2 concentrations by weighting each with its standard electrode potential (a thermodynamic measure of oxidizing power), yielding a single biologically motivated composite exposure metric.
  • City-level time-series regression (Stage 1): quasi-Poisson regression with distributed lag non-linear models (DLNM) fit to each city independently, controlling for temporal trends, season, day-of-week, temperature, and other confounders. Lag structure selected by AIC, focusing on LAG01 (0–1 day).
  • Two-stage multilevel meta-analysis (Stage 2): city-specific estimates pooled using the extended mixed-effects meta-analysis framework (Sera et al. 2019) to obtain regional and global pooled effects with 95% CIs, accounting for between-city heterogeneity.
  • Concentration-response functions: non-linear exposure–mortality curves estimated and visualized, checking for threshold effects and deviation from linearity (Fig. 3).
  • Attributable-fraction analysis: excess deaths attributed to Oxwt, O3, and NO2 separately and compared to quantify partial overlap and additive vs. sub-additive effects.
  • Effect modification: associations stratified by geographic region and study period to characterize spatial and temporal heterogeneity in the Oxwt–mortality relationship.

① Spatially disaggregate Oxwt exposures using satellite-derived pollutant fields

Extending this paperThe study assigns a single daily city-level Oxwt value derived from ground monitors — a recognized exposure measurement error issue the authors acknowledge. A direct extension: replace or supplement station-based concentrations with high-resolution satellite-derived NO2 and O3 columns (e.g., TROPOMI/Sentinel-5P) fused with land-use regression or machine-learning downscaling to produce sub-city gridded Oxwt surfaces. This would enable within-city effect heterogeneity analysis — a dimension entirely absent from the current design, which can only compare cities.

Added value to my researchThis is directly applicable to my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and multi-source data fusion agenda: integrating satellite remote sensing with ground truth via spatially explicit models to create continuous pollutant exposure surfaces, which then feed into neighborhood-level health outcome models — extending the city-average DLNM to a spatially varying coefficient framework (MGWR/GWR) over intra-urban gradients.

③ Apply explainable machine learning to attribute Oxwt heterogeneity across cities

Extending this paperThe paper's two-stage meta-analysis produces city-level effect estimates that vary substantially across the 380 cities and 23 regions, but the drivers of that heterogeneity are explored only through pre-specified regional groupings. A principled extension: train an XGBoost or gradient-boosted ensemble to predict city-level Oxwt effect sizes from city-level features (climate zone, baseline pollution levels, population age structure, socioeconomic indicators, healthcare capacity) and apply SHAP decomposition to identify which features most consistently amplify or dampen the mortality response — converting the paper's unexplained I² into an interpretable feature-attribution map.

④ Extend Oxwt to a multi-pollutant oxidative index via PM2.5 oxidative potential

Extending this paperOxwt covers only the gaseous oxidants O3 and NO2, yet particulate oxidative potential (OP) from PM2.5 components also drives cardiovascular harm (referenced in this paper's own review of oxidative stress). A direct extension: construct a unified reactive oxygen species (ROS) index combining Oxwt with PM2.5 OP metrics, test whether this triple-component index better predicts mortality than any pairwise combination, and quantify excess deaths attributable to the added particulate oxidative burden. The interactive PM2.5–O3 analysis in the corpus (372 cities, Brit. Med. J. 2023) provides a direct empirical precedent.
