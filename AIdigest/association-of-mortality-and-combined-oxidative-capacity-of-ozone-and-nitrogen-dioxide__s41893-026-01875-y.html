Association of mortality and combined oxidative capacity of ozone and nitrogen dioxide

Yue Niu · Renjie Chen · Francesco Sera · Aleš Urban · Ana Maria Vicedo-Cabrera · Yasushi Honda · Veronika Huber · Yuming Guo · Shilu Tong · Antonio Gasparrini · Haidong Kan · and 33 co-authors (MCC Collaborative) Published 2026-06-15 DOI 10.1038/s41893-026-01875-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The combined oxidative capacity of O3 and NO2, expressed as an electrode-potential–weighted index (Oxwt), is more strongly associated with mortality than either pollutant alone. Across 380 cities in 23 countries (1985–2020), a 10-ppb increase in Oxwt over a 2-day lag is linked to a 0.82% rise in total mortality, 1.09% in cardiovascular mortality, and 0.88% in respiratory mortality. Deaths attributable to Oxwt exceed those from O3 or NO2 individually but are fewer than their arithmetic sum — confirming a real, non-additive synergistic hazard. Oxwt is proposed as a practical combined indicator for public health surveillance of gaseous oxidant pollution.

Why It Matters

  • First global multi-city quantification of a combined oxidative pollutant indicator. Prior work examined O3 and NO2 separately; this is the first study to derive and validate Oxwt as a joint oxidative hazard metric across 380 cities on six continents.
  • Proposes an actionable composite regulatory indicator. Oxwt — the concentration-weighted average of O3 and NO2 scaled by their standard electrode potentials — is simple enough to compute from routine monitoring networks yet captures chemical synergism that single-pollutant regulations miss.
  • Documents geographic and temporal variation in the oxidant–mortality relationship. Associations vary significantly by world region and decade, providing a roadmap for targeting air-quality governance where the mortality burden per unit of Oxwt is highest.
  • Reveals that current regulatory approaches undercount the joint mortality burden. Because O3 and NO2 are not simply additive in oxidative burden, evaluating each pollutant individually systematically understates the combined health penalty — a finding with direct implications for WHO guideline revision.

Core Methods

  • Composite oxidative index construction (Oxwt): O3 and NO2 concentrations were averaged after weighting by their standard electrode potentials (Bratsch 1989), producing a single metric that reflects chemical oxidizing power rather than raw concentration.
  • City-specific DLNM time-series regression: quasi-Poisson regression with distributed lag non-linear models (DLNMs) estimated exposure–response functions for each city, with natural splines for concentration and lag dimensions. Confounders controlled: long-term trend, seasonality, day-of-week, temperature, and relative humidity.
  • Primary exposure window — LAG01: cumulative 2-day lag (lag 0–1) used as the main short-term exposure window; sensitivity analyses explored up to lag 0–5.
  • Two-stage meta-analysis: city-level log-risk estimates were pooled in the second stage using an extended mixed-effects meta-analytical framework (Sera et al. 2019), producing global pooled estimates and region/period-stratified subgroup analyses.
  • Attributable mortality fraction: city-specific attributable fractions for Oxwt, O3, and NO2 were computed and compared to assess additivity and determine whether Oxwt captures additional burden beyond individual pollutants.
  • Heterogeneity quantification: I2 statistics and Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) assessed between-city variability and guided meta-regression covariate selection for geographic and temporal effect modifiers.

④ Extend Oxwt from short-term to long-term cumulative exposure using spatiotemporal interpolation

Extending this paperThe paper focuses on short-term (2-day lag) acute associations. The Oxwt index has never been tested in a long-term cohort framework where cumulative oxidative exposure integrates daily fluctuations over years of residence. This converts the paper's acute time-series design into a chronic-exposure study capturing long-term oxidative damage mechanisms.

Extending this paperThe paper treats Oxwt as a given exposure — it does not model how urban-planning decisions (compact vs. sprawl, green infrastructure, road network density, emission zones) or policy interventions (vehicle emission standards, electrification mandates) causally shift the Oxwt distribution that produces mortality.
