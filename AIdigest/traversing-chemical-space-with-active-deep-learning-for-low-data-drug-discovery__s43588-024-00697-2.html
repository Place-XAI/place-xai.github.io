Traversing chemical space with active deep learning for low-data drug discovery

Derek van Tilborg · Francesca Grisoni Published 2024-09-27 DOI 10.1038/s43588-024-00697-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Active deep learning is a powerful strategy for low-data drug discovery: iteratively updating models during screening can achieve up to a sixfold improvement in hit discovery over traditional one-shot virtual screening. The choice of acquisition function — not the deep learning architecture — is the dominant driver of hit enrichment. Exploitative and mutual-information-based acquisition outperform exploration and random baselines, while starting molecular diversity has little lasting effect because active learning rapidly corrects any structural bias within the first few cycles.

Why It Matters

  • Resolves 'known unknowns' holding back adoption. The field knew active learning was promising but lacked guidelines on which strategy to use. This study systematically benchmarks six acquisition functions across two architectures and three drug targets, directly answering what has been opaque.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Primary dataset
LIT-PCBA (University of Strasbourg Drug Design group) — a curated benchmark of high-confidence PubChem bioassay data designed to mimic realistic hit/potency distributions in experimental drug screens. Accessed August 2023 at drugdesign.unistra.fr/LIT-PCBA.
Selected targets
Three macromolecular targets from LIT-PCBA with the largest experimentally validated molecule sets: PKM2 (pyruvate kinase M2, agonism), ALDH1 (aldehyde dehydrogenase 1, inhibition), VDR (vitamin D receptor, antagonism). Six additional low-yield LIT-PCBA datasets used for stress testing (FEN1, GBA, KAT2A, IDH1, OPRK1, ADRB2).
Library size
100,000 molecules per target formed the screening library (training pool); 20,000 additional molecules per target served as a held-out external test set. Screening budget: 1,000 molecules per active learning run.
Starting set
64 molecules (step 0); three diversity levels — innate (full library), moderate (Tanimoto distance cut at 0.8) and low (cut at 0.61–0.70 per target) — constructed by hierarchical average-distance agglomerative clustering.
Molecular representations
Extended Connectivity Fingerprints (ECFPs, 1,024 bits, radius 2) and RDKit molecular graphs (atom features + adjacency). 50 curated SMARTS substructure patterns tracked for chemical space monitoring.
Replication
All active learning experiments repeated 10 times with different random seeds. Total compute: ~10,000 GPU-hours on NVIDIA A100 (40 GB) GPUs.

Core Methods

  • Active learning loop: four-step iterative cycle — (1) train model on labeled set, (2) predict bioactivity on full unlabeled library, (3) acquire 64 molecules via an acquisition function, (4) experimentally label (simulated) and add to training set. Repeat until 1,000-molecule budget exhausted.
  • Deep learning architectures: (i) ECFP-based multilayer perceptron (MLP) using 1,024-bit, radius-2 fingerprints; (ii) Graph neural network (GCN) reading atom/bond features directly from molecular topology. Both share the same MLP head and uncertainty-estimation regime.
  • Bayesian uncertainty estimation: anchored ensembling (M = 10 models), providing predictive posteriors that closely approximate full Bayesian inference. Uncertainty (mean entropy) feeds entropy-based acquisition functions.
  • Six acquisition functions benchmarked: molecular similarity (Tanimoto to known hits), exploitation (best predicted activity), exploration (most uncertain), mutual information (BALD — low MI favors high-certainty, high-disagreement molecules), exploitation without retraining (one-shot baseline), and random (control).
  • Structural diversity control: hierarchical average-distance agglomerative clustering on Tanimoto distance matrix; three diversity levels (innate / moderate / low) generated for starting-set ablation.
  • Evaluation metric: enrichment factor — ratio of hits found among acquired molecules to the expected count under random selection. Values > 1 indicate above-random hit discovery.
  • Statistics: two-sided paired Wilcoxon rank-sum tests (no multiple-testing correction); Kendall's τ for rank association; perturbation controls (shuffled features) to rule out spurious enrichment.

① Replace anchored ensembling with a geospatially-structured uncertainty model

Extending this paperThe paper's uncertainty estimates come from anchored ensembling, an approximate Bayesian method that treats all 100,000 molecules as exchangeable. Yet molecules in a screening library are not i.i.d. — they cluster in chemical space the same way locations cluster in geographic space. A direct extension: embed chemical-space topology as a graph prior (analogous to a spatial covariance) so that uncertainty propagates to neighboring molecules, making the mutual-information acquisition more sample-efficient. The paper's own UMAP projections (Fig. 4) already visualize this latent topology but never exploit it for acquisition. This extension also generalizes the active learning loop to hardware-accelerated in-memory Bayesian inference, as demonstrated in the parallel corpus paper on deep Bayesian active learning with analog hardware.

Added value to my researchSpatially-structured uncertainty is exactly the problem I face in GeoAI: predictions at unobserved locations depend on measured neighbors. Treating chemical-space clustering as a spatial covariance gives me a blueprint for spatially-aware uncertainty propagation in geospatial foundation models, directly informing which unmeasured census tracts or health-facility catchments to label next in an active-geospatial-learning loop.

② Extend acquisition functions with a contrastive representation to improve generalization beyond training chemical space

Extending this paperA persistent limitation the paper identifies is that ECFP-based fingerprints outperform graph networks in the low-data regime despite GNNs achieving higher accuracy on held-out test sets — possibly because GNNs need more data to form useful representations. A natural extension: pre-train a molecular contrastive encoder on large unlabeled molecular libraries (using graph augmentations) and use the resulting embeddings as a fixed representation layer for both architectures. This would give GNNs a strong prior even at 2–64 labeled molecules and could close the gap the paper observes. Mutual-information acquisition could then be recomputed in the lower-dimensional contrastive space, reducing acquisition cost significantly.

Added value to my researchContrastive pre-training followed by fine-tuning is the paradigm I apply when fine-tuning geospatial foundation models on city-specific data. This paper's finding — that a good inductive bias outweighs architectural expressiveness at small N — validates my own strategy of using pre-trained representations (remote sensing, urban embeddings) rather than training GeoAI models from scratch on sparse public-health survey data.

Cross-ref (corpus): Molecular contrastive learning of representations via graph neural networks — 10.1038/s42256-022-00447-x

③ Transfer the active learning loop to out-of-distribution chemical space via edge-of-space generalization

Extending this paperThe paper deliberately uses three well-curated LIT-PCBA datasets and acknowledges its retrospective setting. A critical open question it raises is how findings translate to structurally novel targets. An extension would couple this paper's iterative acquisition loop with a model that explicitly tracks its own out-of-distribution confidence — so that when the model is queried near the edge of chemical space, it signals low reliability and switches acquisition strategy. The corpus paper on molecular deep learning at the edge of chemical space directly addresses this failure mode and could be combined with the active learning framework to produce a self-aware screening engine.

Added value to my researchThe "edge of distribution" problem is central to explainable ML for spatial prediction: my MGWR/GWR models also degrade when predicting health outcomes in rural or low-density areas not well represented in training data. A strategy that monitors distributional shift and adjusts model trust accordingly would directly improve spatial extrapolation in accessibility models applied to underserved regions — a core equity concern in my healthcare-access work.

④ Combine active learning with local chemical-space exhaustion to escape acquisition myopia

Extending this paperThe paper notes that exploitative acquisition repeatedly discovers the same chemistry (staying near known hits) while explorative acquisition sacrifices hit enrichment for novelty. A direct extension: incorporate a local exhaustion strategy that systematically enumerates near-neighbors of known hits in chemical space — as the corpus paper on exhaustive local chemical-space exploration via transformer models does — and then switches to global exploitative acquisition once the local neighborhood is saturated. This could provide the "early-cycle similarity boost" the paper observes (similarity search excels in the first 5 cycles) while preserving the later-cycle dominance of mutual-information-based deep learning.

Added value to my researchThe idea of local exhaustion followed by global exploration maps directly onto my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation work, where local density estimation and global interpolation are decoupled. More concretely, in Monte Carlo simulation of service-area accessibility (R2SFCA), I could apply a similar two-phase strategy: exhaust the local neighborhood of known supply points first, then explore regions of high uncertainty in the demand surface — reducing the number of simulation runs needed for stable estimates.

⑤ Recast the iterative screening loop as an active geospatial-data collection protocol for public-health surveillance

Extending this paperThe paper's core contribution is a budget-constrained iterative labeling framework: given a large unlabeled pool, decide which items to experimentally test next in order to maximize discovery within a fixed budget. This framework is entirely domain-agnostic. Extending the paper: replace the molecular library with a geospatial grid of census tracts or health-facility locations, replace bioactivity labels with field-survey measurements (e.g., telehealth uptake, mental-health access score), and use the mutual-information acquisition function to guide where field teams should collect data next. The paper's guidelines (five or more cycles, exploitation/mutual-information over exploration) translate directly to survey design under budget constraints. Drug-response transfer learning (predicting responses for unseen cell types) is an analogous cross-context generalization that further validates the framework's breadth.

Added value to my researchThis is the most direct bridge to my work on health geography and accessibility: field measurement of primary-care or mental-health service quality is expensive, and most surveys use uniform random sampling. Adopting an active-learning acquisition loop — prioritizing unsampled locations that are most uncertain or most likely to yield high-impact findings — would let me close data gaps in underserved areas with fewer field trips, directly improving equity-focused accessibility models (2SFCA/R2SFCA) and digital-health divide analyses. The paper's demonstration that starting diversity barely matters (it is corrected within 5 cycles) suggests that biased convenience samples common in health surveys can be rapidly corrected by subsequent active queries.
