Taking a deep dive with active learning for drug discovery

Zachary Fralish · Daniel Reker Published 2024-10-23 DOI 10.1038/s43588-024-00704-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A systematic benchmarking study by Grisoni and van Tilborg reveals that no single deep learning configuration universally wins at active learning for drug discovery: the best selection function depends on both the model architecture and the dataset. Crucially, shallow random forests remain competitive with deep neural networks in low-data regimes, graph convolutional networks underperform molecular fingerprints in early active-learning iterations, and active learning consistently self-corrects initial training-set biases by autonomously prioritizing hit-enriched substructures.

Why It Matters

  • Sets realistic expectations for a rapidly hyped field. Active deep learning is being adopted across academia and industry for drug discovery; this is one of the first systematic, retrospective benchmarks that disentangles the contributions of selection function, molecular representation, and training-set composition.
  • Warns against uncritical adoption of graph networks. Despite their strong performance on large datasets, graph convolutional networks underperformed molecular fingerprints in the low-data active-learning setting — a counterintuitive finding that tempers enthusiasm and redirects architectural investment.
  • Quantifies how selection function choice and training-set composition interact. The optimal query strategy is not universal: exploitation and mutual-information selection outperformed exploration for deep models, while exploration won for random forests — pointing to model-contingent active-learning design.
  • Demonstrates active learning's bias-correcting property. Active learning rapidly compensated for skewed initial training sets and autonomously surfaced privileged chemical substructures, giving it a competitive edge over passive ML strategies in real-world low-quality-data scenarios.
  • Anchors future prospective studies. The retrospective benchmarking framework provides informed baselines and clear performance expectations, reducing the risk of overclaiming in future prospective and real-world deployments.

Core Methods

  • Active learning loop: train a model on current labeled data → select the next molecule to test via a selection function → update the model iteratively — with the goal of maximizing hit discovery rate per experiment.
  • Selection functions compared: (1) explorative (maximize predictive uncertainty), (2) exploitative/greedy (maximize predicted potency), (3) mutual-information-based (quantify shared knowledge between candidate and training set), (4) random, (5) exploitation without retraining, and (6) hit-similarity-based selection.
  • Molecular featurization: binary molecular fingerprints vs. graph convolutional networks that learn representations directly from molecular graph topology — used to train neural networks for potency prediction.
  • Benchmarking design: retrospective evaluation across multiple molecular datasets; performance measured as number of hits identified as a function of active learning iterations.
  • Baseline comparison: state-of-the-art random forest on fingerprints, used to assess whether deep networks add value over established shallow models in low-data regimes.
  • Training-set analysis: vary initial training set chemical diversity; track trajectories in chemical space to assess how each strategy recovers from biased starting points.

① Replace fixed selection functions with a learned, transferable query policy

Extending this paperThe benchmarked study compares six hand-designed selection functions; this commentary calls for future work on "bespoke model architectures." A direct extension: formulate the molecule-selection step as a sequential decision problem and learn a meta-policy (via reinforcement learning or meta-learning) that adapts the selection criterion on-the-fly based on the current model uncertainty landscape and dataset trajectory. This would convert the paper's message — "calibrate selection to your model and dataset" — from a manual tuning exercise into a self-adapting policy.

This would make the paper's empirical observation into a predictive rule for practitioners choosing between representation types.

③ Couple active learning with self-supervised pre-training to overcome the low-data barrier

Extending this paperThe paper identifies that deep networks struggle with limited initial labeled data — the very regime where active learning operates. The commentary hints at transfer learning as a remedy. A concrete extension: initialize the active-learning loop with a self-supervised contrastive pre-trained molecular encoder (trained on millions of unlabeled molecules), then fine-tune only the prediction head during the AL loop. This pre-training would provide rich latent embeddings from which the selection functions can compute uncertainty and mutual information more reliably even at iteration zero.

Added value to my researchSelf-supervised pre-training on large unlabeled geospatial corpora (remote sensing, street-view imagery, OSM graphs) is exactly the foundation-model paradigm I am pursuing for fine-tuning geospatial models. The drug-discovery finding that deep networks need pre-training to shine in low-data AL parallels my work on fine-tuning geospatial foundation models for sparse healthcare-outcome prediction across underserved regions.

④ Integrate generative models into the active-learning loop for out-of-distribution exploration

Extending this paperThe current benchmarking study evaluates AL over a fixed molecular library (passive pool) — the model selects from molecules that already exist. The paper's commentary on "sampling from expanded solution pools" suggests a forward-looking extension: couple the AL selection step with a generative molecular design model that proposes de novo structures, converting the pool-based AL into a generative-AL loop. The selection function would then jointly score predicted potency, structural novelty, and synthesizability, enabling genuine chemical space expansion rather than efficient search of a fixed library.

Added value to my researchIn facility-location optimization (EV charging, primary care siting), the analogue is a generative site-proposal loop: instead of selecting from a fixed set of candidate locations, a generative model proposes new candidate sites in underserved spatial gaps, which a downstream AL-style iteration then scores for accessibility gain and equity impact. This directly extends my EV-charging efficiency-equity optimization framework into a generative-AL planning pipeline.

⑤ Benchmark active learning under distribution shift — the out-of-chemical-space generalization problem

Extending this paperThe commentary flags that the retrospective benchmarking design may not fully translate to prospective real-world scenarios — a key limitation. The most critical gap: real drug-discovery campaigns routinely demand out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization, where the target molecules are structurally distinct from the training pool. Extending the paper: replicate the benchmark with scaffold-split evaluation (train and test pools separated by molecular scaffold) and measure how each selection function and representation holds up under this distributional challenge. The OOD lens would sharpen the paper's advice for practitioners who must generalize beyond known chemical space.

Added value to my researchOOD generalization is an urgent problem in my spatiotemporal ML work as well: models trained on data-rich cities must generalize to data-sparse or structurally different cities (different street morphologies, health systems, demographics). Framing this as a spatial scaffold-split — where the test region is held-out by geographic cluster rather than random split — directly improves the evaluative rigor of my urban-health prediction pipelines, paralleling the molecular-scaffold-split insight from drug discovery.
