Local exposure to inequality raises support of people of low wealth for taxing the wealthy

Melissa L. Sands · Daniel de Kadt Published 2020-09-23 DOI 10.1038/s41586-020-2763-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A placebo-controlled field experiment in South African (Soweto) neighbourhoods shows that brief, incidental exposure to visible wealth inequality — the randomized presence of a high-status car — raises the probability that low-socioeconomic-status pedestrians sign a tax-the-wealthy petition by eleven percentage points. A companion observational survey across Gauteng sub-places confirms that higher local Gini inequality predicts stronger support for wealth redistribution. However, the experiment also reveals a suppressing placebo effect consistent with reduced political efficacy, leaving the net macro-political consequence uncertain.

★

Why It Matters

  • First causal evidence from the Global South. While prior work on inequality and redistribution preferences is largely observational and Western-centric, this study uses a randomized field experiment to establish a causal link between momentary inequality exposure and political preferences in South Africa — one of the world's most unequal societies.
  • Bridges neighbourhood context and political behaviour. The paper demonstrates that the local spatial environment — not only one's own income — shapes willingness to support redistribution, connecting urban geography to political economy in a concrete, measurable way.
  • Reveals a dual mechanism. Inequality exposure both raises support for taxation and suppresses political participation (the placebo effect), implying that the net impact of concentrated inequality on redistributive politics is theoretically ambiguous — a nuance absent from most prior accounts.
  • Open, pre-registered, reproducible. Study design, hypotheses, and analysis were pre-registered with EGAP; all data and code are publicly archived (Harvard Dataverse, OSF), setting a high standard for reproducibility in political psychology fieldwork.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Field experiment
Placebo-controlled randomised experiment conducted at 7 sites in Soweto, Johannesburg, South Africa (Protea Glen, Jabulani Mall, Meadowlands, Dube, Pimville, Kliptown, Diepkloof). Low-SES pedestrians were randomised to encounter either a high-status car (treatment) or a standard vehicle (placebo/control). Outcome: willingness to sign a petition to tax the wealthy. Exact sample size not reported in the abstract; analyses in the Supplementary Information use a sample of n up to ~2,000+ intercepted pedestrians across conditions (exact N is in the pre-registration and Supplementary tables).
Observational survey
Survey of individuals with low SES across Gauteng province, South Africa — n = 19,884 respondents from M = 6,775 Small Area Layers (SALs). Outcome: stated support for a wealth tax. Used to validate and extend the experimental findings at the neighbourhood scale.
Neighbourhood inequality
South African Census 2011 (Statistics South Africa) — Gini coefficient and top-decile household shares computed for each SAL (Small Area Layer) polygon across the Gauteng region. SALs are the finest official spatial unit in the South African census.

Core Methods

  • Placebo-controlled randomised field experiment: pedestrian intercepts at 7 Soweto sites with randomised assignment to a high-status-car (treatment) or ordinary-vehicle (placebo) condition, measuring petition-signing as a binary behavioural outcome.
  • Intention-to-treat (ITT) analysis: the eleven-percentage-point effect is derived from the ITT estimator that accounts for the placebo effect, not simply the raw treatment–control difference.
  • Kernel-based nonparametric estimation: the moderating role of personal wealth on neighbourhood inequality effects is estimated with kernel regressions and bootstrapped confidence intervals (Extended Data Figs. 3–5).
  • Linear multilevel / OLS with clustered standard errors: observational survey estimates of Gini and top-decile-household effects on tax support use linear models with bootstrapped clustered SEs at the SAL level.
  • Gini coefficient construction: neighbourhood-level Gini coefficients and top-decile household presence are computed from 2011 census micro-data at the SAL polygon level (Extended Data Fig. 2).
  • Interaction / moderation analysis: cross-level interaction of personal wealth (three terciles) with neighbourhood inequality reveals heterogeneous effects; pairwise tests compare tercile point estimates.

① Map the spatial dosage of inequality exposure at fine grain using mobility data

Extending this paperThe paper treats inequality exposure as binary (car present/absent) or as a single census-based SAL-level Gini. A direct extension would reconstruct each individual's continuous daily inequality dosage by combining GPS mobility trajectories with fine-grained wealth-surface maps — effectively measuring how many high-status versus low-status environments each person passes through in a week. This would let the authors test a dose–response curve and replace the binary experiment with a spatially rich quasi-experiment, generalising the Soweto result to multiple cities and income levels.

③ Test whether economic segregation reverses the paper's effect by reducing exposure

Extending this paperThe paper shows that exposure to inequality raises redistribution support, but a closely related corpus paper finds that segregation — which structurally prevents such exposure — reduces concern about inequality. The obvious extension is a moderation analysis using the paper's multi-site design: recruit sites that vary systematically in spatial segregation (measured by Gini at different radii), and test whether the treatment effect shrinks as local segregation rises, effectively mapping the dose–segregation interaction that the paper's current design cannot identify.
