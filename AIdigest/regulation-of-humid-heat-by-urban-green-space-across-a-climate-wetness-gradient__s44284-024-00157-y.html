Regulation of humid heat by urban green space across a climate wetness gradient

Yichen Yang · Chang Cao · Ivan Bogoev · Cosima Deetman · Grace Dietz · Jian Hang · Logan Howard · Xinjie Huang · Nicholas Kendall · Justin Lai · Hainan Lam · Kristen Tam · Christopher Yoo · Keer Zhang · Xuhui Lee Published 2024-11-12 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00157-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Urban green space cools the air but also moistens it, and these two effects offset each other for human heat stress. Across 15 cities, the daytime temperature effect is negatively correlated with the humidity effect, so the daytime change in wet-bulb temperature (Tw) is essentially zero (ΔTw = −0.01 °C). A genuine net reduction in humid heat appears only at night and only in intermediate and wet climates (mean ΔTw = −0.35 °C), driven mostly by reduced soil heat storage under vegetation. The upshot: green space is not a one-size-fits-all humid-heat remedy.

Why It Matters

  • Reframes a default policy. "Plant more green space to beat the heat" is near-universal urban guidance, almost always justified by air-temperature cooling. This work shows the relevant human-stress metric — humid heat — barely moves in the daytime, challenging the headline benefit.
  • Centers the right variable. Heat stress is set by both temperature and humidity; using wet-bulb temperature instead of dry-bulb temperature changes the verdict on greening from "clearly helpful" to "context-dependent."
  • Climate context decides the outcome. Net humid-heat relief is conditional on summer precipitation regime and on time of day — green space helps at night in wetter climates, but offers little daytime relief anywhere.
  • Mechanistic, not just empirical. A model simulation attributes the nighttime benefit to reduced soil heat storage, giving planners a physical lever rather than a correlation.
  • Open, reusable evidence. Mobile microclimate transects, neighbourhood imagery, and figure code are released on figshare, enabling reanalysis and extension.

Core Methods

  • Paired mobile sampling: vehicle-mounted transects measure air temperature and humidity across green-space and adjacent built-up neighbourhoods in each of 15 cities, computing the green-minus-built difference in T, RH and Tw.
  • Wet-bulb temperature as the heat-stress metric: derive Tw from temperature and humidity to capture humid heat, then quantify daytime vs. nighttime ΔTw.
  • Climate-gradient stratification: group cities into dry / intermediate / wet by ERA5-based summer precipitation to test how the greening effect varies with background wetness.
  • Effect-correlation analysis: test the relationship between the temperature effect and the humidity effect of green space (Fig. 3), revealing their offsetting (negative) correlation.
  • Process-based attribution: a model simulation decomposes the nighttime Tw reduction, attributing it mainly to reduced soil heat storage in vegetated land (Fig. 4).

① Scale the 15-city transects to a global, satellite-trained humid-heat surface

Extending this paperThe paper's evidence is dense but sparse in space — 15 cities along a wetness gradient. A direct extension: use the released transect data as ground truth to train a geospatial model that predicts the green-vs-built ΔTw from NDVI, land cover, LCZ, and ERA5 precipitation, then map the daytime/nighttime humid-heat effect of greening across all cities. This turns the paper's local diagnosis into a transferable global product and tests whether the offsetting daytime effect holds everywhere.

Extending it: overlay the predicted humid-heat relief onto population and heat-vulnerability layers, and ask whether green spaces that genuinely reduce humid heat are accessible to at-risk residents — distinguishing "cosmetic" greenery from heat-protective greenery.
