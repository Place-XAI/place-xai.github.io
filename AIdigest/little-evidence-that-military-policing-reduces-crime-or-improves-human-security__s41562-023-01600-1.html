Little evidence that military policing reduces crime or improves human security

Robert A. Blair · Michael Weintraub Published 2023-05-11 DOI 10.1038/s41562-023-01600-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A randomized controlled trial of Colombia's Plan Fortaleza military patrol programme across 1,255 city blocks in Cali found little to no evidence that military policing reduced crime or improved residents' perceptions of safety. If anything, crime increased after the intervention ended, and survey data indicate elevated human rights abuses by police (not soldiers) in patrol zones. The benefits of military policing are probably small and not worth the costs.

★

Why It Matters

  • First experimental evidence on military policing. Observational studies of militarized domestic law enforcement are common; a fully randomized controlled trial at the block level is, to the authors' knowledge, the first of its kind — closing a major gap in criminology.
  • High-stakes policy relevance. Governments across Latin America, Africa, and Southeast Asia routinely deploy troops for domestic policing; this result directly challenges the evidential basis for that policy and the "mano dura" framing.
  • Unexpected adversarial effects. The intervention not only failed to deter crime; it is associated with a post-intervention increase of ~69% in crime on treatment blocks relative to control, and elevated police (not military) abuse — an important causal distinction for accountability policy.
  • Multi-source triangulation across data types. The simultaneous use of administrative records, two-wave surveys (over 10,000 respondents), GPS patrol logs, and civilian monitors enables a unusually robust null-finding with Bayes factors supporting the null, not just failing to reject it.
  • Open, fully reproducible design. Pre-registered PAP, openly archived data and code on Harvard Dataverse, and EGAP registration make the study a reproducibility benchmark for experimental criminology.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Study site
Cali, Colombia — communes 18 and 20 (combined population ~215,000 at time of study). Cali is Colombia's third-largest city, with a homicide rate of 46.7 per 100,000 in 2018.
Experimental design
Block-level RCT: 1,255 city blocks across 30 neighbourhoods, stratified by neighbourhood. Assignment: 214 treatment, 765 spillover, 275 control blocks. Intervention period: 30 September – 18 November 2019.
Administrative crime data
Timestamped, geocoded records from Cali's Mayor's Office / Colombian National Police / Attorney-General's Office covering homicides, armed robberies, thefts, illegal drug sales, and illegal weapons possession. Span: 1 Jan – 31 Dec 2019 (9 months pre-intervention through 6 weeks post).
Human rights data
Timestamped, geocoded abuse allegations from the Office of the Attorney-General. Coverage: all of 2019.
Monitoring survey
Original household survey of 2,096 randomly selected residents across 416 blocks (202 treatment, 109 spillover, 105 control), October–December 2019. Three residents + two business owners per block.
Endline survey
Original household survey of 7,921 randomly selected residents and business owners, January–February 2020 (2–3 months post-intervention). On average six residents + two business owners per block.
GPS / civilian monitors
Firsthand patrol observations via a custom smartphone app and GPS geo-fences (25 m radius per treatment block); compliance: 85–100% of treatment blocks per night. Data period: duration of intervention.
Homicide benchmark
Global homicide rates for Fig. 1 from Seguridad, Justicia y Paz (NGO), 2019 dataset.
Replication archive
All data and code at Harvard Dataverse doi:10.7910/DVN/WAJ9SR. Pre-analysis plan at osf.io/95cz3.

Core Methods

  • Randomized controlled trial (block level): 1,255 city blocks stratified by neighbourhood (30 neighbourhoods); ~1/6 per neighbourhood assigned to treatment. Spillover group defined as blocks adjacent to at least one treatment block.
  • Weighted least squares (WLS) regression: inverse probability weights computed via 1,500 bootstrap replications of the randomization (exact weights not analytically derivable due to spillover structure). Block-level model (eq. 1) and individual-level model with neighbourhood fixed effects and clustered SEs (eq. 2).
  • Randomization inference (RI): reported alongside conventional p-values as a more conservative test under "fuzzy clustering" — RI p-values test the sharp null of zero treatment effect for every unit.
  • Bayes factors (BF₁₀): reported for all null ITTs to distinguish true null evidence from underpowered tests; BF₁₀ < 1/3 threshold used to confirm null.
  • Outcome indices: additive crime index (murders + armed robberies + thefts + drug sales + illegal weapons); standardized additive safety perception index (5-point Likert scale across multiple items); standardized additive crime-witnessing index.
  • Spillover modelling: treatment, spillover (adjacent blocks), and control groups separately estimated; supplementary analyses test linear decay, exponential decay, and saturation models.
  • Software: Stata 17 and R 4.2.0.

① Replace the paper's binary hot-spot targeting with a spatially continuous risk surface learned from data

Extending this paperPlan Fortaleza targeted blocks pre-classified as hot spots by city officials — a discrete, administratively defined treatment. This paper's experimental results are valid for those specific blocks, but the targeting decision itself was un-optimized: it did not use fine-grained, real-time crime risk predictions. A natural extension is to re-run the evaluation framework with patrol assignment driven by a machine-learning crime-risk map estimated from the administrative records the authors already hold, then test whether smarter targeting produces different ITT estimates. The paper's own Supplementary A shows spatially heterogeneous baseline crime rates — a prediction layer is a direct upgrade.

The paper's geolocated, timestamped administrative crime data (archived on Dataverse) provide a clean training set. The explainability angle (which features drive risk, and does military presence shift them?) connects to my GeoAI work on physics-informed prediction.

② Decompose the post-intervention crime rebound using spatial diffusion modelling

Extending this paperThe paper's most striking finding is a post-intervention crime increase of ~69% on treatment blocks relative to control — yet this is measured only as an average ITT. The paper acknowledges it cannot fully explain this rebound (gang displacement? arrest-induced retaliation? reporting artefact?). A direct extension is to fit a spatial contagion / diffusion model to the administrative crime time-series, testing whether the post-intervention surge propagates spatially from treatment blocks outward (consistent with displacement) or appears uniformly (consistent with a reporting artefact). The paper's block-level, timestamped data — 1,255 blocks, full-year coverage — are exactly what such a model needs.

Added value to my researchSpatiotemporal diffusion of crime after a policing shock is a natural application of my SIR-inspired contagion modelling and Monte Carlo simulation toolkit. Treating crime events as a "contagion" spreading across the block-level network (with treatment-block exposure as the seed), I could estimate the reproduction number of post-intervention crime spread and test counterfactual patrol schedules — directly extending my spatiotemporal methods to criminology. The findings would also inform the displacement vs. diffusion of benefits debate in hot-spots policing literature.

③ Use the paper's block-level survey data to build a "perceived safety accessibility" measure analogous to 2SFCA

Extending this paperThe paper treats perceived safety as a single outcome variable per respondent. But the endline survey captures safety perceptions across multiple domains (day/night walking, smartphone use, crime worry, precautionary behaviour) for over 7,000 respondents geo-referenced to blocks — rich enough to construct a spatial accessibility-to-safety metric. Extending the paper: define "safety access" as a spatially smoothed surface over the perceived-safety index, map it against actual crime density and distance to police stations, and examine how military patrol treatment shifted the shape of the surface rather than just a point average. This links the paper's behavioural findings to the spatial accessibility literature.

Added value to my researchPerceived safety as a spatial accessibility dimension is a novel extension of my 2SFCA / R2SFCA framework beyond physical facility access. Safety is a "service" that residents implicitly demand; a catchment-weighted safety-perception surface, calibrated with the paper's treatment-control contrast, would let me operationalize "safe city access" alongside healthcare or transport access in a unified 15-minute-city-style metric. This connects to my healthy-cities and public-facility accessibility research and points toward a multi-dimensional accessibility indicator for urban policy.

④ Test whether urban morphology moderates the null effect across block types

Extending this paperThe paper's ITT is estimated with block-level controls (block area, distance to police station/battalion/transit hub, average age, education, % male) but does not interrogate urban morphology — street connectivity, building density, block perimeter complexity, alley vs. arterial type — as effect modifiers. Given that soldiers navigated unlit alleys in commune 20 via GPS and spent on average only 11 min per patrol per block (not the intended 30 min), morphological features likely moderated compliance and, by extension, treatment exposure. Extending the paper: merge OSM-derived morphological features onto the 1,255 blocks and run heterogeneous treatment effect (HTE) analyses by morphological class.

Added value to my researchThis is a clean application of my spatially explicit explainable ML (XGBoost + SHAP on spatial units) to an experimental dataset — rare in criminology. Identifying which block morphologies are most (or least) amenable to hot-spot policing would generalize to any place-based intervention. For my urban analytics work, it extends the concept of "urban form as a moderator" that appears in 15-minute-city and job-housing network research: the same spatial structure that shapes accessibility to services also shapes the efficacy of security interventions.

⑤ Generalize the experimental design to multi-city comparisons using the paper's pre-registered framework as a protocol template

Extending this paperThe paper explicitly acknowledges single-city external validity as its main limitation, and notes that Cali shares traits with other Latin American cities. Its PAP, block-level randomization code, and outcome measurement instruments are all openly archived, constituting a replicable experimental protocol for multi-city replication. A direct extension: run the same RCT design (with the paper's instruments) across 4–6 Latin American cities that vary in organized crime structure (fragmented vs. consolidated), police corruption levels, and city morphology, then use a meta-analytic framework to estimate a heterogeneity-adjusted effect and identify what city-level moderators (Gini, police per capita, gang structure) predict treatment success or failure.

Added value to my researchMulti-site experimental criminology with spatial heterogeneity analysis is a natural fit for my multi-source data fusion and spatial network community detection methods. Applying network community detection to gang territory maps across cities, and overlaying those community boundaries on the block-level RCT grid, would let me test whether gang territorial fragmentation (a network property) predicts the paper's post-intervention crime rebound — a GeoAI-flavoured extension of the paper's Discussion section. The openly archived data and code lower the barrier to multi-city replication substantially.
