Optimizing retro-reflective surfaces to untrap radiation and cool cities

Xinjie Huang · Elie Bou-Zeid · Ilaria Pigliautile · Anna Laura Pisello · Jyotirmoy Mandal Published 2024-03-11 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00047-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Retro-reflective (RR) surfaces — which bounce incoming sunlight back toward the sky instead of scattering it within the urban canyon — can "untrap" solar radiation that ordinary high-reflectance (HR) surfaces merely redirect onto neighboring walls and people. Detailed energy-budget simulations show RR walls and pavements cut canyon surface temperatures by up to 20 °C and canyon air temperatures by up to 2.6 °C, while improving pedestrian comfort (up to 0.55 °C lower skin temperature and 153 W m⁻² less net radiative gain) — generally outperforming HR surfaces. The authors translate this into climate-, latitude-, season- and geometry-specific design guidance: RR pavements for open, low-rise areas and tailored RR wall strategies for compact, high-rise canyons.

Why It Matters

  • Fixes a hidden flaw in "cool" surfaces. Conventional high-albedo coatings reflect sunlight diffusely, often onto adjacent walls and pedestrians; RR materials redirect it back to the sky, addressing the well-known side effect that bright surfaces can raise mean radiant temperature at street level.
  • Quantifies pedestrian-scale benefits, not just surface albedo. The work reports human skin-temperature and net radiative-gain reductions, connecting a materials property directly to outdoor thermal comfort and heat-health risk.
  • Turns a material into deployable design rules. Instead of a single recommendation, it produces an optimization matrix across climates, latitudes, seasons, and urban aspect ratios — actionable for planners and architects.
  • Expands the urban heat-mitigation portfolio. RR surfaces complement greening, cool roofs, and shading, offering a passive, retrofit-friendly option where vegetation or water is constrained.

Core Methods

  • Retro-reflective radiation physics: extend a shortwave-radiation scheme so that a fraction of incident solar energy is returned along the incoming direction (back to sky) rather than diffusely scattered within the canyon — the "untrapping" mechanism.
  • Surface energy-budget / urban canopy model (UCM): couple the modified radiation scheme to a detailed canyon energy budget to compute surface, air, and human-relevant temperatures.
  • Thermal-comfort coupling: propagate radiative changes to a pedestrian, reporting human skin temperature and net radiative gain as comfort metrics.
  • Parametric optimization: sweep albedo × retro-reflectivity, canyon aspect ratio, latitude, season, and climate to find the most effective RR configuration in each regime (Figs. 3–6).
  • Comparative benchmarking: evaluate RR walls vs. RR pavements vs. high-reflectance (HR) surfaces to derive placement guidance (RR pavements for open/low-rise; RR walls for compact/high-rise).
