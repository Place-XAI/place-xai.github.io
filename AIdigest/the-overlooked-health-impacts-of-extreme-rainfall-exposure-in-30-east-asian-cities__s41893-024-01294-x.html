The overlooked health impacts of extreme rainfall exposure in 30 East Asian cities

He, Cheng · Kim, Ho · Hashizume, Masahiro · Lee, Whanhee · Honda, Yasushi · Kim, Satbyul Estella · Guo, Yue Leon · Schneider, Alexandra · Zhu, Yixiang · Zhou, Lu · Chen, Renjie · Kan, Haidong Published 2024-02-16 DOI 10.1038/s41893-024-01294-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 30 East Asian cities from 1980 to 2020, only rare extreme rainfall events — those with 5- or 10-year return periods — significantly elevate respiratory mortality, with cumulative relative risks of 1.29 and 1.33, respectively. The effect is driven primarily by asthma and COPD among the elderly, persists for up to 4–5 days after the event, and is not explained by concurrent floods, tropical cyclones, or temperature — underscoring a largely overlooked pathway from climate extremes to chronic respiratory death.

Why It Matters

  • Identifies a blind spot in climate-health research. While heat, cold, and floods have been extensively studied, this is the first large-scale multi-city epidemiological study to isolate extreme rainfall intensity thresholds and link them specifically to chronic respiratory deaths.
  • Return-period framing changes the policy calculus. By showing that only 5- and 10-year events (not routine heavy rain) matter, the study gives emergency planners a clear, defensible trigger threshold rather than a continuous dose–response curve.
  • Elderly asthma/COPD patients are disproportionately at risk. The finding that mortality elevation is concentrated in the aged and in obstructive airway diseases — not pneumonia — points to distinct biological pathways (bioaerosols, pressure drops, pollen–thunderstorm synergies) that standard flood-risk plans do not address.
  • Excess mortality is quantifiable and sizable. The study estimates 4.46 additional respiratory deaths per 10,000 per city over the 40-year period attributable to 5-year events — a burden comparable to major air-quality interventions.
  • Multi-country, multi-decade robustness. Consistent results across four East Asian countries/regions and multiple alternative precipitation databases (ERA5-Land, MSWEP, IMERG) strengthen causal inference at a scale rarely achieved in environmental epidemiology.

Core Methods

  • Extreme-value detection via IDF model: intensity–duration–frequency curves fitted to the partial duration series of the 40 annual maximum daily rainfall values (1980–2020) per city using a generalized extreme value (GEV) distribution; thresholds for 2-, 5-, and 10-year return periods derived from cumulative probabilities of the fitted GEV.
  • City-level time-series regression (Stage 1): Generalized Additive Model with quasi-Poisson link; natural cubic B-spline of calendar day (4 df/year) for seasonality and trend; day-of-week and public-holiday indicators; temperature controlled via a cross-basis distributed lag nonlinear model (DLNM) (quadratic B-spline + lag response spline).
  • Lag structure: cumulative effects of extreme rainfall captured over 14 lag days using a natural cubic B-spline lag response (4 df, 2 internal knots); temporal autocorrelation checked via partial autocorrelation function.
  • Meta-regression pooling (Stage 2): univariate random-effects meta-regression with city-level predictors (annual average temperature, annual precipitation, GDP per capita, population size) to pool city-specific estimates and explain between-city heterogeneity (Cochran Q, I²).
  • Absolute risk quantification: risk difference (RD) calculated as Pu × (RR − 1), where Pu ≈ 1 (background non-extreme day rate); excess deaths per 10,000 estimated via Supplementary Methods.
  • Subgroup and sensitivity analyses: stratification by age (<65 / ≥65), time period (1993–2001 vs. 2002–2010 for Japan/South Korea), and rainfall intensity tier; six additional sensitivity checks including alternative precipitation databases, altered DLNM parameters, varied lag windows, flood/cyclone exclusion, and ENSO covariate addition.

① Embed fine-grained urban morphology into the IDF threshold to explain coastal-vs-inland heterogeneity

Extending this paperThe study fits a single IDF curve per city centroid from ERA5-Land at 0.1° resolution, then treats threshold exceedance as a binary city-level event. Yet Figure 1 clearly shows that coastal cities carry systematically higher thresholds — and within large East Asian metropolises, impervious surface fraction, building density, and drainage infrastructure vary at sub-kilometer scales that a point extraction misses. A direct extension: replace the single centroid with a spatially distributed IDF surface built from merged station, ERA5-Land, and IMERG pixels, weighted by urban morphology (impervious fraction, street canyon depth). This would both sharpen the exposure definition and reveal whether within-city spatial gradients — not just cross-city heterogeneity — drive mortality clustering in urban peripheries versus dense cores.

Added value to my researchThis feeds directly into my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation work: rather than using ERA5-Land point extraction, I could construct a fine-resolution precipitation hazard surface fused from multi-source satellite data and serve it as a covariate layer in any GeoAI mortality-prediction model — a reusable data-fusion pipeline for climate-health spatiotemporal analysis.

② Predict city-specific excess mortality before an event using explainable ML on climate-socioeconomic predictors

Extending this paperThe meta-regression stage of this paper already shows that GDP per capita, average precipitation, and population size explain a meaningful portion of between-city heterogeneity in RRs. However, a linear meta-regression cannot capture non-linear interactions among these predictors, nor can it be deployed prospectively. A natural extension: train an explainable ML ensemble (XGBoost + SHAP) on the 34 city-period observations with city-level predictors (socioeconomic, climatological, demographic, urban morphological) to predict city-specific excess respiratory deaths under a forecast extreme rainfall event — turning the paper's retrospective epidemiology into a near-real-time early-warning system.

Added value to my researchMy current MGWR/GWR work models spatial non-stationarity in a single cross-section; pairing SHAP with the DLNM RR estimates from this paper would let me decompose which spatial-structural features (GDP, urbanization rate, green coverage) are the strongest modifiers of rainfall–mortality associations — a spatial-heterogeneity attribution pipeline directly applicable to my health-geography agenda.

③ Map healthcare accessibility collapse under extreme rainfall to quantify compounded mortality risk

Extending this paperThe paper estimates the direct physiological pathway from extreme rainfall to respiratory mortality, but a critical indirect pathway is omitted: extreme rainfall disrupts infrastructure and transport, reducing effective access to emergency respiratory care precisely when demand spikes. Extending the paper: integrate a time-varying accessibility model that degrades travel times to hospitals on extreme rainfall days (using road inundation probability derived from the flood-observatory data already in the paper) and ask whether cities with lower baseline healthcare accessibility suffer significantly higher excess mortality — a mediation analysis linking the paper's exposure to a spatial access mechanism.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my 2SFCA/R2SFCA framework: run a standard R2SFCA on regular days, then re-run it with rainfall-adjusted travel times and quantify the access-deficit induced by extreme events. The delta serves as a spatial vulnerability index that planners can overlay with the paper's excess-death maps to prioritize hospital siting under climate stress.

④ Quantify the unequal distribution of respiratory mortality risk by socioeconomic strata within East Asian cities

Extending this paperThe paper pools mortality across entire city populations and tests heterogeneity only by age group and time period — not by within-city socioeconomic status. Yet the Discussion acknowledges that adaptation capacity differs by socioeconomic setting. A direct extension: link the city-level DLNM RR estimates to within-city census grid data (income, housing quality, green space) to test whether the 1.29 relative risk for 5-year events is itself heterogeneous across neighborhood-level vulnerability quintiles — and whether urban greenery or healthcare proximity moderates the association, turning the paper's single-city-one-number RR into a spatially disaggregated equity map.

Added value to my researchThis mirrors my health-inequality work on telehealth and mental-health access divides: apply spatial entropy and the Gini of DLNM RRs across neighborhoods to produce a climate-health equity index for East Asian cities — a new spatiotemporal metric that feeds both my urban-analytics and health-geography streams.

⑤ Build a physics-informed graph model that couples the IDF precipitation network to a city-level disease diffusion layer

Extending this paperThe paper's two-stage framework (city-level DLNM + meta-regression) treats each city as an independent node and pools effects linearly. It cannot capture how synchronized extreme rainfall across a cluster of spatially proximate cities simultaneously stresses regional healthcare and supply chains — a systemic risk the ecological design misses. A next-generation extension: construct a city-adjacency graph where edge weights reflect precipitation teleconnection (ENSO-driven, already used in the paper) and node attributes carry socioeconomic and respiratory-burden features, then learn a graph neural network that predicts multi-city excess mortality jointly — encoding the paper's return-period exposure as a physics constraint on the graph signal.

Added value to my researchThis is the concrete application of my physics-informed graph learning agenda to climate-health: the IDF return-period exceedance probability functions as the physical prior baked into the GNN loss, while city-network structure (from my urban-agglomeration research) provides the topology. The result would be a transferable graph-based climate-mortality early-warning model — reusable across any monsoon-affected city network worldwide.
