Mapping changes in housing in sub-Saharan Africa from 2000 to 2015

Lucy S. Tusting · Donal Bisanzio · Graham Alabaster · Ewan Cameron · Richard Cibulskis · Michael Davies · Seth Flaxman · Harry S. Gibson · Jakob Knudsen · Charles Mbogo · Fredros O. Okumu · Lorenz von Seidlein · Daniel J. Weiss · Steve W. Lindsay · Peter W. Gething · Samir Bhatt Published 2019-03-27 DOI 10.1038/s41586-019-1050-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The prevalence of improved housing (with clean water, sanitation, sufficient space, and durable construction) across sub-Saharan Africa doubled from 11% to 23% between 2000 and 2015, representing 134 million people gaining access to better conditions. Yet in 2015, 53 million urban Africans (47% of the urban population studied) still lived in unimproved housing, driven above all by inadequate sanitation. High-resolution geostatistical maps at 5 × 5 km2 resolution across 31 countries now provide the first standardized, subnational baseline for tracking SDG 11 progress.

★

Why It Matters

  • First subnational, continent-wide baseline. Prior UN estimates of slum housing were confined to urban areas, nation-level aggregates, and ad hoc years. This study provides the first standardized, high-resolution maps across both urban and rural sub-Saharan Africa, making change measurable at every 5 km grid cell.
  • Quantifies both progress and persistent inequity. The doubling of improved housing is genuine good news; but 595 million rural Africans (82% of the rural population) still lived in unimproved conditions in 2015 — the scale of the remaining gap is now precisely documented.
  • Links housing to wealth, education, and urbanicity. Household-level analyses show that the odds of improved housing are 80% higher in educated households and 2.5× higher in the wealthiest quartile, anchoring housing policy in socioeconomic drivers.
  • Directly actionable for SDG monitoring and targeting. The maps are freely available for visualization and download at map.ox.ac.uk, providing a ready-to-use layer for governments, NGOs, and international agencies targeting SDG 11.
  • Sanitation identified as the critical bottleneck. Unimproved sanitation was the most common housing deprivation in 75% of surveys, pointing to where infrastructure investment matters most — even as households self-invest in building materials.

Core Methods

  • Geostatistical regression via Random Fourier Features (RFF): rather than specifying a parametric kernel (e.g., Matérn), the model learns the spectral measure ω directly from data via an empirical Lebesgue measure, then constructs an explicit high-dimensional feature map z(x|ω). This approximates a nonlinear kernel function without the cubic cost of exact Gaussian processes.
  • Beta-binomial likelihood: accounts for overdispersion inherent in cluster-sampled survey data and for variable household sample sizes within each geographic cluster.
  • Dropout regularization + ADAM stochastic gradient descent on GPU: scalable fitting with automatic relevance determination (ARD) via learned length-scale parameters per covariate, enabling sparse covariate selection.
  • Weighted likelihood bootstrap: generates 100 approximate Bayesian posterior samples of the fitted model, propagated through prediction to yield full 95% confidence intervals on every map pixel.
  • Population-weighted prevalence: grid-cell estimates are aggregated to urban/rural prevalences using annual WorldPop population surfaces and the static GUF urban mask.
  • Conditional logistic regression meta-analysis: for household-level socioeconomic associations, conditional logistic regression (within geographic cluster) was applied to each survey separately, then combined via random-effects meta-analysis — reported as forest plots of pooled odds ratios.
  • Cross-validation: both pixel-level (75/25 random split, 10 repeats) and administrative-unit holdout validation, using MSE, Pearson correlation, and continuous ranked probability score (CRPS).

① Extend the 5 × 5 km maps to a fine-grained slum delineation using satellite deep learning

Extending this paperThe paper maps housing prevalence at 5 km resolution from survey clusters, but cannot pinpoint which specific neighborhoods are informal settlements or slums. A direct extension: fuse the paper's predicted prevalence surfaces with satellite-derived morphological features (building density, roof material signatures from Sentinel-2/SAR) to delineate slum boundaries at 10–30 m resolution across the same 31 countries. The corpus paper on mapping urban slums and inequality in sub-Saharan Africa provides a complementary geospatial-plus-survey integration that could supply fine-scale labels to train such a model on top of the 2000–2015 trend surfaces.

Added value to my researchThis directly addresses my interest in multi-source data fusion (remote sensing + mobile phone + social media) and fine-tuning geospatial foundation models. Leveraging pre-trained building-segmentation foundation models on Sentinel imagery, and then adapting them with the DHS survey labels used here, exemplifies the FAIR/open-science data fusion pipeline I work on. The output — building-level informality scores — would enrich spatial accessibility and housing-price models with a granular deprivation layer.

② Replace the fixed spatial kernel with a spatially varying kernel to capture urban-rural non-stationarity

Extending this paperThe paper's random Fourier feature model uses a single global spectral measure ω fitted to all 31 countries simultaneously. But the relationship between covariates (aridity, night-time lights, urbanicity) and housing quality is almost certainly non-stationary: the same covariate shift means something different in dense coastal cities than in semi-arid rural hinterlands. A natural extension is to replace the global kernel with a spatially varying, locally adapted kernel — akin to geographically weighted regression (GWR) but within the RFF framework — so that covariate effects are allowed to vary spatially. The paper's own finding that a 10% increase in urban-cluster prevalence is associated with a 7.5% improvement in housing quality hints at this heterogeneity.

Added value to my researchThis is the bridge between the paper's geostatistical model and my MGWR/GWR methods toolkit. Implementing spatially adaptive length scales within the RFF kernel gives a scalable, GPU-friendly version of MGWR for count/proportion outcomes — directly applicable to my spatial accessibility prediction tasks where service-area effects vary by urban density zone.

Cross-ref (corpus): Underlying rules of evolutionary urban systems in Africa — 10.1038/s44284-025-00208-y

③ Link the housing maps to health outcomes to quantify malaria and sanitation disease burden at subnational scale

Extending this paperThe paper mentions that house design affects malaria, diarrhoea, respiratory disease, and helminths (SDG 3 links), but does not itself model these outcomes. A direct extension would spatially join the paper's annual 5 km housing-quality grids to malaria parasite-rate maps (e.g., the Malaria Atlas Project's Plasmodium falciparum maps produced by the same Oxford MAP group) and estimate the housing-attributable disease burden at subnational level for each year 2000–2015. The infrastructure deficit paper on sub-Saharan Africa provides an updated, complementary poverty-and-services layer to control for confounding.

Added value to my researchThis extends my health geography work from spatial accessibility to spatial exposure: housing condition functions as an environmental health determinant whose subnational variation can be quantified with the same geostatistical tools I apply to healthcare-facility catchments. The combined exposure-access framework (poor housing + poor healthcare reach = compound vulnerability) is a novel angle for my public health policy analyses, and the 15-year temporal panel enables difference-in-differences estimation of housing-improvement interventions.

④ Forecast housing conditions to 2030 and 2050 using shared socioeconomic pathways and urban growth models

Extending this paperThe paper provides a 2000–2015 baseline and notes that Africa's population will double by 2050, yet makes no quantitative projection. A forward extension would couple the paper's geostatistical model with Shared Socioeconomic Pathway (SSP) scenarios for economic growth, urbanization rates, and population — already encoded in gridded datasets — to project housing-quality prevalence to 2030 (SDG deadline) and 2050. The GUF urban-growth layers and WorldPop projections used in the paper already have SSP-consistent future variants. This would turn the baseline map into a planning-scenario tool: how much does urban infrastructure investment need to accelerate to meet SDG 11 under different growth trajectories?

Added value to my researchScenario-based spatial projection is central to my urban analytics agenda. Applying my spatiotemporal kernel density and agent-based modeling tools to this forecasting problem would let me model how informal settlement expansion interacts with housing improvement rates at fine spatial resolution — informing where public facility placement (clinics, schools, sanitation hubs) should be pre-positioned to serve a growing population before housing conditions deteriorate further.

Cross-ref (corpus): Disproportionate flood exposure for slum populations of the Global South — 10.1038/s44284-025-00273-3

⑤ Incorporate electricity and energy-access layers to build a multi-dimensional housing deprivation index

Extending this paperThe paper's definition of "improved housing" covers water, sanitation, crowding, and materials — but deliberately excludes tenure security (due to data gaps) and omits electricity access entirely. Yet electricity is increasingly recognized as a co-determinant of housing adequacy and health, especially in African cities. A direct extension would augment the paper's four-component index with a fifth dimension — grid electricity access — using the nighttime-light covariate already in the model, plus newly available satellite-derived electrification layers, to produce a five-dimensional deprivation index mapped at the same 5 km resolution. This richer index would better distinguish "improved" from truly "adequate" housing in the SDG 11 sense.

Added value to my researchThis speaks to my work on digital divides and inequitable access to infrastructure: electricity access underpins not just housing quality but also telehealth reach and digital-health equity. A multi-dimensional deprivation index combining housing quality and electricity access would serve as a precision targeting layer for my 2SFCA-based healthcare accessibility analyses, flagging areas where both physical and digital barriers compound the health-access deficit. The GeoAI angle: training an explainable ML model to predict the full index from satellite observables only would enable real-time monitoring without survey dependence.
