Exposures and behavioural responses to wildfire smoke

Marshall Burke · Sam Heft-Neal · Jessica Li · Anne Driscoll · Patrick Baylis · Matthieu Stigler · Joakim A. Weill · Jennifer A. Burney · Jeff Wen · Marissa L. Childs · Carlos F. Gould Published 2022-07-07 DOI 10.1038/s41562-022-01396-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

During major wildfire smoke events, wealthier neighbourhoods are more likely to search for health protection, stay indoors, and exhibit emotional responses to smoke, while lower-income residents show similar awareness but more limited behavioural adaptation. Indoor PM2.5 concentrations often remain 3–4× above health guidelines and vary 20× across neighbouring households, revealing that self-protection as a policy strategy will have modest and deeply unequal benefits.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reveals the hidden inequity of smoke self-protection policies. Governments rely on citizens to protect themselves from wildfire smoke, but this paper is the first to empirically demonstrate that this strategy amplifies, rather than levels, socioeconomic disparities in smoke exposure and health risk.
  • Integrates five novel data streams at scale. By combining private PM2.5 sensors, GPS-based mobility, Twitter sentiment, Google Trends, and Yelp reviews across the US, the study creates a multi-dimensional view of real-time behavioural response that no single data source could provide.
  • Quantifies indoor infiltration heterogeneity. The 20× variation in indoor PM2.5 among neighbouring households during smoke events exposes the building-level drivers of exposure inequality — housing quality, ventilation, air conditioning — that aggregate county-level monitoring misses entirely.
  • Motivates a rethink of air quality monitoring equity. Higher-income census tracts are disproportionately covered by PurpleAir sensors, meaning exposure data itself is biased — monitoring inequity compounds behavioural inequity.

Core Methods

  • Smoke PM2.5 estimation: daily county-level smoke PM2.5 derived from EPA ground monitors, isolating fire-attributable pollution following established decomposition methods.
  • Panel regression with fixed effects: smoke PM2.5 regressed against behavioural outcomes (mobility, search volume, sentiment) using spline fits conditioned on date and state-by-date fixed effects to control for confounders; bootstrapped 95% confidence intervals.
  • Income heterogeneity analysis: marginal effects of smoke exposure estimated as a function of median county or tract household income, revealing the socioeconomic gradient in each behavioural response.
  • Household infiltration modelling: household-specific infiltration rates (δ Indoor PM2.5 / δ Outdoor PM2.5) estimated from monitor-specific time-series regressions with multiple lag structures; pooled via a Bayesian hierarchical model to separate true household heterogeneity from sampling noise.
  • Machine learning predictors of infiltration: random forest and gradient boosted trees used to explain variation in infiltration across monitors using housing characteristics (CoreLogic), A/C prevalence, income, and meteorological controls (HDD/CDD).
  • Sentiment analysis: Twitter text sentiment during smoke events, following the Baylis (2020) approach for temperature–sentiment models, adapted to smoke PM2.5.

① Map the protective-behaviour gap to fine-grained neighbourhood attributes via explainable ML

Extending this paperThis paper uses median county income as its primary heterogeneity axis, revealing a gap in protective behaviour but not explaining what drives it beyond income. A direct extension would incorporate additional neighbourhood-level predictors — housing age, A/C prevalence, greenspace, social capital, language barriers — and use SHAP-attributed gradient boosting to decompose which attributes explain protective behaviour beyond income. This would convert the paper's reduced-form income gradient into a causal feature map that planners can act on at the neighbourhood scale.

② Replace static county-level exposure with GPS-trajectory-based personal exposure estimates

Extending this paperThe paper assigns smoke PM2.5 to individuals based on county of residence — a spatially blunt instrument that ignores intra-day mobility. Someone who commutes from a low-income inner-city neighbourhood to a wealthier suburban workplace (or vice versa) has an actual exposure profile that the county-level assignment systematically misclassifies. Extending the paper with GPS-trajectory-weighted personal exposure — integrating outdoor PM2.5 fields along each device's path — would sharpen both the income gradient and the infiltration model by anchoring indoor/outdoor transitions to real movement.

Added value to my researchThis is a natural application of my mobile-phone GPS trajectory fusion work: computing person-specific environmental exposure from spatiotemporal trajectories is the same problem as computing person-specific healthcare access, but applied to air pollution. The R2SFCA concept (demand-weighted catchment) translates here as an exposure-weighted integration along the trajectory.

③ Extend the indoor infiltration model to a global scale using satellite-derived building attributes

Extending this paperThe paper's finding that indoor PM2.5 varies 20× between neighbouring households is locked to the San Francisco Bay Area PurpleAir network. The infiltration drivers identified — housing age, A/C, building materials — are, in principle, estimable from satellite-derived building footprints, height, and surface materials (Sentinel-2, Google Building Footprints). Extending the paper's Bayesian infiltration model to a building-attribute-based surrogate would allow global indoor exposure prediction without in-situ sensors, covering the low-income countries and regions entirely absent from PurpleAir networks.

The predicted infiltration map becomes a public-health accessibility layer — who can actually achieve safe indoor PM2.5 — extending my healthcare accessibility frameworks to environmental health.

④ Quantify the cost-effectiveness of targeted air purifier distribution as an equity intervention

Extending this paperThis paper shows that indoor PM2.5 concentrations often remain 3–4× above guidelines and that lower-income households have less capacity to protect themselves. A direct policy extension is to use the paper's infiltration model to identify which households would benefit most from subsidised HEPA air purifiers, and then apply an efficiency–equity optimization (facility-location framing: which neighbourhoods to target given a fixed budget) that maximises PM2.5 reduction per dollar while prioritising high-infiltration, low-income tracts. The companion corpus paper on indoor air purification costs already provides global health-benefit estimates to anchor the cost side.

⑤ Build a real-time smoke-event early-warning dashboard combining sensor fusion with social signal detection

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that Google Trends and Twitter signal smoke awareness within days of a fire event — before EPA monitors catch up in sparsely covered areas.
