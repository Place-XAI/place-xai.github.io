Deep learning training dynamics analysis for single-cell data

Jonathan Karin · Reshef Mintz · Mor Nitzan (The Hebrew University of Jerusalem) Published 2024-12-04 DOI 10.1038/s43588-024-00728-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The Annotatability framework repurposes deep neural network training dynamics — the temporal trajectory of per-cell prediction confidence and variability across epochs — to diagnose pre-annotated single-cell and spatial omics data. It reliably distinguishes correctly annotated, erroneously annotated, and ambiguous cells, corrects false labels, infers continuous cellular trajectories from binary annotations, and uncovers disease-associated subpopulations, all without requiring new experimental data.

Why It Matters

  • Annotation quality is the silent bottleneck of single-cell biology. Millions of cells in public atlases carry noisy, discrete labels assigned from sparse high-dimensional data; Annotatability provides a principled, model-agnostic audit of those labels without re-running experiments.
  • Repurposes an NLP/CV concept for biology. Dataset cartography (Swayamdipta et al., EMNLP 2020) was designed to diagnose text datasets; this paper is the first to transfer the principle into the omics domain, demonstrating cross-domain portability of training-dynamics reasoning.
  • Continuous biology from discrete labels. By observing how quickly a DNN learns a binary label, the method infers graded positions along biological processes (e.g., epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition) — recovering continuous structure that conventional discrete annotation discards.
  • Multi-setting utility demonstrated across 8 datasets. Evaluation spans scRNA-seq cell-type annotation, spatial transcriptomics, disease subpopulation discovery (type II diabetes), and treatment efficacy assessment — suggesting broad applicability across study designs.

Core Methods

  • Training dynamics analysis: train a DNN classifier on pre-annotated single-cell data and track per-cell confidence (mean predicted probability for the input annotation across epochs) and variability (standard deviation of predicted probability across epochs).
  • Three-class cell categorization: classify each cell as easy-to-learn (high confidence, low variability — correctly annotated), hard-to-learn (low confidence, low variability — erroneously annotated), or ambiguous (intermediate confidence and variability — biologically intermediate state).
  • DNN overfitting exploitation: the method leverages the observation that DNNs learn clean decision boundaries in early epochs and only begin to overfit erroneous/ambiguous labels in later epochs — the temporal ordering itself encodes data quality information.
  • Reannotation modules: dedicated post-hoc modules correct erroneous annotations using the training-dynamics signal, without requiring external reference datasets.
  • Graph embedding: incorporates annotation congruence scores into a graph-based embedding for downstream biological signal extraction, trajectory inference, and disease-gene identification.
  • Binary-label trajectory inference: by ordering cells along the confidence/variability gradient derived from binary (e.g., epithelial vs. mesenchymal) labels, the method recovers graded continuous positions along a biological process.

① Apply training-dynamics auditing to geospatial label quality in remote-sensing and POI datasets

Extending this paperAnnotatability's core logic — that DNN training dynamics reveal which examples are correctly labeled, erroneously labeled, or ambiguous — is not intrinsically biological. The paper trains DNNs on cell-type annotations and reads quality from the confidence/variability trajectory. A direct extension would apply the same framework to spatial omics with irregular geometries (e.g., low-resolution spatial transcriptomics where each spot contains multiple cell types), which the authors themselves identify as a future direction. Connecting this to corpus work on multi-modal cell-state representation would allow the confidence/variability map to be computed over multi-omic embeddings rather than single-modality inputs, improving sensitivity for ambiguous spots. The framework could also be extended by pre-training a foundation model on large cell atlases and then running training-dynamics analysis on fine-tuned task-specific layers, linking the ambiguity signal to learned representations rather than raw features.

Added value to my researchMy GeoAI work relies on crowdsourced labels (OpenStreetMap POIs, social-media tags, land-use classifications) that are known to be noisy and spatially heterogeneous in quality. Annotatability's training-dynamics principle translates directly: train a spatial classifier (e.g., a GNN predicting land use or facility type) on OSM-labeled data, then compute per-location confidence and variability scores across epochs to flag annotation-uncertain zones. This would give me a principled label-quality map for any spatially labeled dataset — equivalent to a geospatial "data cartography" — without requiring independent ground truth. It is directly applicable to pre-training or fine-tuning geospatial foundation models where label noise is the main quality bottleneck.

③ Use training-dynamics confidence as a pre-filtering layer for single-cell foundation model fine-tuning

Extending this paperFoundation models for single-cell data (e.g., cell atlas models trained on hundreds of millions of cells) are increasingly fine-tuned on smaller annotated datasets for specific tasks. Annotatability's hard-to-learn and ambiguous scores provide a principled curriculum signal that can guide fine-tuning: exclude hard-to-learn cells (likely mislabeled) from early fine-tuning epochs, up-weight easy-to-learn cells, and treat ambiguous cells as soft-label training examples. This extends Annotatability from a diagnostic tool into an active ingredient of the training pipeline for large-scale biological models, connecting to corpus work on Geneformer scaling and foundation models for cell biology. The paper's reannotation modules could feed directly into foundation model pre-training data curation, creating a quality-controlled training corpus at atlas scale.

④ Extend Annotatability to multi-modal data to identify modality-specific vs. shared annotation errors

Extending this paperAnnotatability currently trains a single DNN on one omics modality at a time. In multi-modal single-cell experiments (e.g., simultaneous gene expression + chromatin accessibility + protein), the same cell receives a single annotation, but different modalities may agree or disagree about which class the cell belongs to. An extension would train modality-specific DNNs and compare their confidence/variability trajectories for each cell, categorizing annotation errors as either modality-universal (all modalities agree the label is wrong) or modality-specific (only one modality flags the label as ambiguous). This multi-modal Annotatability would reveal whether annotation errors stem from one assay's noise or from genuine biological heterogeneity expressed through a single modality. The partially shared multi-modal embedding approach in the corpus is a natural integration point for the cross-modal confidence comparison.

Added value to my researchMy multi-source data fusion work regularly integrates mobile phone records, social media data, remote sensing, and POI data to analyze urban phenomena. These sources often disagree about a location's classification (e.g., a venue classified differently in OSM vs. social-media activity patterns vs. census records). The modality-specific training-dynamics comparison would provide a structured conflict-detection framework: compute confidence/variability per data source (modality) for each spatial unit, then flag locations where sources disagree as high-priority targets for manual validation or ensemble fusion. This upgrades my data fusion pipeline from simple majority-vote or weighted averaging to a principled annotation-conflict diagnostics step.

⑤ Transfer the hard-to-learn / ambiguous taxonomy to disease subtype discovery in epidemiological cohorts

Extending this paperAnnotatability demonstrated that hard-to-learn cells in a "disease vs. healthy" classification correspond to clinically meaningful subpopulations (insulin-treated mice with healthy-like β-cell markers). The paper focuses on single-cell data, but the principle extends naturally to patient-level cohort data: train a DNN to predict disease status or treatment outcome from multi-variable clinical or omics profiles, then use training dynamics to identify patients who are hard to classify — likely representing disease subtypes, treatment non-responders, or transitional states. Connecting this to perturbation prediction models from the corpus would create a pipeline where hard-to-learn patients are prioritized for deeper perturbation analysis, identifying which molecular interventions might shift them toward the "easy" regime.
