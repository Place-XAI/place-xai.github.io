Prediction of water stability of metal–organic frameworks using machine learning

Rohit Batra · Carmen Chen · Tania G. Evans · Krista S. Walton · Rampi Ramprasad Published 2020-11-09 DOI 10.1038/s42256-020-00249-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A machine learning classifier — trained on 207 empirically measured MOF water stabilities (the Burtch dataset) — can instantly predict whether a metal–organic framework is water-stable without costly synthesis, achieving 83% weighted accuracy for a two-class model (Random Forest) and 71% for a three-class model (SVM). Chemical features derived solely from the MOF formula unit (metal-node properties, organic-ligand fingerprints, and molar ratios) are sufficient to screen candidates and to extract interpretable stability rules: large metal atomic radius (>1.4 Å), low ionization potential (<7.5 eV), and ligands with few six-member rings strongly associate with water stability. The workflow screened 88 new MOFs and is iteratively improvable as experimental data grow.

Validation set
10 MOFs synthesized and water-stability assessed after 2014 (post-Burtch); withheld entirely from model training for unbiased evaluation.
Screening set
88 new MOFs collected from the literature for adsorption, separation, or catalytic capabilities; water stability previously unreported. Screened by the trained models.
MOF feature set

Reduced via RFE to 37 features (two-class) and 29 features (three-class).

Core Methods

  • Chemical fingerprinting from formula unit: each MOF is represented as a vector of metal-node chemical properties, hierarchical organic-ligand descriptors (atomic triplets, RDKit QSPR, morphological features), and molar-ratio features — requiring only the activated formula unit, not crystal structure.
  • Recursive Feature Elimination (RFE) with linear SVM: a fivefold cross-validated linear-kernel SVM iteratively prunes the 149-dimensional fingerprint, yielding 37 features for the two-class model and 29 for the three-class model; RFE improved test accuracy by 3–7 percentage points.
  • Classification algorithms — SVM, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting: all three benchmarked; Random Forest (RF) selected for the two-class model (83% weighted accuracy); SVM with RBF kernel selected for the three-class model (71% weighted accuracy). Hyperparameters optimized by fivefold CV.
  • Class-imbalance handling: class-weighted accuracy used as training objective, penalizing misclassification of minority classes (LK, U) to prevent the model from defaulting to the dominant HK class (n=118 of 207).
  • Learning-curve and confusion-matrix evaluation: train/test sizes varied across 10 random splits; confusion matrices confirm that misclassifications occur primarily between neighboring stability classes (not S vs. U), consistent with physical expectations.
  • Feature-importance-driven chemical insight extraction: MDI (mean decrease in impurity) from the RF two-class model identifies the most predictive descriptors; threshold-based rules are then verified on the Burtch dataset (e.g., 95% of MOFs with metal radius >1.4 Å and low MQNS36 are stable).

① Replace formula-unit fingerprints with molecular graph embeddings to capture MOF topology

Extending this paperThe paper's featurization uses weighted-average descriptors of individual metal nodes and ligands, deliberately ignoring crystal topology (pore geometry, connectivity) to remain applicable to any MOF with a known formula unit. A direct extension: replace or augment these tabular descriptors with molecular graph embeddings that represent each MOF as a graph of metal-cluster nodes connected by linker edges — trained via contrastive learning on large unlabeled MOF databases (e.g., Cambridge Structural Database) and then fine-tuned on the Burtch dataset's 207 stability labels. This would let the model distinguish polymorphs sharing the same formula unit, an explicit limitation the paper flags, and the pre-training on abundant unlabeled MOFs addresses the small-data challenge head-on.

Added value to my researchThe contrastive graph pre-training strategy — learning representations from abundant unlabeled structural data before fine-tuning on scarce labeled outcomes — is directly transferable to my spatiotemporal prediction work. Just as the MOF graph encodes crystal topology, spatial graphs encode road-network or community-boundary topology. Pre-training GNNs on large unlabeled mobility or POI graphs, then fine-tuning on sparse health or accessibility labels, mirrors this MOF extension and strengthens my physics-informed graph-learning agenda.

② Adopt an active learning loop to expand the training dataset at maximum information gain

Extending this paperThe paper's workflow is passive screening: train once on 207 fixed labels, then rank 88 unlabeled candidates. The obvious next step is an active learning loop: in each cycle, the current model flags the candidates whose experimental synthesis would most reduce predictive uncertainty (e.g., highest predicted-probability entropy or largest expected model change). These are synthesized first; new stability labels are added; and the model is retrained. This is especially impactful for the Burtch dataset's severe class imbalance — targeted acquisition of Unstable (n=22) examples would improve the model's weakest class much faster than passive data collection.

Added value to my researchActive learning for small labeled datasets is equally valuable in geospatial surveys where ground-truth measurements — real travel times to clinics, physical capacity audits of facilities — are expensive to collect. An active learner that selects which spatial units to field-verify next, based on where the accessibility model is most uncertain, could reduce survey costs while most rapidly improving R2SFCA catchment delineations. The MOF active-learning case is a crisp methodological analogue.

③ Benchmark MOF stability ML against community evaluation frameworks to establish reproducibility standards

Extending this paperThe paper validates on 10 post-2014 MOFs — an important but small held-out set that cannot characterize all generalization failure modes. A direct extension: subject the Burtch-trained models to community benchmark protocols for ML materials-stability prediction — time-aware splits (train on pre-2010 syntheses, test on post-2010), chemically clustered splits (train on seen metal families, test on held-out metal families), and calibrated uncertainty quantification. These protocols are now formalized for crystal stability prediction and are immediately applicable to MOF water stability, providing a richer characterization of where the paper's 83% accuracy holds and where it degrades.

Added value to my researchThis reproducibility framing maps precisely to my interest in rigorous GeoAI evaluation. Just as the materials community needs chemically clustered generalization splits, spatial ML models need spatial-fold cross-validation (hold out geographically contiguous blocks, not random points) to avoid spatial-autocorrelation leakage. Adopting benchmark-mindset practices from materials informatics would strengthen reproducibility standards in my explainable ML spatial-prediction work.

④ Encode the mined stability rules as physics-informed constraints in a generative MOF design model

Extending this paperThe paper extracts chemical stability rules post hoc from the trained RF (atomic radius thresholds, ionization potential bounds, ligand topological features). A stronger extension: encode these rules as differentiable soft constraints inside a generative model that proposes entirely new MOF metal-linker combinations — a constrained inverse design approach. Instead of first generating candidates and then filtering with the classifier, the generative model would intrinsically avoid unstable chemical subspaces, combining the paper's interpretable insights with the generative design paradigm. The 207 Burtch stability labels provide compact but principled supervision for such a constrained generator.

Added value to my researchThis physics-constraint-in-generation idea has a direct spatial analogue: empirically mined spatial laws (e.g., decay of accessibility with distance, density-scaling of service needs) can be encoded as soft regularizers in a GNN or spatial diffusion model, ensuring generated spatial configurations respect known geographic regularities. Just as the MOF generator respects chemical stability constraints, a GeoAI model for urban facility placement could be constrained to satisfy accessibility equity thresholds — a concrete application to my EV-charging or primary-care facility optimization work.

⑤ Extend classification to multi-property prediction with uncertainty-aware ensemble stacking

Extending this paperThe paper predicts water stability in isolation, yet industrial MOF deployment requires simultaneous assessment of multiple stability dimensions (thermal, chemical, mechanical). Extending it to multi-task classification — a shared featurization backbone with task-specific heads predicting water, thermal, and chemical stability jointly — would exploit cross-property correlations that may boost accuracy in each individual task, particularly for underrepresented classes. Adding uncertainty quantification via ensemble stacking or Bayesian layers would further flag predictions near decision boundaries, helping experimentalists distinguish high-confidence stable candidates from borderline cases where synthesis risk is higher.
