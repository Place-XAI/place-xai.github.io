The Human Cell Atlas from a cell census to a unified foundation model

Jennifer E. Rood · Samantha Wynne · Lucia Robson · Anna Hupalowska · John Randell · Sarah A. Teichmann · Aviv Regev Published 2024-11-20 DOI 10.1038/s41586-024-08338-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Cell atlases — most prominently the Human Cell Atlas (HCA) — are evolving through five increasingly powerful perspectives: from a cell census cataloguing diversity, to 3D spatial maps, to genotype-to-phenotype causal maps, to 4D developmental maps, and ultimately to unified foundation models of biology that integrate all modalities and scales. This trajectory, enabled by molecular and spatial profiling advances together with AI and machine learning, is poised to transform medicine by providing cell-level understanding of disease, development, and perturbation response across the entire human body.

Core Methods

  • Single-cell transcriptomics (scRNA-seq): core profiling technology generating gene-expression vectors for individual cells, enabling unbiased cell-type discovery and state characterization across organs and conditions.
  • Spatial transcriptomics: platforms (MERFISH, Visium, DBiT-seq, STARmap) add spatial coordinates to expression profiles, enabling 3D tissue architecture reconstruction and cell-neighborhood interaction analysis.
  • Multi-modal integration: simultaneous profiling of RNA, chromatin accessibility (ATAC-seq), and protein (CITE-seq) within the same cells, mapped to a unified reference frame across modalities and scales.
  • Atlas integration and batch correction: harmonizing datasets across studies, platforms, and donors (scVI, Seurat, Symphony) — a prerequisite for foundation-model pretraining; benchmarking approaches are cited from Luecken et al. (Nat. Methods 2022).
  • Genotype-phenotype causal mapping: linking cell-type-specific eQTLs and GWAS loci to disease-critical cell states, combining population genetics with single-cell profiles.
  • Foundation model pretraining: transformer-based models pretrained on tens-to-hundreds of millions of single-cell gene-expression profiles from CZ CELLxGENE — learning a universal latent representation of cell state for downstream tasks including cell-type annotation, perturbation prediction, and disease classification.
  • RNA velocity and trajectory inference: inferring developmental pseudotime and fate decisions from splicing dynamics (scVelo, CellRank) for 4D developmental atlas construction.

① Transfer the HCA foundation model's pretraining paradigm to geospatial data

Extending this paper The paper's fifth perspective — a biology foundation model pretrained on hundreds of millions of cells — rests on the insight that massive unlabeled data from a single domain can yield a transferable representation fine-tunable to diverse downstream tasks. A direct extension: systematically benchmark whether the same "pretrain on a cell census, fine-tune on a phenotype" pipeline transfers across biological domains (plant atlases, microbiome atlases, organoid atlases) using multi-task pretraining. The paper asserts this extensibility but does not empirically test cross-domain transfer; multi-task learning strategies from the corpus would fill this gap.

④ Apply atlas multi-modal integration to multi-source urban data fusion

Extending this paper The paper's 3D-map perspective describes integrating RNA, chromatin, protein, and spatial coordinates from the same or neighboring cells via multi-modal assays and joint embeddings. A direct extension the paper itself flags: moving from destructive tissue sections toward non-destructive in vivo spatial atlases (Raman microscopy-derived spatial transcriptomics, MRI-derived histology via SCHAF). Systematically benchmarking how many modalities are needed before the joint embedding saturates — a "modality ablation" experiment — would sharpen the case for full multi-modal atlases versus cheaper partial-modal alternatives.

⑤ Use the HCA foundation model's reliability challenges as a design checklist for GeoAI foundation models

Extending this paper While the paper is optimistic about biology foundation models, the broader corpus reveals that foundation models in healthcare violate core reliability principles — distribution shift between pretraining and deployment data, poor confidence calibration, and catastrophic forgetting during fine-tuning. A direct extension of the paper: a systematic reliability audit of HCA-derived foundation models (Geneformer, scGPT, the cell atlas search model) along axes of robustness, calibration, and fairness across tissue types and rare-cell populations — producing an actionable safety checklist before clinical deployment of biology foundation models.

Added value to my research The same reliability concerns apply to geospatial foundation models fine-tuned for urban health: a model pretrained on US cities may be poorly calibrated when applied to global cities with different spatial structures. This maps onto my explainable ML for spatial prediction agenda — integrating SHAP-based explanations with calibration diagnostics to audit where a GeoAI foundation model's predictions should not be trusted, and using spatially varying reliability scores (analogous to MGWR local R²) as deployment guardrails for health geography applications.
