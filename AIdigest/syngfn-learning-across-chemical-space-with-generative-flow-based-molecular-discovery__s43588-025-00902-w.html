SynGFN: learning across chemical space with generative flow-based molecular discovery

Yuchen Zhu · Shuwang Li · Jihong Chen · Donghai Zhao · Xiaorui Wang · Yitong Li · Yifei Liu · Yue Kong · Beichen Zhang · Chang Liu · Tingjun Hou · Chang-Yu Hsieh Published 2025-11-13 DOI 10.1038/s43588-025-00902-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

SynGFN bridges the design–make gap in AI-driven drug discovery by treating molecular generation as a cascade of simulated chemical reactions over synthesizable building blocks. Its hierarchically pretrained GFlowNet policy explores a chemical space up to an order of magnitude larger than competing synthesis-aware models (as measured by #Circles coverage), while simultaneously producing molecules that are diverse, synthesizable, and high-performing. In a real-world wet-lab validation against a neuropsychiatric target (GluN1/GluN3A), ten SynGFN-proposed compounds were synthesized within 30 days and six showed measurable inhibitory activity — demonstrating that AI-generated synthetic routes can be directly executed in the laboratory.

Why It Matters

  • Closes the "synthesizability dilemma." Most generative models optimize for predicted activity but produce molecules that cannot actually be made. SynGFN integrates synthesis planning directly into generation, yielding molecules with explicit, lab-executable synthetic routes.
  • Order-of-magnitude larger chemical space coverage. Using the topology-inspired #Circles metric, SynGFN-XL achieves up to 70× greater chemical-space coverage than the top-1,000 subset of baseline models — a qualitative leap rather than incremental improvement.
  • Efficient exploration with multifidelity acquisition. By combining a GFlowNet sampler with multifidelity Bayesian optimization, SynGFN matches or exceeds the diversity of brute-force virtual screening of ~1 million compounds while evaluating only ~100,000 — roughly a 10× reduction in scoring cost.
  • Accelerated DMTA cycle in practice. The wet-lab case study on GluN1/GluN3A inhibitors doubled synthesis throughput: traditional teams produce 2–3 compounds per synthesizer per month; SynGFN's synthesizable-route output enabled 10 compounds in 30 days, with 6 showing biological activity.
  • Scalable to industrial-size libraries. The XL reactant library contains ~100,000 Enamine building blocks; the hierarchical pretraining strategy solves the high-dimensional action-space problem that stymies simpler GFlowNet formulations at that scale.

Core Methods

  • GFlowNet with synthesis-aware MDP: molecule generation is formulated as a Markov decision process on a directed acyclic graph; the GFlowNet policy samples molecules with probability proportional to their reward — inherently producing diverse outputs unlike greedy or RL-argmax approaches.
  • Hierarchical dual-agent policy network: two separate neural networks handle action-1 (reaction-rule selection, conditioned on current molecular state via Morgan fingerprints) and action-2 (reactant selection, conditioned on state + chosen reaction). Reaction-compatible masking removes invalid actions before sampling.
  • Multilabel classification pretraining: policy model 2 is pretrained via a multilabel task to recognize template-compatible reactants before GFlowNet training begins — critical for the high-dimensional XL action space (~100,000 reactants).
  • Trajectory balance (TB) training objective: the loss ensures the partition function Z times forward-path probability matches reward times backward probability; backward probabilities collapse to 1 due to the unique parent structure of synthesis trees.
  • #Circles coverage metric: topology-inspired packing-number metric (Tanimoto distance) measuring the maximum number of non-overlapping chemical-space "circles" — robust to naive diversity hacking and used for both evaluation and diversity-adjusted scoring.
  • Multifidelity Bayesian optimization: SynGFN acts as the sampler within a multifidelity acquisition loop, using cheap QSAR proxy models for most evaluations and expensive docking scores sparingly — balancing exploration, exploitation, and compute cost.
  • Virtual screening comparison: for the S-scale space (~300M compounds), full enumeration followed by random 1M sampling and top-1% selection provides a reference; SynGFN matches or exceeds coverage using only 100,000 evaluations.
  • Wet-lab validation: ROCS 3D shape alignment (OMEGA conformers + OEOverlay Tanimoto Combo Score) guides generation toward known active scaffolds; 10 top candidates synthesized and assayed for GluN1/GluN3A inhibitory activity.

① Transfer SynGFN's hierarchical GFlowNet policy to spatial facility-placement optimization

Extending this paperSynGFN's core innovation is converting a combinatorial synthesis problem into a sequential, reward-proportional sampling task that achieves superior coverage over greedy enumeration. The same MDP formulation — dual agents selecting an action type first (reaction rule) then a component (reactant) — maps directly onto facility-location problems where an agent first selects a service category (e.g., healthcare, transit, retail) and then selects a spatial unit for placement. Extending SynGFN beyond molecular space: replace the reaction-compatibility mask with a spatial-feasibility mask (zoning, capacity, equity constraints), replace Morgan fingerprints with spatiotemporal embeddings, and replace QSAR rewards with accessibility or equity scores. The hierarchical pretraining trick (multilabel classification identifying compatible reactants) directly translates into pretraining that identifies spatially appropriate locations per service type.

Added value to my researchThis is a concrete blueprint for upgrading my EV-charging station siting and healthcare facility-location optimization work. The GFlowNet's diversity guarantee means the optimizer would not collapse onto a single high-scoring spatial configuration but instead produce a set of Pareto-diverse siting plans, directly addressing the efficiency-vs-equity dilemma I study. The #Circles spatial-coverage analog would also serve as a new diversity-adjusted equity metric for comparing siting strategies.

③ Extend the #Circles coverage metric to evaluate spatial diversity of facility networks

Extending this paperThe #Circles metric is introduced as a topology-inspired, scale-aware, and diversity-manipulation-resistant coverage measure for chemical spaces. The paper demonstrates it outperforms standard diversity metrics (internal diversity, QED, novelty) at capturing the breadth of explored space. A direct extension: port #Circles into molecular discovery evaluation benchmarks as a standard metric alongside QED and SA score. The paper currently validates it only for Tanimoto distance on Morgan fingerprints; testing it with graph-edit distance or 3D-shape similarity would increase its scope.

Added value to my researchSpatial accessibility research lacks a rigorous analog to #Circles for measuring the geographic diversity of facility networks. I can adapt the packing-number concept to a spatial metric space (e.g., travel-time distance), defining #Circles as the maximum number of facilities whose pairwise travel times all exceed threshold t — a diversity-adjusted facility-coverage score. Applied to healthcare facility siting or EV-station placement, this produces a spatial analogue of the enrichment-factor analysis in SynGFN: does the siting algorithm achieve higher #Circles-coverage per facility added than a random or greedy baseline?

④ Replace Morgan-fingerprint state representations with graph neural network embeddings for richer reward landscapes

Extending this paperSynGFN currently represents molecular states as Morgan fingerprints (4,096-bit hashing) fed into MLPs for both policy networks. Morgan fingerprints are fixed and lose 3D/topological nuance for large, complex scaffolds — a limitation that becomes more acute as reactant libraries grow toward the XL scale. A direct extension: replace the MLP+fingerprint architecture with a message-passing GNN operating directly on the molecular graph at each synthesis step, sharing parameters between action-1 and action-2 networks. This would (a) encode structural context more expressively, (b) enable the policy to exploit substructure similarity across reactions, and (c) generalize better to new building-block libraries without re-pretraining from scratch.

Added value to my researchThis extension aligns with my physics-informed graph learning work: the synthesis tree is itself a graph where intermediate states are nodes and reaction steps are edges. Encoding this structure with a GNN that respects chemical invariances (atom permutations, bond rotations) is exactly the kind of structure-aware representation I pursue for spatial graphs. More concretely, developing GNN-based policy networks for SynGFN is methodologically parallel to developing GNN-based accessibility estimators where road-network topology replaces molecular topology.

⑤ Use SynGFN as a template for generative region design in urban planning

Extending this paperThe conceptual framework of SynGFN — assemble complex objects from a library of building blocks via a constrained sequence of composable transformations, guided by a GFlowNet to sample high-reward, diverse outputs — is domain-agnostic. Within the molecular discovery domain, an immediate extension is applying SynGFN to multi-target or multi-property co-optimization (e.g., potency + selectivity + toxicity simultaneously), which requires multi-objective GFlowNet training — a known open problem the paper does not address. Such extensions would substantially broaden applicability from single-target inhibitor design to ADMET-aware lead optimization.

Added value to my researchFor urban analytics, this suggests a generative urban region design framework: urban zones are "assembled" from land-use blocks (residential, commercial, green space, transit) through zoning "reaction rules" (adjacency constraints, density limits, accessibility targets). A GFlowNet trained to maximize a multi-objective reward (walkability, equity, carbon footprint, economic viability) would generate diverse, constraint-compliant urban configurations — a direct extension of my healthy-cities and 15-minute-neighborhood research. The diversity guarantee would surface non-obvious spatial designs that greedy planners would miss, while the synthesis-route analog (the explicit sequence of zoning changes) would make the generated plans interpretable and actionable.
