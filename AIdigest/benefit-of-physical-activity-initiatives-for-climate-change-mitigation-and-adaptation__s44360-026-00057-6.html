Benefit of physical activity initiatives for climate change mitigation and adaptation

Erica Hinckson · Rodrigo Reis · Marina Romanello · Ding Ding · Ibidun Adelekan · Ana Luiza Favarão Leão · Ellis Ballard · Tarik Benmarhnia · James F. Sallis · Adrian Bauman · Andrea Ramirez Varela · Deborah Salvo · Ulf Ekelund · Michael Pratt · I-Min Lee · Harold W. Kohl · Pedro C. Hallal · et al. Published 2026-03-09 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00057-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Physical inactivity and climate change are interconnected crises that should be tackled together, not separately. The paper presents the Physical Activity and Climate Change (PACC) conceptual model, showing how the WHO's "eight investments that work for physical activity" — especially active transport and active urban design — can simultaneously cut greenhouse-gas (GHG) emissions, build climate-resilient communities, and advance health equity. A double scoping review and a Denver cycling case study illustrate both the co-benefits and the research gaps and unintended consequences (gentrification, geographic bias toward high-income countries, electric-car over-reliance) that systems-based, community-co-designed solutions must avoid.

★

Why It Matters

  • Merges two of the largest public-health agendas. Physical inactivity and climate change are each linked to roughly 5 million deaths annually; the PACC model is a practical foundation for aligning them so that one investment buys two returns.
  • Reframes physical activity as a population- and systems-level lever. It moves beyond individual behaviour-change to multisectoral action — active transport, urban design, schools, workplaces, healthcare, sport — that doubles as climate mitigation and adaptation.
  • Exposes a stark equity and evidence gap. The scoping review finds the built-environment / active-transport / GHG evidence is overwhelmingly from high-income countries (only two African studies; almost all GHG studies in HICs), while the populations most harmed by climate change are the most inactive.
  • Flags unintended consequences explicitly. Compact-city densification can drive gentrification and displacement; an electric-car-only transition entrenches car dependency and inequity. The model treats these trade-offs as design problems, not afterthoughts.
  • Centres Indigenous and community knowledge. Māori (Aotearoa New Zealand) and Turkana (Kenya) case studies, plus Sport for Development and Peace programmes, show culturally grounded, co-designed pathways to climate-resilient physical activity.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Study design
A perspective/framework paper combining three evidence streams: (1) a group model building (GMB) participatory systems-mapping process, (2) a double scoping review, and (3) a quasi-experimental case study. No primary human-subjects data were collected.
Participatory model
GMB workshops (St. Louis, Jan–Feb 2023; sessions Jul 2023; final review Auckland, Oct 2023) with a core group (n = 7) and a broader expert group (n = 8–10); internationally diverse (Australia, Brazil, Colombia, New Zealand, Mexico, Norway, USA), 40% female. Causal-loop maps digitized in Kumu.
Scoping review
71 studies met inclusion for built environment → active-transport outcomes; only 12 studies linked built environment → GHG emissions. Searched via Google Scholar; three exposure groups (population/residential density, land-use mix, streetscape). Most studies city/county-level, predominantly USA and Europe; only 2 African studies; GHG studies almost all in HICs. Data in Supplementary Dataset 1.
Case study
Denver, Colorado, USA — 92 consecutive days (1 Jun–31 Aug 2022), 12 extreme-heat days ≥100 °F vs 80 control days. Cycling from Strava Metro (251,068 street/trail segments; ~5–15% of cyclists tracked). Daily maximum temperature/precipitation from NOAA Climate Data Online (station USC00052223).
Key result
Estimated ratio effect of extreme heat on cycling = 0.95 (95% CI 0.87–1.05); difference effect −1,478 bicycle-km (95% CI −4,186 to 1,360) — essentially null, reflecting behavioural adaptation by motivated Strava users, not the general population.
Availability
Kumu PACC maps open under ODbL (multiple project links, 2023–2024); NOAA temperature data public; scoping-review data in Supplementary Dataset 1. No custom code; standard linear regression + bootstrap available on request.

Core Methods

  • Group model building (GMB) / system dynamics: structured, iterative participatory workshops with experts; seed questions → variable elicitation → causal-loop diagrams → "confidence-building" validation, narrative (polarity-free) reinforcing/balancing loops digitized in Kumu.
  • PACC conceptual model: bidirectional pathways linking the "eight investments that work for physical activity" to climate mitigation (green arrows/GHG +/−) and adaptation (orange arrows), distinguishing direct, supportive and catalytic contributions plus unintended consequences.
  • Double scoping review: parallel Google-Scholar searches mapping three built-environment exposures to active-transport outcomes and to GHG metrics (vehicle-miles-travelled, CO₂, speed/volume change); spatial distribution mapped to expose geographic bias.
  • Quasi-experimental case study: linear-regression counterfactual — fit daily cycling distance on control days (month, day-of-week, precipitation), predict counterfactual on 12 treatment (≥100 °F) days, estimate difference and ratio "effect on the treated."
  • Bootstrap uncertainty: 1,000 replications resampling residuals (normal approximation) to derive 95% confidence intervals for the extreme-heat effect on cycling.

Expanded Ideas — this paper's extensibility & added value to my research

Each idea stays anchored to this paper: first how the paper itself could be extended (drawing on our corpus), then the added value for my research lines (migis.net — GeoAI, 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility, explainable & physics-informed graph learning, facility-location optimization).

① Quantify the PACC co-benefits the model only qualitatively asserts

Extending this paperThe PACC model is a qualitative causal-loop framework — it says active transport and urban design cut GHGs and raise activity, but never estimates the magnitudes, and its own scoping review found only 12 GHG studies, almost all in HICs. A direct extension: turn each PACC arrow into a quantified dose–response edge using large-scale natural-experiment evidence on built environment → physical activity, then propagate to emissions. This converts the model from a heuristic into a calibrated systems estimator and directly fills the LMIC evidence gap the authors flag.

Added value to my researchThe same density / land-use / streetscape exposures are the inputs to my 2SFCA accessibility work; quantified built-environment dose–response curves let me fuse access-to-services and access-to-active-living into a single GeoAI surface, with explainable-ML attribution of which environmental features actually move behaviour.

② Fix the Denver case study's representativeness problem with mobility-data fusion

Extending this paperThe authors openly caution that their null heat→cycling effect reflects self-selected Strava "serious exercisers", not the general population. Extending it: re-estimate the extreme-heat counterfactual on a fused, debiased mobility dataset (mobile-phone records, bike-share, transit taps) with a population-representativeness re-weighting layer, and replace the single-station temperature with gridded heat exposure. That would let the PACC adaptation pathway be tested for the vulnerable populations the paper says matter most.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my multi-source data-fusion / spatiotemporal-KDE agenda: a heat-conditioned, equity-weighted activity surface from GPS trajectories and mobile-phone data — and a reusable debiasing recipe for crowd-sourced exercise data I already use.

Cross-ref (corpus): Ubiquitous mobility data enable near-real-time emission estimates for urban traffic — 10.1038/s41893-026-01798-8

③ Close the global-south evidence gap with transferable GeoAI

Extending this paperThe paper's central empirical finding is a geographic imbalance: 71 active-transport studies but only 2 in Africa, and GHG evidence concentrated in HICs. Rather than waiting for primary studies in every LMIC, extend the paper by fine-tuning a geospatial foundation model on remote-sensing + OSM to predict built-environment exposures and active-transport potential where survey data are absent — turning the map of "missing studies" into a map of model-imputed co-benefit estimates with uncertainty.

Added value to my researchDirectly advances my foundation-model fine-tuning and open-science/FAIR lines: a transferable accessibility-and-activity predictor for under-mapped cities, with explicit uncertainty so LMIC planners can act on imputed rather than absent evidence.

④ Operationalize the paper's "compact city → gentrification" warning as a constrained optimization

Extending this paperThe paper warns that PACC-aligned densification can drive gentrification and displacement but offers only governance prescriptions. Extending it: encode this as a multi-objective facility/land-use optimization — maximize active-transport accessibility and emission reduction subject to a displacement-risk and affordability constraint — and trace the efficiency–equity Pareto frontier so planners see exactly where the trade-off bites.

Added value to my researchThis generalizes my EV-charging efficiency-vs-equity optimization and public-facility/housing-price work into a 3-way active-living / emissions / displacement trade-off, with network community detection to flag socially vulnerable catchments.

Cross-ref (corpus): Co-benefits of transport demand reductions from compact urban development in Chinese cities — 10.1038/s41893-024-01271-4

⑤ Couple PACC adaptation pathways with heat-mitigating green/blue infrastructure

Extending this paperThe paper repeatedly invokes blue/green infrastructure (parks, shade, Singapore's Marina Barrage) as the adaptation arm that keeps outdoor activity safe under extreme heat, but never models where cooling and activity co-benefits maximize per dollar. Extending it: overlay heat-exposure, green-cooling and physical-activity surfaces to find the leverage points the PACC model calls for, prioritizing the under-served neighbourhoods the paper says are most exposed.

Added value to my researchFeeds my healthy-cities and spatial-equity work: a physics-informed graph layer where green-space cooling is a node attribute modulating the activity edge — joint siting of cooling and active-living infrastructure as a single GeoAI allocation problem.
