Predicting schistosome transmission in rural Uganda using water contact data from wearable GPS devices

Fabian Reitzug · Melissa A. Iacovidou · Narcis B. Kabatereine · Betty Nabatte · Max T. Eyre · Benjamin Tinkitina · Anatol M. Byaruhanga · Renaud Lambiotte · Goylette F. Chami Published 2026-05-22 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00118-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Ten days of wearable GPS logger data from 452 individuals in rural Uganda revealed that simple exponential spatial decay functions of household distance predict individual-level open-water contact (AUROC = 0.87) and tap/borehole usage (AUROC = 0.92), without needing mobility or WASH covariates. Integrating these decay functions into a spatially explicit individual-based transmission model produced realistic one-year Schistosoma mansoni reinfection estimates (observed: 40.5%) and ranked water sites by transmission risk, providing scalable tools for focal schistosomiasis interventions. Safe-water provision (WASH) alone did not reduce open-water contact: even in counterfactual scenarios, fewer than 2% of individuals would fully substitute open-water visits with taps or boreholes.

Why It Matters

  • Bridges the data gap in schistosomiasis control. Previous water-contact assumptions (nearest-site only, household distance proxies) were untested; this study is the first to validate individual-level spatial decay from objective GPS logger data at scale, across three geographically diverse Ugandan districts.
  • Rethinks the role of WASH interventions. The crowding-out model consistently failed to outperform a purely distance-based model; this challenges optimistic meta-analytic claims about WASH reducing open-water contact and aligns with a major randomized trial finding no WASH effect beyond MDA alone.
  • Provides a reusable, low-burden field tool. As few as 15 individuals wearing GPS loggers for 10 days suffice to estimate decay parameters; building-footprint data (Google-Microsoft Open Buildings) can then extrapolate predictions to whole communities without additional surveys.
  • Links micro-level behavior to macro-level transmission. By embedding empirically learned visitation rules into an individual-based model, the study connects household-to-site distances directly to predicted village-level infection prevalence and miracidial contamination per site.
  • Produces a priority-ranking tool for focal interventions. Site-specific visit counts and contamination estimates enable planners to target mollusciciding, biocontrol, or test-and-treat strategies to the subset of open-water sites that sustain the majority of transmission.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Study sample
452 participants (children and adults aged 5–82; 50% female) drawn from 12 villages across three Ugandan districts: Pakwach (n = 183), Buliisa (n = 164), Mayuge (n = 105). Nested within the SchistoTrack cohort of 2,885 individuals across 38 villages enrolled January–February 2022.
GPS logger data
i-gotU GT-600 wearable GPS loggers (Mobile Action Technology, Taiwan); 2-minute recording intervals, 05:00–20:00, motion-activated. Observation window: 10 days per participant (first day excluded for Hawthorne-effect guard); field accuracy estimated at 8.6 m. 8,249 open-water contact events and 1,181 tap/borehole usage events observed.
Water-site mapping
Field team georeferenced 143 open-water sites (clustered into 69 distinct sites) and 63 taps/boreholes (clustered into 32 locations) covering up to 26 km from study villages. Site mapping included type, GPS location, and snail sampling.
Population / buildings
Google–Microsoft Open Buildings Dataset V3 (source.coop/vida/google-microsoft-open-buildings; >2 billion structures globally); building centroids within 10 km × 10 km around Pakwach villages used as household proxies for out-of-sample prediction. WorldPop gridded population (Tatem, 2017; open data) cited as an alternative.
Basemaps & shapefiles
OpenStreetMap base maps (openstreetmap.org); Uganda waterbodies shapefile from World Resources Institute (wri.org).
Survey instrument
Household questionnaires administered digitally via Open Data Kit Collect (v2022.4) on Lenovo TB-8505F tablets to 1,459 SchistoTrack households; collected sociodemographics, WASH, and self-reported water contact.
Infection data
Kato-Katz microscopy for S. mansoni at three time points (baseline: prevalence 47.6%; post-praziquantel follow-up at 4–5 weeks: 17.0%; one-year reinfection: 40.5%) for all 452 GPS participants.
Code availability
All analysis code available on FigShare at doi.org/10.25446/oxford.31573027. GPS data not publicly available (sensitive PII); available via data-sharing agreement with University of Oxford.

Core Methods

  • GPS data processing & contact detection: recursion analysis (R package recurse) applied to 2-min GPS traces to identify contact events within a 20 m buffer of mapped water sites; DBSCAN clustering collapsed nearby sites into 69 open-water and 32 tap/borehole clusters.
  • Human mobility characterization: radius of gyration (Rg) computed per individual as Euclidean displacement from household; aggregate mobility fitted to a truncated power-law distribution; participants stratified into district-specific mobility terciles.
  • Bayesian exponential spatial decay models: nonlinear Bayesian regression (R package brms with Stan/No-U-Turn Sampler) predicting binary or duration outcomes; model selection via expected log pointwise predictive density (ELPD) leave-one-out cross-validation; performance reported as stratified fivefold cross-validated AUROCs.
  • Competing-opportunities framework: Stouffer’s intervening-opportunity model adapted to rank open-water sites by household distance (1st nearest, 2nd nearest, ..., 5th+ nearest) and estimate separate decay functions per rank.
  • Crowding-out (WASH substitution) model: extended decay model where tap/borehole usage probability multiplicatively reduces open-water-contact probability; alpha parameter quantifies the crowding-out magnitude; compared via ELPD.
  • Spatially explicit individual-based transmission model (IBM): modular IBM coded in Julia v1.11.3, integrating snail dynamics from SchiSTOP and human dynamics from SCHISTOX via weighted bipartite household-to-site networks; 100 replicates over 365 days for four visitation rule scenarios.
  • Out-of-sample prediction: district-specific decay parameters applied to building-footprint centroids from Google–Microsoft Open Buildings V3 to generate population-wide contact-probability maps for Pakwach without new surveys.

① Replace the fixed exponential decay with a learned, data-adaptive distance function

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that an exponential decay outperforms logistic regression and power-law alternatives for this specific Ugandan context, but the functional form is still hand-chosen. A direct extension: replace the hand-specified decay with a gravity-like model learned automatically from the GPS data, as demonstrated by the closed-form gravity models recently auto-discovered from mobility corpora. For schistosomiasis, the learned decay could be conditioned on environmental covariates (waterbody type, district ecology) to capture why decay rates differ across Pakwach, Buliisa, and Mayuge. This would also test whether the paper's call for "generalizability to other waterborne diseases" holds with a universal learned kernel rather than an assumed form.

Added value to my researchThis directly maps onto my distance-decay parameterization problem in 2SFCA/R2SFCA healthcare-access models, where the Gaussian or power-law decay is conventionally assumed. Replacing the fixed kernel with a GPS-data-learned decay function — drawing on the same Bayesian nonlinear regression framework here — would yield a data-driven catchment delineation rather than an analyst-chosen radius, improving the realism of facility-access scores for rural health facilities in similar low-income settings.

② Embed the spatial decay directly into a fine-scale population-density model of epidemic waves

Extending this paperThe paper's IBM uses spatial decay to distribute contact across sites but does not model how transmission waves propagate spatially at the village or district scale. A natural extension: combine the site-usage probabilities (output of equation 2) with fine-scale population-density heterogeneity to predict whether epidemic velocity and wave dynamics in schistosomiasis resemble those observed for dengue — where intra-urban density heterogeneity was shown to predict the timing and amplitude of epidemic waves. This would test whether the "focality" of schistosome transmission is analogous to the dengue heterogeneity effect and would motivate spatially adaptive MDA targeting.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal KDE and GWR/MGWR work on spatial non-stationarity would be directly applicable here: the site-level visit counts from this paper's IBM output are a natural input for a spatially varying transmission intensity surface, which I could estimate with bandwidth-adaptive KDE conditional on site-level density covariates. This bridges between point-level GPS evidence and grid-level epidemic prediction — a methodological pipeline applicable to any environmentally mediated infectious disease.

③ Combine GPS-derived site rankings with snail habitat suitability to optimize focal mollusciciding

Extending this paperThe paper produces miracidial contamination rankings per site and notes a mismatch between visit counts and contamination that could identify high-priority sites. An extension: fuse these rankings with a climate- and land-use-driven snail habitat suitability model to produce a joint human-snail transmission priority map. The schistosomiasis snail literature (e.g., Biomphalaria habitat shifts under climate and urbanization in Brazil) provides the environmental side of the model, while this paper contributes the human exposure side. Together they would allow targeting of mollusciciding to the intersection of high-visitation and high-snail-density sites.

The XAI layer (SHAP values or GWR coefficients) would reveal which environmental or behavioral features drive site-specific transmission risk — a generalizable pipeline for targeting any vector- or snail-mediated disease with focal interventions.

④ Apply transportability methods to assess where this paper’s decay parameters generalize

Extending this paperThe paper shows good cross-district transportability of the global decay model (AUROC > 0.82 when one district is held out), but does not formally quantify whether the decay parameters would transfer to different ecological zones, seasons, or countries. Extending it with transportability and generalizability analysis — the same approach recently applied to assess whether MDA trial effects generalize across malaria populations — would produce a principled framework for predicting when and where the paper's decay parameters can be directly reused versus when new GPS data collection is needed.

⑤ Use mobility terciles as a latent variable linking water contact to disease importation across villages

Extending this paperThe paper finds that mobility terciles do not significantly improve open-water contact prediction within the study villages, but it does not examine whether high-mobility individuals disproportionately import infection between villages through long-distance water contacts. An extension: model the interaction between high-radius-of-gyration individuals and inter-village transmission using a bipartite mobility graph, analogous to malaria-importation models on Bioko Island that used GPS-derived movement to identify importation corridors. This would test whether the paper's null mobility result is specific to intra-village dynamics and reframe long-distance movers as "super-spreaders" at the inter-village scale.

Added value to my researchLong-distance movers as spatial connectors maps directly to my network community detection and job-housing network research: the bipartite household-to-site graph (already central to the IBM in this paper) is formally identical to the bipartite worker-to-workplace graphs I analyze. Applying the same community-detection pipeline (e.g., InfoMap or modularity optimization) to household-to-site water-contact networks would reveal which households share exposure communities — a spatial risk-cluster that MDA planners could target at the village rather than district scale.
