Determining structures of RNA conformers using AFM and deep neural networks

Maximilia F. S. Degenhardt · Hermann F. Degenhardt · Yuba R. Bhandari · Yun-Tzai Lee · Jienyu Ding · Ping Yu · William F. Heinz · Jason R. Stagno · Charles D. Schwieters · Norman R. Watts · Paul T. Wingfield · Alan Rein · Jinwei Zhang · Yun-Xing Wang Published 2024-12-18 DOI 10.1038/s41586-024-07559-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

HORNET — a novel pipeline combining atomic force microscopy (AFM), unsupervised machine learning, and deep neural networks — can determine 3D topological structures of individual heterogeneous RNA conformers in solution without signal averaging. Applied to six benchmark RNAs (≈56 million trajectory models), RNase P RNA (three distinct conformers, ≈10 million models), and the HIV-1 Rev response element (five conformers), HORNET achieves top-model accuracies consistently below 6 Å r.m.s.d., directly addressing a long-standing gap in structural biology left by NMR, crystallography, and cryo-EM, which cannot resolve highly flexible RNA molecules in distinct conformational states.

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the signal-averaging bottleneck. All existing high-resolution structural methods (NMR, crystallography, cryo-EM) average over many molecules; HORNET is the first demonstrated method to determine 3D structure from a single-molecule AFM image, making heterogeneous and flexible RNAs tractable.
  • Fills the RNA structural gap left by AlphaFold. The lack of a large RNA structure database and the absence of a clear sequence-to-structure correlation mean AlphaFold-style prediction does not transfer to RNA; HORNET provides an orthogonal, experiment-driven route using individual-molecule topographic restraints.
  • Resolves a long-standing HIV-1 structural debate. HORNET directly visualizes five distinct conformational states of the HIV-1 RRE RNA, unambiguously confirming an A-like topology and identifying the Rev-binding site geometry — opening a new route to anti-viral drug design via structural complementarity.
  • Generalizes across RNA families without retraining. A DNN trained solely on RNase P RNA catalytic domain trajectories (3.5 million models) successfully estimates structural accuracy for entirely different RNAs (cobalamin riboswitch, group II intron), demonstrating broad transferability.
  • Provides quantitative accuracy estimation without a known reference. Unlike conventional structure determination, HORNET estimates r.m.s.d. from an unknown ground truth, giving researchers a built-in confidence metric — analogous to AlphaFold's pLDDT score but for experimental single-molecule data.

Core Methods

  • AFM-restrained coarse-grained molecular dynamics: Langevin dynamics (CafeMol) with a weighted AFM topographic pseudo-potential that restrains trajectory models to match the 3D height map of a single-molecule AFM image (5 Å/pixel). Go-model energetics maintain hierarchical RNA folding.
  • Unsupervised machine learning (UML) pipeline: three-stage process — (1) energy outlier filtering; (2) PCA of all energy terms (Go, local, total) + CCAFM with successive clustering to select the lowest-energy cluster; (3) cohort selection combining lowest energies with highest topographic fit. Achieves average top-10 r.m.s.d. ~5 Å across all benchmarks.
  • Supervised deep neural network (DNN) for accuracy estimation: 3-hidden-layer network (128→64→16 neurons, ELU activation, ~11 k parameters) trained on the psDatabase to predict r.m.s.d. from the unknown ground truth using energy terms + CCAFM as inputs. Pearson r = 0.95 on training-validation; generalizes to unseen RNA families.
  • DNN architecture optimization: random search over number of layers (1–10), neurons per layer (8–256, multiples of 8), activations (relu/leaky-relu/elu/gelu/selu), regularization (L2 ridge regression + dropout ~20%), batch size 128, Huber loss, Adam optimizer (lr=0.001). Trained on NVIDIA V100/K80 GPUs (NIH HPC Biowulf).
  • Non-convergence detection and recovery: HORNET monitors DNN-estimated accuracy distribution; if top-model r.m.s.d. exceeds 7 Å, the pipeline triggers re-fitting with a different initial model or unconstrained MD escape from local minima, followed by UML without AFM restraints.
  • Image resolution assessment: auto-correlation value (ACV) method using 2D FFT low-pass Fourier ring filtering to estimate effective AFM image resolution (11–12.5 Å for experimental particles); images upsampled to 4096×4096 pixels using SPIP/MountainsSPIP software.
  • All-atom conversion: top 10 coarse-grained models selected by DNN are converted to full atomic coordinates for downstream structural analysis and drug design.

① Replace the fixed DNN with a conformational generative model for RNA

Extending this paperHORNET's DNN is a discriminative accuracy estimator: given a candidate structure, it predicts r.m.s.d. from the ground truth. It does not generate structures — those come from Langevin MD starting from hand-picked initial models. A direct extension: recast the generative step as a 3D molecular generative model conditioned on the AFM topographic surface, so that new conformer proposals are drawn from a learned distribution over topologically consistent RNA folds rather than from FARFAR2 or MD. This would substantially reduce the dependence on expensive trajectory sampling (~10 million models per benchmark) and could generate physically plausible initial structures for any RNA given only its AFM image and secondary structure. The corpus paper on learning on topological surfaces and geometric structures for 3D molecular generation provides an immediate methodological bridge.

② Extend HORNET's accuracy-estimation DNN to a conformational-ensemble predictor

Extending this paperHORNET's DNN currently estimates the r.m.s.d. of a single candidate model from an unknown ground-truth structure. A key limitation acknowledged in the paper is that accuracy estimation degrades beyond ~7.5 Å r.m.s.d. A direct extension: reformulate the DNN as a probabilistic conformational ensemble predictor — instead of a point estimate of r.m.s.d., output a full distribution over possible conformers, analogous to AlphaFold's pLDDT or predicted aligned error. This requires augmenting the psDatabase with conformational diversity metrics and training with uncertainty-aware losses (e.g., evidential regression). The corpus paper on learning conformational flexibility of immune receptors provides a direct blueprint for how deep learning can model the ensemble of accessible conformations, rather than a single structure.

The transition from point r.m.s.d. estimation to full probability distributions over conformational states is the structural-biology analogue of quantile regression or conformal prediction in GWR/MGWR models, directly informing how I design uncertainty-aware explainable models for spatiotemporal health outcomes.

③ Replace CafeMol Langevin dynamics with a graph neural network-based surrogate force field

Extending this paperThe most computationally intensive step of HORNET is coarse-grained molecular dynamics: each RNA particle requires 10–60 million simulation steps (~2.7 µs). A direct extension of the paper: train a graph neural network force field — with RNA residues as nodes and covalent/stacking contacts as edges — to replace the CafeMol Go + AFM potential, enabling orders-of-magnitude faster trajectory generation. Because the input features (energy terms, topographic CC) are already graph-structured by chemical connectivity, this is architecturally natural. The corpus paper on rapid protein model refinement by deep learning via a graph-neural-network framework provides exactly this template for bio-macromolecular structures.

Added value to my researchThe concept of a GNN force field — where nodes are entities, edges encode physical interactions, and message passing replaces explicit simulation — maps directly onto my physics-informed graph learning agenda for spatiotemporal systems. ④ Extend experimental data integration to complementary low-resolution probes (SAXS, ion mobility)

Extending this paperHORNET currently uses AFM topography as its sole experimental restraint. The paper itself uses SAXS-derived RS3D models as starting structures (BM4, BM5), but does not integrate SAXS intensities as a live restraint during fitting. A direct extension: build a multi-modal restraint engine that simultaneously satisfies AFM topography, SAXS scattering curves, and ion mobility collision cross-sections within the Langevin dynamics framework. This would provide orthogonal global shape information, reducing conformational ambiguity at the current ~7.5 Å accuracy limit. The corpus paper on protein shape from ion mobility mass spectrometry shows that shape information from a low-resolution probe can consistently improve structural predictions when integrated with sequence-based methods.
