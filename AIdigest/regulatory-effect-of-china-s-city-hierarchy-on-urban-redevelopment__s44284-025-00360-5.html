Regulatory effect of China's city hierarchy on urban redevelopment

Yu Deng · Kexin Cao · Mingxing Chen · Ran Liu · Tao Pei · Ci Song · Kaiyong Wang · Tim Schwanen · Cecilia Wong · Pengjun Zhao · Ya Ping Wang · Bojie Fu Published 2026-01-05 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00360-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

China's administrative city hierarchy exerts a regulatory effect on urban redevelopment. Analyzing state-owned land transactions from 2012–2022 across 326 cities, the authors show that higher-ranked cities prioritize government regulation over market forces, whereas lower-ranked cities rely more on market-oriented approaches — a divergence that explains differing redevelopment patterns across the hierarchy. The pattern reflects a broader shift toward top–down urban governance aligned with central-government policy, with higher-tier cities leading in diverse redevelopment practices pursuing both economic and non-economic goals.

Why It Matters

  • Maps the geography of redevelopment at national scale. Urban redevelopment is widely implemented but its spatial distribution, mechanisms and driving forces have been "insufficiently understood"; this is a 326-city, decade-long empirical account rather than a single case study.
  • Names administrative power as a driving force. The study shows that a city's position in the administrative hierarchy, not just its market, shapes whether redevelopment is government-led or market-led — linking political-administrative structure directly to land-use outcomes.
  • Explains divergence, not just description. The regulation-vs-market split is offered as the mechanism behind varying redevelopment patterns, giving planners a lens to anticipate behavior by city tier.
  • Translates into tier-specific policy. Because the drivers differ across the hierarchy, the paper argues for tailored strategies rather than one-size-fits-all national redevelopment policy.

Core Methods

  • Land-transaction data engineering: Python web-scraping of the China Land Market portal to assemble a decade-long, 326-city state-owned land transaction database as the operational proxy for urban redevelopment.
  • City-hierarchy stratification: classify cities by China's administrative rank and analyze redevelopment distribution across tiers (Figs. 1–2).
  • Grouped factor analysis: model the factors affecting redevelopment under different grouping (hierarchy) models to test how drivers differ across city ranks (Fig. 3).
  • Mediation analysis: estimate mediating effects to disentangle the regulation-vs-market mechanism behind redevelopment divergence (Fig. 4).
  • Tooling: analysis implemented in Python and R; covariate integration from official statistical yearbooks; full method detail in Supplementary Sections 1–4.

① Replace tier dummies with explainable ML over the land-transaction record

Extending this paperThe paper diagnoses regulation-vs-market divergence through grouped factor models and mediation across administrative ranks (Figs. 3–4), but the hierarchy enters largely as a categorical stratifier. A direct extension: train an explainable ML model (gradient boosting + SHAP) on the full 2012–2022 transaction database to predict whether a parcel is government-led vs market-led, then read off which features — and at which tier — actually drive the split, turning fixed groupings into a continuous, attributable driver surface.

② Recast the city hierarchy as a network, not a ladder

Extending this paperThe paper treats hierarchy as a vertical ranking, but Chinese cities are also bound by horizontal economic linkages (provincial capitals, city clusters, twin-city pairs). Extending it: build an intercity network where redevelopment intensity propagates along economic ties, and test whether a higher-tier city's regulatory mode spills over to linked lower-tier cities — a community-detection / spatial-spillover layer on top of the rank stratification.

③ From land-mode classification to a redevelopment-allocation policy learner

Extending this paperThe paper documents how redevelopment is allocated across tiers but is descriptive about outcomes. Extending it: pose tier-differentiated redevelopment as a sequential planning problem — given a city's rank, budget and parcel inventory, learn a placement/conversion policy (deep RL) that reproduces observed government-vs-market choices and then explores counterfactual, welfare-improving allocations.

⑤ Test the regulatory mechanism against discourse and natural constraints

Extending this paperThe paper attributes divergence to administrative power, but two confounders deserve testing: media/political discourse (top-down narratives that legitimize government-led redevelopment in high-tier cities) and physical geography (terrain fragmentation that constrains where redevelopment is even feasible). Extending it: add a discourse-intensity covariate and a geographic-constraint covariate to the mediation model to check whether the hierarchy effect survives both.
