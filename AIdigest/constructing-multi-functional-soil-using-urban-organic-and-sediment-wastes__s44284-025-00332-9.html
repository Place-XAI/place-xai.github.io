Constructing (multi)functional soil using urban organic and sediment wastes

Lauren Porter · Franziska B. Bucka · Natalie Páez-Curtidor · Monika Egerer · Ingrid Kögel-Knabner Published 2025-10-13 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00332-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Functional urban topsoil can be built from the city's own waste stream — upcycled construction subsoils amended with municipal green-waste compost and waste-derived biochar. Compost is the indispensable base for fertility (nitrogen, cation exchange capacity), while high-temperature wood-waste biochar matches or beats benchmark granulated activated carbon for stormwater pollutant immobilization and stable carbon accrual. The highest joint multifunctionality comes from mixing amendments of differing qualities and reactivities — but the optimal mix is geology-dependent, so practitioners must know their city's regional geology.

★

Why It Matters

  • Challenges "land-take." Importing fertile topsoil from surrounding ecosystems is unsustainable; mineral construction wastes alone make up ~40% of annual EU waste. This work shows those wastes can be re-engineered into living urban soil instead of being landfilled.
  • Designs for multiple services at once. Rather than optimizing a single property, it quantifies fertility, runoff infiltration, stormwater contaminant immobilization, and stable carbon accrual jointly in one standardized multifunctionality score (0–1).
  • A cheaper, lower-carbon substitute for activated carbon. Waste biochar reaches a multifunctionality balance (0.81) equal to energy-intensive granulated activated carbon (0.80), while valorizing organic waste and storing carbon belowground.
  • Actionable recipe with caveats. It gives soil-designing practitioners concrete amendment guidance — and a clear warning that effectiveness hinges on the geogenic (carbonate, clay, texture) properties of locally available mineral material.

Core Methods

  • Constructed-soil fabrication: upcycle excavated mineral subsoils (sieved to <2 mm active fraction) and dry-mix with combinations of compost and biochars (single and dual-biochar mixes) at a fixed 4% w/w organic loading.
  • Batch adsorption testing: measure immobilization of Cu, Zn, mecoprop, terbutryn from a synthetic stormwater matrix (n = 3), benchmarking biochars and mixtures against GAC.
  • Fertility & physical characterization: bulk density, water content at field capacity, total N, pH, total/effective cation exchange capacity, specific surface area (N2-BET).
  • 30-day incubation (pedogenesis proxy): track microbial respiration (CO2 titration), water-stable aggregate size distribution and mean weight diameter, and organo-mineral surface associations to assess rapid soil-structure formation.
  • Carbon-stability assessment: 13C NMR carbon-bonding profiles plus H/Corg ratios to gauge aromaticity and recalcitrance of accrued carbon.
  • Cascade multifunctionality scoring: standardize each measured property, translate to function scores (fertility, pollutant retention, stable-carbon accrual), and average into a single multifunctionality index (0–1) with equal weighting; statistics via one-way ANOVA with targeted orthogonal contrasts.

① Geo-tag the recipe: a city-wide map of "which mix where"

Extending this paperThe paper's central caveat is that the optimal compost/biochar mix is geology-dependent — yet it tests only two Munich-region subsoils in the lab. A direct extension: couple the constructed-soil function model to regional geogenic maps (carbonate, clay, texture) and a spatial inventory of construction-excavation flows, producing a city-scale recommendation surface that tells planners which amendment recipe to apply on each parcel of available mineral waste.

② Explainable ML for amendment–geology interaction

Extending this paperThe paper's multifunctionality score is a simple equal-weight average over standardized indicators, and its mechanistic claims (carbonate gluing, micropore sorption, trade-offs between fertility and pollutant retention) are read off bivariate plots. Extending it: train an interpretable ML model on the measured property matrix and use SHAP/interaction analysis to quantify which amendment × geogenic interactions drive each function — converting qualitative hypotheses into ranked, testable effect sizes.
