Programmable 200 GOPS Hopfield-inspired photonic Ising machine

Nayem Al-Kayed · Charles St-Arnault · Hugh Morison · A. Aadhi · Chaoran Huang · Alexander N. Tait · David V. Plant · Bhavin J. Shastri Published 2025-12-17 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09838-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A room-temperature optoelectronic oscillator (OEO) inspired by Hopfield networks achieves over 200 giga operations per second (GOPS) for spin coupling, solving fully connected problems with up to 256 spins (65,536 couplings) and sparse problems with over 41,000 spins. The system sets best-in-class solution quality for max-cut problems (2,000 and 20,000 spins) among photonic Ising machines, and for the first time addresses number partitioning and lattice protein folding benchmarks on a photonic platform — demonstrating that embedding DSP from optical communications within optical computation can unlock scalable, ultrafast hardware solvers for NP-hard problems.

Why It Matters

  • Breaks a scalability deadlock in physical computing. Quantum annealers suffer quadratic qubit overhead on dense graphs; this OEO-based platform achieves linear scaling in spin representation at room temperature, removing the cryogenic barrier and enabling commercial deployment.
  • Speed jump: >200 GOPS. The system's cascaded thin-film lithium niobate (TFLN) modulators operating at high baud rates deliver a compute throughput that sets a new benchmark for photonic Ising machines — directly comparing favorably against existing optical and electronic solvers.
  • New benchmark territory for photonics. Number partitioning and lattice protein folding had never been solved on a photonic platform before; demonstrating ground-state solutions for both expands the known application envelope of photonic computing.
  • Noise-as-resource paradigm. Rather than suppressing shot noise from high baud-rate operation, the system exploits it for annealing — escaping local minima and accelerating convergence — a conceptual shift applicable to analog AI and neuromorphic hardware broadly.
  • DSP–photonics co-design. Embedding digital signal processing (standard in optical communications) within the recurrent optical loop enhances solution quality, proposing a new hybrid design philosophy for photonic computing systems.

Core Methods

  • OEO-based time-encoded spin representation: each spin is encoded as a time slot in a high-baud-rate optical waveform, enabling linear (rather than quadratic) scaling; the recurrent loop implements the Hopfield-inspired update rule.
  • Matrix–vector multiplication (MVM) via modulation: TFLN modulators perform the spin-coupling MVM in the optical domain at >200 GOPS — the core computational primitive replacing electronic or spatial-light-modulator approaches.
  • Noise-annealing: amplified spontaneous emission (ASE) from the SOA provides a tunable stochastic perturbation; bias current controls noise variance, enabling exponential annealing schedules to escape local minima.
  • DSP integration: a digital signal processing (DSP) engine borrowed from coherent optical communications is embedded in the loop for signal equalization, thresholding, and nonlinearity application — enhancing spin-state fidelity.
  • Benchmarking protocol: solution quality measured as approximation ratio (cut value / best-known) for max-cut; ground-state recovery rate for protein folding and number partitioning; convergence speed as iterations-to-ground-state.
  • Bifurcation analysis: phase-transition behavior of the OEO is characterized experimentally to identify the operating regime that yields optimal spin discrimination (Fig. 2).
