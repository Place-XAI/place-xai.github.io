Nature Computational Science NMR Chemical Shifts Deep Learning Molecular Spectroscopy Pretraining & Fine-tuning

Toward a unified benchmark and framework for deep learning-based prediction of nuclear magnetic resonance chemical shifts

Fanjie Xu · Wentao Guo · Feng Wang · Lin Yao · Hongshuai Wang · Fujie Tang · Zhifeng Gao · Linfeng Zhang · Weinan E · Zhong-Qun Tian · Jun Cheng Published 2025-03-28 DOI 10.1038/s43588-025-00783-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

NMRNet — a deep learning framework built on the SE(3) Transformer architecture — achieves state-of-the-art NMR chemical shift prediction across both liquid-state and solid-state systems by combining large-scale self-supervised pretraining on >4.8 million crystal/molecular structures with supervised fine-tuning on curated NMR datasets. The authors also introduce nmrshiftdb2-2024, a cleaned and expanded benchmark dataset, establishing the first comprehensive, unified evaluation suite that covers diverse chemical systems. The framework generalizes well to large, complex molecules (150+ atoms) and solid-state materials, demonstrating its practical utility for spectral interpretation, structure revision, and stereochemistry determination.

Why It Matters

  • Unifies liquid-state and solid-state NMR under one architecture. Prior frameworks were siloed; NMRNet uses a single SE(3) Transformer backbone that handles molecular and periodic crystal environments, enabling a truly cross-domain NMR prediction system.
  • Addresses the data scarcity problem with large-scale pretraining. By pretraining on 4.8 million structural databases (Aflow, CSD, Materials Project) — orders of magnitude more than available labeled NMR data — the model overcomes the fundamental bottleneck of expensive experimental NMR acquisition.
  • Introduces a community benchmark (nmrshiftdb2-2024). The first systematically cleaned, deduplicated NMR database with consistent splits enables apples-to-apples comparison across future models — a public-good infrastructure contribution.
  • Demonstrates real-world utility beyond numerical accuracy. Applications to peak assignment (94% accuracy for 13C), structure revision, and chiral stereochemistry determination show NMRNet as a practical tool for chemists, not merely a benchmark competitor.
  • Ships as open infrastructure. Code (GitHub + Zenodo), datasets (Zenodo), and an online inference service (ai4ec.ac.cn) lower the barrier to adoption across chemistry and materials science labs.

Core Methods

  • SE(3)-equivariant Transformer (Uni-Mol backbone): the core architecture operates on 3D atomic coordinates, producing representations invariant to rotation and translation — essential for atoms whose chemical shifts are determined by their 3D local environment.
  • Self-supervised pretraining on 4.8M+ structures: two tasks — masked atom-type prediction and 3D coordinate reconstruction (with ±1 Å noise injection) — train the model to recognize chemical environments without any NMR labels. Log-weighted resampling addresses element-frequency imbalance (H and C alone constitute >84% of local environments in CSD).
  • Separate organic vs. inorganic crystal pretraining: mixed pretraining was empirically inferior; the model learns disjoint representations for organic (CSD) and inorganic (Aflow + Materials Project) crystal systems.
  • Cutoff-radius local environment encoding (6 Å): for periodic solids, each atom's environment is defined as all neighbors within 6 Å of the infinite crystal, with four progressive strategies (S1–S4) evaluated to determine the optimal representation of periodic boundary conditions.
  • Supervised fine-tuning with 5-fold cross-validation: an atom-level feedforward head (G_nmr) predicts chemical shifts from atom representations; fivefold CV on training data; reported results are test-set averages.
  • Evaluation metrics: MAE, RMSE, R2, MAD, RMSD, and a combined Total RMSD = 13C RMSD + 10 × 1H RMSD to normalize scale differences between carbon and proton shifts.
  • Downstream applications: peak assignment (matching predicted shifts to experimental signals), configuration/stereochemistry determination (ranking isomers by RMSD to experimental shifts), and representation interpretability via t-SNE and attention-map visualization.

① Extend the pretraining paradigm to multi-modal spectral data fusion

Extending this paperNMRNet pretrained exclusively on 3D structural databases and fine-tuned on NMR chemical shifts — one modality at a time. The paper itself notes that combining 1H and 13C NMR improves chiral isomer identification, hinting at modality synergy. A direct extension: adopt contrastive multi-modal pretraining that jointly aligns 3D structure representations with multiple spectral modalities (NMR, IR, mass spectrometry) in a shared latent space. This would create a "spectral fingerprint" model capable of cross-modal retrieval and prediction — and directly addresses the paper's stated limitation of relying on available datasets for each modality separately.

② Replace the fixed 6 Å cutoff with a learned adaptive receptive field

Extending this paperThe paper selects a fixed 6 Å cutoff radius for solid-state local environments via a convergence check (Supplementary Fig. 12), but this is a global hyperparameter applied identically to all elements and crystal systems. A natural extension: replace the fixed cutoff with an attention-gated, element-specific receptive field that the model learns during pretraining. This would let the architecture adaptively capture long-range Mn–Na electronic interactions (shown in attention maps, Extended Data Fig. 4c) or short-range H-bonding environments without manual tuning — and would directly generalize the cutoff strategy from S3/S4 into a differentiable operation.

Extending this paperThe paper addresses a severe element-frequency imbalance (H and C >84% of CSD local environments) using a log-weighted resampling scheme (Eq. 1) during pretraining — an elegant technique that prevents rare-element representations from being drowned out. ⑤ Adapt the physics-infused graph pretraining to spatiotemporal urban graph learning

Extending this paperNMRNet's pretraining is a form of physics-constrained self-supervision: the SE(3)-equivariant Transformer encodes a rotation/translation invariance prior that reflects actual physics of atomic environments. The paper demonstrates that this physically meaningful pretraining transfers far better to downstream NMR tasks than structure-agnostic pretraining. A direct extension: investigate whether additional physics priors — such as periodic boundary conditions as a group-theoretic constraint, or DFT-based energy surfaces as auxiliary pretraining signals — further improve generalization to exotic crystal topologies not well-covered by Aflow/CSD/Materials Project.
