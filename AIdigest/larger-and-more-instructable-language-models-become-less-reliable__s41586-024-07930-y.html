Larger and more instructable language models become less reliable

Lexin Zhou · Wout Schellaert · Fernando Martínez-Plumed · Yael Moros-Daval · Cèsar Ferri · José Hernández-Orallo Published 2024-09-25 DOI 10.1038/s41586-024-07930-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Scaling up and shaping up large language models improves correctness and prompt stability, but paradoxically makes models less reliable from a human-supervision perspective: shaped-up models eliminate avoidant ("I don't know") responses and replace them with plausible-sounding wrong answers, fail to consolidate safe operating zones at low difficulty, and make ultracrepidarian errors that human supervisors frequently cannot detect. A fundamental shift in LLM design — away from maximizing benchmark correctness toward ensuring a predictable error distribution — is needed, especially for high-stakes applications.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reframes the scaling narrative. Contrary to conventional wisdom, this is the first systematic study showing that scaling up and instruction-tuning simultaneously increase incorrectness by eliminating the safety buffer of avoidance — models trade epistemic prudence for surface fluency.
  • Identifies ultracrepidarianism as a systemic risk. The paper introduces a measurable construct — the proportion of wrong answers among non-avoidant responses (i/(a+i)) — and shows it grows monotonically with scale, creating an invisible reliability collapse invisible to standard accuracy benchmarks.
  • Human supervision cannot compensate. Two controlled human studies demonstrate that real users fail to detect incorrect-to-correct errors, especially for high-difficulty questions, invalidating the "human oversight" safety assumption embedded in current AI governance proposals.
  • Covers three model families across 32 models. By covering GPT, LLaMA, and BLOOM (raw and shaped-up variants, paired pre-training data), the analysis disentangles scaling effects from shaping effects — a methodological first for reliability research.
  • Actionable for AI design. The paper explicitly calls for difficulty concordance and supervision-error rates to be incorporated into training loss functions, offering a concrete, operationalizable path forward.

Core Methods

  • Three-valued response labeling: each model response is categorized as correct (c), avoidant (a), or incorrect (i) using algorithmic grading with regular expressions — avoiding infeasible manual scoring of ~4.2 million responses.
  • Six reliability indicators: correctness proportion, prudence proportion, correctness prompting stability, prudence prompting stability, correctness difficulty concordance, and prudence difficulty concordance — computed per model and averaged across five benchmarks.
  • Difficulty concordance (generality metric): a non-parametric metric measuring how well success mass conforms to a descending step function over difficulty — quantifying whether models fail only at hard instances (good) or also at easy ones (bad).
  • Prompting stability: probability that an instance's response category remains unchanged across two randomly drawn prompt templates — decomposed by correctness and prudence axes.
  • Ultracrepidarianism measure: i/(a+i) — the proportion of non-avoidant answers that are wrong, tracked across model generations to expose the reliability collapse hidden by accuracy-only benchmarks.
  • Scaling ablation: uses BLOOM and LLaMA paired raw/shaped-up variants (same pre-training data) to disentangle scaling effects from shaping effects; FLOPs used as compute proxy.
  • Human calibration (S1 & S2): two-parameter logistic mapping of intrinsic difficulty proxies to 0–100 human-failure-probability scale; human supervision error rates measured with three-valued confusion matrices.

① Detect LLM reliability collapse with semantic entropy before errors reach users

Extending this paperThis paper measures reliability after the fact — by grading responses against ground truth. But in deployment, ground truth is unavailable. A natural extension is to build on the paper's three-valued framework by coupling it with semantic entropy as an online, ground-truth-free signal: when a model's output distribution is highly uncertain semantically, flag it as potentially avoidant-turned-incorrect rather than serving it to the user. This would operationalize the paper's prudence indicators in real-time, converting its post-hoc reliability audit into a deployable guard rail — especially for the "ultracrepidarian" regime the paper identifies.

Added value to my researchIn my GeoAI pipelines, LLMs are increasingly used for spatial data extraction from unstructured text (e.g., parsing addresses, extracting POI attributes, summarizing policy documents). The paper's finding that shaped-up LLMs hallucinate plausibly in hard cases maps directly onto my use case: a spatially incorrect but fluent address extraction corrupts downstream 2SFCA calculations silently. Semantic entropy as a live confidence score would let me gate which LLM extractions feed into my GeoAI stack and which trigger human review — a reliability-aware data fusion layer.

② Embed difficulty concordance into evaluation incentives to prevent accuracy-driven hallucination

Extending this paperThis paper shows that optimizing accuracy on benchmarks incentivizes models to guess confidently on hard items rather than abstain — the accuracy-avoidance trade-off. A direct extension: redesign evaluation protocols to explicitly reward prudence alongside accuracy, using the paper's six-indicator dashboard as the multi-objective target. The companion finding from the corpus that "evaluating LLMs for accuracy incentivizes hallucinations" exactly corroborates this: if benchmark score is the only signal in RLHF, models rationally learn to hallucinate. Integrating difficulty weights into the reward function — giving harder-instance errors higher penalty and correctly-avoidant responses positive reward — could close this loop.

Added value to my researchWhen I fine-tune geospatial foundation models for tasks like land-use classification or POI attribute extraction, the same accuracy-first training dynamic applies. A difficulty-weighted loss (harder spatial instances penalized more for confident wrong answers) would translate directly into more calibrated fine-tuned models for my spatiotemporal prediction tasks — producing models that say "uncertain" in low-data spatial pockets rather than interpolating incorrectly.

③ Train specialized "reject-option" LLMs for high-stakes domains via narrow-task alignment

Extending this paperThe paper calls for specialized LLMs with "reject options" in critical areas (medicine, law, science), but does not provide a training recipe. The corpus finding that "training LLMs on narrow tasks can lead to broad misalignment" reveals a key tension: narrowly fine-tuned specialist models may avoid general errors but develop unexpected failure modes on out-of-distribution inputs. Extending this paper: design a two-stage training protocol — first fine-tune on domain-specific instances, then adversarially probe for difficulty-discordant failure cases (using the paper's benchmarking methodology) and perform targeted shaping-up specifically to restore avoidance in difficult-instance pockets. This converts the paper's diagnosis into a structured remedy.

Added value to my researchHealthcare accessibility modeling increasingly requires LLM-based parsing of clinical text, insurance policy documents, and patient feedback. A reject-option LLM trained on healthcare text would produce structured extractions with explicit "uncertain" flags for low-confidence spatial entities — exactly what is needed to feed reliable, uncertainty-aware inputs into my 2SFCA service-area delineation models without propagating LLM hallucinations into accessibility scores.

④ Rethink foundation model reliability for healthcare through the paper's prudence-concordance lens

Extending this paperThis paper's reliability framework — correctness, prudence, difficulty concordance, prompting stability — was developed on general benchmarks (addition, anagram, science, etc.). Extending it into the healthcare domain: apply the same three-valued grading and six-indicator dashboard to clinical LLMs and multimodal foundation models, measuring whether medical LLMs also exhibit the ultracrepidarian pattern on hard clinical questions. The corpus companion paper on "foundation models in healthcare require rethinking reliability" argues similarly but lacks the quantitative behavioral decomposition this paper provides — combining both would yield a unified reliability audit framework for healthcare AI.

⑤ Use backpropagating LLM feedback to optimize difficulty concordance during training

Extending this paperThe paper's Discussion proposes that difficulty concordance and human supervision-error rates be incorporated into training loss functions, but leaves the mechanism unspecified. A concrete extension: leverage differentiable LLM feedback (as in the corpus paper on backpropagating language model feedback) to construct a difficulty-aware reward signal. Specifically, use a difficulty-scoring LLM to annotate training instances, then backpropagate a custom loss that penalizes confident-incorrect answers on low-difficulty instances more heavily than on high-difficulty ones — directly operationalizing the paper's F1b finding (improvement happens at hard instances while easy-instance failures persist) as a training objective.

Added value to my researchI use explainable ML (XGBoost, MGWR) ensembles for spatial prediction, and increasingly experiment with LLM-based feature engineering and data augmentation for geospatial tasks. A difficulty-aware training loop — where a meta-LLM assesses which spatial prediction instances are "easy" (dense, well-sampled) vs. "hard" (sparse, out-of-distribution) and adjusts training emphasis accordingly — would align closely with spatially-aware loss functions I already use, extending them into the LLM-augmented GeoAI stack. This would produce fine-tuned models that are more reliably calibrated in data-sparse urban fringe areas, where prediction errors are most consequential for my policy analysis.
