Partial flood defenses shift risks and amplify inequality in a core–periphery city

Ashish S. Kumar · Rajarshi Majumder · Vivek P. Kapadia · Udit Bhatia Published 2025-08-15 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00299-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Partial structural flood defenses in Surat, India lower aggregate flood losses (₹31.24 billion / US$380M in core urban wards; ₹10.34 billion / US$125M in suburbs) but redistribute risk inequitably. Both damage and exposure become more unequal — the Gini index rises ~20% for damage (0.55→0.66) and ~26% for exposure (0.31→0.39). Levees buy protected near-river wards up to 12 extra flood-free hours, while some downstream areas flood up to 7 hours earlier. Defenses that benefit some can escalate vulnerability for others.

  • Exposes the hidden cost of "good news" flood numbers. Aggregate damage-reduction headlines hide that partial levees transfer risk spatially and temporally — net savings can mask growing inequality.
  • Brings a Global South case into a literature dominated by the Global North. Surat, a fast-growing core–periphery Indian city on the Tapi River, is exactly where partial protection is common but understudied.
  • Adds the time dimension to flood equity. Beyond "who floods," the work measures when — early warning lead time itself becomes an unevenly distributed resource via the new protection-induced time shift (PITS).
  • Directly actionable for infrastructure decisions. Quantifies the equity trade-off of staged/partial levee construction, arguing for integrated strategies so localized protection does not externalize harm downstream.

Core Methods

  • Hydrodynamic flood simulation: coupled 1D/2D modeling forced by an extreme (100-year return period) discharge, producing time-resolved inundation depth/extent for baseline vs. partial-levee scenarios.
  • Depth–damage translation: apply depth–damage curves to simulated depths to estimate ward-level economic losses (₹/US$).
  • Exposure mapping: overlay simulated inundation with ward-level demographic data to compute exposed population per scenario.
  • Inequality quantification: compute the Gini index for both damage and exposure to measure distributional shifts induced by partial protection.
  • Flood stripes: a novel visual diagnostic encoding how the non-submerged area evolves over the flood, per ward/scenario.
  • Protection-induced time shift (PITS): a new metric capturing temporal redistribution of flooding — how many hours earlier/later each area floods under levee coverage.

① From one 100-year event to a probabilistic, multi-event risk surface

Extending this paperThe study rests on a single 100-year discharge and two levee scenarios. Its equity conclusions could flip across return periods. A direct extension: run the hydrodynamic engine over an ensemble of return periods and storm hyetographs, then integrate the Gini-of-damage and Gini-of-exposure into expected annual inequality — and couple it to climate-driven changes in flood frequency so the levee verdict is probabilistic, not event-specific.

Added value to my researchThis Monte-Carlo-over-scenarios framing maps onto my spatiotemporal risk and SIR/Monte-Carlo work: expected-value distributional metrics let me report not just mean accessibility but the full risk-equity distribution of a service or hazard.

② Shift the unit of analysis from wards to people and life-activity

Extending this paperThe paper resolves damage/exposure at the static ward level, so an exposed ward is treated as uniformly exposed. Extending it: replace static residential counts with time-resolved population from mobile-phone/GPS trajectories, so that exposure (and the PITS lead-time benefit) is measured against where people actually are when the flood arrives — turning ward exposure into life-activity exposure.

Added value to my researchThis is my multi-source data fusion agenda (mobile-phone records, GPS, social media): a dynamic, behavior-aware exposure layer that I can reuse to make telehealth/primary-care accessibility reflect realized presence rather than night-time residence.

③ Treat protection lead-time (PITS) as an accessibility resource

Extending this paperThe paper's protection-induced time shift shows lead time (+12 h / −7 h) is distributed unevenly — but it stops at description. Extending it: fuse PITS with evacuation accessibility — for each ward, how many people can reach safe ground / shelters within the time the levee buys them — converting the temporal shift into a 2SFCA-style evacuation-supply-to-time-demand ratio.

Added value to my researchThis directly extends my 2SFCA/R2SFCA machinery to a temporal hazard: the "catchment" becomes a shrinking flood-timing window, and supply is shelter capacity — a clean reuse of service-area delineation in disaster geography.

④ Optimize levee placement for efficiency AND equity, not just damage

Extending this paperThe paper evaluates one given partial-levee layout. Extending it: pose levee siting/length as a multi-objective optimization — minimize aggregate damage while constraining the Gini increase — and trace the efficiency–equity Pareto frontier, exposing layouts that protect the core without externalizing 7-hour-earlier floods downstream.

Added value to my researchThis is the same efficiency-vs-equity structure as my EV charging-station optimization; the levee case lets me transplant that Pareto/equity-constrained allocator to grey-infrastructure siting and pair it with explainable ML to attribute which design choices drive the inequality.

⑤ Replace the costly 1D/2D solver with a physics-informed graph surrogate

Extending this paperThe coupled hydrodynamic model is accurate but expensive, which is why the study is confined to one city and one event. Extending it: train a physics-informed graph neural surrogate on the MIKE/HEC-RAS runs — nodes = ward/DEM cells, edges = flow connectivity, with mass/momentum conservation as soft constraints — to emulate inundation and PITS across many cities and many storms at low cost.
