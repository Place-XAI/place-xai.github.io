A virtual rodent predicts the structure of neural activity across behaviours

Diego Aldarondo · Josh Merel · Jesse D. Marshall · Leonard Hasenclever · Ugne Klibaite · Amanda Gellis · Yuval Tassa · Greg Wayne · Matthew Botvinick · Bence P. Ölveczky Published 2024-06-11 DOI 10.1038/s41586-024-07633-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A biomechanically realistic virtual rodent, trained via deep reinforcement learning to imitate freely moving rats, produces network activity that predicts real neural recordings in the sensorimotor striatum (DLS) and motor cortex (MC) better than any kinematic feature of the real rat's movement — consistent with both regions implementing inverse dynamics. Moreover, the network's latent variability replicates the structure of neural variability across behaviours in a manner consistent with the minimal intervention principle of optimal feedback control, demonstrating that physics-based virtual animals are a powerful interpretive lens for systems neuroscience.

Why It Matters

  • Closes the loop between control theory and neuroscience. By building a virtual rat that imitates real behaviour, the paper creates a direct empirical test of theoretical motor-control principles — inverse dynamics and the minimal intervention principle — something previously impossible with kinematic data alone.
  • The virtual agent outperforms movement features. The finding that artificial network activity predicts real neural activity better than actual body kinematics is a striking result: it implies the brain encodes control commands, not just movement trajectories.
  • Latent variability as a functional signal. Neural variability is usually treated as noise; this work shows the virtual rodent's latent stochasticity mirrors the structure of real neural variability across behaviours — recasting variability as a purposeful component of the control architecture.
  • Scales across the full behavioural repertoire. The MIMIC pipeline covers the complete ethogram of freely moving rats (locomotion, grooming, rearing, and more), not a single constrained task — making the conclusions broadly applicable.
  • Open data and reusable tools. Neural recordings are publicly available on Harvard Dataverse; code for STAC (skeletal registration) and motion-mapper (behaviour classification) is open-source — enabling community reuse and extension.

Core Methods

  • MIMIC pipeline: an end-to-end framework — Multi-camera video acquisition → Inverse kinematics (STAC) → Motion imitation via deep RL → Interpretation via Comparison of neural activity.
  • Deep reinforcement learning (RL) for motion imitation: a variational autoencoder (VAE) + LSTM decoder controller is trained with RL to match the rat's pose trajectory frame by frame, minimizing imitation error while actuating a physics-constrained biomechanical model.
  • Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) for neural prediction: spike counts of individual neurons are predicted from either kinematic features (joint angles, velocities) or ANN network features (hidden-layer activations of the controller). Cross-validated log-likelihood ratios (CV-LLR) quantify predictive gain.
  • Representational Similarity Analysis (RSA): representational dissimilarity matrices (RDMs) are built from both neural population activity and ANN layer activations; WUC (weighted uncentred correlation) similarity between RDMs tests whether the network's representational geometry matches neural geometry across behaviours.
  • Latent variability analysis: the VAE's KL regularization controls the amount of latent noise; the resulting motor variability is compared to neural variability structure to test the minimal intervention principle — i.e., whether variability is suppressed for task-critical dimensions and amplified along task-irrelevant ones.
  • Inverse vs. forward vs. sequential model comparison: alternative control architectures (forward model, sequential model) are trained on the same data and evaluated on both imitation performance and neural predictivity, establishing that an inverse dynamics model best explains the data.

① Scale MIMIC to a multi-agent, multi-timescale RL framework

Extending this paperThe virtual rodent is trained as a single-agent, single-timescale imitation learner. The brain, however, operates across multiple temporal scales simultaneously — from milliseconds (motor reflexes) to seconds (action planning) to minutes (habit formation). A direct extension: replace the paper's single LSTM decoder with a hierarchical, multi-timescale RL controller where higher layers set behavioural goals and lower layers execute them. This would test whether multi-timescale network activity better predicts the temporal structure of DLS/MC recordings, and whether distinct neural subpopulations align with distinct controller levels.

Added value to my researchThe multi-timescale decomposition is directly analogous to my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation work, where different bandwidth selections capture different interaction scales. A hierarchical temporal model trained on movement trajectories could inform how I parameterize temporal decay in spatiotemporal accessibility models — particularly for health-seeking behaviour where decisions unfold over multiple time horizons (real-time, weekly routine, seasonal).

Cross-ref (corpus): Multi-timescale reinforcement learning in the brain — 10.1038/s41586-025-08929-9

② Use population-level neural geometry to benchmark foundation models of brain activity

Extending this paperThe paper's RSA framework compares an ANN controller's representational geometry to real neural population geometry. This is a principled neural-benchmarking approach that has been used in vision neuroscience but rarely in motor neuroscience at this scale. An extension: train a foundation model of motor-cortical neural activity (analogous to visual cortex foundation models) using the paper's open Harvard Dataverse recordings, then evaluate whether its learned representations generalise to predict responses to novel movement types not seen during training — directly extending the paper's MIMIC framework from a comparison tool to a generative neural model.

Added value to my researchFoundation model ideas transfer to my interest in fine-tuning geospatial foundation models. Just as a motor-cortex foundation model must generalise across behavioural contexts, a geospatial foundation model must generalise across urban contexts. The paper's RSA benchmarking pipeline — comparing representational geometry across conditions — could be adapted to evaluate geospatial embeddings: do representations learned in one city transfer their geometric structure to another?

Cross-ref (corpus): Foundation model of neural activity predicts response to new stimulus types — 10.1038/s41586-025-08829-y

③ Bridge virtual-animal simulation to arousal and state-dependent neural variability

Extending this paperThe paper's latent variability analysis shows that the virtual rodent regulates motor noise in a behaviourally structured way. But it treats arousal and internal brain state as implicit background variables. Extending it: inject an arousal signal (pupil dilation, heartrate proxy, or a learned latent arousal embedding) into the VAE's prior, and test whether the resulting latent-variability structure better predicts the across-behaviour neural variability observed in real animals — particularly for transitions between active and quiescent states where arousal fluctuates most.

Added value to my researchInternal state modulation of variability parallels how human mobility patterns shift under different contextual states (commute vs. leisure, weekday vs. weekend). My agent-based models of urban accessibility could incorporate a "behavioural state" variable — analogous to arousal — that modulates the distance-decay function, allowing the model to capture why the same individual accesses facilities differently under different motivational states.

④ Transfer the MIMIC benchmarking philosophy to explainable AI for spatial prediction

Extending this paperThe paper's core insight is methodological: build a model that mimics the system, then interrogate the model's internals to understand the real system. This "mimicry-then-interpret" philosophy could be extended beyond motor control to social behaviour. A follow-up: apply MIMIC to a model trained to imitate human social interactions recorded via GPS/mobile sensors, and ask whether network representations of a virtual social agent predict the structure of neural activity in social brain regions — linking the virtual-animal paradigm to human behaviour in naturalistic environments.

Added value to my researchThe mimicry-then-interpret philosophy is exactly the logic of my explainable machine learning for spatial prediction work: train a flexible ML model, then use SHAP/attention maps to interpret which spatial features drive predictions. The paper validates this approach at a neuroscientific level — the model's internal activations are the interpretable signal, not just the output. This gives theoretical grounding for reporting SHAP feature importances as genuine mechanistic indicators rather than post-hoc correlations.

⑤ Encode physics-of-movement priors into spatiotemporal mobility graph learning

Extending this paperThe virtual rodent's controller is physically constrained: it must respect joint limits, inertia, and the biomechanics of the rat skeleton — and these constraints are encoded as soft losses during RL training, analogous to physics-informed learning. A direct extension of the paper: add a musculoskeletal energy constraint as an explicit Lagrangian term so that the optimal controller minimises both imitation error and metabolic cost, then test whether the resulting energy-aware representations better predict the speed-dependent encoding patterns in DLS/MC seen in Extended Data Fig. 3C.

Added value to my researchThe analogy to my physics-informed graph learning agenda is tight: just as the virtual rodent encodes biomechanical laws as constraints on ANN weights, a physics-informed GNN for urban mobility can encode conservation laws (flow conservation at nodes, capacity constraints on edges) as architectural priors rather than regularisation terms. The paper's demonstration that physics constraints improve biological realism supports the premise that physics priors improve generalisation in urban graph models — a strong argument for my ongoing work on physics-informed GNNs for origin-destination flow prediction.
