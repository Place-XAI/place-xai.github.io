A thalamus–brainstem attractor network drives history-biased decisions

Shan Zhao · Heying Shan · Xiao Liu · Yu Qian · Jingyao Huang · Yi-Ran Liu · Zhenfei Jiao · Lichen Ye · Lin Cong · Xiaoou Wang · Zhi-Yuan Wang · Danyang Li · Ming-Quan Chen · Kai Wang · Ling Fu · Xu-Fei Du · Si Wu · Yu Mu Published 2026-06-10 DOI 10.1038/s41586-026-10623-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A hierarchical thalamus–brainstem attractor–integrator circuit underlies history-biased decisions: discrete attractors in the dorsal thalamus encode the most recent sensory event as persistent categorical memory (lasting 10–20 s), while a downstream hindbrain integrator accumulates multi-trial history and combines it with incoming sensory cues. Optogenetic manipulation causally confirms that the dorsal thalamus drives serial dependence. A whole-brain computational model — built on the larval zebrafish brain atlas — recapitulates this behaviour and identifies heterogeneous inhibitory subtypes as the key mechanism enabling flexible state transitions.

★

Why It Matters

  • First circuit-level causal account of serial dependence. Serial dependence — the tendency to let recent experience bias current decisions — is a ubiquitous motif in neuroscience, but this paper is the first to pinpoint the dedicated circuit (dorsal thalamus + hindbrain integrator) that implements it, moving from correlation to causal mechanism via optogenetics.
  • Unifies two canonical models of memory. The attractor–integrator architecture bridges categorical working memory (bistable, robust, history-specific) and graded integration (flexible, multi-trial) in a single modular circuit — a conceptual advance for theories of memory-guided action.
  • Provides a normative advantage for serial bias. Agent-simulation data show that exploiting recent history (serial dependence) outperforms purely reactive navigation in spatially regular environments, grounding a neural bias as an adaptive strategy.
  • Identifies inhibitory diversity as a tuning knob. The model predicts — and anatomy supports — that distinct fast and slow GABAergic subtypes in the dorsal thalamus enable flexible state transitions, opening a new target for understanding memory disorders.
  • Whole-brain atlas approach enables cross-region resolution. By combining light-sheet imaging at cellular resolution across the entire zebrafish brain with a comprehensive connectome atlas, the work offers a template for systems-level dissection of other cognitive functions.

Core Methods

  • Whole-brain light-sheet calcium imaging: custom selective plane illumination microscope (SPIM) enables simultaneous single-neuron-resolution recording across the entire larval zebrafish brain during virtual-reality behaviour. Fictive motor signals from tail nerve roots decode navigation in real time.
  • Principal component analysis & linear discriminant analysis of population activity: PC projections visualise state-space trajectories of neural ensembles; a discriminability index quantifies how well each brain region encodes left vs. right trial history across the inter-obstacle interval.
  • Blind sparse deconvolution (BSD): estimates the intrinsic activity timescale of individual DT neurons from spontaneous activity, providing a cellular-level measure of memory persistence.
  • Optogenetic manipulation (inhibition & activation): DT-selective transgenic GtACR2/ChrimsonR lines and holographic stimulation of the hindbrain integrator causally test the roles of each circuit node in serial dependence, controlling for motor and locomotor confounds.
  • Whole-brain spiking neural network (SNN) model: a three-layer hierarchical attractor–integrator architecture, with parameters constrained by the zebrafish brain atlas. The attractor layer (L2) uses recurrent excitation and heterogeneous GABAergic inhibition; the integrator layer (L3) uses NMDA recurrence. Model reproduces behaviour and predicts the role of inhibitory subtypes.
  • Agent-based simulation for adaptive advantage: serial-dependent vs. reactive navigation agents are benchmarked across obstacle maps with varying spatial regularity (parameter σ), demonstrating the fitness advantage of history exploitation in regular environments.

① Extract the attractor–integrator architecture as a spatiotemporal memory module for GeoAI

Extending this paperThe paper's key computational insight — a discrete attractor encodes categorical state robustly while a graded integrator accumulates history — is an abstract circuit motif, not a zebrafish-specific finding. A direct extension is to test whether this two-module architecture generalises to other animals and to artificial agents: train a recurrent neural network with separate attractor (low-rank, bistable) and integrator (NMDA-like slow recurrence) modules on sequential decision tasks, and compare to standard LSTMs. This would operationalise the paper's own claim of a "general principle" by benchmarking it computationally across tasks.

Added value to my researchThe attractor–integrator decomposition maps naturally onto spatiotemporal modelling: the attractor module could encode discrete urban-state categories (e.g., land-use regimes, neighborhood typologies) that persist across time steps, while the integrator module accumulates fine-grained mobility or health signals. This is a concrete architectural blueprint for a memory-augmented spatiotemporal graph network that I can plug into my physics-informed graph-learning pipeline — giving it robust categorical memory over slow-changing urban structure while remaining sensitive to recent sensor data.

② Use the paper's inhibitory-subtype heterogeneity prediction to guide explainable circuit inversion

Extending this paperThe model predicts that two distinct GABAergic subtypes — fast transient and slow sustained — cooperate to enable flexible state transitions in the dorsal thalamus. The paper validates this anatomically but does not perturb the subtypes independently in vivo. An extension: use intersectional genetics or cell-type-specific two-photon ablation to selectively silence each subtype and quantify the predicted effects on bistability and transition speed. This would transform the model's prediction into a precise, testable mechanistic claim and strengthen the paper's broader theoretical impact.

The paper's insight that heterogeneous temporal dynamics within a population enable flexible state switching motivates a multi-timescale MGWR with adaptive bandwidth schedules, where the "subtype" analogy becomes a regulariser that allows rapid local adaptation without erasing global spatial context.

④ Formalise serial dependence as a multi-timescale temporal credit-assignment problem

Extending this paperThe paper's agent simulation shows that serial dependence is adaptive in spatially regular environments. Yet the paper does not cast this in a formal learning framework: when should the brain up-weight recent history and when should it suppress it? Extending the paper: reframe serial dependence as a multi-timescale eligibility trace problem — the dorsal thalamus provides the trace for the most recent trial, and the hindbrain integrator maintains an exponential decay over earlier trials. Fitting an optimal forgetting-rate to the behavioural data would reveal the environmental statistics the brain is implicitly inferring, and could be tested by manipulating obstacle sequence regularity (the paper's σ parameter).

Added value to my researchThis formalisation maps onto spatiotemporal kernel density estimation in my work: the temporal bandwidth of the kernel determines how much past observations influence the current estimate — exactly the forgetting-rate problem above. The paper's attractor as a "hard reset" trigger (one switch clears the integrator) suggests a change-point detection strategy: detect structural breaks in spatiotemporal data and reset kernel memory, rather than using a fixed bandwidth — a methodological improvement for outbreak detection or urban mobility shifts.

⑤ Translate the paper's bistable attractor concept into a categorical health-state encoder for urban health geography

Extending this paperThe dorsal thalamus in this paper acts as a categorical memory: it collapses a continuous sensory experience into a discrete, persistent left-or-right label. A direct conceptual extension is to ask whether the same principle — a low-dimensional discrete state that persists and biases future processing — applies in cortical circuits underlying more complex categorical judgements (e.g., "safe" vs. "threatening" environments). Mapping this onto perceptual decision experiments in humans would test whether the attractor–integrator motif is phylogenetically and phenomenologically universal.

Added value to my researchThe paper's attractor as a categorical health-state encoder is a direct inspiration for my health geography work: urban areas can be modelled as having latent discrete health-risk states (e.g., "deprived," "transitional," "resilient") that persist over time and bias future health outcomes — analogous to the DT's bistable L/R memory.
