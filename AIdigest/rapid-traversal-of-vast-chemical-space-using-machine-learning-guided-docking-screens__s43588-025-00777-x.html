Rapid traversal of vast chemical space using machine learning-guided docking screens

Andreas Luttens · Israel Cabeza de Vaca · Leonard Sparring · José Brea · Antón Leandro Martínez · Nour Aldin Kahlous · Dmytro S. Radchenko · Yurii S. Moroz · María Isabel Loza · Ulf Norinder · Jens Carlsson Published 2025-03-13 DOI 10.1038/s43588-025-00777-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A workflow combining Mondrian conformal prediction with CatBoost classification on Morgan2 fingerprints can reduce the computational cost of structure-based virtual screening of a 3.5-billion-compound library by more than 1,000-fold, while retaining 80–90% of the top-scoring compounds. Prospective application to G protein-coupled receptors (D2R and A2AR) identified novel agonists and, strikingly, a dual-target ligand for Parkinson's disease, demonstrating that access to multi-billion-scale chemical libraries via ML-guided docking can unlock complex polypharmacology that smaller libraries cannot deliver.

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the billion-compound barrier. Make-on-demand chemical libraries now exceed 70 billion compounds and are growing toward the trillion scale; even the fastest docking methods cannot enumerate them. This workflow is the first to screen 3.5 billion compounds at modest computational cost (~10,000 core-hours per target, vs. tens of millions for brute-force docking).
  • Calibrated uncertainty, not just a score. By using the conformal prediction framework, the method provides guaranteed error rates for both the active and inactive classes — a rare statistical validity guarantee in virtual screening.
  • Bridges computational and experimental drug discovery. Predictions are followed by make-on-demand synthesis (4–5 weeks) and radioligand binding assays, yielding actual Ki values for D2R agonists — a complete validation loop rarely completed in computational screening papers.
  • Multi-target polypharmacology from a single screen. The identification of an A2AR–D2R dual-target ligand illustrates how vast chemical space, combined with ML guidance, can identify compounds with correlated binding profiles that are statistically rare in smaller libraries.
  • Fully open science. Benchmarking datasets for eight diverse protein targets (88 million protein–ligand complexes) and source code are deposited on Zenodo and GitHub, enabling reproducibility and community benchmarking.

Core Methods

  • Molecular docking (DOCK3.7): structure-based scoring of 1 million training compounds per target to the protein binding site; scores used to label virtual actives (top 1%) and inactives. Benchmarked against 8 therapeutically relevant proteins.
  • Molecular featurization: three descriptor types compared — Morgan2 (ECFP4) fingerprints (RDKit), continuous data-driven descriptors (CDDD), and transformer-based embeddings (RoBERTa); Morgan2 emerged as best-performing and most efficient.
  • CatBoost gradient boosting classification: five independent classifiers trained per target on 80%/20% proper-training/calibration splits; ensemble P-values aggregated by median across models.
  • Mondrian conformal prediction (CP) framework: provides class-specific calibrated P-values (P1 for virtual active, P0 for virtual inactive) with guaranteed error rate ≤ ε; compounds assigned to one of four sets (virtual active, inactive, both, null). Significance level ε tunable to control database reduction vs. recall trade-off.
  • Quality-of-information ranking: compounds in the predicted-active set sorted by P1 − P0 (confidence margin) to further reduce the docking workload; >90% of top-10,000 molecules recovered after evaluating 3–5% of the library.
  • Dual-target polypharmacology screen: compounds ranked by sum of quality-of-information across two targets (A2AR + D2R) and top 5 million docked; 191-fold enrichment of dual virtual actives achieved.
  • Experimental validation: radioligand competition binding assays (D2R: [3H]-spiperone; A2AR: [3H]-ZM241385) and cAMP functional assays; 31 compounds tested for D2R, 45 for dual-target screen.
  • Comparison and benchmarking: performance assessed via sensitivity, precision, efficiency, and prediction error rate; exchangeability criterion verified; model robustness to noise, scrambling, and hyperparameter variation tested.

① Replace fixed Morgan2 fingerprints with graph-neural-network molecular representations

Extending this paperThe paper's conformal predictor relies on static Morgan2 (ECFP4) fingerprints and demonstrates they outperform more recent data-driven descriptors in this setting — but this benchmark was conducted with relatively simple architectures. A direct extension of the paper: replace the fingerprint input layer with a graph neural network (GNN) that learns message-passing representations from the atom graph, then feeds the learned embedding into the CatBoost/CP stack. GNNs trained on protein–ligand affinity datasets (e.g., MISATO) carry structural priors that Morgan2 lacks, potentially improving recall for structurally novel chemotypes the conformal predictor currently misses at the tails of chemical space.

Added value to my researchThis parallels my interest in physics-informed graph learning: just as I embed spatial priors (network topology, density fields) into graph operators for urban accessibility, one could embed protein–ligand interaction physics as constraints on the GNN message-passing rules — yielding a structure-aware, calibrated virtual screener. The conformal calibration layer translates directly to my need for prediction-interval guarantees in spatiotemporal models.

② Extend the conformal prediction framework to out-of-distribution chemical space detection

Extending this paperThe paper validates the CP framework on test sets drawn from the same distribution as the training library (ZINC15/Enamine REAL). However, the authors note that effectiveness is target-dependent and that ML acts as a proxy for the training-distribution docking landscape. When the multi-billion library contains chemotypes far from the training set actives (a genuine risk with >3.5 billion compounds), recall degrades silently. A direct extension: use the paper's UMAP and Tanimoto analysis (Fig. 3d–e) as an explicit out-of-distribution (OOD) detector — flagging compounds outside the applicability domain and routing them to direct docking instead of relying solely on the CP assignment. This would make the workflow's uncertainty quantification truly adaptive.

Added value to my researchOOD detection is a central challenge in my fine-tuning of geospatial foundation models: spatial predictions degrade when a neighborhood's feature distribution shifts outside the training corpus (e.g., new urban form, post-disaster changes). The CP-based applicability-domain concept from this paper can be imported as a spatial OOD alarm — conformal coverage rates become a map of model reliability, guiding where re-calibration or additional ground-truth data is needed.

③ Convert the single-step classifier into an active-learning loop for progressive chemical space coverage

Extending this paperThe paper's workflow is a one-shot screen: train on 1M, predict the rest, dock the shortlist. A natural extension is to iterate: after docking the top-5M shortlist, retrain the conformal predictor on the enlarged labeled set (now 6M compounds), re-score the remaining billions, and dock a second smaller shortlist. This active deep learning loop would progressively explore structurally diverse regions the initial model rates as uncertain (P1 ≈ P0 / "null" class), recovering compounds with novel scaffolds that the one-shot model systematically undervalues — directly addressing the diversity limitation acknowledged in the discussion (13% unique Bemis–Murcko scaffolds vs. 23% for pure docking).

Added value to my researchActive learning loops mirror my multi-source data fusion agenda: in spatiotemporal prediction, I progressively incorporate mobile phone records, social media, and remote sensing in an iterative refinement cycle. The same logic — uncertainty-driven query selection followed by calibration update — can tighten 2SFCA service-area estimates each time new GPS or POI data arrives, replacing static model fits with an online, uncertainty-aware facility-access predictor.

④ Apply the dual-target scoring strategy to spatial multi-objective facility siting

Extending this paperSection "Machine learning-guided design of polypharmacology" introduces a two-target ranking score (PA2AR,1 − PA2AR,0 + PD2R,1 − PD2R,0) — a linear scalarization of quality-of-information across objectives. The paper shows this achieves 191-fold enrichment of dual virtual actives. Extending it: generalize to N ≥ 3 simultaneous targets using Pareto-front sampling instead of linear scalarization, enabling explicit trade-off visualization between binding objectives — especially relevant when selectivity against an off-target is needed alongside potency for two on-targets.

Added value to my researchThe dual-score aggregation is structurally identical to multi-objective facility-location optimization in my EV-charging-station siting work, where efficiency (serving demand) and equity (covering underserved populations) are two competing objectives. The paper's sum-of-quality-of-information trick is a fast linear scalarization; importing Pareto-front enumeration from my optimization toolkit into the docking-shortlist step — and, conversely, importing the CP calibration guarantee into my siting framework — creates a bidirectional methodological bridge between cheminformatics and spatial optimization.

⑤ Embed the workflow's confidence maps into a spatial accessibility analogy for health-resource allocation

Extending this paperThe paper's conformal predictor outputs a 2D map over (P1, P0) space for every compound — a calibrated confidence landscape of chemical space. An underexplored extension: visualize this landscape over the UMAP projection (Fig. 3d) as a confidence density map, then use it to guide synthesis diversity: instead of always picking the highest-confidence virtual actives (structurally similar to training actives), deliberately sample from medium-confidence but novel-scaffold regions, converting the uncertainty map into a scaffold-exploration strategy and increasing the novelty of identified hits.
