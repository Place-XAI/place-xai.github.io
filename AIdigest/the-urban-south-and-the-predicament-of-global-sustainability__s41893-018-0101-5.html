The urban south and the predicament of global sustainability

Harini Nagendra · Xuemei Bai · Eduardo S. Brondizio · Shuaib Lwasa Published 2018-07-16 DOI 10.1038/s41893-018-0101-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Urban growth since the 1970s has been overwhelmingly concentrated in the global south, yet the knowledge system governing urban sustainability is dominated by the global north — in authorship, theory-building, and citation influence. A meta-analysis of the 100–1,000 most-cited urban sustainability papers on Web of Science (2007–2017) shows that 78% have a global-north first author and that cities of sub-Saharan Africa and the Amazon remain near-invisible in high-impact literature. Because global-south urban challenges (informality, infrastructure deficits, governance diversity) are structurally different from northern ones, northern-derived solutions do not translate, creating a dangerous knowledge-practice gap. The authors call for systemic reform of journal editorial boards, funding structures, academic incentives, and capacity-building to center global-south scholarship.

Why It Matters

  • Quantifies a structural knowledge bias. Using two independent meta-analyses of the top 100 and top 1,000 most-cited papers, the study demonstrates that 78% of influential urban sustainability research originates from global-north institutions — turning a perception into a statistically documented fact.
  • Stakes are planetary-scale. Ninety percent of the projected 2.5-billion urban population increase will occur in African and Asian cities by 2040; frameworks calibrated on European/North American cities cannot adequately guide this growth.
  • Reveals overlooked innovation capacity. The paper reframes global-south cities — typically characterized as problem-laden — as sites of radical frugal and grassroots innovation (bus rapid transit, sponge cities, mobile payment, water ATMs), undermining deficit narratives.
  • Offers actionable structural levers. Rather than abstract advocacy, the paper proposes four concrete mechanisms: editorial board reform, equal-access international funding, de-centering Web-of-Science metrics in evaluation, and south-to-south capacity building.

Core Methods

  • Statistical comparison of urban indicators: Wilcoxon–Mann–Whitney nonparametric tests used to assess whether inter-regional differences in CPI sub-indices are statistically significant at P < 0.05, comparing global north versus Africa, Latin America, and Asia sub-groups.
  • Bibliometric meta-analysis: systematic coding of the top 100 and top 1,000 most-cited Web of Science articles on urban sustainability (2007–2017) by first-author regional affiliation, geographic focus of study, and citation count — enabling quantitative decomposition of the knowledge production bias.
  • Comparative authorship analysis: authorship shares from global north vs. south compared across three thematic foci (global/north issues, China, rest of global south), reported in Table 1.
  • Narrative synthesis and case documentation: systematic review of published examples of global-south urban innovations (Curitiba BRT, sponge cities, water ATMs, mobile payment, bamboo bikes) to ground the "innovation capacity" argument.
  • Editorial board audit: institutional-affiliation mapping of editorial board members of the 59 journals publishing the top 100 cited papers, providing structural evidence of north-dominated gatekeeping.

② Build the paper's innovation inventory into a spatially explicit diffusion model

Extending this paperThe paper lists notable global-south urban innovations (Curitiba BRT, sponge cities, bamboo bikes, water ATMs) but treats them as isolated vignettes. A structural extension: compile a georeferenced database of documented global-south urban innovations and fit a spatial diffusion model to track how innovations spread within and across southern cities over time — testing whether adoption follows contiguous geographic proximity, GDP proximity, or institutional linkage. The paper's own framing of south-to-south knowledge transfer provides the normative motivation; the diffusion model would give it empirical teeth.

③ Extend the CPI benchmark to slum-disaggregated spatial inequality within cities

Extending this paperThe paper's core quantitative analysis (Figs. 2–3) uses the UN-Habitat CPI at the city aggregate level, which masks severe intra-urban inequality between formal and informal settlements. A key extension: disaggregate the CPI sub-indices to the neighborhood or settlement level using satellite-derived slum boundaries and integrate them with the paper's framework. The corpus paper on mapping urban slums in sub-Saharan Africa (Nature Cities, 2025) provides precisely this sub-city resolution for a large sample of African cities, enabling a within-city version of the paper's inter-regional analysis.

Disaggregating to slum vs. formal settlement resolution would make my service-area delineation informal-settlement-aware — a major step toward equitable healthcare facility access modeling in low-income urban contexts, and a natural extension of my healthy cities and 15-minute neighborhood work to global-south settings.

④ Apply explainable ML to disentangle the drivers of the citation-reward gradient

Extending this paperThe paper shows (Fig. 4) that north-authored papers receive more citations, but treats authorship geography as the sole explanatory variable. A richer extension: train a gradient-boosted or random forest model on the top-1,000 paper corpus, using features including author affiliation region, study geography, journal prestige, topic category, year, and network embeddedness, and apply SHAP attribution to quantify each feature's contribution to citation count. This would decompose the citation gap into structural bias vs. confounders (e.g., language, journal prestige), making the paper's argument more actionable for editors and funders.

Added value to my researchExplainable ML for spatial prediction is a core method in my toolkit. Applying it here to a bibliometric corpus extends the method to a new domain — science-of-science — and produces a concrete demonstration that SHAP-attributed ML can reveal structural inequities in academic systems, complementing my use of GeoAI for revealing spatial inequities in urban service access and public health.
