Socio-demographic gaps in pain management guided by large language models

Mahmud Omar · Shelly Soffer · Reem Agbareia · Nicola Luigi Bragazzi · Benjamin S. Glicksberg · Yasmin L. Hurd · Donald U. Apakama · Alexander W. Charney · David L. Reich · Girish N. Nadkarni · Eyal Klang Published 2026-02-06 DOI 10.1038/s44360-025-00017-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Ten LLMs tested on 3.4 million responses to 1,000 acute-pain vignettes systematically diverge from clinical guidelines along socio-demographic lines: historically marginalized groups — particularly unhoused individuals, Black patients, and LGBTQIA+ individuals — receive disproportionately high opioid recommendations (sometimes >90% in cancer settings) even when the same models flag them as high-risk, while low-income and unemployed groups receive elevated risk scores yet fewer opioid prescriptions. These internally contradictory patterns reveal model-driven bias, not acceptable clinical variation, underscoring the urgent need for rigorous bias audits and guideline-anchored checks before LLMs are deployed in pain care.

Core Methods

  • Structured clinical vignette design: 1,000 acute-pain scenarios (500 cancer, 500 non-cancer) with standardized clinical details, each varied across 34 socio-demographic dimensions (race, housing, income, employment, sexual orientation, control) to isolate the effect of demographic labelling on LLM output.
  • Multi-model parallel prompting: all vignette-demographic combinations submitted to ten LLMs simultaneously, ensuring a common experimental base across open-access and closed-source models.
  • Logistic mixed-effects models: binary outcomes (opioid recommended: yes/no; anxiety treatment: yes/no) modeled with demographic group, cancer/non-cancer status, and their interaction as fixed effects, and model identity as a random effect.
  • Linear mixed-effects models: continuous outcomes (addiction risk score, psychological stress rating, monitoring intensity) with the same fixed-effect structure.
  • Guideline benchmarking: LLM recommendations compared against ASCO (ref 24), NCCN (ref 50), and WHO (ref 51) pain management guidelines to distinguish model-driven bias from clinically justified variation.
  • Cancer vs. non-cancer stratification: analyses run separately by diagnosis type and interaction terms tested to determine whether cancer diagnosis amplifies or attenuates observed demographic disparities.

⑤ Build an explainable bias dashboard that attributes demographic disparities to specific prompt tokens

Extending this paperThe paper quantifies that bias exists but not where in the LLM's reasoning it originates — which demographic tokens (e.g., "unhoused," "Black," "LGBTQIA+") drive the largest output shifts. An extension: apply token-level attribution methods (SHAP, attention rollout, or integrated gradients over the vignette text) to the paper's ten models to produce a bias attribution map, ranking which demographic labels most strongly alter each outcome dimension. This converts the paper's aggregate statistics into mechanistic insight and a practical red-teaming tool.
