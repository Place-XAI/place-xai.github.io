Author Correction: Predicting the state of charge and health of batteries using data-driven machine learning

Man-Fai Ng · Jin Zhao · Qingyu Yan · Gareth J. Conduit · Zhi Wei Seh Published 2020-06-12 DOI 10.1038/s42256-020-0191-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

This is an Author Correction to the original Review article 10.1038/s42256-020-0156-7 (published 2020-03-02). The original review establishes that data-driven machine learning — spanning linear regression, random forests, support-vector machines, Gaussian processes, and neural networks — can achieve mean prediction errors of approximately 4% for SOC, 5% for SOH, and 4.1% for RUL of Li-ion batteries, outperforming conventional equivalent-circuit and physics-based models in speed–accuracy trade-offs, while identifying explainable inference, in-situ embedded computation, and high-throughput data generation as the three principal remaining challenges.

Why It Matters

  • Bridges modelling paradigms for battery management. The review consolidates three competing model families — equivalent-circuit models (ECMs), physics-based models (PBMs), and data-driven models (DDMs) — and locates each on the accuracy-vs-CPU-time frontier, giving practitioners a clear decision framework.
  • Benchmarks five ML families on a common task. By tabulating 30+ studies (Table 2) across linear regression, random forest, SVM, Gaussian process, and neural networks against SOC/SOH/RUL targets, the paper provides the first systematic comparison for battery state prediction published in a high-impact AI venue.
  • Names three unsolved "holy grail" challenges. Holistic multi-scale/multi-timescale modelling, in-situ lightweight inference on embedded devices, and high-throughput automated data generation are identified as critical bottlenecks shaping the research agenda for years after publication.
  • Links domain knowledge to interpretability. The paper explicitly advocates moving from black-box neural networks toward explainable "white box" ML that incorporates electrochemical physics — a prescient position now realized in physics-informed ML for batteries.
  • Directly enables EV commercialization. Accurate SOC and SOH predictions alleviate range anxiety, enable second-life battery redeployment for stationary grid storage, and accelerate cell-sorting in manufacturing — all with concrete commercial and sustainability impact.

Core Methods

  • Taxonomy of battery state models: structured comparison of ECM (Rint, Randles, Thevenin resistor–capacitor networks), PBM (pseudo-two-dimensional P2D, single-particle model SPM, reduced-order models), and DDM (ML-based) mapped on an accuracy-vs-CPU-time plot.
  • ML technique survey: systematic review of five ML families applied to SOC, SOH, and RUL prediction: linear/polynomial regression, random forest / decision trees, support-vector machines, Gaussian processes, and neural networks (CNN, RNN/LSTM).
  • Validation protocols: documents hold-out (80/20 train–test) and cross-validation (k-fold) procedures; highlights over-fitting risks on small battery datasets.
  • Hybrid PBM+ML strategy: proposes combining physics-based models with ML to capture residual model–data discrepancy — improving accuracy, interpretability, and reducing over-fitting simultaneously.
  • High-throughput data pipeline: describes automated synthesis, in-situ characterization (XRD, XAS), fabrication, and electrochemical testing workflows for generating large standardized ML training corpora.
  • Active learning cycle: sketches a closed-loop workflow where ML prediction uncertainty triggers new simulations or experiments, progressively reducing the data burden for accurate state prediction.

① From black-box SOC prediction to spatially-aware, in-field battery diagnostics for EV infrastructure

Extending this paperThe review explicitly identifies in-situ, on-device inference as a holy-grail challenge: ML models must run on lightweight embedded controllers with limited RAM and latency constraints. A direct extension: recast the best-performing neural-network SOC predictor as a quantized, edge-deployable model and couple it with real-world GPS trajectories from EV fleets to learn route-conditioned SOC dynamics, where road topology and elevation become covariates. The paper already identifies high-throughput experimental data as the bottleneck; large-scale EV telemetry solves that problem while adding spatial structure the original review lacks.

Added value to my researchMy work on EV charging-station efficiency–equity optimization requires knowing not just where chargers are placed but where batteries will actually need charging — a demand signal tied to SOC dynamics along real routes. A route-conditioned SOC predictor trained on GPS data would yield a spatially-explicit battery-depletion map to feed my facility-location optimizer, directly bridging battery ML and GeoAI.

② Gaussian-process SOH uncertainty as a spatial equity signal for second-life battery deployment

Extending this paperThe review highlights Gaussian processes as uniquely capable of producing calibrated uncertainty estimates for SOH — critical for safety-sensitive applications. An extension: deploy GP-SOH models across a heterogeneous fleet of retired EV batteries and ask which batteries are safe for stationary grid storage in which locations. This turns GP uncertainty into a spatial assignment problem: match batteries to second-life use cases (solar microgrid, emergency backup) based on remaining capacity and confidence bounds, minimizing wasted lifetime and stranded energy resources.

Added value to my researchUncertainty-aware spatial assignment is structurally identical to my R2SFCA supply–demand matching: instead of matching healthcare supply to population demand, we match battery supply (capacity, uncertainty) to energy-access demand across communities. GP uncertainty propagates directly into 2SFCA-style catchment weights, producing equity-aware second-life deployment plans — a new application domain for spatial accessibility methods.

③ Physics-informed graph learning for multi-cell battery pack state estimation

Extending this paperAll ML models surveyed operate on individual cells; real battery packs contain dozens to thousands of cells in series/parallel with coupled thermal and electrical interactions. The paper's call for "holistic modelling across length scales" (Section: Future outlook) is naturally addressed by a physics-informed graph neural network (PI-GNN) where nodes are cells, edges encode thermal and electrical coupling, and the P2D electrochemical equations serve as soft physics constraints. The paper's own hybrid PBM+ML idea sketches this direction for single cells but does not extend it to the pack level.

Added value to my researchThis PI-GNN formulation directly advances my physics-informed graph learning agenda: the same architecture that encodes electrochemical conservation laws for battery packs can encode spatial interaction laws (gravity, flow balance) for urban accessibility networks. Battery packs provide a cleaner, physics-grounded test bed for validating PI-GNN designs before deploying them in GeoAI contexts where ground truth is messier and spatial relationships are less precisely defined.

④ Explainable ML for battery health as a template for spatially-explicit health prediction

Extending this paperThe review warns that neural network SOH predictions are "black boxes" and advocates for models where physical variables (voltage curve shape, capacity fade slope) appear as named, interpretable features. A concrete extension: apply SHAP or inherently interpretable tree ensembles (e.g., XGBoost with monotonicity constraints encoding degradation physics) to public battery datasets, recovering electrochemical features ranked by importance — then test whether the same feature set transfers across battery chemistries via domain adaptation. This operationalizes the paper's interpretability call with reproducible, open-source code.

Added value to my researchMy explainable ML for spatial prediction work faces the same interpretability–accuracy trade-off: SHAP-ranked spatial covariates for health outcomes mirror SHAP-ranked electrochemical features for SOH. The battery domain offers a high-quality, physics-grounded test bed for evaluating explainability methods before applying them to spatial health data where ground truth is noisier and confounding is harder to control.

⑤ Transfer learning across battery chemistries as a template for cross-context geospatial generalization

Extending this paperThe review notes that almost all surveyed models are chemistry-specific (LFP, LCO, NMC) and rarely generalize. A natural extension: train a foundation model on the most data-abundant Li-ion cycling corpus (using Table 2's 30+ datasets as pre-training data), then fine-tune with a few hundred cycles of a scarce chemistry (sodium-ion, solid-state) using electrochemical state equations as structural priors. This realizes the review's "bespoke model perturbed from default" idea at the cross-chemistry scale, leveraging the large heterogeneous battery corpus the review itself catalogs.

Added value to my researchCross-chemistry transfer learning is methodologically identical to cross-city transfer of geospatial foundation models: a model pre-trained on data-rich cities fine-tunes to data-scarce cities using shared urban-physics priors. Battery datasets provide a smaller, cleaner analogue for developing and validating transfer-learning protocols before deploying them in GeoAI, where data availability varies enormously across geographies and model interpretability requirements are higher.
