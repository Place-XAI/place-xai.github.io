A roadmap for evaluating moral competence in large language models

Julia Haas · Sophie Bridgers · Arianna Manzini · Benjamin Henke · Joshua May · Sydney Levine · Laura Weidinger · Murray Shanahan · Kristian Lum · Iason Gabriel · William Isaac Published 2026-02-18 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-10021-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Current LLM evaluations test only moral performance — whether outputs are morally acceptable — but not moral competence, the deeper capacity to produce appropriate outputs for the right underlying reasons. Three fundamental challenges block this upgrade: the facsimile problem (imitation without genuine reasoning), moral multidimensionality (context-sensitive trade-offs that LLMs may handle brittlely), and moral pluralism (global deployment demands culturally diverse moral standards simultaneously). A suite of adversarial, parametric, and cross-cultural evaluations is required to move toward scientifically grounded attribution of moral competence.

Why It Matters

  • Raises the bar for AI safety assessment. Passing moral benchmarks is no longer enough — the paper argues that high performance without verifiable competence cannot support the trust needed for LLMs in companionship, therapy, and medical advising roles.
  • Names and formalizes a structural blind spot. The "facsimile problem" — that a model can produce correct moral outputs through memorized patterns rather than genuine reasoning — has practical consequences: a system that passes tests may still catastrophically fail at novel edge cases.
  • Calls for adversarial evaluation design. By proposing out-of-distribution, normatively altered test cases (e.g., intergenerational sperm donation vs. incest), the paper moves evaluation practice from benchmark curation toward experimental disconfirmation.
  • Redefines pluralism as a system-level requirement. Individual humans hold consistent personal values; globally deployed LLMs must simultaneously hold multiple culturally appropriate value sets — a fundamentally new standard with no precedent in ethics or AI policy.
  • Positions the field for regulatory use. Rigorous, scientifically grounded moral competence assessments would give policymakers, civic stakeholders, and courts evidentiary tools for auditing AI deployment in high-stakes settings.

Core Methods

  • Conceptual framework construction: the paper's primary method is structured philosophical analysis — defining moral competence and performance as distinct constructs borrowed from cognitive science and extending them to LLMs.
  • Adversarial evaluation design: proposes out-of-distribution moral test cases (normatively altered scenarios) specifically engineered so that memorization-based facsimile processes fail; success on such cases provides limited but meaningful evidence for genuine competence.
  • Parametric experimental control: advocates for evaluations that systematically modulate moral and non-moral variables (e.g., context, framing, active/passive voice, multiple-choice vs. open-ended format) to disentangle substantive moral sensitivity from brittleness artifacts.
  • Multiturn sycophancy probing: adapts multiturn evaluation protocols to test whether models change positions under social pressure rather than evidence — a behavioral signal for RLHF-induced sycophancy.
  • Overton-window and steerable-pluralism frameworks: two complementary operationalizations of moral pluralism — outputting the range of culturally acceptable responses (Overton), or conditioning outputs on explicit cultural/domain parameters (steerable).
  • Mechanistic interpretability (prospective): discusses the promise and current limitations of reverse-engineering internal representations as a future pathway toward directly verifying moral competence rather than inferring it from behavior.

⑤ Extend the paper's competence-vs-performance distinction to LLM hallucination detection in spatial knowledge retrieval

Extending this paperThe paper argues that morally correct outputs do not imply moral competence — a model that "gets it right" via memorization is not reliably competent. An important structural parallel: LLMs retrieving spatial facts (population figures, geographic boundaries, facility locations) may produce accurate outputs via memorization while lacking genuine geographic understanding, making them fragile to novel or updated queries. Extending the paper's framework: apply its adversarial, disconfirming methodology to spatial fact retrieval — construct queries about recently changed geographic entities (new hospitals, rezoned areas) to test whether LLMs retrieve facts through genuine geographic knowledge or cached text patterns. Coupling this with semantic entropy as a confidence measure would produce a hallucination-aware spatial-competence evaluation.
