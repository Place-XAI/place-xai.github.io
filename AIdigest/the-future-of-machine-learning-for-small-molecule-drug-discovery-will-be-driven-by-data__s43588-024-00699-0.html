The future of machine learning for small-molecule drug discovery will be driven by data

Guy Durant · Fergus Boyles · Kristian Birchall · Charlotte M. Deane Published 2024-10-15 DOI 10.1038/s43588-024-00699-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Despite decades of hype, increasingly sophisticated ML architectures have not delivered step-change improvements in small-molecule drug discovery — three major benchmarks (CASF-2016, USPTO-50k, HIV MoleculeNet) show little or no progress over time. The bottleneck is not algorithms but data: scarcity, noise, publication bias toward positive results, and biased training splits cause models to learn spurious dataset artifacts rather than generalizable chemical knowledge. The path forward requires data-centric interventions — augmentation, federated learning, crowd-sourced datasets, rigorous negative-data collection, and ablation-grounded benchmarking — rather than yet another novel architecture.

Core Methods

  • Benchmark audit (Fig. 1): systematic collection of published model results on three widely used benchmarks (CASF-2016, USPTO-50k, HIV MoleculeNet), plotted against publication date and colored by ML architecture, with Pearson's R between date and metric calculated to quantify temporal progress (or lack thereof).
  • Ablation analysis (literature synthesis): reinterpretation of prior work showing that decision-tree scoring functions relying only on ligand/protein-based features — unable to learn protein–ligand physics — match complex GNNs and CNNs on standard benchmarks, attributing most reported accuracy to dataset bias rather than physics learning.
  • PAIN compound analysis: cross-referencing HIV MoleculeNet actives against the Brenk and NIH PAIN substructure catalogs to quantify false-positive contamination (70% of actives flagged).
  • Noise estimation: citing cross-assay reproducibility analyses showing IC50 vs. Ki agreement R² of 0.53–0.81 to bound the maximum achievable accuracy for current benchmarks.
  • Solution mapping: structured narrative review of data-centric solutions — augmentation (cross-docking poses, synthetic pockets, self-distillation), negative-data recovery (thesis mining, text extraction via ChemDataExtractor, DECIMER.ai, Nougat), federated learning (MELLODDY, Effiris), crowd-sourcing, and validation reform (leaderboards, blind competitions).

① Formalize the benchmark audit into a living leaderboard with OOD-stratified splits

Extending this paperThe paper's central empirical contribution is a static audit of three benchmarks: it aggregates published results but does so once, at time of writing. A direct extension is to automate this audit as a continuously updated leaderboard that not only tracks performance over time but enforces out-of-distribution (OOD) data splits — temporal, scaffold, and protein-family holdouts — that the paper identifies as far more informative than random splits. This would operationalize the paper's call for rigorous validation into a community infrastructure that adjusts itself as new publications appear, and would surface whether any future architecture actually breaks the plateau.

Added value to my researchThe benchmark-audit methodology transfers directly to spatial prediction: my explainable ML pipelines for health outcomes and urban analytics suffer from the same data-split pathology (random splits within the same city or region reward spatial memorization). Building an OOD-stratified spatial benchmark — using left-out cities, held-out time periods, or held-out socioeconomic strata — using the same audit logic would reveal whether my GeoAI models learn transferable spatial patterns or city-specific artifacts.

② Turn federated learning from a privacy tool into a multi-city GeoAI training protocol

Extending this paperThe paper presents federated learning (MELLODDY: +10% applicability domain; Effiris: +83%) as a solution to data siloing in pharmaceutical companies. However, the paper treats federation purely as a quantity problem — more data from more silos. A deeper extension would investigate whether federation also reduces bias: if each institutional silo has its own anthropogenic preferences (preferred scaffolds, reagents, assay conditions), a well-designed federated aggregation scheme might systematically cancel out those idiosyncratic biases and yield a less biased global model — turning a privacy mechanism into a de-biasing mechanism.

Added value to my researchCity-level health and mobility datasets are precisely analogous to pharmaceutical company silos: each city's mobile phone records, health records, or hospital claims are siloed for privacy or policy reasons, each city has its own "anthropogenic biases" (urban form, cultural patterns). Applying federated multi-city GeoAI — training spatiotemporal models across cities without centralizing individual-level data — would let me build generalizable spatial accessibility or health-outcome models that transfer across cities, directly paralleling MELLODDY's drug-discovery gains.

③ Apply the paper's dark-data mining pipeline to unlock negative spatial evidence

Extending this paperThe paper proposes mining dark data — theses, raw high-throughput results, non-machine-readable literature — using tools like ChemDataExtractor, DECIMER.ai, and Nougat to recover negative chemical results currently lost to publication bias. This is framed as a drug-discovery solution but the same pipeline applies anywhere negative results are systematically underreported. Extending the paper: develop a domain-agnostic dark-data recovery framework that adapts these extraction tools to other scientific domains, measuring how much recoverable negative signal exists in the grey literature of any given field and how much it shifts model calibration.

Added value to my researchPublication bias in spatial health research is real and structurally identical: failed interventions, null healthcare access outcomes, and non-significant accessibility findings are rarely published. Using NLP-based extraction tools adapted from the paper's framework to mine spatial health theses and grey reports would let me build a more balanced training dataset for my 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility models — increasing calibration and reducing the systematic overestimation of healthcare access that comes from fitting only positive case studies.

④ Replace static train/test splits with adversarial data-split generation

Extending this paperThe paper calls for asymmetric validation embedding and rigorous splits to penalize models that memorize dataset biases (e.g., LBFE/SBFE asymmetric splits, scaffold splits, temporal splits). An extension of the paper's validation reform agenda: develop an adversarial data-split generator that automatically searches for the hardest possible split — the split that maximally degrades a given model's performance — then uses that worst-case split as the official benchmark. This "worst-case benchmarking" directly addresses the paper's key concern that current splits are too easy and inflate apparent accuracy.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly onto a challenge in my spatial ML work: I routinely use random or cross-validation splits that may share spatial autocorrelation between train and test. An adversarial spatial cross-validation scheme — which finds the geographic split that maximally reduces model accuracy — would provide a true lower bound on generalizability and reveal which spatial features are genuinely predictive versus which are artifacts of neighborhood autocorrelation. This would strengthen reproducibility claims in my GeoAI papers.
