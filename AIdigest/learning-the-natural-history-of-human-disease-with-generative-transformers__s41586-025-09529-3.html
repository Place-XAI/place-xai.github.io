Learning the natural history of human disease with generative transformers

Artem Shmatko · Alexander Wolfgang Jung · Kumar Gaurav · Søren Brunak · Laust Hvas Mortensen · Ewan Birney · Tom Fitzgerald · Moritz Gerstung Published 2025-09-17 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09529-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A GPT-based model called Delphi-2M, trained on 402,799 UK Biobank participants, simultaneously predicts the rates of more than 1,000 diseases with accuracy matching single-disease clinical models. Its generative capacity enables sampling of synthetic future health trajectories for up to 20 years, enabling population-scale disease burden estimation and privacy-preserving AI training. Explainable AI (SHAP) reveals clusters of co-morbidities and their time-dependent consequences, but also exposes systematic biases inherited from UK Biobank's data-collection heterogeneity — underscoring that such models are powerful complements, not yet replacements, for clinical pipelines.

Why It Matters

  • First population-scale, pan-disease generative health model. Delphi-2M is the first transformer model to simultaneously predict rates across the full ICD-10 spectrum (>1,000 diagnoses) for every individual, at every age — a qualitative leap beyond single-disease risk tools.
  • Generative trajectories unlock new use cases. By sampling future health sequences, the model produces synthetic cohorts from birth that preserve population-level co-morbidity statistics without exposing personal data — directly enabling privacy-safe AI development.
  • Cross-national transferability without retraining. Delphi-2M trained on UK data achieves AUC 0.67 on 1.93 million Danish individuals with zero parameter changes — demonstrating that learned disease patterns generalize across healthcare systems.
  • Temporal XAI for epidemiology. SHAP analysis at scale quantifies how each past diagnosis modulates future disease rates over time — revealing, for instance, that cancer elevates mortality for years while septicaemia's effect decays within 5 years.
  • Honest bias disclosure. The paper explicitly characterizes "immortality bias," healthy-volunteer selection, data-source missingness artifacts, and demographic disparities — setting a transparency standard for health AI.

Core Methods

  • Modified GPT-2 architecture (Delphi): replaces discrete positional encoding with continuous age encoding (sine/cosine basis), adds an exponential waiting-time output head for inter-event timing (competing-exponentials model), and amends causal attention masks to exclude same-time co-occurring tokens.
  • Multi-disease incidence prediction: autoregressive next-token prediction across 1,258 states (ICD-10 codes + lifestyle + padding) yields calibrated per-day rate estimates for each disease, evaluated by age–sex-stratified AUC (average ≈ 0.76 internal; 0.67 external).
  • Generative trajectory sampling: iterative sampling of (token, time) pairs from the competing-exponential model generates full synthetic health timelines, starting from birth or any partial history, for up to 20 years.
  • Scaling-law hyperparameter screen: systematic grid over embedding dimensions, layers, and heads confirms optimal model size of ~2.2 M parameters for the UKB dataset size.
  • SHAP-based explainability: PartitionExplainer SHAP applied to all 100,639 validation trajectories to quantify how each past diagnosis token shifts future disease rates — producing a 778 × 778 influence matrix per time horizon.
  • Bias and fairness analysis: immortality/recruitment bias characterization, data-source missingness analysis, ethnic-background and deprivation-index stratified AUC, DeLong's method for CI estimation, and Bonferroni-corrected subgroup testing.
  • Cross-national zero-shot transfer: UKB-trained weights applied to Danish data without parameter adjustment, with ICD-8-to-ICD-10 code mapping for pre-1995 Danish records.

① Fuse spatial access-to-care signals into Delphi's trajectory prompt

Extending this paperDelphi-2M conditions predictions on past ICD-10 tokens and a handful of lifestyle variables, but ignores the spatial context of care access — a patient's distance to a GP, specialist, or hospital. The paper itself shows that data-source missingness (GP records absent vs. present) creates systematic biases in predicted rates (Fig. 5e,f). A direct extension: add a geocoded care-access token (derived from each participant's residential area) to Delphi's input vocabulary, so the model can disentangle true disease risk from reporting artifacts caused by poor access. This turns the paper's acknowledged bias limitation into a solved modeling problem.

Added value to my researchThis is the precise intersection of health geography and disease-progression AI. My 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility measures — typically used to flag spatial gaps in primary care — become input features for a population health forecasting model, creating a bidirectional link: access measures drive disease-trajectory predictions, and trajectory outputs indicate future service demand. This operationalizes my accessibility research as a component in clinical AI rather than a standalone spatial analysis.

② Use mobile-phone mobility data to generate spatial disease-burden maps from Delphi's synthetic trajectories

Extending this paperDelphi-2M predicts cumulative disease burden at the individual level. The paper explicitly suggests that aggregating individual predictions "may provide substantial system-wide modelling benefits, particularly in projecting the expected disease burden at local, regional and national levels." However, UK Biobank participants are not a spatially representative sample — they are geographically clustered and have "healthy volunteer" bias. Extending the paper: weight Delphi's synthetic cohort trajectories by mobile-phone-derived mobility patterns and population distributions to produce spatially disaggregated, bias-corrected disease burden maps at sub-regional scale.

Added value to my researchMobile-phone records are a central data stream in my GeoAI agenda. This extension gives me a concrete use case: mobile-phone data anchor the spatial downscaling of a health-AI model's outputs. The resulting fine-grained disease maps would directly feed my healthcare facility access analyses (where is demand likely to surge?) and public-health policy work on spatially heterogeneous morbidity burdens — connecting my spatiotemporal kernel density methods to clinical AI outputs.

③ Introduce algorithmic fairness constraints into Delphi's training objective

Extending this paperThe paper devotes an entire figure (Fig. 5) to documenting biases related to ethnicity, deprivation, and data-source availability, and acknowledges they are "learnt from training data." Yet Delphi-2M's loss function (cross-entropy + exponential waiting time) has no fairness term. An extension of the paper: reformulate the objective with a demographic-parity or equalized-odds regularizer, or adopt a post-hoc deep reinforcement-learning bias-mitigation strategy, and evaluate whether Delphi-2M's already-documented disparities (e.g., differential AUC across deprivation groups) can be reduced without degrading overall predictive accuracy.

Added value to my researchAlgorithmic fairness is directly relevant to my equity-focused GeoAI agenda, especially for healthcare accessibility. If Delphi-derived disease-burden predictions are spatially disaggregated (idea above), ensuring demographic fairness in those predictions becomes a prerequisite for equitable resource allocation. This links my explainable ML methods (MGWR capturing spatial non-stationarity in health outcomes) to the model's internal fairness constraints.

Cross-ref (corpus): Algorithmic fairness and bias mitigation for clinical machine learning with deep reinforcement learning — 10.1038/s42256-023-00697-3

④ Pre-train a multi-task medical foundation model using Delphi's architecture as one task head

Extending this paperDelphi-2M is a specialist model: it encodes health trajectories via ICD-10 tokens but cannot ingest imaging or genomics. The paper's Discussion explicitly calls for "multimodal extensions" adding imaging, metabolomics, and wearable data via the same embedding layer. A structured extension: treat Delphi as one branch of a multi-task medical foundation model — sharing transformer layers across diagnosis-sequence prediction (Delphi's task), medical image classification, and biomarker regression — so that each modality can inform the others through shared representations, rather than sequential integration.

Added value to my researchMulti-task foundation model architecture maps directly to my interest in fine-tuning geospatial foundation models. Just as geospatial foundation models pre-trained on remote sensing data can be fine-tuned for urban health tasks, a medical foundation model pre-trained on EHR + imaging + genomics could be fine-tuned with spatial access features — creating a spatially-aware health foundation model where my GeoAI expertise adds a novel input modality not currently present in the medical AI literature.

⑤ Use Delphi's SHAP co-morbidity matrix as a structured prior for spatial disease-clustering models

Extending this paperFig. 4c of the paper is a 778 × 778 matrix of SHAP influence values between disease tokens — essentially a learned, continuous-time co-morbidity graph. Currently, this matrix is used only for qualitative interpretation. Extending the paper: feed the SHAP matrix as a learned adjacency prior into a graph neural network that predicts spatial disease clusters — encoding the known temporal influence of one disease on another (e.g., mental disorders on each other, cancers on mortality) as structural inductive biases for community-level disease mapping.

Added value to my researchThis bridges my physics-informed graph learning agenda with clinical epidemiology: the Delphi SHAP matrix becomes the "physics" (known disease co-progression rules) embedded in a spatial GNN. Applied to my work on urban health, this means a spatiotemporal model of disease incidence across census tracts could use Delphi-derived temporal co-morbidity weights as edge attributes — integrating the clinical knowledge of disease interactions with the spatial structure of healthcare accessibility and deprivation that I routinely measure.
