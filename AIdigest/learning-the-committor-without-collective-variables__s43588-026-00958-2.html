Learning the committor without collective variables

Sergio Contreras Arredondo · Chenyu Tang · Radu A. Talmazan · Alberto Megías · Cheng Giuseppe Chen · Christophe Chipot Published 2026-02-17 DOI 10.1038/s43588-026-00958-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A graph neural network architecture based on geometric vector perceptrons (GVP-GNNs) — called qGNN — learns the molecular committor function directly from raw atomic coordinates, bypassing the need for hand-crafted collective variables (CVs). Applied to four molecular systems of increasing complexity (NANMA dipeptide, trialanine, a Diels–Alder reaction, and Trp-cage protein folding), the method recovers accurate committor maps, infers precise rate constants consistent with known benchmarks, and identifies key atoms driving transitions via native node-sensitivity analysis — all without prior chemical intuition about relevant degrees of freedom.

Why It Matters

  • Eliminates the collective-variable bottleneck. CV selection is the hardest and most assumption-laden step in rare-event simulation; qGNN removes it entirely, enabling unbiased discovery of reaction coordinates from atomic coordinates alone.
  • Delivers atom-level interpretability. The node-sensitivity analysis ranks every heavy atom by its contribution to the transition — providing human-readable mechanistic insight that is architecture-native, not a post-hoc approximation.
  • Generalizes across chemistry scales. From a 10-atom dipeptide in vacuum to a 20-residue protein in explicit solvent and a quantum-chemical reaction, the same GVP-GNN architecture is reused unchanged, demonstrating genuine architectural universality.
  • Accurate kinetics without manual tuning. Rate constants computed from the learned committor agree with experimental and theoretical benchmarks across all four systems, providing a path toward automated, quantitative kinetic characterization.
  • Opens Markov-state and enhanced-sampling integration. The trained committor can serve directly as a CV for enhanced sampling or as input to multistate Markov-state models, bridging data-driven learning with established simulation infrastructure.

Core Methods

  • GVP-GNN architecture (qGNN): each molecular conformation is encoded as a graph where atoms are nodes and edges encode interactions. Geometric vector perceptron layers process joint scalar and 3D-vector features while preserving rotation and reflection equivariance. A permutation-invariant sum pooling produces a scalar committor value per configuration.
  • Variational committor loss: training minimizes the net forward scalar flux JAB[q; τ] from transition path theory, augmented by boundary-condition penalties enforcing q = 0 at state A and q = 1 at state B (equation 2 in Methods).
  • Reweighting for biased simulations: WTM-eABF biasing forces corrupt the dynamics; an importance-reweighting scheme using the perturbed potential of mean force recovers unbiased time-correlation functions, validated by convergence of the free energy profile.
  • Node-sensitivity analysis: for each atom i, the absolute sensitivity si is the trajectory-averaged Euclidean norm of the gradient of q with respect to the atom's vector feature — a per-atom importance score arising natively from the model (equation 5 in Methods).
  • Rate-constant estimation: transition rates kAB are derived from the learned committor and the MD statistics; benchmarked against experimental values, shooting-based estimates, and Eyring/coupled-cluster theory (Table 1).
  • K-means clustering of transition ensemble: configurations with q ∈ (0.45, 0.55) are clustered to visualize structurally distinct transition-state ensembles (applied to Trp-cage folding).

① Transfer qGNN's collective-variable-free paradigm to spatial accessibility modeling

Extending this paperThe qGNN's central innovation is eliminating hand-crafted CVs: instead of pre-specifying dihedral angles, the GVP-GNN learns relevant coordinates from raw atom positions via a variational loss. A direct extension of this paper: apply the same philosophy to systems where "collective variables" are spatial features rather than molecular ones — the variational principle could be reframed around a spatial flux objective (e.g., flow between population zones and facilities), enabling the network to discover which spatial embeddings best discriminate reachable vs. inaccessible areas without pre-engineering travel-time radii or decay functions. Extended molecular systems such as villin (470 heavy atoms) demonstrate that the architecture scales; analogously, city-scale graphs with millions of nodes are the natural stress-test.

Adapting the variational training strategy to a GNN on street-network graphs could yield a CV-free spatial accessibility allocator: the network learns which geographic features explain healthcare utilization patterns from GPS mobility data, directly analogous to qGNN learning which atoms drive molecular transitions — without imposing a Gaussian decay or fixed radius threshold.

② Use node-sensitivity analysis as a blueprint for explainable spatial GNNs

Extending this paperThe node-sensitivity score (equation 5) is a gradient-based, architecture-native interpretability measure that ranks atoms by causal importance to the transition — without SHAP, LIME, or other post-hoc approximation tools. A direct extension of the paper: generalize from per-atom sensitivity to subgraph sensitivity (residue-level, secondary-structure-level), enabling hierarchical explanation of folding transitions at multiple scales. The paper already steps in this direction with edge-sensitivity for Trp-cage top-four atoms; extending this to full residue subgraphs and secondary-structure motifs would provide a richer multi-resolution mechanistic narrative.

③ Embed hard physical constraints into the GVP-GNN loss for spatiotemporal processes

Extending this paperThe qGNN loss is physically grounded in the variational flux principle, but additional physical constraints — detailed balance (microscopic reversibility), or conservation of total probability flux — are currently only soft penalties via the boundary-condition term. Extending the paper: enforce these as hard equality constraints (Lagrangian or projected-gradient approach) so the learned committor satisfies exact physical symmetries, improving data efficiency and extrapolation to configurations unseen during training. This would be especially valuable for large systems (villin-class or beyond) where trajectory coverage is necessarily sparse.

Added value to my researchThis extends directly to my physics-informed graph learning agenda in spatial contexts: embedding continuity equations (flow conservation at network nodes) or epidemic flux balance (SIR compartments) as hard constraints in a GNN trained on mobility or health surveillance data achieves the same physically-anchored learning the qGNN demonstrates for molecular dynamics — producing spatiotemporal predictions that provably respect known physical or epidemiological conservation laws.

④ Extend qGNN to multistate systems for rare-event detection in health networks

Extending this paperThe paper explicitly acknowledges generalizing to more than two metastable states via state-resolved committor functions as a natural future direction. Implementing this: learn a full matrix of pairwise committors qAB, qAC, qBC … for k states simultaneously — sharing the GVP-GNN backbone across all pairs while differentiating by state-boundary penalties — and derive the MSM transition matrix directly from the multistate committor set. This would simultaneously provide the slow degrees of freedom (via shared sensitivity analysis) and the kinetic network, replacing multiple pipeline steps with a single end-to-end training.

Added value to my researchIn spatiotemporal health analytics, rare-event detection is directly analogous: identifying infrequent but high-consequence state transitions — sudden deterioration of primary-care access, emergence of telehealth deserts, outbreak ignition in a community network — requires exactly the multistate committor framework. Learning which spatial features drive these regime shifts without pre-specifying indicators maps to monitoring health-access networks for phase transitions using the same variational GNN principle, with sensitivity analysis revealing which community-level attributes act as the true reaction coordinates.

⑤ Combine equivariant GVP backbone with contrastive pre-training for data-efficient transfer

Extending this paperThe qGNN is trained from scratch for each molecular system, requiring sufficient biased trajectory data per system. For expensive or data-scarce systems (e.g., large proteins, novel reactive materials), pre-training the GVP-GNN backbone via contrastive molecular representation learning across a large library of molecules — then fine-tuning only the committor prediction head — would drastically reduce the trajectory budget needed per new system. The paper's demonstration that the same architecture generalizes across NANMA, Diels–Alder, and Trp-cage provides strong evidence that a shared feature space already exists; pre-training would make it explicit and reusable.

Added value to my researchThe pre-train–fine-tune paradigm mirrors exactly what I pursue for geospatial foundation models: a GVP-GNN backbone pre-trained on molecular graphs is directly analogous to a spatial GNN backbone pre-trained on city-scale networks, where the backbone captures generic graph geometry and the fine-tuned head solves a domain-specific task (health access, EV charging efficiency, telehealth penetration). The qGNN's demonstration that equivariant GNNs learn physically meaningful functions from limited system-specific data strengthens the theoretical basis for geospatial GNN pre-training with embedded physical priors.
