Reward magnitude determines reinforcement learning efficiency

(Authors not listed in public metadata — see article) Published 2026-05-21 DOI 10.1126/science.aeb0813 Source science.org ↗
Core Conclusion

Contrary to decades of animal learning practice that minimizes reward magnitude to maximize behavioral repetitions, outsized rewards dramatically accelerate learning efficiency in mice across five task types — through dissociable effects on within-session learning, across-session consolidation, and task engagement. Ventral striatal dopamine release duration and magnitude scale with reward size, and prolonged optogenetic boosting of dopamine responses partially recapitulates this benefit. Animals' reinforcement learning capacity has been systematically underestimated, and absolute reward magnitude — not just presence — is a primary driver of task engagement via dopamine signaling.

Why It Matters

  • Overturns a foundational assumption in animal learning research. The century-old practice of using small rewards to enable more trials is shown to trade speed of learning for volume of trials — a false economy that has systematically underestimated the learning capacity of animals.
  • Reveals a graded dopamine-magnitude link. The finding that ventral striatal dopamine release scales continuously with reward size — not just its presence or absence — reframes dopamine signaling from a binary reinforcement signal to a quantitative learning-rate controller.
  • Dissects multiple routes from reward to learning. The work separates within-session acquisition, across-session consolidation, and task engagement as three distinct channels through which large rewards act — providing a mechanistic decomposition that guides future neuroscience and AI.
  • Bridges biological and artificial reinforcement learning. By connecting absolute reward magnitude to learning efficiency, the paper has direct implications for reward-shaping design in AI systems that draw on biological learning principles.

Core Methods

  • Multi-paradigm behavioral testing: five distinct learning paradigms applied to naïve mice, testing whether the reward-magnitude effect generalizes across task types (classical conditioning, operant, etc.).
  • Reward magnitude manipulation: systematic variation of reward size to isolate the effect of absolute magnitude on learning rate and task engagement, independent of total reward quantity.
  • Dissociation of learning components: within-session acquisition curves and across-session retention measured separately to identify which learning phases are most sensitive to reward magnitude.
  • In vivo dopamine monitoring: real-time measurement of ventral striatal dopamine release to characterize how duration and magnitude of the dopamine signal co-vary with reward size.
  • Optogenetic causal intervention: prolonged optogenetic stimulation of dopamine reward circuits to directly test whether extended dopamine signaling is sufficient to replicate the large-reward learning advantage.
  • Task engagement quantification: behavioral metrics (e.g., trial initiation rate, response vigor) used to quantify engagement as a distinct component separable from accuracy/learning per se.
