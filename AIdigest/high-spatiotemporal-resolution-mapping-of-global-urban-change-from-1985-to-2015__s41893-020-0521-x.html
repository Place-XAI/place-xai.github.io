High-spatiotemporal-resolution mapping of global urban change from 1985 to 2015

Xiaoping Liu · Yinghuai Huang · Xiaocong Xu · Xuecao Li · Xia Li · Philippe Ciais · Peirong Lin · Kai Gong · Alan D. Ziegler · Anping Chen · Peng Gong · Jun Chen · Guohua Hu · Yimin Chen · Shaojian Wang · Qiusheng Wu · Kangning Huang · Lyndon Estes · Zhenzhong Zeng Published 2020-05-04 DOI 10.1038/s41893-020-0521-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Mapping 30 years of global urban change at 30 m annual resolution using the full Landsat archive, this study finds that global urban land expanded by 9,687 km² per year from 1985 to 2015 — four times faster than prior estimates — and at a rate substantially exceeding population growth, meaning new urban land was largely not driven by housing demand. Roughly 70% of that expansion consumed agricultural land, and China experienced the largest green recovery of any nation as part of its urban renewal policy.

Why It Matters

  • Unprecedented spatial-temporal precision. Prior global urban products were limited to coarse resolutions (0.5–1 km) or specific epochs; this is the first 30 m, annual, globally consistent urban-change database spanning three decades.
  • Reveals a four-times undercount. The new rate of 9,687 km² yr⁻¹ is four times the previously accepted estimate from meta-analyses of city-level studies, fundamentally revising our understanding of urbanization's pace and environmental footprint.
  • Decouples urban land from population growth. Because urban area grew 80% while urban population grew only 52% (1985–2015), much new development served commercial and industrial uses rather than housing — with profound implications for sustainability policy.
  • Quantifies agricultural land loss at scale. Urban expansion consumed agricultural land at 61,567 km² per decade, threatening food security especially in China (49,077 km²) and India (12,082 km²).
  • Open, freely available dataset. GAUD maps and validation samples are publicly archived at figshare, enabling derivative climate, ecology, and planning research worldwide.

Primary imagery
Landsat archive (Landsat 5 TM, 7 ETM+, 8 OLI) — full archive processed on Google Earth Engine (GEE), annual time series 1985–2015 at 30 m resolution. Provides surface reflectance for computing NUACI (Normalized Urban Areas Composite Index).
Spatial coverage
Global — all continents. Analysis organized within 1,692 urban-cluster boundaries derived from a morphological approach using nighttime light data.
Time period
1985–2015 (31 annual maps); land-conversion analysis covers 1992–2015 (limited by ESA-CCI availability).
Urban extent fusion
Four existing global urban products fused to define the 1985 and 2015 endpoints: Global Human Settlement Layer (GHSL), Global Urban Footprint (GUF), Global Urban Land (GUL), and Global Artificial Impervious Area (GAIA). See Supplementary Table 1.
Land-cover change
ESA Climate Change Initiative (ESA-CCI) annual land-cover product at 300 m resolution — used to assign conversion source (agriculture, forest, grassland, wetland) for each newly urbanized pixel from 1992 to 2015.
Population data
World Bank Database (country-level, annual 1985–2015) and Gridded Population of the World v4 (GPW) at ~1 km resolution for 2000, 2005, 2010, 2015 — used to compare urban-area growth rates with population growth rates.
Validation
~200,000 object-based samples from 200 globally distributed 6×6 km blocks (kappa assessment); 4,010 human-interpreted points (1985–2000 period) and 8,020 points (2000–2015 period) from 140 cities globally — interpreted against Google Earth and Landsat time series.

Core Methods

  • Data fusion for urban extent endpoints: Overlapping three global urban maps per epoch to define high-confidence urban areas; uncertain regions reclassified with a locally adaptive random forest classifier trained on high-confidence samples within one-degree tiles globally.
  • NUACI time series construction: Annual composite of three Landsat-derived indices — NDVI (vegetation), NDWI (water), NDBI (built-up area) — integrated into a single Normalized Urban Areas Composite Index per pixel, computed on Google Earth Engine from the full Landsat archive.
  • Temporal segmentation for change detection: Urbanization year detected as the year of maximum NUACI residual relative to a regression line — an abrupt-change detector for the built-up signal. Green-recovery year detected analogously on the post-urbanization temporal segment.
  • Area-under-the-curve analysis: Normalized urban growth curves per city integrated to classify whether urbanization accelerated (developing nations) or decelerated (developed nations) over the 30-year period.
  • Land-conversion accounting: Overlay of annual GAUD urbanization maps with ESA-CCI land-cover to attribute each newly urban pixel to its prior land-cover type (agriculture, forest, grassland, wetland, other).
  • Population-vs-area growth comparison: Relative growth rates of urban area (Rate_u) and population (Rate_p) computed per country for three decadal intervals, using World Bank and GPW data.
  • Multi-stage accuracy assessment: Object-based kappa at 200 global blocks (~200,000 samples); change-year accuracy at 140 cities with 12,030 human-interpreted Landsat/Google Earth validation points, with one-year tolerance.

A direct extension would couple the GAUD annual rasters with caloric-yield maps to translate each urbanized pixel into lost food-production potential, and then cross-reference those losses with population nutritional vulnerability layers — operationalizing the paper's own warning about food insecurity into actionable risk contours around urban growth fronts.

② Use GAUD as ground truth to fine-tune geospatial foundation models for continuous urban monitoring

Extending this paperThe paper's NUACI-based change detector was hand-engineered from three spectral indices and a regression residual heuristic. Since Landsat scenes are available to 2024, a natural extension is to use the paper's own validated GAUD labels as a 30 m annual training corpus for fine-tuning geospatial vision transformers (e.g., SatMAE, Scale-MAE, or Prithvi) — replacing the hand-crafted NUACI pipeline with a learned representation that generalizes across sensor generations (Landsat 9, Sentinel-2) and requires far less expert tuning per region.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my agenda on fine-tuning geospatial foundation models: GAUD's 31 years of annual 30 m labels constitute a uniquely large and globally distributed labeled dataset that most foundation-model projects lack. Using it as supervised signal for a temporal geospatial encoder could yield a transferable urban-change head deployable in real-time monitoring workflows, supporting dynamic data fusion across mobile-phone, social media, and remote sensing streams.

③ Couple GAUD's green-recovery signal with urban heat island and population health outcomes
