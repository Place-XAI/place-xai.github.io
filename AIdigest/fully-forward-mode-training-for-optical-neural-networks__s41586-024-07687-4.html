Fully forward mode training for optical neural networks

Zhiwei Xue · Tiankuang Zhou · Zhihao Xu · Shaoliang Yu · Qionghai Dai · Lu Fang Published 2024-08-07 DOI 10.1038/s41586-024-07687-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Fully Forward Mode (FFM) learning eliminates the need for backward light propagation and offline digital simulation by exploiting Lorentz reciprocity and spatial symmetry in physical optical systems. Training is executed on the physical hardware itself — not on a digital emulator — enabling the deepest optical neural networks yet demonstrated (8-layer, millions of parameters) with accuracy matching ideal models, all-optical imaging through scattering at the diffraction limit, non-line-of-sight imaging at >1 kHz frame rate, and 5.40 × 1018 OPS/W energy efficiency at sub-photon-per-pixel light levels.

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the modelling bottleneck of optical AI. All prior optical neural network training relied on digital emulation of the physical system; any model mismatch degraded real hardware performance. FFM trains on the physical system directly, making model accuracy irrelevant.
  • Achieves the deepest experimentally trained ONNs on record. Previous reconfigurable ONNs reached at most 5 layers (free-space) and 3 layers (photonic integrated circuits); FFM reaches 8 layers (free-space) and 5 layers (PICs) with accuracy matching digital in silico targets.
  • Unlocks ultra-low-light operation. FFM's on-site gradient descent adaptively corrects photon noise, achieving accurate classification with fewer than 1 photon per pixel — an order of magnitude fewer than the best ANN alternative.
  • Orders-of-magnitude energy efficiency gain. Operating at 5.40 × 1018 OPS/W at room temperature, FFM opens a practical path to exaOPS/W optical computing beyond GPU-scale power envelopes.
  • Model-free physics exploration. FFM automatically locates non-Hermitian exceptional points in a dual-waveguide system without any analytical model — extending the paradigm from AI to fundamental physics discovery.

Core Methods

  • Fully Forward Mode (FFM) gradient descent: reformulates the optical system as a differentiable embedded photonic neural network via Maxwell-equation reparametrization (y = W(M·x)). Exploits Lorentz reciprocity and spatial symmetry to replace backward light propagation with a second forward pass, computing gradients entirely on the physical system without a digital model.
  • On-site training protocol (4 steps): (1) data forward propagation, (2) loss computation at output, (3) error field forward propagation (symmetric direction), (4) gradient calculation + Adam optimizer update of refractive index parameters — all implemented on physical optics/electronics.
  • Nonlinear FFM extension: nonlinear activations (electro-optic intensity-induced phase nonlinearity) inserted between layers; gradients multiplied by recorded activation derivatives before error propagation — supports arbitrary measurable nonlinear functions.
  • Particle swarm optimization (PSO) baseline: used as a state-of-the-art comparison for scattering focus optimization; FFM converges in 25 iterations vs. PSO's ≥400 with better FWHM (81.2 µm vs. 120.0 µm) and PSNR (8.46 dB vs. 2.29 dB).
  • Photon-noise model fitting: experimental photon-count curves fitted with a theoretical noise model to benchmark minimum required photons for fixed accuracy; establishes the <1 photon/pixel operating regime.
  • Non-Hermitian exceptional point search: targets asymmetric output (breaking lateral reciprocity) as the learning objective in a dual-waveguide gain/loss system; FFM self-designs the gain/loss traversal path over the exceptional point without requiring an a priori Hamiltonian model.

① Combine FFM's on-site learning with analogue neuromorphic hardware for geospatial foundation models

Extending this paperFFM currently demonstrates gradient descent entirely on free-space optics and PICs, but the learning loop still relies on electronic loss/gradient calculation steps. A direct extension of the paper would be to couple FFM's forward-propagation paradigm with memristor crossbar arrays that perform the gradient accumulation in-memory — eliminating the remaining electronic bottleneck. The paper's on-site protocol already separates propagation from gradient bookkeeping; mapping the latter onto an analogue memory fabric is a natural next step that the corpus paper on fully analogue reinforcement learning with memristors shows is feasible for RL update rules.

Added value to my researchGeospatial foundation models (fine-tuned for remote sensing, POI embedding, trajectory analysis) are rapidly approaching parameter counts where GPU energy costs become a barrier to iterative retraining on new city data. FFM's exaOPS/W regime combined with analogue gradient accumulation could enable edge-deployed, continuously updated geospatial models that adapt to incoming mobile-phone or satellite streams without shipping data to the cloud — directly relevant to my work on open-science, multi-source data fusion and fine-tuning geospatial foundation models.

② Translate FFM's reciprocity-based forward gradient into a digital "optical surrogate" training rule for graph neural networks

Extending this paperThe mathematical core of FFM is the observation that Lorentz reciprocity converts backward error propagation into a second forward pass through a spatially mirrored system. This is a physical instantiation of the broader idea of forward-mode automatic differentiation. Extending the paper: formalize the FFM gradient formula as a purely digital forward-gradient rule for general differentiable physical simulators (not just optics), and benchmark whether FFM-style updates converge competitively against backprop on graph-structured physical systems such as wave simulators on irregular grids or PDE-defined sensor networks.

Added value to my researchMy physics-informed graph learning work (spatial flow networks, SIR propagation on mobility graphs) relies on backpropagation through physics-constrained GNNs. An FFM-style "forward graph gradient" — computing gradients by propagating error signals forward along the graph's own edges — could make training of large spatiotemporal graph models substantially more memory-efficient, since it avoids storing intermediate activations for backprop, crucial when graphs span millions of POIs or GPS trajectory nodes.

③ Apply FFM's on-site scattering-imaging pipeline to urban remote sensing through atmospheric turbulence

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that FFM can learn to focus and image through random scattering media (scotch tape, random phase plates) by treating the scatterer as part of the trainable optical system. A natural extension: apply the same paradigm to imaging through atmospheric turbulence in satellite or UAV-based remote sensing, where turbulence layers act as spatiotemporally varying scatterers. FFM's model-free onsite design is advantageous precisely when the scattering statistics are unknown or time-varying — matching the turbulence regime. The corpus paper on on-chip backpropagation PICs suggests integrated photonic wavefront correctors could be co-packaged with imaging sensors.

Added value to my researchHigh-resolution urban remote sensing (building footprints, land-use change, informal settlement mapping) is the raw data layer for much of my GeoAI work. FFM-based adaptive optics on satellite payloads or UAV sensors could deliver diffraction-limited urban imagery in turbulent conditions without ground-based reference stars — improving the spatial resolution of the remote sensing inputs to my spatiotemporal kernel density and MGWR models.

⑤ Extend FFM's model-free exceptional-point search to discover hidden phase transitions in urban socioeconomic systems

Extending this paperThe paper's final demonstration is remarkable: FFM locates non-Hermitian exceptional points — singular spectral degeneracies where eigenvalues coalesce — without knowing the system's Hamiltonian. The only input is a target (asymmetric output) and the system's physical response. Extending this: formalize FFM's exceptional-point search as a general model-free phase-transition finder for any parameterized dynamical system, and apply it to coupled differential-equation models of urban dynamics (e.g., coupled SIR compartments on mobility networks, housing price–accessibility feedback loops) where similar "bifurcation points" exist but are analytically intractable.

Added value to my researchMy SIR-based epidemic modeling on mobile-phone networks and my job-housing community detection work both involve dynamical systems with critical thresholds (epidemic R0=1, community fragmentation points). FFM's target-oriented phase-transition search — adapted to digital simulation — could automatically locate critical mobility thresholds or housing-price tipping points in spatiotemporal networks without requiring closed-form analytical models, providing a new computational tool for urban health policy analysis.
