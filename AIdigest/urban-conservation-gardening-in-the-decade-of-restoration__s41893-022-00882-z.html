Urban conservation gardening in the decade of restoration

Josiane Segar · Corey T. Callaghan · Emma Ladouceur · Jasper N. Meya · Henrique M. Pereira · Andrea Perino · Ingmar R. Staude Published 2022-05-12 DOI 10.1038/s41893-022-00882-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Cultivating declining native plant species in urban public and private green spaces — called "conservation gardening" — is an economically viable, participatory approach that complements traditional habitat-protection methods. Using Germany as a case study, the authors show that cultivated plants show higher occupancy trends, that urban green spaces are spatially complementary to protected areas, and that reforming the horticultural market with labelling and cross-sectoral integration can scale this citizen-led conservation instrument to help meet global biodiversity targets in the UN Decade on Ecosystem Restoration.

Why It Matters

  • Fills a critical gap between protected areas and biodiversity targets. Conservation science has long focused on protected areas, yet species populations keep declining even inside them; conservation gardening offers a scalable, complementary mechanism that requires no additional land designation.
  • Turns urban dwellers into active conservation actors. With 55% of the global population already urban (projected 92% by 2100), cities are where most people encounter nature — conservation gardening channels that exposure into tangible stewardship and awareness.
  • Empirically grounded case study. The authors quantify occupancy changes across six decades for 1,249 German vascular plant species, demonstrate a positive cultivation-occupancy link with Mann–Whitney U tests, and map spatial complementarity between the 96 largest German cities and protected-area networks.
  • Policy blueprint, not just a concept. The paper proposes specific instruments: a conservation gardening label, cross-sectoral integration of the native seed sector into the horticultural market (valued at US$109 billion globally), EU funding pathways, and community social-diffusion strategies.
  • Timely global relevance. Published during the UN Decade on Ecosystem Restoration (2021–2030) and directly responsive to the Post-2020 Global Biodiversity Framework gap on halting native species decline.

Core Methods

  • Correlation analysis (Fig. 1): linear regression of species grid-cell occupancy frequency (1960–1987) against Ellenberg nutrient indicator values (N) for 1,249 German plant species — fitted with 95% confidence interval ribbons to show the positive niche-occupancy relationship.
  • Mann–Whitney U test (Fig. 2): non-parametric comparison of percentage occupancy change over six decades between cultivated and non-cultivated native plants and neophytes; sample sizes and P-values reported per group.
  • Spatial complementarity analysis (Fig. 3): overlay mapping of protected area networks and Urban Atlas green space polygons for 96 German cities; Berlin case study to illustrate network connectivity and spatial complementarity.
  • Red List retail survey (Supplementary Table 2): random sample of 100 threatened species from the German Red List searched across online retailers to estimate current market availability (35% available).
  • Tiered species selection framework (Fig. 5): a conceptual decision tree classifying declining native species by propagation viability, habitat specificity, and genetic risk — from specialist ex situ conservation to citizen-led mainstreaming.
  • Review and synthesis: the paper is framed as a review/perspective, drawing on global literature and case examples from New Zealand, oceanic islands, and China to generalize findings beyond Germany.

① Spatial accessibility of conservation gardening resources as a GeoAI problem

Extending this paperThe paper documents that only 35% of 100 sampled threatened German species are available from online retailers, but it does not spatially characterize where those retailers are or whether access to native plant material is equitably distributed across urban areas. A direct extension: map the spatial accessibility of horticultural outlets stocking conservation-gardening species using network-based catchment analysis (e.g., 2SFCA), overlaid on socio-economic gradients, to identify "conservation gardening deserts" — urban zones where residents have both low access to native plants and limited ecological knowledge.

② Citizen-science plant occurrence data as training signal for geospatial foundation models

Extending this paperThe paper relies on long-term floristic monitoring data (ref. 25) covering ~60 years and 5 × 5 km grid cells, but acknowledges that "floristic inventory data of domestic gardens are far from complete." An extension: integrate crowd-sourced plant occurrence data (iNaturalist, GBIF) with the paper's tiered species framework to continuously update occupancy estimates at fine spatial resolution and detect early signals of conservation gardening uptake — testing whether community-reported plantings predict subsequent wild-population recovery.

③ Quantifying nature-inclusivity equity: green gentrification risk of scaled conservation gardening

Extending this paperThe paper proposes community outreach programs and consumer labelling without addressing whether conservation gardening investment (public green space upgrades, premium-labelled native seeds) disproportionately benefits higher-income neighborhoods — a "green gentrification" risk noted in the parallel Nature Cities literature. Extending this paper: apply the Urban Atlas 2012 spatial layer to correlate green space improvement potential with neighborhood socio-economic indices, using the paper's own 96-city Germany dataset to test whether conservation gardening policy outcomes could inadvertently widen equity gaps.

④ Explainable ML to predict species suitability for urban conservation gardening at scale

Extending this paperThe paper's tiered species-selection framework (Fig. 5) uses qualitative ecological criteria — propagation viability, habitat specificity, invasiveness risk — to classify species for conservation gardening. This classification is expert-guided and laborious. An extension: train an explainable ensemble model (gradient boosting + SHAP values) on the known cultivated/non-cultivated and occupancy-trend data to learn which trait combinations predict successful conservation gardening candidates, then apply it to thousands of species in the Plants of the World Online and GloNAF databases to automate the tiered triage globally.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my explainable machine learning for spatial prediction: the outcome variable is species occupancy change (a spatial process), the features are ecological traits and spatial niche indicators, and SHAP gives interpretable rankings of which traits matter most — satisfying both the conservation practitioner (which species to plant?) and the researcher (which ecological mechanisms drive success?). The pipeline would be reusable for any country where Red List and trait databases are available.
