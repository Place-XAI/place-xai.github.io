Closed-loop transfer enables artificial intelligence to yield chemical knowledge

Nicholas H. Angello · David M. Friday · Changhyun Hwang · Seungjoo Yi · Austin H. Cheng · Tiara C. Torres-Flores · Edward R. Jira · Wesley Wang · Alán Aspuru-Guzik · Martin D. Burke · Charles M. Core Conclusion

Closed-loop transfer (CLT) integrates AI-guided Bayesian optimization with physics-based feature selection and supervised learning to simultaneously optimize objective functions and generate interpretable chemical knowledge. Applied to photostability of light-harvesting donor–acceptor molecules, CLT identified that high-energy triplet state density governs photodegradation by characterizing only ~1.5% of the 2,200-molecule theoretical chemical space — and validated this insight by solvent-mediated triplet-energy tuning to escape optimization plateaus.

  • Transforms black-box optimization into a knowledge engine. Prior AI-guided closed-loop systems optimized properties without explaining why; CLT extracts interpretable, physics-grounded hypotheses in parallel with optimization — a methodological shift with broad implications.
  • Radical efficiency in chemical space exploration. Only ~33 out of 2,200 molecules (~1.5%) needed experimental synthesis and characterization to uncover the mechanistic role of the triplet state manifold — a drastic reduction in experimental cost.
  • Fundamental insight, not just a better molecule. The discovery that high-energy triplet state density (TDOS at ~4 eV) predicts photostability represents new mechanistic understanding of organic electronics degradation, not merely an optimized compound.
  • Generalizable beyond the case study. CLT was applied to additional materials systems, supporting its use as a broadly applicable strategy for AI-augmented closed-loop experimentation in chemistry and materials science.
  • Hypothesis validation by design. The triplet-energy tuning validation experiment — changing the solvent to shift excited-state energetics and break a plateau — demonstrates that CLT generates testable, falsifiable scientific hypotheses.

Core Methods

  • Closed-loop Bayesian optimization (Gryffin): iterative rounds in which the optimizer proposes molecules for synthesis, experiments are run, results are returned, and the optimizer updates its surrogate model — balancing exploration and exploitation across the combinatorial chemical space.
  • Physics-based feature selection: DFT-computed quantum chemical features (especially triplet density of states, TDOS, at various energies) are combined with RDKit descriptors and ranked by predictive power via exhaustive 2- and 4-feature model comparisons using leave-one-out cross-validation.
  • Supervised regression (SVR): support vector regression models trained on each closed-loop round's experimental data; model coefficients and feature rankings provide interpretable hypotheses about the molecular determinants of photostability.
  • Closed-loop transfer (CLT) protocol: CLT = closed-loop optimization (phases I and II) + physics feature extraction + supervised learning, run in parallel so that each round of optimization also yields a hypothesis that can be tested in the next round.
  • Hypothesis validation by solvent tuning: the identified role of high-energy triplet states was experimentally validated by testing molecules in solvents (chlorobenzene, toluene, decane) with different triplet energies to confirm Dexter energy transfer as the degradation mechanism.
  • Automated modular synthesis: automated small-molecule synthesizer (described in Extended Data Fig. 3) enables rapid synthesis of suggested molecules with minimal human intervention, closing the experimental loop efficiently.

① Replace exhaustive feature enumeration with active feature learning

Extending this paperCLT's current physics-feature selection evaluates all possible 2- and 4-feature combinations via leave-one-out cross-validation — a brute-force approach that becomes combinatorially intractable as the feature set grows. A direct extension: embed the CLT loop within a deep Bayesian active learning framework where the acquisition function selects not only the next molecule to synthesize but also the next feature dimension to compute (e.g., which DFT property to calculate next), treating feature acquisition as another expensive experiment to optimize. This would radically compress the DFT computation budget alongside the synthesis budget, and naturally produce uncertainty-quantified predictions at every round.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly onto my work with explainable ML for spatial prediction: in GeoAI pipelines, acquiring additional remote sensing layers or POI data is costly and not always worth it. A Bayesian active-feature-acquisition loop — analogous to CLT's round-by-round experimental design — would let spatiotemporal models adaptively select which new data source (a new satellite band, a social media feature, a census variable) most reduces prediction uncertainty, creating a principled multi-source data fusion strategy aligned with my FAIR/open-science agenda.

② Transfer the physics-informed SVR model across related chemical families

Extending this paperCLT phases I and II characterize ~1.5% of a specific 2,200-molecule D-B-A space and then validate on the same family. The paper asserts generalizability to "additional materials systems" but the mechanism of transfer is not formalized. A direct extension: treat the physics-based SVR model (and its learned feature weights) as a source-domain prior and perform transfer learning to a structurally related chemical family — e.g., perovskite light harvesters or polymer donors — starting from the frozen TDOS feature coefficients and fine-tuning on a handful of new experimental data points. This would reduce the data budget for new material classes from ~1.5% to an even smaller fraction.

③ Couple interpretable hypothesis extraction to causal intervention design

Extending this paperCLT's insight — TDOS at ~4 eV drives photostability — was validated by a clever solvent-tuning experiment that isolates the causal variable. But this validation was designed by domain experts post-hoc after reading the SVR coefficients. A direct extension: formalize hypothesis extraction as a causal active learning step where the system automatically proposes optimal intervention experiments (which molecular modification or environmental condition to change) to most efficiently distinguish correlation from causation in the feature importance rankings. This would systematize the paper's most scientifically exciting step — turning a black-box correlation into a mechanistic explanation.

④ Scale CLT's interpretable loop to high-dimensional molecular graphs

Extending this paperCLT's supervised learning component uses physics-curated low-dimensional features (4 DFT features) and linear SVR — a deliberately interpretable but limited model. A direct extension: replace the fixed feature set with a molecular graph neural network that learns both the feature representation and the property predictor jointly, but adds an interpretability bottleneck (e.g., graph attribution or contrastive explanation) so the closed loop still extracts symbolic hypotheses. This would allow CLT to scale to larger, more complex molecular spaces where hand-curated DFT features are not available, without sacrificing the knowledge-generation goal.

Extending this paperCLT demonstrates that an optimization loop — traditionally used only to find the best molecule — can simultaneously generate mechanistic knowledge if paired with interpretable models and physics features.
