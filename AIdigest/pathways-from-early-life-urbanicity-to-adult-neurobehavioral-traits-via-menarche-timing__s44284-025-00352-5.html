Pathways from early-life urbanicity to adult neurobehavioral traits via menarche timing

Lining Guo · Feng Liu · Wenshuang Zhu · Jingliang Cheng · Wen Qin · Meng Liang · Xi-Nian Zuo · Yongqiang Yu · Hui Zhang · Chunshui Yu · and the CHIMGEN Consortium Published 2025-12-15 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00352-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

In 2,950 young Chinese women, higher early-life urbanicity was associated with earlier age at menarche (AAM), which in turn correlated with reduced medial prefrontal cortex volume and lower agreeableness and reward dependence in adulthood. Family socioeconomic status was the dominant urbanicity factor acting through AAM. Crucially, the same urbanicity- and AAM-related brain and personality signatures are altered in patients with major depression and schizophrenia, suggesting that life history theory — accelerated maturation under environmental pressure — is a candidate mechanism linking the urban environment to the adult brain, personality and mental-disorder risk in women.

★

Why It Matters

  • Opens the "black box" between city and mind. Urbanicity is a well-known risk factor for mental disorders, but the causal chain was vague. This work proposes a concrete, testable mediator — pubertal timing — turning a correlation into a developmental pathway.
  • Connects urban planning to neurodevelopment. It shows that the place a girl grows up in is statistically linked to when she matures, how her prefrontal cortex develops, and the personality traits she carries into adulthood — a striking spatial-to-biological coupling.
  • Identifies family SES as the key lever. Among many urbanicity components (built-up area, nighttime lights, air pollution, city economy), family socioeconomic status emerges as the dominant factor acting through AAM — a policy-relevant, modifiable target.
  • Bridges population exposure and clinical disorder. By mapping the urbanicity/AAM brain-and-personality signature onto schizophrenia and major depressive disorder via meta-analysis, the study links everyday environmental exposure to clinical phenotypes.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Study sample
2,950 young Chinese women drawn from the CHIMGEN (Chinese Imaging Genetics) cohort — a multi-site consortium designed for cross-ethnic, cross-region imaging-genetics research. Analyses use age at menarche (AAM), personality questionnaires, structural MRI (sMRI) and genotype data; participants without residential addresses or failing sMRI quality control are excluded (see Extended Data Fig. 1).
Urbanicity exposure
Reconstructed from lifetime residential histories using gridded geospatial products: percentage of built-up area and nighttime lights (NL), averaged both from birth-to-AAM and over a fixed window (ages 0–9). Built-up area derives from Annual 30-m land use/land cover maps of China, 1980–2015 (AVHRR/MODIS/Landsat integration, BFAST algorithm); NL from DMSP Operational Linescan System products.
Air pollution
PM2.5 from a combined geophysical–statistical method (satellite + models + monitors, van Donkelaar et al.) and NO2 inferred from satellite observations (Geddes et al.).
Socioeconomic context
Family SES (parental education, occupation, income, financial difficulties/crisis, unemployment stress), adverse-SES (neighborhood violence/indifference, dwelling-space inadequacy) and city-SES (GDP, education funding, medical practitioners and hospital beds per 10,000). City-level economy/GDP supported by gridded GDP inference products for the Chinese mainland.
Genetics
Polygenic score for AAM (PGSAAM) built from a trans-ancestral GWAS meta-analysis (European + Japanese cohorts; loci incl. LIN28B, KISS1, GNRH1); best-fit model (PT=0.0014) explained 5.1% of AAM variance.
Clinical anchoring
Coordinate/effect-size meta-analyses of case–control gray-matter, agreeableness and reward-dependence differences in schizophrenia (SCZ) and major depressive disorder (MDD), assembled from published literature (Extended Data Figs. 7–9).
Availability
Individual-level CHIMGEN data are restricted (contractual + Human Genetics Resources of China regulations); access via committee-approved proposal and data-access agreement (contact C. Yu). Neuroimaging statistical maps available on reasonable request. All analysis tools are publicly available (described in Methods); Supplementary Tables 1–15 provide statistics. Core Methods

  • Life-course exposure reconstruction: for each participant, link residential history to gridded built-up%, nighttime lights, PM2.5 and NO2, computing early-life urbanicity both as a birth-to-AAM average and a fixed 0–9-year window; all variables Z-scored.
  • Mediation analysis: test whether AAM mediates the effect of early-life urbanicity on adult regional brain volumes (voxel/region-based morphometry) and personality (Big Five Inventory; Tridimensional Personality Questionnaire).
  • Structural equation modeling (SEM): model latent constructs (air pollution, family SES, adverse SES, city SES) → observed exposures → AAM → five neurobehavioral outcomes, separating direct vs. indirect (through-AAM) effects.
  • Genetic instrumentation: derive PGSAAM via P-value-threshold optimization to probe the role of genetically-predicted pubertal timing.
  • Sensitivity / robustness: adjust for residential mobility and urbanicity fluctuation; cross-check individual- vs. group-level associations and alternative exposure windows.
  • Clinical translation: meta-analyze case–control differences in gray-matter volume, agreeableness and reward dependence for SCZ and MDD, then overlay onto the urbanicity/AAM signature.

① Build a continuous, high-resolution early-life exposome instead of coarse urbanicity proxies

Extending this paperThe paper reconstructs urbanicity from built-up%, nighttime lights, PM2.5 and NO2 on coarse annual grids tied to residential addresses. A direct extension: fuse finer-grained, time-varying exposures (greenness/NDVI, noise, heat, street-level imagery, mobility-derived activity space) into a person-specific spatiotemporal exposome from conception through menarche, replacing the categorical urbanicity index with a continuous, multi-channel exposure field — sharpening exactly which environmental dimension drives the AAM acceleration.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my multi-source data fusion agenda (remote sensing + social media + mobile-phone + POIs): a reusable life-course exposome pipeline that I can also plug into telehealth/digital-divide and mental-health accessibility studies as a covariate stack.

② Replace linear SEM mediation with an explainable spatial ML pathway model

Extending this paperThe mediation here is estimated with linear SEM, which assumes additive, stationary effects of urbanicity on AAM and downstream brain/personality. Extending it: re-estimate the urbanicity→AAM→neurobehavior chain with explainable ML (gradient boosting + SHAP, or causal forests) to surface nonlinear thresholds and interactions (e.g., does air pollution only matter above a built-up threshold?), and test whether the family-SES dominance is stable across exposure regimes.

Added value to my researchDirectly feeds my explainable machine learning for spatial prediction line — turning a mediation diagram into an interpretable, nonstationary attribution that pairs naturally with MGWR/GWR for spatially-varying urbanicity effects.

③ Add the built form: from urbanicity index to street-network morphology

Extending it: append street-network and 15-minute-neighborhood metrics to the exposure set and ask whether physical form independently shapes AAM and prefrontal volume — bridging this developmental-mediation result with the urban-design literature linking connectivity to brain structure.

Extending it: combine the SEM effect sizes with neighborhood SES surfaces to map where the urbanicity→AAM→mental-disorder burden concentrates, and frame intervention as an efficiency-vs-equity targeting problem — which neighborhoods, if SES-supported, yield the largest reduction in projected risk.
