Free-energy machine for combinatorial optimization

Zi-Song Shen · Feng Pan · Yao Wang · Yi-Ding Men · Wen-Biao Xu · Man-Hong Yung · Pan Zhang Published 2025-03-24 DOI 10.1038/s43588-025-00782-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The free-energy machine (FEM) unifies combinatorial optimization within a single statistical-physics framework: by minimizing a variational mean-field free energy via GPU-parallel gradient descent with annealing, it solves MaxCut, balanced minimum cut, and Max k-SAT problems — scaled to millions of variables — outperforming state-of-the-art specialized algorithms in both accuracy and speed. FEM reaches best-known solutions in 448 of 454 MaxSAT competition instances and reduces FPGA inter-chip communication by up to 26.6% relative to METIS on a real-world chip-verification graph with 1.5 million nodes, demonstrating that combining statistical physics with modern machine-learning optimization tools yields broad-applicability, GPU-native solvers.

Why It Matters

  • One framework, many NP-hard problems. Existing high-performance solvers (simulated bifurcation, coherent Ising machines) are locked to QUBO/Ising formulations. FEM handles multivalued Potts-model problems and higher-order k-SAT with a single codebase change — a genuine unification.
  • Supercharges parallel hardware. All mean-field probabilities across an entire batch of replica distributions are updated simultaneously on a GPU, yielding ~10x speedup over CPU and making million-variable problems tractable in seconds.
  • Industry-grade validation. The paper includes a confidential real-world FPGA chip-verification graph (1,495,802 nodes, 3,380,910 edges from Huawei) where FEM significantly outperforms the production tool METIS, bridging academic benchmarks and industrial practice.
  • Automatic differentiation lowers the barrier. Practitioners only need to specify an energy function; PyTorch's autograd engine computes gradients automatically, so adaptation to a new COP requires changing literally one line of code.
  • Transparent benchmarking and open code. All benchmark datasets and source code are publicly available on GitHub and Zenodo, enabling reproducibility and comparison by the community.

Core Methods

  • Variational mean-field free-energy minimization: the COP cost function is cast as a Boltzmann energy; a factored mean-field distribution PMF(σ) = ∏i Pi(σi) approximates the Boltzmann distribution at zero temperature by minimizing the Kullback–Leibler divergence, equivalent to minimizing the variational mean-field free energy FMF = UMF − SMF/β.
  • Simulated annealing via inverse temperature schedule: β is gradually increased from βmin to βmax using either exponential or inverse-proportional schedules, allowing the system to escape local minima and converge to approximate ground states.
  • Automatic differentiation and Adam optimizer: the N×q local-field parameters {hi(σi)} are updated by PyTorch autograd; Adam, SGD, and RMSprop are all supported, enabling fast and stable gradient descent on GPU.
  • Batched replica distributions (RSB-inspired): inspired by replica symmetry breaking theory, m independent mean-field distributions are optimized in parallel (batch size R up to 1,000), capturing the rugged energy landscape diversity and selecting the minimum-energy configuration at the end.
  • Explicit gradient formulations and manipulated gradients: for MaxCut and bMinCut, closed-form gradient expressions are derived; a normalization technique counteracts node-degree heterogeneity, stabilizing gradient magnitudes and boosting performance on scale-free graphs.
  • Softmax parameterization: marginal probabilities Pi(σi) = softmax(hi) enforce probabilistic constraints; the framework naturally handles q-state (Potts) and higher-order (k-SAT) problems without Ising reformulation overhead.
  • Benchmark comparison: FEM is compared against dSBM (discrete simulated bifurcation machine), METIS, KaFFPaE, Max-CTDS, and MaxSAT 2016 competition solvers, using TTS (time-to-solution) and cut/clause-satisfaction quality metrics.

① Apply FEM to spatial facility-location optimization as a general COP

Extending this paperThe paper benchmarks FEM on MaxCut, bMinCut, and MaxSAT — all abstract combinatorial problems. A direct extension is to encode spatial facility-location problems (p-median, p-center, coverage maximization) as Potts-model COPs and benchmark FEM against integer-programming and local-search solvers. The softmax parameterization already handles multi-category assignment (which facility type a location receives), and the balanced-partition (bMinCut) objective maps naturally onto equitable service-area delineation with demand-balance constraints. The paper's framework needs only a new energy function specifying distance-weighted coverage.

③ Replace FEM's naive mean-field ansatz with a graph neural network variational distribution

Extending this paperThe paper's Discussion acknowledges that the naive mean-field approximation struggles with full replica-symmetry-breaking (RSB) regimes on large random graphs, where the energy landscape is deeply rugged. The natural upgrade — noted but not implemented — is a GNN-parameterized variational distribution: replace the independent-marginal factorization PMF(σ) = ∏i Pi(σi) with a GNN that generates correlated marginals respecting graph topology. The free-energy objective remains identical; only the parameterization becomes expressive, building on established neural network ansatze for variational inference.

Added value to my researchA GNN-FEM hybrid is precisely what I need for my physics-informed graph learning agenda: the GNN encodes spatial autocorrelation (geographic topology) as an inductive bias, while the free-energy objective provides a principled training signal without labeled ground-truth solutions. ④ Encode spatial accessibility as a k-SAT problem and solve with FEM for optimal service coverage

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates FEM's Max k-SAT capability: each clause represents a logical constraint over Boolean variables. A direct extension is to encode spatial accessibility compliance as a k-SAT problem: each Boolean variable indicates whether a facility is opened at a candidate site; each clause encodes whether at least one open facility lies within the catchment of a demand node. Maximizing satisfied clauses is then equivalent to maximizing population coverage — a natural COP that FEM already solves. Scaling to city-level grids (millions of demand nodes) would leverage FEM's GPU parallelism.

Added value to my researchThis gives my spatial accessibility research a direct pipeline to near-optimal facility placement without integer programming solvers. The Max k-SAT formulation is especially attractive for multi-modal catchment problems — where a clause is satisfied if any of k transport modes provides access — enabling me to quantify digital/physical hybrid healthcare access in the telehealth-vs-in-person framework I study, with guaranteed near-optimality rather than heuristic coverage.

⑤ Benchmark FEM against photonic/quantum Ising machines for geospatial graph partitioning

Extending this paperThe paper compares FEM with software-simulated Ising machines (dSBM) but not against physical hardware realizations (photonic Ising machines, D-Wave quantum annealers). The paper's framing of the Potts-model bMinCut as a natural extension of Ising naturally invites this hardware comparison.
