Urban cooling and energy-saving effects of nature-based solutions across types and scales

Hailu Wei · Xiaohang Bai · Qinhui Lu · Jiyuan Wu · Fengmin Su · Tianzhen Hong · Qinran Hu · Wei Wang · Steven Jige Quan · Zhixing Luo · Yilong Han Published 2025-11-17 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00349-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across a meta-analysis of 373 peer-reviewed studies (2013–2025) spanning all 16 Köppen–Geiger climate zones, nature-based solutions (NBS) cut daytime temperatures by 2.04 ± 0.17 °C during hot periods and lower annual building cooling loads by 1.32 ± 0.06% globally. Green infrastructure outperforms blue infrastructure for both thermal regulation and energy savings in most climates, and outcomes are scale-dependent: neighborhood-scale NBS deliver the strongest cooling (−2.22 ± 0.25 °C) while building-scale strategies yield the largest energy savings (8.62 ± 0.78%). These climate- and scale-specific patterns give planners actionable guidance for prioritizing NBS.

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a fragmented evidence base. Hundreds of NBS cooling studies disagree because they use different climates, scales, and metrics; this work synthesizes them into comparable, climate-stratified effect sizes for the first time at this breadth.
  • Separates "what" from "where" and "how big." It disentangles NBS type (green vs. blue), scale (building / neighborhood / city), and climate zone — three axes that planners actually choose along.
  • Connects cooling to energy and carbon. By reporting both temperature reductions and building cooling-load savings, it links heat mitigation directly to energy demand and urban energy resilience.
  • Counters "more green is always better" intuition. The finding that the strongest cooling and the strongest energy savings occur at different scales means a single intervention rarely optimizes both goals at once.

Core Methods

  • Systematic literature synthesis: screen and extract effect sizes from 373 NBS studies (2013–2025) covering temperature and building-energy outcomes.
  • Pooled effect estimation: aggregate reported cooling and energy-saving effects into global means with uncertainty (± standard error), e.g. 2.04 ± 0.17 °C and 1.32 ± 0.06%.
  • Climate-zone stratification: partition effects across all 16 Köppen–Geiger zones to expose climate-specific performance.
  • Type comparison: contrast green vs. blue infrastructure for thermal regulation and energy savings.
  • Scale decomposition: compare building / neighborhood / city scales, isolating where cooling vs. energy benefits peak.
  • Mechanistic framing: map evaporative, shading and albedo mechanisms (Fig. 2) onto the multiscale outcome patterns.
