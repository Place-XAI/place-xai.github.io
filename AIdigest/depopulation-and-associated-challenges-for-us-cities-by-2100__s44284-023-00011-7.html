Depopulation and associated challenges for US cities by 2100

Uttara Sutradhar · Lauryn Spearing · Sybil Derrible Published 2024-01-11 DOI 10.1038/s44284-023-00011-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

By 2100, close to half of the nearly 30,000 US cities are projected to lose population under at least one scenario — representing 12–23% of the population and 27–44% of the populated area of these cities. This decline threatens basic services (transit, clean water, electricity, internet), while simultaneous growth in resource-intensive suburban and periurban places diverts resources away from shrinking areas. The authors argue that immigration can help but cannot fix it alone: avoiding a service-provision crisis requires a paradigm shift away from growth-based planning toward planning for decline.

★

Why It Matters

  • Names a blind spot in planning. Almost all infrastructure, finance and zoning practice assumes growth; this is a near-nationwide, city-level quantification showing that shrinkage will be the dominant trajectory for half of US cities within the century.
  • Connects demography to service viability. Depopulation erodes the ratepayer/tax base that keeps fixed-cost networks — water mains, transit, the grid, broadband — solvent, so the paper frames decline as an infrastructure-financing crisis, not just a count of fewer people.
  • Exposes a spatial inequity loop. Growth concentrates in resource-intensive suburbs and periurban areas, pulling resources and investment away from already-shrinking cores and exacerbating their decline.
  • Scenario-honest. Results are reported across multiple Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs) rather than a single forecast, making the range of outcomes — and their dependence on policy and migration — explicit for decision-makers.

Core Methods

  • Trend estimation: derive historical population trajectories per city from three decennial censuses (2000–2020), classifying places as growing or declining (Fig. 1).
  • Scenario projection: propagate Hauer (2019) SSP-controlled county projections to 2100, with SSP2 as the central case and the SSP range bracketing uncertainty (Fig. 2).
  • Spatial disaggregation: use GeoCorr 2022 crosswalks to allocate county-level projections to the city/place level across four US regions and degree-of-urbanization classes (Fig. 3).
  • Socioeconomic cross-tabulation: overlay projected trends with ACS median income/tax rate (Fig. 4) and vehicle ownership (Fig. 5) to surface fiscal and mobility vulnerability of depopulating areas.
  • Immigration analysis: identify cities with increasing international immigration using Hispanic and Asian population share and correlations from historical data (Fig. 6), testing immigration as a partial counterweight to decline.

① From "where will people leave" to "where will services collapse"

Extending this paperThe paper maps projected depopulation and qualitatively warns of disruptions to transit, water, electricity and internet, but never quantifies the service threshold at which each network becomes financially or operationally unviable. A direct extension: couple the SSP city trajectories to per-capita fixed-cost models for each utility and compute, city by city, the year each network crosses its break-even ratepayer density — converting the paper's qualitative warning into a dated, rankable risk surface.

Added value to my researchThis is exactly my 2SFCA/R2SFCA machinery run in reverse and over time: instead of measuring access given supply, I project when shrinking demand can no longer sustain supply, yielding a temporal accessibility-decay map for primary care, telehealth and broadband in depopulating places.

Cross-ref (corpus): Rising infrastructure inequalities accompany urbanization and economic development — 10.1038/s41467-025-56539-w

② Replace crosswalk apportionment with a learned spatial-disaggregation model

Extending this paperThe paper pushes county-level Hauer projections down to ~30,000 places via GeoCorr crosswalks — an areal/share-based apportionment that ignores within-county heterogeneity in who actually leaves. Extending it: train an explainable ML disaggregator (gradient boosting / spatial regression) on 2000–2020 observed change to learn place-level shrinkage as a function of income, vehicle access, age structure and built form, then redistribute the SSP totals with these learned weights.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my explainable machine learning for spatial prediction agenda: SHAP/feature attributions reveal which place attributes drive decline, and the same multi-source-fusion pipeline (census + ACS + remote sensing + POIs) sharpens dasymetric downscaling I use elsewhere.

Cross-ref (corpus): Spatial structure of city population growth — 10.1038/s41467-022-33527-y

③ Make immigration endogenous via a migration-flow network

Extending this paperThe paper treats immigration as an exogenous proxy (Hispanic/Asian share correlated to historical data) and concludes it "could play a vital role" but cannot fix decline alone. Extending it: embed a domestic + international migration-flow network so that one city's loss is another's gain, letting depopulation and growth be solved jointly rather than per-city — directly testing the paper's claim that suburban growth drains shrinking cores.

Added value to my researchThis connects to my network community detection and job-housing / urban-agglomeration work: migration as a directed weighted graph lets me detect functional regions of redistribution and quantify which collaborations could rebalance services across a metro.

Cross-ref (corpus): Domestic migration and city rank dynamics — 10.1038/s44284-024-00170-1 · Global divergence in urban demographic change and migration patterns — 10.1038/s44284-026-00447-7

④ Add a health-and-mortality consequence layer to the depopulation map

Extending this paperThe paper stops at service disruption and never links shrinkage to population health outcomes. Extending it: overlay projected depopulation with health-system access and stress indicators to estimate where service withdrawal translates into excess morbidity/mortality — making the human cost of "planning for decline" explicit rather than implicit.

Added value to my researchThis is the core of my health geography line: combine the SSP trajectories with spatial accessibility of primary care and mental-health services and telehealth/digital-divide layers to flag depopulating places that become simultaneous care deserts — a public-health-policy targeting tool.

Cross-ref (corpus): Association between urban shrinkage and excess mortality during the COVID-19 pandemic — 10.1038/s44284-025-00278-y

⑤ Right-size shrinking transit as an equity-aware facility-location problem

Extending this paperThe paper flags transit and vehicle-dependence (Fig. 5) as vulnerabilities but offers no remediation. Extending it: pose network right-sizing under decline — which stops/lines/facilities to consolidate as demand falls — as an efficiency-vs-equity optimization with a resilience constraint, so service contraction does not strand low-income, low-vehicle households (Fig. 4–5 populations).

Added value to my researchThis reuses my EV-charging efficiency-vs-equity optimization and physics-informed graph learning directly: the same multi-objective solver that sites facilities under growth can retire them under shrinkage while protecting access for vulnerable groups — a reusable shrinking-city allocator.
