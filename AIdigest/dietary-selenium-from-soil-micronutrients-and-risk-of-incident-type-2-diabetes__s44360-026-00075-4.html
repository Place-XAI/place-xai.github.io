Dietary selenium from soil micronutrients and risk of incident type 2 diabetes

Wang, Fang-Fang · Sang, Miao-Miao · Lü, Qi-Xin · Gu, Yi · Wu, Tong-Zhi · Tang, Zhu · Xie, Wan-Ying · Qiu, Shan-Hu · Yuan, Yang · Zhou, Xiao-Ying · Schomburg, Lutz · Kopittke, Peter M. · Shi, Xiao-Ming · Zhao, Fang-Jie · Sun, Zi-Lin · Wang, Peng Published 2026-03-20 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00075-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Soil selenium content — determined by local geochemistry — is a significant predictor of serum selenium in Chinese residents, even after controlling for diet, socioeconomic status, and air quality. In a prospective cohort, men in the highest serum selenium tertile (107.2–162.8 µg L−1) faced a 35% higher risk of incident type 2 diabetes (RR = 1.35; 95% CI = 1.02–1.77) versus the lowest tertile — an effect driven by men (RR = 1.96) but not significant in women. Globally, an estimated 14% of land area (~1 billion people) sits on high-selenium soils where residents may already exceed the threshold at which further selenium intake increases diabetes risk.

Why It Matters

  • Connects landscape to metabolism at scale. The study is the first to link high-resolution soil trace-element maps directly to serum ionomics and metabolic disease risk across multi-region Chinese cohorts — demonstrating that where you live shapes your internal chemical environment independent of lifestyle choices.
  • Challenges common supplement advice. Selenium is widely sold as a health supplement; this prospective evidence that high selenium doubles diabetes risk in men implies supplementation guidance must be geography-aware — harmful in selenium-rich regions, potentially beneficial in deficient ones.
  • Reveals a global diabetes burden driven by geology. Projecting serum selenium from a global soil-selenium map identifies approximately 1 billion people in high-risk zones (14% of land area), providing a new geographically explicit framing of preventable diabetes risk.
  • Opens the ionomics-as-epidemiology paradigm. By profiling 20+ elements in both serum and urine from 3,648 participants, the study demonstrates that multi-element ionomic signatures — not single nutrients — characterize geographic health gradients.
  • Sex-specific risk highlights mechanistic gaps. The absence of association in women points to hormonal or physiological buffering mechanisms, with implications for personalized public-health interventions.

Core Methods

  • Human ionomics profiling: ICP-MS measurement of 20+ trace elements in serum and urine samples from 3,648 participants; elements with detection rates ≥ 60% retained; principal component analysis (PCA) used to characterize geographic and demographic patterns in multi-element profiles.
  • Multi-element mixture models: Weighted quantile sum (WQS) regression, quantile g-computation (qg-comp), and Bayesian Kernel Machine Regression (BKMR) applied to identify elements and element mixtures most predictive of diabetes, hyperlipidaemia, and hypertension risk, with full confounder adjustment.
  • Dose–response modeling: Restricted cubic spline (RCS) regression to characterize non-linear relationships between serum Se and metabolic outcomes; sex-stratified analyses to detect effect modification.
  • Random forest for soil-to-serum prediction: Random forest model trained to predict serum Se from soil Se plus regional dietary, socioeconomic, and air-quality indicators; variable importance analysis confirmed soil Se as the dominant predictor (Extended Data Fig. 7).
  • Proximity-based spatial assignment: Seven spatial scenarios (county, city, provincial, 50/100/200/300 km buffers) tested for matching predicted soil Se concentrations to survey participants; validates robustness of the soil-to-serum pathway across spatial scales (Extended Data Fig. 8).
  • Global and future-scenario projection: Global soil Se maps combined with climate-change projections to estimate future serum Se distributions and associated diabetes risk worldwide; ~1 billion people identified in high-selenium land areas.

① Build a spatially explicit ionomics risk surface for public-health screening

Extending this paperThe paper uses soil selenium maps to infer serum selenium and projects diabetes risk nationally and globally. However, the spatial assignment relies on proximity-buffer heuristics tested post-hoc. A direct extension is to replace this with a geostatistical or machine-learning interpolation of serum selenium at fine spatial resolution — integrating soil Se, dietary survey data, air quality, and socioeconomic layers into a continuous risk surface — then validate it against the paper's cohort data. This would convert the paper's binary "high/low region" framing into a probabilistic, granular population-level screening tool, enabling public-health services to target selenium monitoring where it matters most.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my GeoAI and spatiotemporal prediction work: building a soil-selenium-to-diabetes risk surface using ensemble ML (XGBoost / MGWR) with multi-source data fusion (soil maps, remote sensing, POI, census) — an extension of my explainable spatial prediction framework into environmental epidemiology. The resulting risk surface could feed a 2SFCA-style accessibility model asking whether high-risk populations can reach diabetes screening facilities, closing the gap between environmental exposure and healthcare access.

② Integrate plasma/metabolomic mediators to unpack the selenium-to-diabetes biological pathway

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates a statistical association between soil Se → serum Se → diabetes risk, but the biological mechanism is not identified. The ionomics cohort already has serum and urinary measurements that could be extended by integrating plasma proteomics or metabolomics (e.g., insulin signaling proteins, selenoproteins, oxidative stress markers). This would allow mediation analysis to quantify what fraction of the selenium-diabetes association runs through specific molecular pathways — turning a risk association into a mechanistic model and strengthening the causal claim for sex-differential effects.

Added value to my researchThe mediation chain (soil → dietary exposure → circulating biomarker → disease) is a template for my geospatial health work: it demonstrates how to formalize environmental exposure pathways with structured causal models, which could apply to other exposure-disease pairs I analyze (e.g., air-pollution PM2.5 → proteome → cardiometabolic risk). This motivates multi-source data fusion pipelines that link remote-sensing environmental layers to clinical biomarker data, an area where my mobile-phone and remote-sensing fusion methods can contribute.

③ Add dietary pattern heterogeneity to disentangle soil selenium from food choice

Extending this paperThe paper shows soil Se predicts serum Se even after dietary adjustment, but it adjusts for diet at the individual level as a confounder rather than examining how dietary patterns moderate the soil-to-serum pathway. Extending it with a dietary-pattern stratification (e.g., seafood-heavy vs. plant-based vs. grain-based diets) would reveal whether specific foods amplify or buffer the geological selenium signal — crucial for recommending dietary strategies in high-Se regions. The same multi-region cohort already has dietary data, making this a low-cost extension.

Added value to my researchThe interaction between a geographic determinant (soil) and a behavioral determinant (diet) of disease risk mirrors the interaction between neighborhood food environment and individual food choice in my healthy-cities work. Spatial accessibility of food outlets (e.g., seafood vendors, processed-food stores) in high-Se regions could be mapped using POI data and 2SFCA methods to determine whether dietary selenium intake is shaped partly by what foods are spatially accessible — linking environmental health and urban food access in a single framework.
