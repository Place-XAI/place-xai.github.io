Counterevidence of crime-reduction effects from federal grants of military equipment to local police

Anna Gunderson · Elisha Cohen · Kaylyn Jackson Schiff · Tom S. Clark · Adam N. Glynn · Michael Leo Owens Published 2020-12-07 DOI 10.1038/s41562-020-00995-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The widely cited finding that the US federal 1033 Program — which transfers surplus military equipment (SME) to local law enforcement — reduces crime is not credible. Prior studies relied on fundamentally flawed 2014 FOIA data where only 2% of records match the agency-level audit data. When more accurate agency-level LESO inventory data are used across approximately 9,000 US law enforcement agencies (2010–2015), no evidence emerges that military equipment transfers reduce violent or nonviolent crime rates. Discrepant conclusions from prior county-level studies are likely driven by ecological fallacy and faulty measurement rather than any genuine deterrence effect.

Why It Matters

  • Directly overturns a federal policy rationale. The Trump Administration cited two AEJ studies in 2017 to justify restoring military equipment transfers to police. This paper shows those studies rested on deeply flawed data, exposing how unsound empirical foundations can drive consequential national policy decisions.
  • Demonstrates the ecological fallacy danger in public-safety research. County-level aggregation masks heterogeneity within counties — urban crime trends wrongly attributed to suburban or rural military equipment transfers. Moving to the agency level eliminates this systematic bias.
  • Reveals a data-quality crisis in government policing records. Only 2% of records match exactly between the 2014 NPR FOIA dataset and the more recent LESO inventory — a level of data inconsistency that invalidates prior empirical conclusions and warns scholars about trusting government-released administrative data.
  • Opens accountability questions about the 1033 Program. Over 7,000 law enforcement agencies participate and approximately US$7 billion of equipment has been transferred; yet the data to evaluate even basic outcomes remain unreliable, calling for systematic data reform.
  • Provides a replication template. By reproducing two leading studies with original code plus improved data, the paper demonstrates how granular data availability can — and should — be used to audit policy-relevant empirical findings.

Core Methods

  • Data quality audit: match LESO agency-level inventory records to the 2014 NPR FOIA county-level dataset on national stock number (NSN), county, state, and shipping date; quantify discrepancy rates (only 2% exact match; 78% present in NPR data only; 7.4% present in LESO data only).
  • Exact replication: reproduce BG and HPBM results using original authors' code in both Stata and R; confirm coefficient-level match to validate replication fidelity.
  • Instrumental variables (IV) estimation — BG design: instrument for SME value using the interaction of a county's historical propensity to receive aid with US military spending (quantity per lagged US military spend). Panel fixed-effects at county/agency and state×year level; ~9,000 agencies, 2010–2015.
  • Instrumental variables (IV) estimation — HPBM design: four instruments based on proximity to field activity centres (FACs), county land area, and drug-trafficking designation. Two-stage least squares; agency and year fixed effects; clustered standard errors.
  • Multi-level unit-of-analysis comparison: run identical IV specifications at both county-level (aggregated) and agency-level (disaggregated), isolating ecological fallacy as a driver of prior results.
  • Equivalence testing: apply Gross (2020) and Rainey (2014) equivalence-test framework to formally rule out substantively large effect sizes and all significant effects from BG and HPBM.
  • Power analysis: show that null findings are not an artifact of low statistical power — confidence bounds at agency level are tighter than or equal to those from original studies.

③ Replace IV estimation with causal ML to recover heterogeneous treatment effects

Extending this paperBoth the BG and HPBM designs — and the authors' replications — estimate a single average treatment effect of SME on crime. The paper itself notes heterogeneity: some equipment categories (weapons, vehicles, optics) may work differently; urban versus rural agencies differ substantially. An extension directly motivated by this paper's null average result: apply causal forest or double machine learning to the agency-level dataset to recover conditional average treatment effects (CATEs) by agency size, urbanicity, equipment type, and demographics. This would determine whether the null average conceals meaningful heterogeneity (e.g., a crime-reduction effect only for small rural agencies with tactical vehicles).

④ Cross-national replication using military policing experiments in the Global South

Extending this paperThis paper is explicitly limited to the US 1033 Program and acknowledges the possibility that SME effects differ across contexts. The corpus contains a randomized experiment from Cali, Colombia showing military policing likewise fails to reduce crime. A direct extension of this paper: synthesize the US observational evidence (this paper) with Global South experimental evidence into a meta-analytic causal framework, and test whether the null finding generalizes across legal systems, institutional contexts, and equipment types. This would elevate the paper's US-centric conclusion to a more universal empirical claim about military policing efficacy.

⑤ Data-quality auditing as a GeoAI pipeline: automated detection of record-linkage failure in government datasets

Extending this paperThe paper's most novel methodological contribution is not the IV regression but the data audit itself — comparing two administrative datasets item-by-item on NSN, county, state, and date. This was done manually. An extension of this paper's core technique: build a generalizable automated pipeline for government administrative data auditing, using probabilistic record linkage, entity resolution with learned embeddings, and anomaly detection to flag discrepant records at scale. Applied to the LESO data plus future 1033 releases, this would permanently improve the empirical infrastructure for policing research.
