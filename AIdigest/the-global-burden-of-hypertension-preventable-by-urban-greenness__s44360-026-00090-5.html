The global burden of hypertension preventable by urban greenness

Jiayu Wu · Jidian Ruan · Wenjie Di · Jiayao Ying · Jiali Zhou · Zeyu Luo · Igor Rudan · Peige Song Published 2026-03-26 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00090-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

If urban greenness were raised to the 95th percentile of national distributions, an estimated 11.7% of global hypertension prevalence in 2019 could have been prevented — equivalent to a major structural public-health lever across 187 countries. African regions and countries of intermediate socioeconomic development carry the highest preventable burden, older adults (65–79 years) benefit most consistently, and cross-country inequalities in preventable burden grew between 2001 and 2019, underscoring the need for equitable urban greening policies.

Core Methods

  • Systematic umbrella review & meta-analysis: identified and pooled epidemiological studies linking residential NDVI to hypertension risk; derived a pooled relative risk (RR) via random-effects meta-analysis; validated with funnel-plot publication-bias assessment and leave-one-out sensitivity analysis.
  • Population-weighted NDVI extraction: for each of the 30,492 cities, population-weighted mean NDVI was computed from MODIS MOD13Q1 composites, enabling nationally-comparable greenness distributions.
  • Scenario-based health impact framework: three counterfactual scenarios were applied to shift the observed NDVI distribution toward higher levels; for each scenario, the preventable fraction (PAF — population attributable fraction) of hypertension was estimated using the pooled RR and country-level prevalence data.
  • Age-stratified and SDI-stratified analysis: PAF estimates were disaggregated by age group (30–44, 45–64, 65–79 years) and by GBD Socio-Demographic Index quintiles, allowing comparison across demographic and development strata.
  • Temporal trend analysis (2001–2019): average annual percent change (AAPC) in preventable age-standardized prevalence rates (ASPR) computed to track how the preventable burden evolved over time in each country.
  • Cross-country inequality measurement: slope index of inequality (SII) and concentration index (CI) were used to quantify absolute and relative inequality in preventable burden across countries stratified by SDI.

① Replace the constant pooled RR with a spatially varying exposure–response surface

Extending this paperThe current framework applies a single pooled relative risk from the meta-analysis to all 30,492 cities — treating the greenness-hypertension relationship as globally homogeneous. Yet climate, baseline vegetation cover, urban form, and population density vary enormously across cities. A direct extension: model the exposure–response as a spatially non-stationary function using geographically weighted regression (GWR/MGWR), deriving a city-specific RR surface. This would allow the PAF estimate to vary with local context — likely revealing that some regions currently underestimate and others overestimate the preventable burden, which the paper's inequality analysis cannot yet capture.

Added value to my researchThis is exactly the MGWR application space I work in: fitting location-specific regression coefficients to reveal spatial non-stationarity in health–environment relationships. Applying MGWR to the greenness-hypertension linkage would produce a spatially explicit RR surface that feeds directly into spatially-aware burden estimation — connecting my spatial statistics toolkit to global NCD epidemiology.

② Couple the health burden model with greenness accessibility rather than just greenness presence

Extending this paperThe paper measures residential NDVI — the greenness visible or reachable from home — but does not account for whether residents can access that greenness through safe, walkable routes or nearby parks. Two neighborhoods with identical NDVI can differ enormously in effective exposure if one has parks behind security fences and the other has them on every corner. An extension: substitute the MODIS NDVI indicator with a proximity-adjusted greenness accessibility score (walking distance to green space weighted by park area and connectivity), recalculate PAF under the same scenario framework, and compare geographic patterns of preventable burden between the two formulations. The paper in the corpus on small accessible urban parks mitigating heat-mortality provides a worked example of this kind of spatial configuration analysis.

Added value to my researchThis links my 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility methods directly to environmental health burden estimation. Instead of healthcare facility access, the supply side becomes parks and green corridors; the demand side is urban residents at hypertension risk. A "greenness-adjusted R2SFCA" would produce a more policy-relevant exposure metric than NDVI alone — and the resulting spatial patterns would feed into my work on health geography and inequitable access.

③ Extend from national SDI stratification to intra-urban socioeconomic gradient analysis

Extending this paperThe paper's inequality analysis operates at the country level (between countries stratified by SDI), but within any city the preventable burden may be even more unequally distributed: low-income neighborhoods consistently have less greenness than affluent ones (environmental justice gradient). An intra-urban extension: disaggregate the PAF computation to sub-city administrative units using census socioeconomic data and fine-resolution NDVI, then apply the SII/CI inequality metrics within cities as well as between them. This would reveal whether urban greening policies that raise national-average NDVI also reduce or amplify within-city disparities — a question the current global framework cannot answer.

Added value to my researchThis extension mirrors my urban analytics agenda on healthy cities and accessibility equity. Linking fine-resolution greenness shortfalls to neighborhood-level hypertension risk maps would provide a spatially explicit tool for targeting urban greening interventions — directly applicable to my work on spatial accessibility and health geography, and methodologically aligned with spatiotemporal kernel density estimation for exposure mapping.

④ Use explainable machine learning to identify the urban form predictors of greenness-preventable burden

Extending this paperThe paper establishes how much hypertension is preventable by greenness but not which urban characteristics — building density, street connectivity, land-use mix, tree-canopy standard benchmarks — explain why some cities are far below the 95th-percentile threshold. An ML-based extension: train an ensemble (XGBoost + SHAP) on city-level features (density, compactness, land-use entropy, baseline canopy cover from the 3-30-300 benchmark, population growth rate) to predict the preventable PAF; use SHAP decomposition to rank which features most impede or enable greening. This would transform the paper's burden estimates into actionable design levers for urban planners.

⑤ Fuse greenness burden with built-environment physical-activity interventions for a multi-pathway NCD counterfactual

Extending this paperThis paper isolates the greenness pathway to hypertension, but urban environments simultaneously affect NCD burden through physical activity (walkability, cycling infrastructure, park proximity), air pollution (trees filter PM2.5), and stress pathways — all partially mediated by the same greenness variable. An extension using a multi-pathway counterfactual: run the scenario framework with separate exposure–response functions for each pathway (greenness → lower BP; walkability → more activity → lower BP; tree canopy → less PM2.5 → lower BP), estimate joint vs. independent preventable fractions, and test for synergies. This would reveal whether greening and walkability improvements are substitutes or complements in hypertension prevention — a key question for co-benefit urban policy.

Added value to my researchThis multi-pathway framework aligns with my interest in data fusion and healthy cities analysis. Integrating mobile-phone movement data (physical activity proxy), satellite NDVI (greenness), and street-network walkability into a joint burden model would be a novel multi-source fusion application — producing a richer, more policy-coherent picture of urban NCD prevention than any single pathway can provide alone.
