Random sketch learning for deep neural networks in edge computing

Bin Li · Peijun Chen · Hongfu Liu · Weisi Guo · Xianbin Cao · Junzhao Du · Chenglin Zhao · Jun Zhang Published 2021-03-25 DOI 10.1038/s43588-021-00039-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Rosler (random sketch learning) is a compressing-while-training framework that directly learns a compact DNN without pre-training, achieving ~50–90× memory reduction (16-bit quantization) versus fully connected networks. Deployed on a low-cost DSP, it accelerates hardware inference by >180× and cuts energy consumption by ~10×, while also enabling computationally efficient on-device federated learning — closing the fundamental gap between DNN complexity and edge-device resource constraints.

★

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the pre-training bottleneck. All current lightweight DNN methods follow the same pipeline of high-dimensional pre-training followed by post-compression; Rosler is the first to show that a compact model can be learned from scratch, bypassing this computationally expensive stage entirely.
  • Enables real on-device learning. By removing the pre-training requirement, Rosler makes federated learning tractable on sub-watt embedded hardware — enabling privacy-preserving, low-latency intelligence at the network edge.
  • Demonstrated on real silicon. Results are not simulation-only: the method is validated on a DSP C6478 platform (CPU 375 MHz, 256 KB on-chip RAM), achieving 300 μs inference latency for a 500-sample bearing diagnosis task.
  • Unifies MLP and CNN compression. The BUFF structure and aRes-BP algorithm apply to both fully connected and convolutional architectures, yielding a single universal framework across model families.
  • Opens tiny AI to science and industry. With ~20 KB model footprint fitting entirely in on-chip RAM, Rosler removes the off-chip DRAM bottleneck — enabling real-time diagnostics, remote sensing, and IoT analytics at milliwatt power levels.

Core Methods

  • BUFF structure (Butterfly-Unfolding): each dense layer W is represented as a product of three small matrices — a left sketch C, a central body U, and a right sketch R — so W ≈ CUR with rank(CUR) ≤ s, where s is far smaller than min(M, N).
  • Leverage-score random sampling: column and row sketches are initialized by sampling indices according to the leverage-score distribution of the weight matrix's singular vectors, providing theoretical near-optimality guarantees for the approximation.
  • Approximate rank-restricted back-propagation (aRes-BP): a novel gradient update that first updates C and R in parallel using the previous epoch's values, then derives U analytically via a pseudo-inverse, before a successive per-layer fine-tuning pass.
  • Compressing-while-training: no pre-training of a large network is performed; the compact tiny model is learned directly from random initialization, fundamentally changing the training complexity from O(nE KMlNl) to O(nE[KMls + KNls + (Ml+Nl+K)s2]).
  • Feature-map sketching for CNN: extends BUFF to convolutional feature maps X ≈ XcXuXr, reducing off-chip DRAM access by 4.3× beyond weight compression alone.
  • Cooperative parallel federated training: two processors on a local device update C and R concurrently, exchanging only the small sketch matrices rather than full-gradient tensors, halving local computation and reducing communication overhead by ~2× versus SNIP.
  • Hardware energy measurement: instantaneous power computed from voltage-drop across a 6-ohm series resistor plus oscilloscope timing; energy split into full-load and interrupt-sleep periods.

① Deploy Rosler for spatiotemporal prediction on urban edge sensors

Extending this paperRosler is validated only on time-series bearing diagnosis and standard vision benchmarks (MNIST, CIFAR-10). A direct extension is to apply its BUFF structure to spatiotemporal sequence models (LSTMs, temporal graph networks) used for urban sensing tasks such as air quality, traffic flow, or pedestrian density. These models are computationally heavier than the MLP/CNN examples in the paper yet must run on low-cost sensors distributed across a city. The aRes-BP algorithm's architecture-agnostic nature makes this a natural extension, and the federated training capability directly addresses the privacy constraint of per-district sensor data.

A Rosler-compressed spatiotemporal model could enable privacy-preserving on-device inference of health geography indicators (e.g., real-time contact intensity from mobile sensors), removing the need to upload raw GPS trajectories to a server and directly supporting my digital-divide and telehealth-access work.

② Replace fixed sketch dimension s with a learned architecture-search policy

Extending this paperIn Rosler, the sketch dimension s (equivalently the compression ratio per layer) is a user-specified constant, and the paper acknowledges this as its primary limitation. A direct extension: cast the selection of per-layer sampling lengths as a reinforcement learning (RL) problem on the DNN graph, where the agent observes layer-wise gradient norms and memory budgets, and outputs the per-layer sketch dimension that jointly minimizes test error and on-chip footprint. The RL formulation would also generalize across architectures, removing the need for manual grid search of compression ratios.

Added value to my researchThe same RL-driven architecture search is relevant to my physics-informed graph learning work: deciding which graph convolution channels to retain (analogous to sketch dimensions) under a computation budget is structurally identical, and a Rosler-style aRes-BP update on the graph weight tensors would enable on-device inference of spatially heterogeneous phenomena with minimal memory.

Cross-ref (corpus): Efficient and scalable reinforcement learning for large-scale network control — 10.1038/s42256-024-00879-7

③ Extend the BUFF structure to graph neural networks for geospatial prediction

Extending this paperThe paper focuses on dense weight matrices (FC) and 2D convolution kernels. Graph neural networks (GNNs), which operate on sparse adjacency and dense feature matrices simultaneously, are not addressed. Extending Rosler: apply the BUFF decomposition W ≈ CUR to the learnable transformation matrices in each GNN message-passing layer, with the adjacency sparsity handled separately. This would create a tiny GNN that is deployable on edge infrastructure nodes (e.g., 5G base stations, urban sensing hubs) without cloud round-trips, while the aRes-BP convergence guarantees carry over directly.

Added value to my researchMy physics-informed graph learning for spatial prediction (e.g., disease spread, accessibility networks) requires running GNN inference over city-scale spatial graphs. A Rosler-compressed GNN could be deployed as a lightweight geospatial foundation model fine-tuned on-device per city, reducing both cloud transfer cost and privacy exposure of location data — directly supporting my FAIR/open-science data agenda.

⑤ Federated Rosler for privacy-preserving healthcare accessibility modeling

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates federated learning on 3 local clients with bearing data. A more ambitious extension: deploy Rosler-powered federated learning across hospital/clinic networks where each facility holds patient-flow or appointment records that cannot be centralized. The BUFF-based model compression reduces both communication bandwidth (the C and R sketches rather than full weight tensors are transmitted) and on-device training time, making it feasible for low-resource clinical edge hardware. Differential privacy could be added at the sketch level, providing a lightweight privacy amplification beyond what the paper currently offers.

Added value to my researchMy R2SFCA healthcare accessibility work relies on aggregated patient-flow data that health authorities rarely release openly. A Rosler-style federated model could learn demand-side accessibility parameters (distance decay, catchment utilization) directly from privacy-protected facility records, without raw data leaving each site — directly addressing the data-availability bottleneck in my health-geography and telehealth-access research.
