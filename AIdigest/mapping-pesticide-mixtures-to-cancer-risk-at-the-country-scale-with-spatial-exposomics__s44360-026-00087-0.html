Mapping pesticide mixtures to cancer risk at the country scale with spatial exposomics

Jorge Honles · Juan Pablo Cerapio · Claudia Monge · Agnès Marchio · Eloy Ruiz · Ramiro Fernández · Sandro Casavilca-Zambrano · Juan Contreras-Mancilla · Tatiana Vidaurre · Thomas Condom · Swann Zerathe · Olivier Dangles · Éric Deharo · Javier Herrera-Zuñiga · Pascal Pineau · Stéphane Bertani Published 2026-04-01 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00087-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

An integrative spatial Bayesian framework merging a 100 m × 100 m process-based pesticide risk model (31 active ingredients, 2014–2019) with the Peruvian national cancer registry (158,072 cases, 2007–2020) identifies 436 pesticide-associated cancer hotspots concentrated in rural Andean and Amazonian territories, with relative risks of 1.14–9.38. Transcriptomic profiling of hepatocellular carcinoma tissue from Indigenous patients in hotspot zones reveals a non-genotoxic, lineage-specific disruption of master transcription factor networks in histologically normal pre-tumour liver — providing molecular-level evidence that complex pesticide mixtures can drive carcinogenesis through epigenetic destabilization even when individual ingredients are individually deemed non-carcinogenic.

Why It Matters

  • Closes the "mixture gap" in pesticide carcinogenicity. Individual regulatory assessments of pesticides are conducted one compound at a time, yet humans are exposed to mixtures. This is the first nationwide study to model cumulative mixture risk and link it statistically and molecularly to cancer, challenging decades of reductionist toxicology.
  • Unprecedented spatial precision for a low-income setting. A 100 m resolution risk surface covering 95.7% of Peru's territory (1,874 districts), calibrated with hair biomonitoring from 50 individuals, demonstrates that fine-scale geospatial modelling is feasible and informative even without dense sensor networks.
  • Reveals socio-ecological inequity as a cancer driver. Hotspots disproportionately overlap with Andean–Amazonian Indigenous and peasant territories, exposing how land-use pressures, poverty, and environmental degradation co-create cancer burdens in marginalized communities.
  • Introduces lineage-based cancer stratification for environmental epidemiology. By classifying 158,072 cancer cases by embryonic developmental lineage rather than organ site, the study improves Bayesian model fit and uncovers hallmark patterns obscured by conventional ICD-10 groupings.
  • Provides a scalable, transferable blueprint. The process-based model + Bayesian inference + transcriptomic validation pipeline can be replicated in any country with pesticide registration data and a cancer registry, offering a reusable global environmental health assessment architecture.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Cancer registry
INEN (Instituto Nacional de Enfermedades Neoplásicas) Cancer Registry, Peru — 158,072 primary malignant neoplasm cases (ICD-10-CM C00–C96), 2007–2020, geocoded to district level using a self-hosted Nominatim API v4.5.0 with OpenStreetMap data. Residential history cross-validated against Peru's 2007 and 2017 national censuses (INEI); minimum 5-year residency required before diagnosis.
Pesticide use survey
650 agrochemical retailers randomly sampled across five Peruvian regions (Ayacucho, Huancavelica, Huánuco, Junín, Pasco) in 2020, yielding a list of 31 active ingredients (19 insecticides, 7 fungicides, 5 herbicides) modelled over 2014–2019. Data collected via KoboToolbox; commercial names standardized with OpenRefine and matched to PubChem CAS numbers.
Environmental model
Process-based OECD run-off model on a 124,545,177-cell grid (100 m × 100 m; 1,245,451.8 km²) covering 95.7% of Peru. 2,232 monthly raster layers (31 pesticides × 72 months). Environmental inputs: PISCO_HyM_GR2M v1.1 (monthly precipitation/run-off), EarthEnv-DEM90 v1 (terrain slope), GSOCmap-FAO v1.5 (soil organic carbon), Harmonized World Soil Database v1.2 (bulk density).
Land cover calibration

Deforestation covariate
Global Forest Watch (GFW) — emerging forest loss clusters 2002–2023, used as fixed covariate in INLA sensitivity analysis (accessed 23 January 2025).
Hair biomonitoring
50 individuals from 4 Peruvian regions (Huancavelica, Ica, Junín, Lima), 25 rural / 25 urban, equal sex balance; 67 AIs and degradation products quantified (pg/mg hair). Global bivariate Moran's I = 0.42 (P = 0.044) confirms spatial alignment with modelled risk.
Transcriptomic data

Comparative cohorts: France (GSE62232, n=81 HCC / 10 NTL), Taiwan (GSE45436, n=81 HCC / 37 NTL), Turkey (GSE17548, n=17 HCC / 20 NTL). Platform: GeneChip Human Transcriptome Array 2.0. Analysis data at figshare:29728463.

Core Methods

  • Process-based pesticide run-off model (OECD lumped-parameter): computes monthly pesticide fate (run-off mobilization, sorption, degradation) for each of 31 active ingredients on a 100 m × 100 m national grid (124.5 million cells), aggregating 2,232 raster layers into a normalized cumulative risk score (0–100 scale).
  • Spatial autocorrelation & biomonitoring validation: global and local bivariate Moran's I statistics (10,000 Monte Carlo permutations) to validate modelled risk against hair-sample pesticide concentrations from 50 individuals.
  • Developmental lineage stratification of cancer cases: 158,072 ICD-10-CM cancer cases classified into 6 lineage groups (endoderm/ectoderm surface/parenchyma, mesenchyme, non-mesenchymal mesoderm, neuroectoderm, primitive cells) using Berman's 55,000-term neoplasia taxonomy linked to NCI Thesaurus via ICD-10-CM crosswalk.
  • Standardized Incidence Ratios (SIRs): indirect standardization across 6 sex × age strata (0–39, 40–59, ≥60 years) at district level using 2007/2017 national census denominators.
  • Bayesian spatial epidemiology (INLA): latent Gaussian Poisson model with spatially structured (BYM) and unstructured random effects; posterior RR estimates identify hotspots where the lower bound of the 95% credible interval exceeds 1. Model comparison via DIC and WAIC; deforestation covariate sensitivity analysis included.
  • Single-sample gene-set enrichment (SES / AutoCompare): applied to microarray transcriptomes (GeneChip HTA 2.0) from 36 Peruvian HCC / NTL pairs and 3 international cohorts; curated gene sets for non-genotoxic exposure, known HCC risk factors, and 9 lineage-specific master transcription factors.
  • Statistical tests: Kruskal–Wallis with Dunn post-hoc (Bonferroni), Friedman test, two-sided paired Wilcoxon, paired Student's t-test for raster comparisons; all at α = 0.05.

① Transfer the spatial Bayesian pipeline to multi-country pesticide–cancer mapping

Extending this paperThe paper's geospatial pipeline — process-based run-off model calibrated with local retailer surveys, INLA Bayesian hotspot detection, and lineage-stratified cancer registry — is explicitly described as a "transferable blueprint," yet it is applied only to Peru. A direct extension would replicate the framework in neighboring agricultural nations (Bolivia, Ecuador, Colombia) sharing Andean geography and similar pesticide markets, then aggregate national risk surfaces into a harmonized Latin American exposomic atlas. The OISST-based ENSO sensitivity analysis already provides a cross-country climate covariate. The main obstacle — country-level cancer registry comparability — could be addressed through WHO-ICD-10 harmonization, the same approach used to integrate the four transcriptomic cohorts.

② Integrate ENSO-phase climate dynamics into real-time cancer risk nowcasting

Extending this paperThe paper reports a statistically significant increase in modelled pesticide risk during the 2015 "Godzilla" El Niño compared to the 2019 neutral phase (P = 2.2 × 10−16 across 124.5 million cells), yet this finding is treated as a sensitivity check rather than a forecasting tool. A natural extension of the paper: couple the OISST-derived ONI index with the process-based run-off model in a monthly nowcasting loop, generating updated risk maps as ENSO evolves, and test whether leading ENSO indicators (SST anomalies 3–6 months ahead) predict subsequent spatial shifts in cancer incidence with a biologically plausible latency.

③ Replace the OECD lumped-parameter run-off model with an explainable ML ensemble calibrated on biomonitoring

Extending this paperThe process-based OECD model has clear physical grounding but relies on simplified assumptions about slope, interception, and sorption that may not hold across Peru's heterogeneous biomes (Pacific desert, Andean highland, Amazonian lowland). The 50-individual hair biomonitoring dataset already provides a spatial validation signal (bivariate Moran's I = 0.42). Extending the paper: train an ensemble ML model (e.g., gradient boosted trees or a GWR-XGBoost hybrid) that takes the same 2,232 monthly raster layers as inputs and predicts hair biomonitoring concentrations, then use SHAP feature attribution to identify which environmental variables (precipitation ratio, slope, SOC content) are most predictive in each biome — effectively producing a data-driven spatial correction to the OECD equation.

Added value to my researchMy explainable ML agenda is precisely this: fitting spatially non-stationary models where feature importance varies by location. A MGWR or GWR-XGBoost applied to pesticide run-off prediction would generate biome-specific importance maps — directly analogous to my health geography work where spatial heterogeneity in accessibility drivers is decomposed with geographically weighted regression. The biomonitoring samples provide a small but geographically stratified ground-truth set, a setting where spatially-informed cross-validation (blocking by biome) is essential to avoid overfitting.

④ Quantify healthcare access deficits in pesticide-associated cancer hotspots

Extending this paperThe paper identifies 436 cancer hotspots concentrated in rural areas under intense anthropogenic pressure and notes that "limited healthcare" compounds exposure risks in these zones — but never quantifies the healthcare access gap. A direct extension: overlay the paper's district-level Bayesian posterior RR surface with spatial accessibility scores for oncology facilities (chemotherapy, radiotherapy, pathology) computed with a 2SFCA or E2SFCA model. This would produce an "double burden" index — districts with both high pesticide-cancer risk and low healthcare access — enabling prioritization of facility investment and mobile health outreach with explicit equity weighting.

⑤ Integrate soil-micronutrient spatial data to decouple pesticide risk from co-exposures

Extending this paperThe paper's INLA model controls for spatial structure and population but acknowledges residual confounding from "environmental, socio-economic or lifestyle factors." One under-examined confounder is soil geochemistry: the Andes are geologically active, and heavy-metal or selenium concentrations vary dramatically across the same districts flagged as pesticide hotspots. Extending the paper: add a soil micronutrient layer (selenium, arsenic, cadmium) derived from national or global soil databases as an additional fixed covariate in the INLA model, then compare ΔDIC/ΔWAIC against the deforestation-covariate model already implemented — providing a cleaner isolation of the pesticide signal.

Added value to my researchThis extension requires exactly the multi-source data fusion methodology at the core of my work: harmonizing remote-sensing-derived soil data (at ~1 km resolution) with the 100 m pesticide risk surface through disaggregation and spatial interpolation. My physics-informed graph approach could encode geochemical transport (runoff, leaching) as structural constraints on the fusion, ensuring that imputed micronutrient surfaces respect watershed boundaries — the same topographic logic the paper uses for pesticide run-off routing. The result is a richer exposomic covariate space that directly serves the paper's own call for "multi-tissue analyses and longitudinal molecular profiling."
