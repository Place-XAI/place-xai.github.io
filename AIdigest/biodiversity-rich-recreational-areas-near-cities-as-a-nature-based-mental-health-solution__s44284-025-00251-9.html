Biodiversity-rich recreational areas near cities as a nature-based mental health solution

Cong Xia · Shougeng Hu · Zongnan Hu · Yanyun Yan · L. Roman Carrasco Published 2025-06-04 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00251-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 9,034 cities worldwide, more than 96% have a biodiversity-rich recreational area (recreation-permitted protected areas, Key Biodiversity Areas or intact forest landscapes) reachable within two hours, but affordability and visiting rates are highest in Europe, Oceania and North America. The extra mental-health benefit of these high-biodiversity nature experiences (beyond managed urban green space) avoids an estimated 137,299 DALYs (90% uncertainty range 10,368–598,509) of depression and anxiety, and is a cost-effective public-health intervention in developed settings. Establishing new highly biodiverse, recreation-permitted protected areas near cities to lower travel costs could jointly benefit biodiversity and urban mental health.

Core Methods

  • Travel-time accessibility: compute least-cost travel time from every city's pixels to the nearest PA, KBA and IFL using a global friction surface, then summarize per-city access (min/mean/max within a two-hour threshold).
  • Affordability & visiting rates: convert travel time into monetary travel cost (car purchase, fuel, train fares) and normalize by minimum wage / income to derive affordability and estimated visiting rates per capita.
  • Extra mental-health benefit: attribute an additional reduction in depression/anxiety to high-biodiversity experiences beyond managed urban green space, applying effect sizes to GBD 2019 burden.
  • Monte Carlo uncertainty: propagate uncertainty in effect sizes and visiting rates via Monte Carlo simulation (R 4.3.3) to produce DALY estimates with 90% uncertainty ranges.
  • Cost-effectiveness analysis: compare intervention cost per DALY averted against regional thresholds to flag where the intervention is cost-effective (Europe, North & South America).
  • Scenario / policy framing: evaluate lowering travel cost by siting new near-city recreation-permitted protected areas.

Expanded Ideas — this paper's extensibility & added value to my research

Each idea stays anchored to this paper: first how the paper itself could be extended (drawing on our corpus), then the added value for my research lines (migis.net — GeoAI, 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility, explainable & physics-informed graph learning, facility-location optimization).

① Turn one-way travel-time access into a competition-aware 2SFCA of biodiverse nature

Extending this paperThe paper's accessibility metric is supply-side and one-directional: it measures how long it takes a city to reach the nearest PA/KBA/IFL, but ignores that many cities compete for the same finite, congestible biodiverse sites. Recasting its friction-surface travel times inside a two-step floating catchment (2SFCA/R2SFCA) framework would let supply (site capacity / carrying capacity) and demand (urban population) interact, exposing crowded, under-supplied catchments that a nearest-site metric hides.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my R2SFCA service-area delineation to a new supply type — biodiverse recreation rather than clinics — and shows the method transferring from healthcare facilities to ecosystem services, strengthening the case for a unified accessibility engine.

② Replace borrowed effect sizes with an explainable model of the dose–response

Extending this paperThe DALY estimate rests on transferred effect sizes for the "extra" benefit of high biodiversity, with huge uncertainty (10k–600k DALYs). Extending it: assemble field-experiment and meta-analytic evidence into an explainable ML dose–response surface — benefit as a function of biodiversity level, exposure duration, distance and socio-environmental context — so the Monte Carlo draws from a data-driven distribution rather than a single pooled estimate.

Added value to my researchThis feeds my explainable machine learning for spatial prediction line: SHAP/feature-attribution over the dose–response surface tells planners which biodiversity and access features actually buy mental-health gains, turning a black-box benefit into an interpretable policy signal.

Cross-ref (corpus): Acute mental health benefits of urban nature — 10.1038/s44284-025-00286-y

③ Make the proximity-vs-affordability gap a Global-South equity diagnostic

Extending this paperThe paper's striking result — near-universal physical proximity but affordability concentrated in Europe, Oceania and North America — is reported but not decomposed. Extending it: build a city-level spatial-entropy / inequality index separating the proximity component from the cost-barrier component, and test whether Global-South cities are proximity-rich but access-poor, which would reframe the policy target from "build closer" to "lower the travel-cost barrier."

Added value to my researchThis connects to my work on digital divides and accessibility equity: the same decomposition (availability vs. effective use) I apply to telehealth maps cleanly onto nature access, and spatial entropy is already in my methods toolkit.

④ Site new near-city protected areas as an efficiency–equity facility-location problem

Extending this paperThe paper's headline policy — establish new highly biodiverse recreation-permitted PAs near cities — is stated qualitatively. Extending it: formalize it as a multi-objective facility-location optimization that maximizes DALYs averted (efficiency) and minimizes the access-cost gap across income groups (equity), subject to land-availability and biodiversity-value constraints, yielding an actual ranked list of candidate sites.

⑤ Couple biodiverse-nature access with the 15-minute / urban-green network via physics-informed graph learning

Extending this paperThe paper isolates biodiversity-rich, two-hour-away nature, while everyday urban green sits in a separate literature.
