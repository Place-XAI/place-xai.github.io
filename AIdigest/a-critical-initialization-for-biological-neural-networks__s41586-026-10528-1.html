A critical initialization for biological neural networks

Marius Pachitariu · Lin Zhong · Alexa Gracias · Amanda Minisi · Crystall Lopez · Carsen Stringer Published 2026-05-20 DOI 10.1038/s41586-026-10528-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Spontaneous, brainwide neural activity in mice follows a power-law eigenvalue spectrum consistent with a critically normalized, symmetric random connectivity matrix — not chaos or arbitrary structure. This "critical initialization" produces high-dimensional global activity modes that outperform non-symmetric and echo-state alternatives on zero-shot working memory tasks, suggesting the mouse brain may be randomly yet optimally initialized for memory-guided computation.

Why It Matters

  • Bridges two worlds with a single principle. The paper explains a long-standing puzzle in neuroscience — why spontaneous neural activity spans thousands of dimensions — using elementary random matrix theory, making the principle testable and transferable.
  • Identifies an exception that proves the rule. Hippocampal CA1 activity violates the symmetric-critical model (power-law exponent ~0.45 vs ~0.75–0.85 for cortex), pointing to a functionally distinct code optimized for information storage rather than temporal dynamics.
  • Provides the first mechanistic account of brainwide timescales. The paper shows that macroscopic long-timescale activity (persisting seconds) can emerge from neurons with fast biophysical timescales (20 ms) purely through critical normalization — resolving a key gap in circuit theory.
  • Implications for AI initialization. Symmetric, critically normalized weight matrices outperform standard non-symmetric initializations on zero-shot working memory — motivating a biologically inspired approach to transformer and state-space model initialization.

Core Methods

  • Stochastic Ornstein–Uhlenbeck dynamics: model neural population activity as a high-dimensional linear stochastic process driven by independent noise, with connectivity matrix A stabilized by subtracting the global mean (implementing inhibitory feedback).
  • Random matrix theory (semicircle law): exploit the Wigner semicircle law for symmetric random matrices to derive analytically that critically normalized symmetric connectivity produces a 2/3 power-law decay of covariance eigenvalues; validated for non-symmetric case (~1.25 exponent) via simulation.
  • SVCA2 (new eigenspectrum estimator): split neurons into two spatial halves (checkerboard grid) and estimate shared variance components; shown to be less biased than direct SVD or the prior SVCA estimator in realistic Poisson + GCaMP noise simulations.
  • Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) at time lag 0.23 s: fit a linear predictor in 1,000-dimensional PCA space using ridge regression; compare eigenvalue imaginary parts between real recordings and simulations to quantify rotational vs. relaxation dynamics.
  • Critical normalization: scale A so its largest eigenvalue is 0.998 (spectral radius ≈ 1); show that this is both necessary and sufficient for macroscopic global activity modes with long timescales.
  • Simulations of structured connectivity: test sparse (Bernoulli with probability ≥ 0.4%), clustered and spatially localized (exponential distance kernel, 250 µm length scale, 8,000 × 8,000 µm torus) symmetric matrices; show power-law exponents persist as long as global connections exist at ≥ 1% of local rate.
  • Zero-shot working memory benchmark: evaluate symmetric vs. non-symmetric linear dynamics and echo-state networks on 1,000-input zero-shot recall; decoder trained via ridge regression; classification based on nearest-neighbor in 100-dimensional input feature space.

① Replace the paper's fixed random reservoir with a biologically pre-trained foundation

Extending this paperThe paper treats the connectivity matrix A as randomly initialized and fixed throughout — a principled but static scaffold. A direct extension is to ask: can the critical symmetric initialization serve as a better prior for pre-training a neural-activity foundation model? Instead of learning representations from scratch with arbitrary initialization, a critically normalized symmetric weight matrix could be used as the starting point before unsupervised pre-training on multi-area recordings. The paper's own zero-shot working memory result (Fig. 5e,f) shows that inputs represented in the spontaneous activity subspace improve recall — precisely the kind of structured prior a foundation model would exploit.

② Exploit the paper's CA1 outlier to design a dual-mode spatial coding architecture

Extending this paperThe most intriguing finding is that CA1 breaks the model: its power-law exponent (~0.45) is far below the symmetric-critical prediction (~0.69), and is not changed by single-neuron shuffling, suggesting a fundamentally different code — likely an efficient, uncorrelated representation optimized for information capacity rather than temporal persistence. The paper leaves this as an open question. An immediate extension: train a comparative model where one module is symmetric-critical (cortex-like, persistent) and a second is near-diagonal covariance (CA1-like, efficient). Such a dual-head architecture could be tested on tasks requiring both rapid pattern separation and sustained maintenance — the hippocampal–cortical collaboration — directly extending Fig. 5's benchmark.

④ Map the paper's criticality condition onto spatial network percolation thresholds

Extending this paperThe paper shows (Fig. 4) that global activity modes persist as long as at least 0.4% of a 10,000-unit network's connections are global — a connectivity percolation threshold. This is not phrased as percolation by the authors, but the underlying mathematics (sparse symmetric random matrices, semicircle law) maps directly onto classical random-graph theory. A formal extension: derive the percolation threshold as a function of network size N and global connection probability p, connect it to the spectral gap of the graph Laplacian, and test whether the 2/3 power-law survives near the threshold in exactly the way random-graph criticality predicts.

⑤ Use the paper's aperiodic spectral signature as a biomarker for cognitive health geography

Extending this paperThe paper establishes that a 2/3 power-law in the neural eigenspectrum signals a healthy, critically initialized cortical state — deviations (like CA1's 0.45 exponent) indicate a qualitatively different functional regime. The paper explicitly notes that behavioral states (running vs. not running) shift the exponent slightly but significantly. An extension: track the power-law exponent longitudinally across learning, aging or disease, using the paper's SVCA2 estimator on publicly available EEG/LFP datasets where ground-truth cognitive status is known. This would test the paper's implicit hypothesis that exponent shifts are biomarkers of network-state transitions.
