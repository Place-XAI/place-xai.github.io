Core Conclusion

Physical neural networks (PNNs) — analogue systems performing neural computations in the physical domain — represent a credible pathway to AI models that are orders of magnitude larger and more energy-efficient than digital counterparts, and to private edge-device inference. Both backpropagation-based and backpropagation-free training strategies are now being actively developed for PNNs, but no approach yet matches digital backpropagation at scale. A diverse and rapidly evolving ecosystem of training techniques — spanning equilibrium propagation, forward-mode training, local learning rules, and in situ gradient extraction — is converging on solutions that could eventually make large-scale, efficient analogue AI practical.

  • Connects to edge AI and data privacy. PNNs performing inference locally without digitizing intermediate states offer a fundamentally different privacy model for sensitive on-device applications — a policy-relevant implication with direct relevance to healthcare, autonomous systems, and consumer IoT.

Core Methods

  • Two-branch training taxonomy: organizes all PNN training approaches into backpropagation-based methods (in situ adjoint, homodyne gradient extraction, surrogate gradients, direct/random feedback alignment) versus backpropagation-free methods (equilibrium propagation, contrastive Hebbian learning, forward-forward algorithm, node/weight perturbation, SPSA, RL-based, decentralized coupled-learning), enabling structured comparison across hardware requirements and scalability.
  • Hardware-physics constraint analysis: frames each training method through substrate-specific constraints — non-reciprocity limits for optical in situ backpropagation, noise floors for perturbation methods, measurement overhead for equilibrium-state readout, locality requirements for coupled-learning — connecting algorithm design directly to physical realizability.
  • Equilibrium propagation (EP): an energy-based backpropagation-free method where the network settles to a free-phase equilibrium; an external nudge toward the target produces a clamped-phase equilibrium; the gradient is estimated from the difference — hardware-native because no backward signal needs to be routed through the physical medium.
  • Perturbation-based gradient estimation: SPSA and node/weight perturbation methods estimate gradients from pairs of scalar loss measurements, avoiding any structured backward-signal routing through the physical substrate at the cost of higher sample complexity.
  • Scalability assessment and roadmap: evaluates each approach against the question of whether it can scale to "analogue large models" (Fig. 3) — systems with parameter counts comparable to current foundation models — identifying scalable gradient propagation as the central obstacle and projecting pathways toward it.

① Replace in situ gradient loops with fully analogue reinforcement learning for hardware-native PNN training

Extending this paperThe review identifies in situ backpropagation as the most gradient-faithful PNN training method but notes it requires reciprocal physical substrates and precise bidirectional signal routing that is difficult to implement across substrates. A direct extension: replace the gradient-signal loop with a fully analogue RL policy — as demonstrated for memristor networks — that adjusts physical parameters (phase shifters, memristive weights, spin orientations) using only scalar reward signals measured at the output. This sidesteps the reciprocity and signal-routing constraints while keeping the weight update inside the physical substrate, directly addressing the paper's central bottleneck of hardware-compatible gradient propagation. The paper discusses RL briefly (Refs. 38–39) but does not position it as the scalable alternative to in situ backpropagation it may be.

Added value to my researchMy explainable ML work for spatial prediction relies on gradient-based attribution (SHAP, LIME), which assumes differentiable digital pipelines. If PNN-style analogue computing is eventually applied to spatial models, RL-based attribution — where sensitivity is estimated from reward differences under input perturbation — would become the necessary framework. This motivates developing reward-signal-compatible spatial explanation tools that do not require gradient access, extending my explainable GeoAI toolkit toward hardware-agnostic attribution methods applicable to both digital and analogue spatial inference pipelines.

② Benchmark equilibrium propagation and reservoir computing jointly on structured spatiotemporal tasks

Extending this paperThe paper taxonomizes equilibrium propagation (EP) and reservoir computing as separate backpropagation-free training families with distinct trade-offs, but never compares them empirically on a common task. A direct extension: design a benchmark suite of structured temporal tasks with spatial correlation — which neither MNIST nor CIFAR captures — and measure gradient quality, convergence rate, and noise robustness for EP versus reservoir training across photonic and memristive substrates. This would transform the paper's qualitative taxonomy (Fig. 2) into a data-driven selection guide for practitioners who must choose training methods based on their physical substrate's dynamics. The paper's own call for comparative studies (implicit in the trade-off discussion) would be answered.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and mobile-phone trajectory analysis involve high-volume, time-sequenced data streams with strong spatial autocorrelation — exactly the task structure missing from current PNN benchmarks. Understanding which training method best handles spatially correlated temporal inputs directly informs whether future GeoAI inference pipelines (real-time urban mobility monitoring, disease spread estimation) can be offloaded to analogue neuromorphic accelerators, reducing cloud dependency for privacy-sensitive location data.

③ Couple on-chip photonic backpropagation with physics-informed graph architectures for spatial inference

Extending this paperThe review positions in situ optical backpropagation (citing Pai et al. Science 2023) as promising but limited to small photonic MLP architectures. An on-chip photonic neural network with full backpropagation training now exists (cross-ref). A direct extension of the paper: apply on-chip photonic backpropagation to graph-structured PNN architectures — where the mesh connectivity encodes a spatial or relational graph — and test whether the physical wave-propagation dynamics naturally implement approximate message passing, potentially enabling backpropagation-trained photonic GNNs without a separate digital graph module. The paper's hardware taxonomy (Fig. 1) describes mesh-based photonic networks but does not connect them to graph learning.

If PI-GNN inference can be offloaded to an on-chip photonic network trained with backpropagation, this opens a pathway to real-time, energy-efficient spatial accessibility computation at edge nodes (e.g., city-level IoT infrastructure), directly enabling my GeoAI models to run locally in deployed urban systems without cloud latency.

④ Exploit forward-only PNN training for privacy-preserving geospatial inference on edge devices

Extending this paperThe review highlights forward-mode and forward-forward training as backpropagation-free strategies particularly suited to physical systems where signal reversal is impractical. The "fully forward mode" for optical NNs (cross-ref) demonstrates competitive task performance with no backward pass. An extension: analyze whether forward-only PNN training can maintain accuracy when the input distribution is spatially structured and non-i.i.d. — as in real-world geospatial datasets — since all existing evaluations use i.i.d. image benchmarks. This would expose a critical gap: the paper's forward-mode methods are validated exclusively on tasks where spatial autocorrelation is absent, which is the opposite of the regime where they would be most useful for location-sensitive applications.

Added value to my researchPrivacy is a central concern in health geography and telehealth: patient location data, mental-health utilization records, and GPS trajectories cannot be transmitted to cloud servers without de-identification risk. Forward-only PNN inference — where raw analogue inputs never leave the edge device and no backpropagation signal encodes sensitive spatial patterns — offers a hardware-level privacy guarantee complementary to differential privacy algorithms. This directly motivates my interest in analogue edge hardware for 2SFCA-style accessibility calculations on private location data, and positions forward-only PNNs as the privacy-preserving layer beneath a GeoAI accessibility stack.
