Machine learning interatomic potentials at the centennial crossroads of quantum mechanics

Bhupalee Kalita · Hatice Gokcan · Olexandr Isayev Published 2025-12-22 DOI 10.1038/s43588-025-00930-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Machine learning interatomic potentials (MLIPs) have matured into transformative tools bridging quantum mechanical accuracy with classical computational efficiency, but face four persistent tensions: achieving chemical accuracy (<1 kcal mol−1), scaling to large systems, enabling physical interpretability, and reaching universal generalizability. The field is approaching a "GPT-4 moment" for chemistry — exemplified by the OMol25 dataset (100 million configurations, 6 billion CPU core hours) and pretrained foundation models such as UMA and MACE — yet fundamental challenges in rare-event sampling, open-shell systems, and catastrophic forgetting remain unsolved.

Why It Matters

  • Marks a centennial inflection point. As quantum mechanics turns 100 in 2025, MLIPs are positioned as the unifying technology enabling sub-millisecond molecular dynamics at near-ab-initio accuracy — orders of magnitude faster than DFT or coupled-cluster methods.
  • Frames four universal challenges for the field. Chemical accuracy, computational efficiency, interpretability, and universality are presented as a coherent research agenda, giving the community a shared benchmark for progress.
  • Catalogs the architectural revolution from descriptors to equivariant GNNs. The review traces the evolution from hand-crafted symmetry functions (Behler–Parrinello, 2007) through message-passing networks (SchNet, NequIP) to modern equivariant many-body architectures (MACE, UMA), making the progression legible to practitioners.
  • Highlights the big-data turning point. OMol25 (100M+ configurations from Meta AI and Berkeley Lab) signals that computational resource availability — not methodological barriers — is becoming the primary limit, changing how the community thinks about training data.
  • Drug discovery and materials science at stake. Production-ready MLIPs enable high-throughput computer-aided drug design, protein–ligand binding affinity prediction, and conformational search at DFT accuracy but force-field speed.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

OMol25
Open Molecules 2025 (Meta AI & Berkeley Lab, 2025) — the largest molecular simulation dataset to date: over 100 million 3D configurations (small organic/inorganic molecules, biomolecules, transition-metal complexes) with energies and forces at hybrid-DFT level. Generated using 6 billion CPU core hours (equivalent to running 1,000 typical computers for 50+ years). Builds on ANI-2x, SPICE2, and Transition-1x datasets; used to train the UMA and MACE-OMOL foundation models.
GMTKN55
General Main Group Thermochemistry, Kinetics and Non-Covalent Interactions benchmark — 55 subsets, used throughout as the primary accuracy benchmark for evaluating MLIPs against hybrid DFT (ωB97X-D3). Results shown for a 14-element closed-shell subset covered by AIMNet2.
ANI datasets
ANI-1 (20 million DFT calculations for organic molecules); ANI-2x (extended to F, Cl, S; subset used in OMol25 construction); ANI-1ccx (transfer learning to coupled-cluster). 20M DFT calculations is the only explicitly stated size in this paper.
AIMNet2
Trained on 20 million hybrid-DFT calculations; covers organic and elemental-organic molecules with up to 14 different elements; 2 million trainable parameters. Benchmark protein–ligand systems: tyk2, cdk2, p38 (complex avg. 874 atoms, protein avg. 829 atoms, ligand avg. 45 atoms). Demonstrated scalability to 100,000 atoms in sub-second gradient calculations.
SPICE2 / Transition-1x
Component datasets of OMol25. SPICE2 also used for FeNNix-Bio1. Exact sizes not restated in this Perspective (SPICE2 covers biologically relevant molecules; Transition-1x covers reaction pathways). Exact volumes not fabricated here.
FeNNix-Bio1
Equivariant foundation model trained on augmented SPICE2, ANI-2x conformations, and biologically relevant ions. Demonstrated scaling to ~1 million atoms (fully solvated tobacco mosaic virus simulation).

Core Methods

  • Architectural taxonomy of MLIPs: systematic comparison of five architecture families — (1) hand-crafted invariant descriptors (Behler–Parrinello, ANI); (2) message-passing invariant networks (SchNet, DimeNet, HIP-NN); (3) E(3)-equivariant networks (NequIP, MACE, PaiNN, eSEN, UMA); (4) attention-based potentials (So3krates, EScAIP); (5) physics-informed hybrid models (AIMNet2, OrbNet-Equi, SO3LR, OrbitAll).
  • Benchmark evaluation on GMTKN55: accuracy comparison of MACE-OMOL, UMA-S, eSEN-sm, and AIMNet2 against ωB97X-D3 hybrid DFT on a 14-element closed-shell subset, charting the accuracy–efficiency frontier.
  • Computational scaling analysis: wall-clock timing of invariant vs. equivariant MLIPs on protein–ligand benchmarks on NVIDIA A100; memory scaling characterized up to ~100,000 atoms; parallel efficiency analysis for GNN message-passing.
  • Multi-fidelity and transfer learning strategies: delta-learning (AIQM), multi-fidelity data mixing (Messerly et al.), and meta-learning approaches to overcome scarcity of high-accuracy CCSD(T) training data.
  • Active learning with uncertainty quantification: ensemble methods and Bayesian uncertainty estimates for epistemic/aleatoric error, guiding strategic addition of QM calculations in undersampled configuration-space regions.
  • Open-shell and spin-resolved modeling: evaluation of MACE and ANI-2x for homolytic bond-dissociation curves; AIMNet2-NSE neural spin–charge equilibration for singlet/triplet potential energy surfaces.
  • Physics-informed representation learning: orbital-based GNNs (OrbNet, OrbitAll), stereoelectronics-infused molecular graphs, explicit long-range Coulomb/dispersion corrections (AIMNet2, SO3LR), and symbolic regression for interpretability.

① Adapt the MLIP active-learning pipeline as a physics-informed spatial sampling strategy

Extending this paperThe paper's active-learning loop — estimate epistemic uncertainty via ensemble disagreement, identify the most uncertain configuration-space regions, strategically request new QM calculations there — is framed as a solution to rare-event undersampling in chemical space. A direct extension would test whether this uncertainty-driven dynamics framework generalizes to any high-dimensional prediction problem by formally abstracting the "configuration space" away from atoms and benchmarking the sampling efficiency gain against passive sampling baselines across multiple scientific domains. The paper stops short of this generalization; making it explicit would substantially broaden its impact.

Added value to my researchThe MLIP active-learning paradigm maps directly onto active spatial sampling in GeoAI. In my explainable ML workflows for spatial prediction of health outcomes, training data are chronically sparse in peri-urban or rural areas — the "rare events" of geographic space. Adopting an ensemble-disagreement active-learning loop analogous to the uncertainty-driven MLIP dynamics reviewed here would allow my spatiotemporal models to adaptively request ground-truth labels from exactly the geographic regions where prediction is most uncertain, improving MGWR/GWR transferability across heterogeneous spatial contexts without proportionally increasing data-collection costs.

② Encode conservation constraints into spatial-network GNNs following MLIP equivariant design principles

Extending this paperA central contribution of the paper is showing that injecting physical priors — energy conservation, rotational equivariance, translational invariance — into MLIP architectures simultaneously improves accuracy, interpretability, and data efficiency. The paper's E(3)-equivariant networks (NequIP, MACE) achieve this through architectural constraints rather than penalty terms. A productive extension would formalize a mapping between atomic symmetries in MLIPs and conservation laws in other graph-structured prediction problems, testing whether the same design principles transfer and whether they yield analogous data-efficiency gains outside chemistry.

Added value to my researchMy physics-informed graph learning agenda seeks exactly such transferable priors. Applied to job-housing flow networks or healthcare utilization networks, this would yield a GNN whose predictions satisfy population accounting identities by construction — the spatial analogue of energy-conserving MLIPs — reducing the data volume needed to achieve geographically transferable predictions.

③ Apply MLIP delta-learning (multi-fidelity training) to heterogeneous geospatial data fusion

Extending this paperThe paper's multi-fidelity training strategy — train on abundant low-accuracy (DFT) data and learn corrections toward scarce high-accuracy (CCSD(T)) targets via delta-learning — addresses a universal data-economics problem. AIQM and ANI-1ccx exemplify this approach. An extension would investigate whether the favorable data ratios achieved in chemistry (thousands of high-fidelity examples sufficient when pre-training on millions of DFT examples) are predictable from domain-independent properties of the fidelity gap, establishing a transferable recipe for multi-fidelity training across scientific disciplines.

Added value to my researchMy multi-source data fusion work (mobile phone records, social media, remote sensing, POIs) is structurally identical to the MLIP multi-fidelity problem: census data are the "gold standard" (high-fidelity, sparse, expensive), while mobile-phone mobility proxies are "DFT-level" (low-fidelity, abundant, cheap). Adopting the MLIP delta-learning approach — pre-train on mobile-phone-derived population estimates, then fine-tune on a small census ground-truth set — would give my geospatial population models a principled training recipe with explicit uncertainty quantification on the correction layer, analogous to multi-fidelity MLIPs.

④ Use MLIP continual-learning strategies (elastic weight consolidation) to adapt geospatial foundation models without catastrophic forgetting

Extending this paperThe paper identifies catastrophic forgetting as the chief obstacle to universal foundation MLIPs: fine-tuning a general potential for catalysis may inadvertently degrade performance on biological systems. Elastic weight consolidation (EWC) is highlighted as a technique that selectively protects important network parameters during new training. A direct extension of the paper would empirically measure the "chemical forgetting curve" — how per-domain performance degrades as a function of fine-tuning data volume — across MACE, UMA, and AIMNet2, revealing which architectural choices best resist forgetting and guiding foundation-model design principles.

Added value to my researchGeospatial foundation models (Prithvi, SatCLIP, UrbanCLIP) face the same catastrophic-forgetting challenge: a model pre-trained on global remote sensing may degrade on local urban health outcomes when fine-tuned with small city-level datasets. The MLIP literature's toolkit — EWC, continual learning, modular fine-tuning — is directly applicable. Importing MLIP-validated fine-tuning recipes into my geospatial foundation-model work would enable domain adaptation to telehealth access prediction, primary-care desert mapping, or 15-minute city analysis without erasing global spatial priors, with a principled alternative to ad-hoc transfer learning.

⑤ Transfer MLIP interpretability methods — symbolic regression and attention visualization — to explainable GeoAI

Extending this paperThe paper's outlook calls for physics-aware interpretability via symbolic regression (extracting human-readable equations from NN weights), attention visualization (revealing which molecular features drive predictions), and causal inference. A concrete extension would apply symbolic regression (e.g., PySR) to the latent representations of MACE or AIMNet2 and test whether the extracted equations recover known chemical scaling laws — validating that the network has learned chemistry rather than curve-fitting — and quantifying the correlation between symbolic-regression fidelity and model generalizability across held-out chemical families.
