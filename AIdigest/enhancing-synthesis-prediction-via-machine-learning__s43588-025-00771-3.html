Enhancing synthesis prediction via machine learning

J. C. Schön Published 2025-02-11 DOI 10.1038/s43588-025-00771-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Identifying promising synthesis targets and designing synthesis routes is a grand challenge in chemistry and materials science. Machine learning, combined with traditional approaches such as energy-landscape exploration, is opening powerful new avenues to tackle this problem: neural-network interatomic potentials can efficiently navigate high-dimensional configuration spaces to discover stable crystal structures, and ML-driven active learning frameworks accelerate the identification of viable synthesis pathways. The work highlights how the hybrid ML + physics approach is transforming synthesis prediction from an expert-intuition exercise into a systematic, computationally guided workflow.

Core Methods

  • Energy-landscape exploration: systematic traversal of the high-dimensional configuration space of a chemical system to find stable and metastable crystal structures, pioneered by Schön & Jansen (1996) and extended by random/minima-hopping approaches (Pickard & Needs, 2011).
  • Neural network interatomic potentials (NNPs/MLIPs): ML models trained on DFT energy/force data that replace expensive first-principles calculations in the inner loop of structure search, enabling millions of energy evaluations at near-DFT accuracy.
  • Graph neural networks for universal potentials: M3GNet-style architectures (Chen & Ong, 2022/2023) train a single model covering the full periodic table, enabling transfer to new chemical systems with minimal re-training.
  • Deep-learning potentials for crystallization (Aykol et al.): NNPs coupled with molecular dynamics to simulate amorphous-to-crystal transitions, predicting which crystalline polymorphs emerge from amorphous precursors.
  • Active learning / closed-loop iteration: iterative sampling of new configurations, DFT labeling of uncertain structures, and re-training of the ML potential, progressively improving coverage of relevant configuration space.
  • Tempering and structure-building heuristics: simulated-tempering walk followed by crystalline building-block identification and periodic structure construction (the pipeline illustrated in Fig. 1).

① Transfer the active-learning loop from configuration space to spatial prediction

Extending this paperThe paper's key insight is that ML potentials improve iteratively by focusing new DFT labels on uncertain regions of configuration space — a closed-loop active learning protocol. The same architecture has a direct extension: rather than halting once a candidate crystal is found, couple the ML potential to a Bayesian uncertainty estimator that steers exploration toward synthesis-relevant thermodynamic conditions (temperature, pressure, composition), making the loop adaptive not just in geometry but in process variables.

Added value to my researchThe closed-loop active-learning paradigm maps directly onto spatiotemporal prediction tasks where labeled ground-truth data are expensive to collect (e.g., ground-truthing remote-sensing land cover or field-validating accessibility surveys). Adopting the uncertainty-driven sampling strategy from this paper would let me prioritize which spatial locations to sample next in an R2SFCA field validation or a geospatial foundation-model fine-tuning pipeline — directly reducing annotation cost while improving model coverage of underrepresented spatial regimes.

② Replace the per-system ML potential with a universal spatial foundation model

Extending this paperThe paper celebrates universal interatomic potentials (e.g., M3GNet by Chen & Ong) as a milestone — one model covering the entire periodic table instead of per-element re-training. The natural extension: scale the same universality ambition to synthesis prediction itself, learning a foundation model over the joint space of (chemical composition, structure, synthesis conditions) so that synthesis routes for any target material can be retrieved via few-shot prompting rather than re-running full energy-landscape searches. This would require integrating the MLIP backbone with reaction-route libraries and conditioning on processing variables.

Added value to my researchThe universality argument resonates with my work on fine-tuning geospatial foundation models: just as a universal MLIP trained on the periodic table transfers to new chemistries, a geospatial foundation model trained on diverse city morphologies should transfer to new urban environments with minimal labeled data. The lesson here is to encode domain physics (energy conservation for MLIPs; spatial autocorrelation and scale invariance for GeoAI) as architectural inductive biases rather than dataset-specific fine-tuning targets.

③ Encode synthesis feasibility constraints as physics-informed loss terms

Extending this paperThe paper frames synthesis prediction as energy-landscape navigation but notes that thermodynamic stability alone does not guarantee synthesizability — kinetics, precursor availability, and processing windows matter. A direct extension: augment the ML potential training objective with physics-informed regularizers encoding synthesis constraints (e.g., known phase boundaries, reaction enthalpies, metastability windows), so that the model's energy surface is calibrated not just for accuracy but for synthesis relevance. This brings the Aykol-style crystallization approach closer to closed-loop laboratory synthesis planning.

Added value to my researchThis mirrors a challenge in spatial prediction: physical constraints (e.g., road network topology, administrative boundaries, facility capacity caps) are rarely encoded in ML models but are crucial for policy-relevant predictions. Adapting the physics-informed loss idea, I can encode network-topology constraints in graph neural networks for accessibility modeling — ensuring predicted catchment areas respect the physical road graph rather than smoothed statistical surfaces, which is directly applicable to my physics-informed graph-learning agenda.

④ Apply the ML + traditional hybrid framework to multi-objective synthesis optimization

Extending this paperThe paper positions ML as an accelerator for traditional energy-landscape search but treats synthesis prediction as a single-objective problem (find a stable structure). A natural extension: recast the pipeline as a Pareto optimization over multiple synthesis objectives — thermodynamic stability, kinetic accessibility, precursor cost, environmental impact, and yield — using the ML potential as the surrogate and multi-objective evolutionary algorithms (e.g., NSGA-II) as the search driver. This would let chemists explicitly trade off objectives rather than accepting the energy minimum uncritically.

Added value to my researchMulti-objective Pareto framing is the natural language of my EV-charging efficiency-vs-equity optimization. The lesson here is to use ML surrogates (rather than expensive agent-based or simulation models) to build the Pareto front — drastically reducing the number of full simulation runs needed. Applying this to spatial facility-location optimization (accessibility vs. equity vs. cost) with an ML surrogate trained on city-scale simulation data is a direct translation of the synthesis-prediction hybrid framework into urban analytics.

⑤ Collective intelligence and human-in-the-loop synthesis prediction

Extending this paperThe paper notes that the space of possible synthesis targets is astronomically large and that ML must be combined with expert judgment to prioritize candidates. The frontier extension: adopt a collective intelligence architecture where distributed expert annotations (chemist rankings of candidate structures, synthesis feasibility scores) are continuously fed back to update the ML potential's uncertainty estimates and search priorities. This crowdsources the "is this synthesizable?" judgment that no single expert or model can answer reliably, while accumulating a growing human-validated training set over time.

Added value to my researchCollective intelligence frameworks are directly applicable to open science and multi-source data fusion in my research: incorporating community-contributed local knowledge (e.g., crowdsourced POI corrections in OpenStreetMap, citizen-reported healthcare access barriers) alongside remote-sensing and mobile-phone data. The synthesis-prediction lesson is that structured human feedback loops — not just passive open data — can be the highest-value ingredient in a hybrid ML pipeline, which motivates designing explicit feedback mechanisms into GeoAI accessibility tools.
