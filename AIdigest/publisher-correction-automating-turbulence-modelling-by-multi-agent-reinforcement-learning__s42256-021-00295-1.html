Automating turbulence modelling by multi-agent reinforcement learning

Guido Novati · Hugues Lascombes de Laroussilhe · Petros Koumoutsakos Published 2021-01-15 (Correction; original 2021-01-04) DOI 10.1038/s42256-021-00295-1 Original DOI 10.1038/s42256-020-00272-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Note: this is a Publisher Correction (Erratum) of the original research article. The digest below covers the substantive findings of the original paper. Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) can automate the discovery of turbulence closure models for large-eddy simulations (LES) of isotropic turbulence, outperforming classic physics-based models (Smagorinsky, dynamic Smagorinsky). The MARL-derived policy generalizes across Reynolds numbers and grid sizes unseen during training, overcoming the compounding-error problem that plagues supervised learning approaches to subgrid-scale (SGS) modelling.

★

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the supervised-learning bottleneck in scientific simulation. All prior data-driven turbulence models trained by supervised learning on filtered DNS data; MARL instead optimizes directly against simulation statistics, eliminating the a priori / a posteriori gap and accumulation of modelling errors.
  • Delivers genuine generalization. The learned policy trained on Reλ ∈ {65–163} remains stable and accurate up to Reλ = 205 and transfers across grid resolutions (323 → 1283) — a capability that purely supervised models systematically lack.
  • Opens a solver-agnostic path. Because the reward can be any measurable statistic (energy spectra, wall shear stress, or even experimental measurements), MARL does not require DNS capability or a differentiable solver — it can train on experimental targets.
  • Establishes RL as a discovery tool for PDEs. The framework is explicitly not restricted to Navier–Stokes; any non-linear conservation law with a closure problem can be attacked in the same way, broadening the scope far beyond turbulence.

Core Methods

  • Multi-agent RL (MARL) for subgrid-scale modelling: Nagents = 43 cooperative agents distributed across the LES grid; each observes 5 invariants of the velocity-gradient tensor and 6 of the Hessian (local, dims = 28) plus global energy-spectrum modes — acting to set the Smagorinsky coefficient Cs2(x, t) via linear interpolation.
  • Reward design: reward functions based on (1) minimizing Germano identity error (rG) and (2) maximizing log-likelihood of LES energy spectrum against DNS statistics (rLL); rLL policy performs best and remains stable across all tested Reλ.
  • V-RACER + ReF-ER algorithm: off-policy policy gradient with experience replay; ReF-ER adds a trust-region constraint (importance-weight ratio C = 1.5) and KL-penalty to stabilize multi-agent training where single-agent update rules are imprecise.
  • Policy network: 2 hidden layers, 64 units each, tanh activations, skip connections; outputs Gaussian policy mean μw and standard deviation σw, plus state-value estimate vw.
  • Baselines: standard Smagorinsky model (SSM, empirically tuned Cs) and dynamic Smagorinsky model (DSM, Germano identity) — both classical physics-derived approaches.
  • Evaluation metrics: log-likelihood of LES energy spectra vs. DNS statistics; velocity structure functions S2(r); turbulent kinetic energy and integral length scale across Reλ and grid sizes not seen in training.

① Transfer the MARL closure-discovery framework to spatiotemporal urban flow problems

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates MARL as an automated closure-model discoverer for Navier–Stokes LES, a problem structurally analogous to other high-dimensional spatiotemporal fields with unresolved scales. A direct extension: apply the same MARL + ReF-ER framework to urban microclimate and pollution dispersion models, where street-canyon flow dynamics at sub-grid scale are crudely parameterized just as SGS turbulence is. The cooperative-agent architecture (each agent sees local invariants + global spectrum) maps naturally onto a grid of urban blocks sensing local wind invariants and global pollution concentration.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal analysis work typically uses statistical or ML regression models that treat space as a feature rather than resolving it mechanistically. Adopting the MARL closure paradigm would let me build physics-constrained spatiotemporal prediction models for urban health outcomes (e.g., air-quality exposure) where fine-scale dynamics cannot be fully resolved — baking conservation-law priors into the closure rather than relying purely on data-fit.

② Replace the Smagorinsky parametric backbone with a graph neural network operating on the grid

Extending this paperThe MARL policy currently outputs the scalar Smagorinsky coefficient Cs2(x, t) — a single eddy-viscosity parameter. A natural extension: replace the Smagorinsky backbone with a message-passing graph neural network (GNN) operating on the LES grid graph, so the MARL policy directly predicts the full residual-stress tensor τR(x, t) at each node. This removes the isotropic eddy-viscosity assumption, lets the policy learn anisotropic closure terms, and generalizes to non-uniform or unstructured grids — extending the paper's own discussion of transferability limits to different numerical discretizations.

Added value to my researchThis is directly aligned with my physics-informed graph learning research line. The MARL-GNN architecture — where each graph node is an RL agent with both local and global state — provides a blueprint for my work on learning spatially varying processes (e.g., health-exposure fields) that obey conservation-like constraints. The ReF-ER stability mechanism also addresses instability issues I encounter when training GNNs on unbalanced spatial data.

③ Learn reward functions from sparse experimental observations rather than DNS

Extending this paperThe paper explicitly names learning from experimental data as an open direction: "energy spectra, wall shear stresses or drag coefficients may be measured from experiments and used to train SGS models for LES." Extending it: use inverse reward learning (IRL) to infer the reward function from sparse, noisy experimental turbulence measurements — turning the paper's MARL framework into a fully experiment-driven pipeline that does not require any DNS. This would be the first MARL closure model trained on real rather than synthetic data, and would be directly relevant to flows (ocean eddies, atmospheric boundary layers) where DNS is computationally impossible.

Added value to my researchIn my urban and health geography work, "ground truth" is always sparse and noisy (mobile-phone records, clinic visit counts, GPS trajectories). The IRL extension offers a principled approach to learning objective functions from observational data — analogous to learning what accessibility metric communities implicitly optimize from their revealed mobility patterns, a key question in my 2SFCA accessibility research.

④ Exploit the cooperative-agent architecture for multi-scale geospatial prediction

Extending this paperThe MARL architecture's key insight is that each agent combines local state (invariants at x(i)) and global state (full energy spectrum) — a two-scale information fusion that enables spatially adaptive control. Extending the paper: test whether the same local+global two-scale architecture (without the turbulence-specific invariants) generalizes to other multi-scale physical fields, such as atmospheric boundary-layer flows over heterogeneous terrain, as a demonstration of the architecture's domain-transferability.

⑤ Apply the ReF-ER stability mechanism to off-policy training of geospatial foundation models

Extending this paperThe ReF-ER algorithm — which combines experience replay with a KL trust-region constraint to stabilize off-policy updates in cooperative multi-agent settings — is presented as a general RL algorithm, not specific to turbulence. The paper's ablation (ReF-ER is necessary to stabilize MARL training) shows it fills a genuine gap. Extending the paper: benchmark ReF-ER against TRPO, PPO, and SAC in other continuous-state multi-agent environments (cooperative robotics, multi-agent traffic signal control) to establish whether ReF-ER's stability advantage persists across domains.

Added value to my researchFine-tuning geospatial foundation models (my fourth research theme) is itself an off-policy update problem: a pre-trained model is updated from a small, domain-specific dataset that is out-of-distribution relative to pre-training. ReF-ER's trust-region approach — constraining KL divergence from the behaviour policy — maps directly onto parameter-efficient fine-tuning with distributional stability guarantees, potentially replacing ad-hoc learning-rate schedules I currently use when fine-tuning remote-sensing foundation models on local urban data.
