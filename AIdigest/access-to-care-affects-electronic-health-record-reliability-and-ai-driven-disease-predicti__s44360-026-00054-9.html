Access to care affects electronic health record reliability and AI-driven disease prediction

Anna Zink · Hongzhou Luan · Irene Y. Chen Published 2026-02-03 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00054-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Healthcare access is not just a clinical inequity — it is a data-quality inequity. Analysing 205,186 All of Us participants, patients with cost-constrained or delayed care had worse electronic health record (EHR) reliability for 73% of examined conditions (measured against participant self-reports), driven partly by lower visit rates. In a type 2 diabetes prediction task, adding self-reported conditions significantly improved predictive performance and targeting precisely for the lowest-access patients. Closing the equity gap therefore requires fixing both data-collection bias and algorithmic limitations, not models alone.

★

Why It Matters

  • Names a hidden failure mode of clinical AI. EHRs are treated as ground truth, but this work shows the record itself is systematically thinner for low-access patients — the people prediction models are most often deployed to help.
  • Quantifies the bias at scale. Across a 205k-participant national cohort, EHR reliability is worse for 73% of conditions among cost-constrained/delayed-care patients, with a mechanism (fewer visits, longer time-to-first-visit) rather than a black-box correlation.
  • Offers a concrete, low-cost fix. Folding in participant self-reported conditions lifts diabetes prediction and improves identification of low-access patients who later develop disease — a cheap survey signal repairs an expensive data gap.
  • Reframes fairness work. It argues that algorithmic-fairness patches alone are insufficient when the upstream data-generating process is itself access-dependent — relevant to any disparity-aware modeling effort.

Core Methods

  • EHR reliability measurement: treat self-reported conditions as a reference and quantify how often the EHR captures each condition, computing reliability rates per condition and stratifying by access group (Fig. 2).
  • Access stratification: partition the cohort into standard-care vs. low-access (cost-constrained / delayed) groups from the Health Care Access & Utilization survey.
  • Mechanism analysis: use visit rates and cumulative incidence of time-to-first-visit (Fig. 3) to explain why low-access patients have thinner records.
  • Clinical prediction modeling: train a type 2 diabetes onset model with and without self-reported features; evaluate AUC and high-risk targeting separately per access group (Fig. 4, Extended Data Table 4).
  • Equity-aware evaluation: measure not only aggregate accuracy but who is correctly flagged — improvement in identifying low-access patients who later develop diabetes.

① Give the access label a spatial geometry

Extending this paperThe paper defines "low access" from a self-report survey (cost/delay) but never places it in space — yet cost and delay are partly produced by where a patient lives relative to care. Extending it: geocode the cohort and join EHR reliability to a spatial accessibility surface (travel time to primary care, provider supply), testing whether the 73%-of-conditions reliability gap is explained by geographic access on top of self-reported access. This converts a binary survey flag into a continuous, mechanistic exposure.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct bridge from my 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility work to a measurable downstream harm: I can show that low spatial access does not merely reduce care, it corrupts the record used to allocate future care — a powerful new outcome variable for service-area delineation.

② Move from correlation to a causal model of the EHR-generating process

Extending this paperThe paper attributes worse reliability "in part" to visit rates, but the access → visits → record → label chain is a causal graph waiting to be drawn. Extending it: formalize EHR completeness as a missing-not-at-random mechanism and use a causal/structural model to separate true prevalence differences from observation differences — so the diabetes model corrects for the selection rather than just adding features.

Added value to my researchThis sharpens my explainable-ML agenda: instead of attributing predictions to features post hoc, I can attribute them to a data-generating mechanism, aligning spatial prediction with causal selection structure rather than surface correlations.

Cross-ref (corpus): A causal perspective on dataset bias in machine learning for medical imaging — 10.1038/s42256-024-00797-8

③ Test whether fairness patches help once the data itself is access-biased

Extending this paperThe paper's central claim — fix data and algorithm — can be made rigorous by head-to-head benchmarking: compare its survey-augmentation fix against standard algorithmic-fairness/bias-mitigation methods on the same diabetes task and access strata, quantifying how much each closes the low-access AUC gap. That turns a qualitative argument into a measured ranking of remedies.

Added value to my researchFor my disparity-aware spatial models this is a reusable evaluation protocol: stratify by access/geography, then ask whether reweighting fixes the harm or merely the metric — a guardrail against "fair on paper" GeoAI.

Cross-ref (corpus): Algorithmic fairness and bias mitigation for clinical machine learning with deep reinforcement learning — 10.1038/s42256-023-00697-3

④ Treat low-access patients as a training-composition problem

Extending this paperBecause low-access patients carry noisier labels and sparser features, they behave like an under-represented, mislabeled stratum during training. Extending the paper: vary the composition and labeling of the low-access subgroup (resampling, label-correction with self-reports) and chart how generalization to that group responds — pinpointing whether the gain comes from features or from rebalanced supervision.

Added value to my researchThis maps onto my multi-source data-fusion work: when I fuse mobile-phone, social-media and remote-sensing signals, coverage is also access-skewed, so the same composition diagnostics tell me when added data helps the underserved versus the already-observed.

⑤ Connect access-driven data gaps to structural / historical inequity

Extending this paperThe paper documents that low-access patients are under-recorded but stops short of asking which populations and neighborhoods these are. Extending it: link the cohort's residence to structural indicators (historical redlining, income inequality, segregation) and test whether EHR-reliability deficits cluster in the same places long-burdened by disinvestment — making the data bias a measurable legacy of place, not just of individual choice.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my health-geography agenda: it lets me frame "data deserts" alongside care deserts, and use spatial entropy / MGWR to attribute record-reliability gaps to place-based structural factors for urban-health policy.
