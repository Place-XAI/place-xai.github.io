Co-benefits of transport demand reductions from compact urban development in Chinese cities

Xiangwen Fu · Jing Cheng · Liqun Peng · Mi Zhou · Dan Tong · Denise L. Mauzerall Published 2024-02-08 DOI 10.1038/s41893-024-01271-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Ambitious compact urban development (CUD) policy in China reduces on-road transport CO2 by 97 Mt (8%) and NOx by 95 kt (7%) under a low-AEV scenario, and avoids 25,000 premature deaths from ambient air pollution by 2050. Even with 100% electric vehicle penetration, CUD still avoids 5,800 premature deaths by cutting non-exhaust and upstream emissions — and saves up to 10% of AEV energy demand — demonstrating that demand-side urban compactness is a durable complement to supply-side electrification across all decarbonization pathways.

Why It Matters

  • First integrated, scenario-wide evaluation of CUD in China. The study jointly models climate, energy, air quality, and health — moving beyond single-metric analyses — under nine cross-cutting scenarios of CUD intensity, AEV penetration, and power decarbonization projected to 2050.
  • Quantifies co-benefits even at 100% EV penetration. The pivotal finding is that CUD remains valuable in a fully electrified future through non-exhaust particulate reduction and upstream emission savings, challenging the assumption that electrification alone is sufficient.
  • Reveals interprovincial heterogeneity with policy relevance. Sprawling provinces (Hainan, Liaoning, Ningxia) gain the most from CUD, while densely populated eastern provinces benefit most on health grounds — giving planners a spatially differentiated prioritization framework.
  • Monetizes multiple co-benefits. Economic valuation — carbon price (US$212.3/tCO2), VSL-based mortality savings, and avoided energy costs — makes CUD directly comparable in cost-benefit planning, with remaining economic value of US$6.6 billion even under full electrification.
  • Meta-analysis grounds the model in observed behavior. The elasticity synthesis across 28 empirical studies on Chinese cities (combined '5Ds' elasticity of −0.33) provides an empirically anchored, reproducible basis for the scenario projections.

Core Methods

  • Meta-analysis of '5Ds' elasticities: synthesizes 28 empirical studies on Chinese cities; weighted-average elasticity of private passenger VKT with respect to each dimension of CUD (density, diversity, design, destination accessibility, distance to transit). Combined '5Ds' elasticity = −0.33.
  • Scenario design (3 × 3 factorial): nine scenarios cross-cutting demand side (NoCUD / LowCUD / HighCUD) with supply side (LowAEV_BasePower / MedAEV_MedCPower / HighAEV_CleanPower), plus an AllBEV_CleanPower variant — all projected to 2050.
  • Integrated Assessment Model (DPEC / GCAM-China / MEIC): translates VKT reductions from CUD into provincial-level energy use and multi-species emissions (CO2, NOx, CO, VOCs, SO2, PM2.5, PM10), including non-exhaust particulate emissions from tire, brake, and road wear.
  • Regional atmospheric chemical transport (WRF-Chem): simulates annual-average PM2.5 and MDA8 O3 concentrations over China and adjacent regions at 27 km resolution, capturing nonlinear NOx–nitrate chemistry.
  • Epidemiological health impact assessment: GEMM model (Global Exposure Mortality Model) for PM2.5-attributed mortality in adults ≥25 yr; log-linear concentration–response function for O3-attributed mortality ≥30 yr. Monte Carlo uncertainty quantification (n=10,000 draws) yields 95% CI for premature death estimates.
  • Economic valuation: social cost of carbon (US$212.3/tCO2) for carbon mitigation; value of statistical life (VSL, income-adjusted to 2050 via GDP elasticity η=0.4) for health benefits; LCOE/LCOH avoided costs for energy savings.

① Disaggregate the provincial emission model to city and neighborhood scale using geospatial AI

Extending this paperThe DPEC model operates at the provincial level, a spatial resolution that conceals within-province heterogeneity — the paper itself notes that cities with low density benefit far more from CUD. A direct extension: disaggregate VKT reductions and resulting emissions to the city and sub-city grid using high-resolution road density maps, GPS trajectory data, and POI distributions, then re-run the health impact assessment at fine spatial scales. This would capture the exposure increase from higher density (a limitation the authors explicitly flag at 27 km resolution) and reveal which neighborhoods bear disproportionate health burdens.

② Build a spatially explicit, empirically calibrated VKT-reduction model using mobile phone and GPS data

Extending this paperThe paper's scenario engine is anchored on a meta-analysis elasticity of −0.33, aggregated across 28 studies with large inter-study variance ("two orders of magnitude" in reported elasticities). A compelling extension: replace or calibrate this scalar with a spatially varying elasticity surface estimated from mobile phone call detail records or GPS trajectory data, using MGWR/GWR to let CUD sensitivity vary across density and transit contexts. This would convert the paper's single national elasticity into a city-tailored, non-stationary predictor and reduce the dominant source of uncertainty in its projections.
