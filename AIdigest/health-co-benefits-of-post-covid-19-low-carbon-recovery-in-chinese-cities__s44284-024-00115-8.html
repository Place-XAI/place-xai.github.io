Health co-benefits of post-COVID-19 low-carbon recovery in Chinese cities

Chenxi Lu · Yingjian Huang · Ying Yu · Jiawei Hu · Huibin Mo · Yun Li · Da Huo · Xuanren Song · Xiaoting Huang · Yun Sun · Kai Liu · Shaohui Zhang · Karyn Morrissey · Jinpyo Hong · Zhu Deng · Zhuanjia Du · Felix Creutzig · Zhu Liu Published 2024-08-28 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00115-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Cities that achieved low-carbon recovery (GDP growth combined with falling CO2) after COVID-19 reaped large public-health dividends. Using a new near-real-time daily carbon dataset for 48 Chinese cities, the six low-carbon-recovery cities (mainly megacities) saved 1.2× as many lives per 100,000 and delivered 1.5× the monetized avoided premature deaths per 100,000 as the other 42 cities, totaling US$ 4.2 billion (95% CI 2.1–6.3) over the post-COVID period. Government spending on electric vehicles raised the likelihood that a city achieved low-carbon recovery — evidence of real synergy between local welfare and global climate goals.

★

Why It Matters

  • Quantifies a "win-win" most cities only assume. It puts a hard number on the idea that decoupling growth from carbon also saves lives — and shows the health benefit is concentrated in cities that actually decoupled, not spread evenly.
  • Brings climate accounting down to the city scale. Subnational green-recovery evidence has been scarce; this is one of the first city-level couplings of near-real-time emissions, PM2.5, mortality and economic statistics in China.
  • Identifies an actionable policy lever. EV-oriented government spending is singled out as a driver that tips a city into low-carbon recovery — a concrete instrument for planners, not a vague exhortation.
  • Monetizes the co-benefit. Translating avoided deaths into US$ 4.2 billion makes the health argument legible to finance and recovery-stimulus decision-makers.

Core Methods

  • Low-carbon-recovery classification: a city is "low-carbon recovery" if it shows GDP growth combined with reduced CO2 between the pre- and post-COVID-19 eras (Fig. 1), splitting the 48 cities into 6 vs. 42.
  • Meteorological normalization of PM2.5: weather effects are statistically removed so that observed air-quality change reflects emissions/activity rather than meteorology (Fig. 2).
  • Health-impact attribution: reduced PM2.5 is mapped to avoided premature deaths per 100,000 via an exposure–response (concentration–response) function (Fig. 3), then aggregated city by city.
  • Economic valuation: avoided deaths are monetized (value-of-statistical-life style) to give annual and accumulated monetary health co-benefits with 95% confidence intervals.
  • Driver decomposition: CO2 changes are decomposed into economic growth, industry-structure change and carbon intensity (CI); a model relates EV/NEV spending to the probability of low-carbon recovery (Fig. 4).
  • Uncertainty analysis: Monte-Carlo-style uncertainty on the average avoided premature deaths per city (n = 48).

① Push the city-mean co-benefit down to intra-city, equity-resolved exposure

Extending this paperThe paper attributes health co-benefits at the city aggregate using station-based PM2.5 and city all-cause mortality. But within a megacity, exposure and baseline mortality are highly unequal. A direct extension: fuse the authors' near-real-time emissions with 1 km gridded PM2.5 and disaggregated population so avoided deaths are mapped at neighborhood scale — testing whether low-carbon recovery's benefit is itself equitably distributed.

Added value to my researchThis is a natural marriage with my spatial accessibility / health geography work: the same gridded exposure surface can drive a 2SFCA overlay of pollution burden vs. primary-care access, revealing where high exposure coincides with low service supply.

② Replace the fixed concentration–response function with explainable ML attribution

Extending this paperThe health-impact step relies on a parametric exposure–response function and a city-classification rule. Extending it: train an explainable ML ensemble (gradient boosting + SHAP) on the 48-city panel to learn how emissions, structure, carbon intensity and EV spending jointly predict avoided deaths — surfacing nonlinearities and interactions the linear decomposition (Fig. 4) cannot.

③ Turn the EV-spending finding into a spatially explicit efficiency-vs-equity policy

Extending this paperThe paper shows EV spending raises the probability of low-carbon recovery, but treats it as a scalar city covariate. Extending it: couple NEV sales with charging-infrastructure siting and a grid carbon-intensity layer, so the co-benefit is conditioned on where EVs and chargers actually deploy — distinguishing genuine emissions reduction from grid-shifted emissions.

Added value to my researchThis plugs straight into my EV charging-station efficiency-vs-equity optimization: avoided premature deaths become a third objective alongside utilization efficiency and access equity, yielding a health-aware siting model.
