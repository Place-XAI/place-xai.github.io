Global mapping of city-level economic growth decoupling from fossil fuels

Amirhossein Hassani · Daniel Dean Moran · Matti Kummu · Sam-Erik Walker · Sina Masoumzadeh Sayyar · Kerstin Stebel · Philipp Schneider Published 2026-05-11 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00440-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Combining satellite tropospheric NO₂ (a proxy for fossil-fuel combustion) with admin-2 gridded GDP per capita (PPP), the authors build a globally consistent, repeatable way to track whether cities are decoupling growth from fossil fuels. Across 5,435 cities (2019–2024), 2,475 showed significant trends; about 80% are "green" (cleaner + richer) — concentrated in China, Europe and North America — while ~16% are "brown" (dirtier + richer), mainly in India and the Middle East. The result is relative, not absolute, decoupling: an early-signal, satellite-based screening tool for monitoring urban green-growth and policy effectiveness.

Why It Matters

  • Solves a measurement bottleneck. City-level emission inventories are rare, inconsistent and slow; gridded products (e.g., EDGAR) are weakly constrained on intra-national spatial change. Using space-observed NO₂ sidesteps self-reported inventories and gives an objective, globally comparable signal of combustion-related activity.
  • Cross-regional comparability at scale. One consistent pipeline ranks 5,435 cities >100,000 residents on the same yardstick, enabling apples-to-apples benchmarking that national averages erase.
  • Turns "green growth" into a screening typology. The four-quadrant scheme (green / brown / gray / red = cleaner-richer / dirtier-richer / cleaner-poorer / dirtier-poorer) is an actionable benchmarking tool for SDG and Paris-Agreement progress.
  • Repeatable and near-real-time. As more TROPOMI data accrue, the method can be re-run regularly to monitor whether policy interventions actually bend a city's trajectory.
  • Honest about scope. NO₂ is explicitly framed as a fossil-fuel-activity proxy, not an air-quality or CO₂ measure, and the result is relative decoupling — a calibrated, validated early-warning indicator rather than a total-emissions ledger.

Core Methods

  • Meteorology-adjusted trend estimation: per-city Generalized Additive Models (AirGAM) fit daily NO₂ TVCD against meteorological + temporal covariates, isolating the anthropogenic long-term trend via smooth nonlinear functions with 95% CIs (preferred over linear trends, which underestimate change).
  • Change-factor construction: population-weighted NO₂ TVCD change factor = ratio of NO₂ per 100,000 residents in Dec 2024 vs Jan 2019; analogous GDP change factor from a singular-spectrum-analysis (SSA) trend on monthly-interpolated GDP.
  • Uncertainty quantification: 10,000-sample Monte Carlo per city propagates AirGAM CI bounds (NO₂) and admin-0→admin-2 s.d. (GDP); a change is "significant" only if the 95% CI excludes 1.
  • Four-quadrant typology: cities classified by signs of the two change factors into green (cleaner+richer), brown (dirtier+richer), gray (cleaner+poorer), red (dirtier+poorer).
  • GDP quality control: regional vs national growth regression (from DOSE + World Bank WDI) builds 95% bounds; cities with >6 of 11 years outside bounds are excluded (41 dropped).
  • Triple validation + EKC framing: cross-checks against national fossil-fuel consumption, EDGAR NOₓ correlations, and 465 European surface stations (satellite underestimates surface trends by ~16.3% on average); a complementary linear model links GDP and city type to NO₂ change, consistent with an environmental Kuznets curve.
