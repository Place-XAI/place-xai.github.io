Persuading voters using human–artificial intelligence dialogues

Hause Lin · Gabriela Czarnek · Benjamin Lewis · Joshua P. White · Adam J. Berinsky · Thomas Costello · Gordon Pennycook · David G. Rand Published 2025-12-04 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09771-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

In pre-registered experiments spanning the 2024 US presidential election, the 2025 Canadian federal election, and the 2025 Polish presidential election, brief AI-driven dialogues produced candidate-preference shifts larger than typical video advertisements. The AI models persuaded primarily through facts and evidence rather than psychological manipulation — yet accuracy was uneven: AI advocates for right-leaning candidates made significantly more inaccurate claims than those for left-leaning candidates, highlighting both the power and the asymmetric reliability risks of AI in electoral politics.

Why It Matters

  • First rigorous multi-country experiment on AI electoral persuasion. Moves the debate from speculation to pre-registered causal evidence: single conversations with an LLM can shift candidate preferences more than traditional political advertising.
  • Documents a new asymmetry in AI political risk. Right-leaning AI advocates generated factually less accurate statements than left-leaning ones — a politically consequential accuracy gap that holds across 12 open-source and proprietary models tested in robustness checks.
  • Clarifies the mechanism: facts, not manipulation. Strategy analysis across 27 coded persuasion techniques shows AI models relied on evidence and facts rather than emotional or psychological manipulation, reframing the "dark-AI" narrative.
  • Extends to direct democracy. Significant persuasion effects on Massachusetts ballot-measure support (psychedelics legalization) show AI influence is not limited to candidate races.
  • Raises urgent dual-use governance questions. The deliberate restriction of conversation transcripts and bespoke code (available only through Harvard Dataverse on vetted request) sets a precedent for responsible dual-use research practice.

Core Methods

  • Randomized controlled experiments: participants were randomly assigned to pro-candidate-A AI, pro-candidate-B AI, or a control condition; pre-registered with hypotheses and analysis plan filed before data collection.
  • Personalized AI advocacy: participants first indicated their top-priority issue (from a pre-determined list of ~17–19 policy issues or personality characteristics); the LLM tailored its advocacy to that stated priority — measuring tailored vs. non-personalized conditions in Canada and Poland.
  • Treatment effect estimation: pre-to-post changes in candidate preference (continuous and categorical) as primary outcomes; voting likelihood as secondary outcome. Causal forest conditional average treatment effects (CATE) explored heterogeneous effects by pre-treatment covariates.
  • Persuasion strategy analysis: each conversation coded by two LLM raters on 27 strategy dimensions (e.g., use of facts/evidence, emotional appeals, framing); no-facts experimental condition used as a manipulation check.
  • Accuracy audit: AI-generated statements assessed by Perplexity with internet access; compared across left-leaning vs. right-leaning advocacy conditions and replicated across 12 AI models.
  • Effect size benchmarking: observed treatment effects compared against published meta-analytic estimates of video advertisement persuasion to contextualize magnitude.
  • Persistence check: supplementary follow-up assessed whether persuasion effects persisted after the conversation (Supplementary Section 6).

① Map the spatial geography of AI persuasion susceptibility

Extending this paperThe paper randomly samples participants nationally but does not ask where persuasion effects are largest geographically — whether suburban swing districts, rural areas, or dense urban cores are differentially susceptible to AI advocacy. A direct extension: geo-tag participants to census-tract or county level and model heterogeneous treatment effects against place-level covariates (partisan history, media environment, demographic composition), turning the paper's causal forest CATE analysis into a spatial persuasion risk map. Urban political geography papers in the corpus — particularly on ideological sorting by proximity — provide the spatial political variables needed for such a layer.

Added value to my researchThis is a natural fusion with my spatiotemporal kernel density and MGWR toolkit: estimate a geographically weighted persuasion-susceptibility surface where the regression coefficients capture how place characteristics (density, political sorting, digital access) moderate AI-induced attitude change. It reframes a behavioral experiment as a spatial policy problem — which districts need stronger AI-disclosure regulation, which lack the media literacy to resist AI advocacy — connecting directly to my public-health accessibility and digital-divide work.

② Use semantic entropy to detect and flag the AI's inaccurate claims in real time

Extending this paperThe paper documents that right-leaning AI advocates made more inaccurate claims, rated post-hoc by Perplexity, but the AI had no real-time accuracy guard during the actual conversations. An extension: integrate a semantic-entropy hallucination detector directly into the dialogue loop as an inline verifier — before each AI turn is shown to the participant, compute semantic entropy across sampled outputs to flag uncertain or potentially false claims. This would test whether a self-monitoring constraint reduces the accuracy gap without substantially reducing persuasion, producing a calibrated AI advocacy system that the paper's current design cannot assess.

Added value to my researchMy interest in explainable ML for spatial prediction maps cleanly onto this: the same semantic-entropy principle can serve as an uncertainty quantifier for geospatial LLM outputs — when fine-tuned geospatial foundation models generate location-level predictions, high-entropy outputs signal regions where human-in-the-loop verification is needed. This extends both AI reliability work and my FAIR/open-science agenda around reproducible spatial AI pipelines.

Cross-ref (corpus): Detecting hallucinations in large language models using semantic entropy — 10.1038/s41586-024-07421-0

③ Test whether "warm" AI persona amplifies or dampens political persuasion

Extending this paperThe paper's AI advocacy system is implicitly presented as neutral-toned, optimized for the accuracy of facts rather than relational warmth. A corpus-motivated extension: replicate the paper's experimental design with a warmth-manipulated AI condition — friendly/warm persona vs. neutral vs. cold/authoritative — and test whether the persuasion magnitude changes, following evidence that warm training increases sycophancy and reduces accuracy. This directly tests an interaction the paper cannot observe: does the accuracy-persuasion trade-off from warmth training make AI political advocacy simultaneously less truthful and more persuasive — a compounded democratic risk?

Added value to my researchFrom a data-science and reproducibility angle, this highlights that the persona of an LLM used in citizen-facing tools (e.g., health chatbots, city planning chatbots queried about accessibility) carries hidden behavioral consequences. When building AI-assisted public-health or urban policy tools — a direct extension of my work on telehealth divides and healthcare accessibility — the warmth dimension must be treated as a design parameter with measurable equity and accuracy trade-offs, not a cosmetic UX choice.

Cross-ref (corpus): Training language models to be warm can reduce accuracy and increase sycophancy — 10.1038/s41586-026-10410-0

④ Cross-national misinformation inoculation as a counter to AI advocacy

Extending this paperThe paper establishes AI as a persuasion tool but does not test any countermeasure. A direct follow-on: embed a pre-bunk inoculation treatment — adapted from cross-national misinformation research — before the AI dialogue and test whether forewarning participants that an AI will try to persuade them (with examples of generic manipulation tactics) attenuates the effect sizes observed in this paper. The paper's cross-national design (US, Canada, Poland) is a natural platform: inoculation effects vary by political culture and media literacy, and the paper already has the infrastructure to measure moderation across countries.

Added value to my researchInoculation maps onto my interest in multi-source data fusion and community resilience: social media signals (from mobile-phone and social-media data streams I already use) can flag when coordinated AI-advocacy content is circulating in a region, acting as an early-warning layer that triggers targeted inoculation messaging. Combining the paper's persuasion effect sizes with fine-grained geographic spread data would let me estimate a spatial transmission model of AI-mediated attitude change — analogous to an SIR epidemic model for belief diffusion.

Cross-ref (corpus): Understanding and combatting misinformation across 16 countries on six continents — 10.1038/s41562-023-01641-6

⑤ From political persuasion to health behavior change: AI dialogues for public health access

Extending this paperThe paper's methodology — personalized LLM dialogue randomized to advocate for a position, with pre/post attitude measurement — is generalized beyond elections. An extension: apply the identical experimental template to health behavior adoption (telehealth uptake, vaccination, mental health service seeking) where evidence-based advocacy by AI could ethically increase access-related behavior in underserved communities. The paper's persuasion-strategy coding (27 dimensions) and no-facts manipulation provide a validated measurement apparatus that can transfer directly to health communication research.

Added value to my researchThis directly bridges the paper's methodology to my core agenda: telehealth adoption and digital health divides. My spatial accessibility work (2SFCA/R2SFCA) tells us where healthcare is inaccessible; this paper's approach tells us whether AI-mediated dialogues could shift behavioral barriers to access among those populations. Combining spatial accessibility maps with AI-advocacy effect-size estimates by area produces a targeted intervention geography — prioritizing which communities need AI-assisted health outreach and how to calibrate its accuracy safeguards — creating a new public-health application of AI dialogue systems grounded in spatial equity principles.
