Neighborhood effects on integrative organizational practices in five global cities

Christof Brandtner · Krystal Laryea · Gowun Park · Wei Luo · Michael Meyer · David Suárez · Hokyu Hwang · Walter W. Powell Published 2024-10-29 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00154-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The integrative work that civil society organizations do is not evenly distributed across the city — it is patterned by neighborhood affluence and diversity. Social integration practices (connecting people to one another) are more common in poorer neighborhoods, whereas systemic integration practices (connecting people and organizations into the wider institutional ecosystem) are more common in heterogeneous neighborhoods, especially affluent ones. Where organizations sit in the urban fabric shapes whether they knit residents together or wire them into the broader system.

★

Why It Matters

  • Treats social cohesion as a spatial outcome, not just a social one. The paper shows that the type of integration a community gets depends on the income and migrant composition of its neighborhood — making cohesion a question of urban geography and resource allocation.
  • Distinguishes two kinds of integration that are usually conflated. By separating social (people-to-people) from systemic (people/orgs-to-institutions) integration, it reveals a hidden division of labor in the civic sector and where each kind is missing.
  • Surfaces an equity gap. If systemic, ecosystem-building work concentrates in affluent, diverse neighborhoods, then poorer areas may receive bonding support but less of the brokerage that channels people into jobs, services and institutions.
  • Cross-city, comparative evidence. Rather than a single-city case, it uses harmonized survey data across five global cities, strengthening claims that the neighborhood-practice link is a general urban phenomenon.

Core Methods

  • Construct measurement via PCA: reduce many survey items on organizational activity into two interpretable dimensions — social integration (people-to-people) and systemic integration (people/orgs-to-institutions).
  • Multilevel linking: connect organization-level practice scores to neighborhood-level income and migrant share, so units are nested within neighborhoods within cities.
  • Regression modeling: OLS models (N = 845) estimating how affluence and heterogeneity — and their interaction — predict each integration type, with marginal-effects (margins) plots and 95% confidence intervals.
  • Interaction focus: test whether the effect of migrant share on systemic integration is amplified in affluent neighborhoods (the affluence × heterogeneity interaction is the central result).
  • Robustness & reproducibility: controls for organizational size and city; full Stata 18 replication pipeline released on Code Ocean.

① Turn "neighborhood income & migrant share" into spatially varying effects

Extending this paperThe paper's OLS estimates one global coefficient for affluence and one for migrant share across all five cities. But the effect of income on social integration almost certainly differs between a dense diverse core and a homogeneous suburb. Extending it: re-fit the affluence × heterogeneity model with MGWR / spatially varying coefficients within each city, mapping where social vs. systemic integration responds most strongly to neighborhood composition — converting a single number into a policy-ready surface.

② Add the demand side: do integrative orgs locate where people need them?

Extending this paperThe paper characterizes which practices exist given where organizations already are, but does not ask whether the supply of integrative work matches local need. ④ Open the black box: explainable ML on the affluence × heterogeneity interaction

Extending this paperThe headline result is an interaction effect (migrant share matters more in affluent neighborhoods), which OLS captures only as a pre-specified product term. Extending it: fit a gradient-boosted / ensemble model on the 845 organizations and use SHAP to discover whether the interaction is the dominant nonlinearity, whether thresholds exist, and which organizational features moderate it — letting the data reveal interactions the regression assumed.
