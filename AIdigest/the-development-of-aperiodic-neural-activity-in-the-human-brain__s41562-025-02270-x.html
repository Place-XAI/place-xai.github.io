The development of aperiodic neural activity in the human brain

Zachariah R. Cross · Samantha M. Gray · Adam J. O. Dede · Yessenia M. Rivera · Qin Yin · Parisa Vahidi · Elias M. B. Rau · Christopher Cyr · Ania M. Holubecki · Eishi Asano · Jack J. Core Conclusion

Aperiodic (1/f-like) neural activity — a proxy for neural noise — flattens with age into young adulthood across both association and sensorimotor cortices, challenging the assumption that sensorimotor regions mature early. In the prefrontal cortex (PFC), attentional state differentially modulates aperiodic slopes in children versus adults, and task-based slopes in the middle frontal gyrus (MFG) predict age-related gains in episodic memory, directly linking PFC cognitive-control maturation to memory development. These findings establish region-specific developmental trajectories of neural noise and illuminate a PFC-memory axis that continues maturing into the third and fourth decades of life.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Cohort
101 participants (children and adults), aged 5.93–54.00 years (63 males), recruited as clinical patients undergoing intracranial EEG (iEEG) monitoring for drug-resistant epilepsy. Participating clinical sites include children's and adults' hospitals across the USA (specific sites not fully enumerated in the abstract; the preregistration at osf.io/gsru7 and supplementary materials contain full site details).
Neural recordings
n = 5,691 intracranial electrodes (iEEG / sEEG / ECoG); electrode placement was clinically determined. Two conditions recorded per participant: task-based (attention to to-be-remembered visual stimuli) and task-free (resting-state).
Aperiodic signal
1/f-like power spectral density slope parameterized using the FOOOF / specparam algorithm (Donoghue et al., 2020, Nature Neuroscience). Steeper slope = less neural noise; flatter slope = more noise.
Structural MRI
Grey matter volume (GMV) extracted via FreeSurfer parcellation (Desikan–Killiany atlas, 34 bilateral regions) and registered to iEEG electrode coordinates for each participant.
Memory task
Incidental encoding paradigm; subsequent memory performance scored as proportion of items correctly recalled, used as the dependent variable in mediation analyses.
Preregistration
Preregistered at osf.io/gsru7. All data and analysis code publicly available at tinyurl.com/m5yfc9ny.
Availability
Fully open; code and data deposited at the OSF-linked repository.

Core Methods

  • Aperiodic slope estimation: for each electrode and condition, fit the 1/f-like power spectral density using the FOOOF/specparam framework, yielding an aperiodic exponent (slope) as the primary measure of neural noise.
  • Region-of-interest assignment: map each electrode to a Desikan–Killiany atlas region and group regions into association cortex vs. sensorimotor cortex to test classical vs. new developmental predictions.
  • Mixed-effects regression: model aperiodic slope as a function of age, attentional state (task vs. rest), brain region, and their interactions, with participant as a random effect, to characterize developmental trajectories.
  • Age × attentional-state interaction in PFC: test whether the direction of task-vs.-rest slope differences reverses between children and adults (the core PFC cognitive-control result).
  • Mediation analysis: test whether task-based MFG aperiodic slopes mediate the relationship between age and episodic memory performance.
  • Structural MRI integration: correlate FreeSurfer-derived GMV with aperiodic slopes across regions and age, and test age × GMV interaction effects on neural noise.
  • Preregistered analyses: primary hypotheses and analysis pipeline were registered prior to data collection/analysis at osf.io/gsru7, minimizing analytic flexibility.

A direct extension would geo-code each participant's residential environment (urban density, green-space exposure, noise pollution, walkability) and test whether the rate at which aperiodic slopes flatten — the trajectory shape — is modulated by urban or rural upbringing. ③ Apply explainable ML to decode the multivariate predictors of aperiodic slope trajectories

Extending this paperThe paper uses linear mixed-effects models, which assume additive, parametric relationships between age, GMV, attentional state, and slope. An extension would train a gradient-boosted ensemble or neural network on the same electrode-level data — treating age, region, GMV, task condition, sex, and clinical epilepsy variables as features — and apply SHAP or attention maps to reveal which features, and in which regional combinations, drive non-linear slope heterogeneity. This would capture developmental inflection points (e.g., puberty-associated transitions) that a linear model cannot detect, and could stratify participants by neural-noise developmental phenotype.

⑤ Connect aperiodic maturation trajectories to digital-divide and mental-health access disparities in older adults

Extending this paperThe paper shows that aperiodic slopes continue to change into the fourth decade of life and links PFC noise to memory. An underexplored extension would follow the developmental trajectory into older age: does the aperiodic slope reflaten in aging, and does this re-flattening predict cognitive decline? Furthermore, do interventions that maintain cognitive engagement — such as internet use — modulate the aperiodic aging curve? The corpus paper on internet use and mental health in adults aged 50+ across 23 countries provides the behavioral epidemiological evidence that digital engagement protects older adult cognition, which could be mechanistically underpinned by the aperiodic framework from this paper.
