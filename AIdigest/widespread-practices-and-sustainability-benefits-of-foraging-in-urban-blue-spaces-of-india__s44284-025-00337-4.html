Widespread practices and sustainability benefits of foraging in urban blue spaces of India

Sukanya Basu · Brenda Maria Zoderer · Harini Nagendra · Peter H. Verburg · Tobias Plieninger Published 2025-12-02 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00337-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A face-to-face survey of 1,200 users across four Indian cities (Bengaluru, Mumbai, Kochi, Kolkata) shows that foraging edibles from urban lakes, rivers and wetlands is a widespread, not marginal, practice: more than half of respondents are frequent foragers. The practice is concentrated among women, the elderly, daily-wage laborers and Scheduled Castes/Tribes, who collect, sell, share and cook edibles. Frequent foragers value foraging for nutrition, income and social capital, while occasional foragers value nature and culture. The paper reframes urban blue spaces as overlooked, multifunctional edible landscapes central to equitable urban food systems.

Why It Matters

  • Makes an invisible food system visible. Urban food research is dominated by agriculture, gardening and tech (vertical farming, aquaponics); this is one of the first empirical, quantified estimates of foraging in urban blue spaces in the Global South.
  • Centers equity and the marginalized. The strongest foragers are women, elders, daily-wage laborers, and Scheduled Castes/Tribes — exposing how degradation and privatization of waterscapes erodes a livelihood and food-security buffer for the urban poor.
  • Reframes blue spaces as multifunctional. Lakes and rivers are usually framed as recreation or regulating ecosystem services that benefit a privileged few; this work adds an overlooked provisioning service and a relational, biocultural dimension.
  • Translates to policy. Findings support an IPBES-aligned agenda: recognize blue spaces as biocultural sites, protect biodiversity, enable sustainable foraging, and integrate it into inclusive food and spatial planning.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Primary data
Original face-to-face questionnaire survey of 1,200 blue-space users (analytic samples 1,184–1,190 after missing-value removal), collected by the authors — there is no external geospatial dataset; this is field-collected social-survey data.
Spatial coverage
Four Indian cities — Bengaluru (13.6M, 741 km²), Mumbai (22.8M, 467 km²), Kochi (2.2M, 98 km²), Kolkata (4.5M, 207 km²) — and 12 urban blue-space sites (3 water bodies per city: lakes, ponds, canals, wetlands).
Sampling
Opportunistic intercept sampling; only residents within a 5-km radius of each water body eligible; ~100 participants per site; ages 18–100; interviews 15–20 min in local language or English.
Time window
Pilot study May 2023; main collection May–September 2023 (summer + monsoon); surveys 5:30 a.m.–5:30 p.m.
Instrument
Three-part questionnaire: (i) sociodemographic/behavioral drivers, (ii) ancestral knowledge & observed practices, (iii) frequency of four practices (collect, sell, share, cook). Values measured on a 5-point Likert scale across 27 foraging benefits in 4 categories (nature, nutrition & income, social capital, culture & identity), adapted from a landscape-products framework.
Ethics
Approved by the Ethics Committee of the University of Göttingen; informed consent from all respondents.

Core Methods

  • Two-step cluster analysis: groups respondents by foraging-practice frequency (collect/sell/share/cook) using Schwarz's BIC and log-likelihood distance, yielding three forager groups (rare, occasional, frequent); cluster quality silhouette ≈ 0.7. (SPSS v28)
  • Chi-square tests: link the three clusters to sociodemographic background (gender, age, caste, education, occupation, income).
  • NMDS ordination: non-metric multidimensional scaling on Bray–Curtis dissimilarity to map foraging-behavior dissimilarity (stress = 0.012, excellent fit) with sociodemographic and city centroids. (R vegan)
  • Multinomial logistic regression: tests behavioral drivers (years in city, garden access, blue-space visit frequency, edible-noticing frequency) on cluster membership; reports odds ratios and 95% CIs; VIF < 5; n = 1,184. (R VGAM)
  • Redundancy analysis (RDA): relates 27 foraging benefit valuations to forager group, sociodemographics and city; explains 70.8% of variance (RDA1 55.5%, adj. R² = 0.70, F = 126.66, 999 permutations). (R vegan)

Expanded Ideas — this paper's extensibility & added value to my research

Each idea stays anchored to this paper: first how the paper itself could be extended (drawing on our corpus), then the added value for my research lines (migis.net — GeoAI, 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility, explainable & physics-informed graph learning, health geography, open/FAIR science).

① Turn perceived foraging benefit into a spatial accessibility surface

Extending this paperThe study locates foragers at 12 discrete water bodies and characterizes who forages, but never maps who can reach an edible blue space. A direct extension: fuse the survey's eligibility rule (5-km residence radius) with a network-based accessibility model so that foraging supply (productive, accessible blue spaces) is matched to demand (marginalized populations) city-wide — converting site-level findings into an equity map of blue-space food provisioning deserts.

Added value to my researchThis is a clean new application of my 2SFCA/R2SFCA machinery: treat productive blue spaces as "supply," caste/income-weighted residents as "demand," and derive a food-provisioning accessibility index — extending my service-area delineation work from clinics to ecosystem-service sites.

② Replace assumed forager classes with explainable, interpretable clustering

Extending this paperThe paper's three forager groups come from a two-step cluster analysis and chi²/NMDS, which describe associations but do not yield per-individual, attributable predictions. Extending it: train an explainable ML model (gradient-boosted trees + SHAP) on the same 1,184-record survey to predict forager group, quantifying the marginal contribution of caste, gender, garden access and visit frequency — sharpening the paper's "odds-ratio" story into feature attributions and interactions it currently cannot capture.

Added value to my researchDirectly feeds my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction line: a worked template for combining survey microdata with SHAP to expose non-linear social drivers, transferable to telehealth-uptake and primary-care-utilization models.

Cross-ref (corpus): Social-ecological and technological factors moderate the value of urban nature — 10.1038/s41893-018-0202-1

③ Scale the survey with remote sensing to map blue-space edibility

Extending this paperThe study acknowledges it cannot delve into intercity differences and relies on intercept sampling at 12 sites. Extending it: use satellite/UAV imagery and water-quality/NDVI products to map the ecological supply of edibles (greens, fish habitat, wetland productivity) across each city's full blue-space inventory, then validate against the survey — moving from 12 surveyed water bodies to a city-complete edibility layer that also flags contamination/heavy-metal risk the paper raises.

Added value to my researchA concrete multi-source data-fusion case (remote sensing + ground survey) and a candidate task for fine-tuning geospatial foundation models to detect productive, safe foraging waterscapes — directly serving my GeoAI and data-fusion themes.

Cross-ref (corpus): Comparing the carbon footprints of urban and conventional agriculture — 10.1038/s44284-023-00023-3

④ Guard against green-gentrification when foraging is "recognized"

Extending this paperThe paper recommends integrating foraging into urban planning, but formalizing or beautifying blue spaces risks displacing the very marginalized foragers it documents. Extending it: pair the forager-group map with a longitudinal monitor of land value, access restrictions and demographic turnover around restored water bodies, testing whether "recognition" delivers benefits to frequent foragers or triggers green gentrification and exclusion.

Added value to my researchConnects to my public-facility accessibility & housing-price work: a natural setting to model how an amenity upgrade reshapes accessibility-vs-equity, using MGWR to localize where recognition helps versus displaces.

⑤ Link foraging engagement to measurable health and well-being outcomes

Extending this paperThe study measures perceived benefits (nutrition, social capital, culture) on a Likert scale but stops short of health endpoints. Extending it: couple the forager typology with dietary-diversity, mental-health and social-cohesion measures in a follow-up cohort to test whether frequent foraging buffers food insecurity and improves well-being — turning perceived value into a quantified nature–health dose–response.
