Stable isotope evidence for pre-colonial maize agriculture and animal management in the Bolivian Amazon

Tiago Hermenegildo · Heiko Prümers · Carla Jaimes Betancourt · Patrick Roberts · Tamsin C. O'Connell Published 2024-12-23 DOI 10.1038/s41562-024-02070-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Stable carbon (δ13C) and nitrogen (δ15N) isotope analysis of 86 human and 68 animal bone-collagen samples from two Casarabe-culture sites in the Bolivian Amazon (ce ~700–1400) reveals that maize was a dietary staple in the earliest occupation phase and then declined in importance after ce ~800. Crucially, muscovy duck (Cairina moschata) — the only domesticated vertebrate of the South American lowlands — shows isotope values indicating intentional maize feeding from as early as ce 800, providing the first direct isotopic evidence for animal management in the entire Amazonian lowland context and helping to overturn the view of the Amazon as a 'counterfeit paradise' incapable of supporting complex human food systems.

Why It Matters

  • First direct isotopic evidence for animal management in Amazonia. Prior knowledge rested on archaeobotanical snapshots; this study's time-averaged bone chemistry demonstrates that the Casarabe people intentionally kept and fed muscovy ducks — rewriting the narrative of South American lowland domestication.
  • Temporal resolution within a single culture. By linking isotope data to five stratigraphic ceramic phases (ce 700–1400), the study tracks dietary change through time — showing peak maize reliance in phase 2 statistically comparable to Classic Maya agriculturalists, followed by later dietary diversification.
  • Challenges the 'counterfeit paradise' hypothesis. High δ13C human values comparable to Mesoamerican maize-staple populations challenge the long-held environmental-determinist view that Amazonian soils and ecology precluded intensive food production.
  • Links crop and animal management. The convergence of muscovy duck and human isotope values implies maize played the same domestication-facilitating role in the lowland tropics that it played for turkeys and guinea pigs elsewhere in the Americas.
  • Demonstrates tropical collagen preservation at scale. Over 90% of sampled remains from a humid tropical context yielded well-preserved, uncontaminated collagen — a methodologically important benchmark for future bioarchaeological work in similar environments.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Human remains
86 individuals (Salvatierra: 65 individuals, phases 2–5, ce ~700–1400; Mendoza: 24 individuals, phases 3–4, ce ~800–1350) — both sexes and all ages; 98.9% collagen yield, 96.6% final acceptable results.
Faunal remains
68 samples from 11 taxa (mammals, birds, reptiles, fish), from Mound 2 at Salvatierra, phases 1–3. Final acceptable yield: 94.1% after contamination screening.
Isotopic measurements
δ13C and δ15N measured by isotope-ratio mass spectrometry (Costech elemental analyser / Thermo Finnigan MAT253, Godwin Laboratory, University of Cambridge). Triplicates of 0.7–0.9 mg collagen. Precision: ±0.09‰ (δ13C) and ±0.08‰ (δ15N) overall.
Chronological span
ce ~500–1400, subdivided into five ceramic phases based on stratigraphic Harris matrices. Isotope data cover phases 2–5 for humans and phases 1–3 for fauna.
Geographic scope
Two monumental-mound Casarabe-culture sites — Salvatierra and Mendoza — in the Llanos de Mojos, Beni department, Bolivia, within a 4,500 km² earthwork complex of >189 large mounds and ~1,000 km of canals and causeways.

Bone archive
Assemblage held at the German Archaeological Institute (DAI), Bonn. Post-study, all human remains returned to Bolivia (Kenneth Lee Museum, Trinidad, Beni). Excavation export permitted under Bolivian UNAR AUT permits 026/02 (Mendoza) and 019/06 (Salvatierra).

Core Methods

  • Collagen extraction (modified Longin/Brown protocol): bone samples (0.5–1.0 g) sandblasted, demineralized in 0.5 M HCl (3–7 days), gelatinized at pH 3 / 75 °C for 48 h, freeze-dried; quality control via C/N ratio thresholds and collagen yield.
  • Stable isotope ratio mass spectrometry (IRMS): δ13C and δ15N measured in triplicates on a Costech elemental analyser coupled to a Thermo Finnigan MAT253; calibrated against IAEA and laboratory standards (caffeine, glutamic acid, nylon, alanine, bovine liver).
  • Bayesian stable isotope ellipse analysis (SIBER): Bayesian inference of standard isotopic ellipses (estimated 40% of population at 95% credible interval) used to compare dietary niches among human ceramic phases and faunal groups; requires ≥10 observations for reliable inference.
  • Parametric and non-parametric statistical tests: Shapiro–Wilk normality tests determined choice of ANOVA / Tukey (parametric) vs. Kruskal–Wallis / Dunn / Mann–Whitney (non-parametric) comparisons of δ13C and δ15N across groups and phases.
  • Diachronic phase segmentation: human isotope data linked to five ceramic phases (via Harris matrices) to reconstruct dietary change across 700 years of occupation.
  • Comparative isotopic benchmarking: human values placed against published Amazon Basin and Mesoamerican reference populations (Supplementary Table 3) to contextualize the degree of maize reliance.

Extending it: apply spatial interpolation and geographically weighted regression (GWR) to isotope values mapped against the earthwork network topology — canals, causeways, and mound hierarchy — to test whether dietary reliance on maize correlates with site centrality (primary vs. secondary centres) or with proximity to raised-field agricultural zones. The lidar-revealed settlement map (Prümers et al. 2022, ref. 27 in the article) already provides the spatial skeleton for such an analysis. ④ Food-production intensification as a driver of low-density urban network formation

Extending this paperThe paper suggests maize agriculture may have been central to the expansion of the Casarabe settlement network — a low-density urban system with a four-tier hierarchy of 189+ mounds linked by ~1,000 km of canals and causeways. Extending it: model this as a spatial network problem — treat the mounds as nodes, the canals and causeways as edges, and use the paper's isotope-derived temporal dietary signal as a proxy for agricultural surplus capacity at each site. Network community detection and agent-based modeling could then test whether higher phase 2 maize reliance correlates with settlement-network centralization, and whether the later dietary diversification corresponds to a restructuring of network topology. The corpus paper "A quantitative analysis of intensification in the ethnographic record" provides the theoretical bridge between food-production intensification and social complexity.

⑤ Coupling dietary isotope signals with mobility proxies to reconstruct human movement in the Casarabe network

Extending this paperThe paper notes a possible east-to-west trade-based diversification of food sources after ce 800 — implying increased human mobility along the canal network. Extending it: complement the carbon/nitrogen isotope analysis with strontium (Sr) isotope analysis of the same skeletal assemblage (a recognized geographic-mobility proxy) to test whether individuals with lower maize-reliance signals are geographically non-local — i.e., whether dietary diversification is mobility-coupled.
