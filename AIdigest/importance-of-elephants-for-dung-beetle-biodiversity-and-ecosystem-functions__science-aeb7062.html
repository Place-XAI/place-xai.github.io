Importance of elephants for dung beetle biodiversity and ecosystem functions

[Authors not available in scraped metadata] Published 2026-05-28 DOI 10.1126/science.aeb7062 Source science.org ↗
Core Conclusion

Elephants are central hubs in the interaction network between dung beetles and large herbivores in East Africa — their extinction would trigger disproportionate, cascading losses of dung beetle abundance, diversity, and ecosystem functions. A 15-year field experiment confirmed that excluding elephants depressed dung beetles far more than excluding smaller ungulates, and observational data from livestock-dominated landscapes corroborated that elephant displacement impairs this critical functional group. Large, extinction-prone animals act as keystone resource-providers sustaining the consumers and services linked to them.

Why It Matters

  • First long-term empirical test of coextinction theory. Although ecologists have long theorized that removing highly connected species triggers cascades of secondary extinctions, direct experimental evidence has been scant. This 15-year exclusion experiment provides rare, controlled proof of the mechanism at landscape scale.
  • Elevates the functional value of megafauna beyond charisma. Elephants are known as ecosystem engineers through physical habitat modification; this study adds a quantitative network role — they are irreplaceable resource nodes for an entire guild of dung beetles that perform dung removal, soil aeration, and seed burial.
  • Bridges network theory and conservation practice. By combining a generalized interaction network, predictive simulation, and experimental validation, the paper delivers a reproducible workflow for anticipating which species losses will cascade — actionable for conservation prioritization.
  • Warns about livestock-driven defaunation. Observational comparisons of livestock versus elephant landscapes translate the controlled experiment into a real-world threat assessment for the vast savannas where elephants are being displaced.

Core Methods

  • Ecological network construction: built a generalized bipartite interaction network between dung beetle species and large herbivore species, quantifying which beetles use which herbivore's dung, then computed network metrics (degree, centrality) to establish elephants as hubs.
  • Network-based extinction simulation: used the empirical network topology in computer simulations of sequential node removal (elephant removal first vs. random removal) to predict the magnitude and pattern of dung beetle coextinction, generating species-loss curves.
  • Manipulative exclosure experiment (15 years): applied factorial treatments excluding mega-herbivores (elephants) and/or smaller ungulates to replicated landscape plots; measured beetle community responses (abundance, diversity, functional metrics) over 15 years — a long-term BACI-style field experiment.
  • Observational landscape comparison: surveyed dung beetle communities in paired landscapes differing in elephant vs. livestock dominance, using the pattern to independently test the experimental finding and assess the real-world threat of elephant displacement.
  • Integrated prediction-validation design: predictions from the network simulation were quantitatively compared to experimental outcomes, explicitly testing whether network topology is a reliable predictor of coextinction risk — a rare methodological contribution in ecology.
