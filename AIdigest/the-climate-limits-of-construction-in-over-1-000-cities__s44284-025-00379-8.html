The climate limits of construction in over 1,000 cities

Keagan Hudson Rankin · André Cabrera Serrenho · Chris Bachmann · I. Daniel Posen · Shoshanna Saxe Published 2026-01-14 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00379-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across over 1,000 cities worldwide, cradle-to-gate construction consumption emissions are converging around 1–3 tCO2e per capita per year — a level that on its own could consume most or all of a city's emissions allowance under a 2 °C target by 2030. To stay within climate limits, cities must cut construction emissions to below 10% of current levels within the next 2–4 decades, which is impossible through efficiency tweaks alone and demands a fundamental rethinking of how and how much we build. The results are delivered through an open city-level dashboard for science-backed planning.

★

Why It Matters

  • Fills a blind spot in city climate policy. Cities lead on household and energy emissions but almost universally ignore construction; this is the first consistent estimate of construction consumption emissions and the matching carbon budgets for more than a thousand cities at once.
  • Shows the problem is structural, not marginal. Construction emissions alone can exhaust a city's entire 2 °C-compatible budget — meaning current build practices are physically incompatible with climate targets, not merely inefficient.
  • Reveals convergence. Despite vastly different incomes and geographies, per-capita construction emissions cluster in a narrow 1–3 tCO2e band, suggesting a shared structural driver that any global decarbonization strategy must confront.
  • Translates targets into a clear ask. The required >90% reduction within 2–4 decades gives planners an unambiguous benchmark against which material choices, reuse, and demand reduction can be measured.
  • Ships as open infrastructure. The companion city carbon-budget dashboard lets stakeholders explore decarbonization scenarios for their own city.

Core Methods

  • Consumption-based emission estimation: compute cradle-to-gate construction emissions for each city using an environmentally-extended MRIO (EXIOBASE 3) fused with material-intensity and life-cycle inventory data, capturing emissions embodied in materials regardless of where they are produced.
  • City carbon-budget downscaling: allocate a share of the global 2 °C (and 1.5 °C) carbon budget to each city and to its construction sector, using equity-based allocation principles (Fig. 3).
  • Convergence / trend analysis: characterize per-capita construction emissions across income and region groups to expose the 1–3 tCO2e convergence band (Fig. 2).
  • Sectoral trade-off analysis: contrast the construction budget against budgets for other urban sectors to show how construction crowds out the remaining allowance (Fig. 4).
  • Demand-projection scenarios: model whether projected population and housing growth can be met within the construction carbon budget, quantifying the required >90% reduction (Fig. 5).
  • Open scenario dashboard: package all city-level results into an interactive tool for stakeholder exploration of decarbonization pathways.

① Make the budget downscaling spatially explicit, not just per-city totals

Extending this paperThe paper allocates one construction carbon budget per city, but the demand for new floor space is itself spatially heterogeneous within a city — concentrated where population, jobs and housing pressure grow fastest. A direct extension: couple the MRIO consumption-emission estimate with an intra-urban demand surface (gridded population + housing growth) so the budget becomes a spatial field, revealing which neighbourhoods will blow the budget first and where reuse should be prioritized.

Added value to my researchThis is exactly my spatiotemporal accessibility toolkit applied to carbon: the same kernel-density and gridded-demand machinery I use for service access can downscale a city budget to the parcel level, turning an aggregate target into a place-based equity diagnostic.

② Explain the 1–3 tCO2e convergence with interpretable ML

Extending this paperThe paper documents a striking convergence band but does not decompose why cities with vastly different incomes land in the same 1–3 tCO2e range. Extending it: train an explainable ML model (gradient-boosting + SHAP) on city features — density, growth rate, material mix, climate, income — to attribute the convergence to its structural drivers and test whether the band is a genuine invariant or an artifact of the MRIO resolution.

Added value to my researchThis drops straight into my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction line: the same SHAP/MGWR attribution stack I apply to health and housing outcomes can quantify how much of construction emissions is destiny (geography) versus policy (material choice), with spatially varying coefficients.

③ Add material-substitution pathways (timber, reuse) to the dashboard

Extending this paperThe paper quantifies the >90% gap but largely treats material mix as given. A concrete extension: embed material-substitution scenarios — mass-timber, low-clinker cement, structural reuse of vacant stock — into the budget engine, so each city's dashboard can show how far each lever closes its construction-emission gap, and where wood supply or reuse stock physically constrains the option.

④ Reconcile consumption-based estimates with city self-reported inventories

Extending this paperThe paper's MRIO consumption accounting captures embodied emissions that city inventories routinely omit — so its numbers will systematically exceed self-reported figures. Extending it: cross-validate the per-city estimates against municipal GHG inventories to quantify the under-reporting gap attributable to construction, giving the consumption-based method an empirical credibility check.

Added value to my researchThis is a reproducibility / data-fusion problem after my own heart: reconciling top-down MRIO with bottom-up reporting is the same FAIR, multi-source-fusion discipline I apply to mobile-phone, social-media and remote-sensing data, and yields an uncertainty-aware accounting layer.

⑤ Link the construction budget to "build less, build smart" — demand reduction via accessibility

Extending this paperThe paper concludes a >90% cut is needed and hints that demand reduction, not just cleaner materials, is essential — but it does not model how much floor space could be avoided. Extending it: couple the budget engine with compact, proximity-based urban form (15-minute / vacancy-reuse strategies) to estimate how much new construction is avoidable when access goals are met by reusing existing stock rather than building anew.
