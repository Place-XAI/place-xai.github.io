The impact of private hosting on the integration of Ukrainian refugees in Germany

Mathis Herpell · Moritz Marbach · Niklas Harder · Alexandra Orlova · Dominik Hangartner · Jens Hainmueller Published 2025-09-10 DOI 10.1038/s41562-025-02303-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Among Ukrainian refugees registered with Germany's largest non-profit matching platform (#UnterkunftUkraine, n = 1,700), being privately hosted improved overall integration by ~20% of a standard deviation (ITT) and ~50% (LATE). Gains were concentrated in social, psychological, and navigational integration, with no discernible effects on linguistic, economic, or political integration. Supplementary analysis shows these gains are driven by host–refugee contact, not by placement in more welcoming neighbourhoods, and the effects are robust across demographic subgroups and both main housing alternatives (public shelters and private rentals).

★

Why It Matters

  • First causal, large-scale evidence on private hosting. Despite >11 million Ukrainians displaced since 2022 and ~27% privately hosted in Europe within the first months, systematic quantitative evidence on what hosting does to refugees was almost entirely absent. This study fills that gap with a credible quasi-experimental design.
  • Reveals a contact mechanism, not a neighbourhood effect. The paper disentangles two competing explanations and shows that it is the frequency of interaction with the host — not placement in wealthier, more diverse or more tolerant municipalities — that drives integration gains.
  • Challenges canonical integration theories. Social and psychological integration improve before linguistic integration, contradicting Gordon's assimilation theory and Esser's four-dimensional framework, and instead supporting Berry's acculturation model and social-capital accounts.
  • Policy-relevant for future mass displacement events. Results suggest private hosting is a cost-effective, civil-society-leveraging complement to overwhelmed public asylum systems, with stable benefits across gender, age, education, and marital status sub-groups.
  • Honest about limits. The study clearly flags that effects are short-to-medium-term (measured ~14 months post-arrival), self-reported, and specific to Ukrainians matched via one platform — setting a realistic scope for generalization.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Platform data
#UnterkunftUkraine (UU) — Germany's largest non-profit host–refugee matching platform. At the time of study, UU had registered over 160,000 potential hosts, of whom ~30,000 completed verification, and had facilitated 25,400 matches connecting over 60,000 Ukrainians to private accommodation.
Survey sample
Original online survey launched 1 June 2023 to all UU-registered refugees with valid contact information. Total respondents: 2,811; fully completed surveys: 1,870; estimation sample (arrived Germany ≥ January 2022, still in Germany at survey): n = 1,700.
Outcome instrument
IPL-12 (Immigration Policy Lab 12-item integration index) — six dimensions: psychological, economic, political, social, linguistic, and navigational integration. Scale: 0–1 (higher = more integrated). Validated across multiple countries and adopted by the International Organization for Migration.
Location variables
Municipality-level covariates linked to refugees' residential locations: AfD vote share, hate crimes per capita, unemployment rate, voter turnout, average income, average rent, average age, population density, share of foreign-born residents, city status, East Germany indicator.
Time coverage
Refugee arrivals: February 2022 – July 2022 (median April 2022); survey conducted June 2023; median residency duration at survey: 14 months. Effects are classified as short-to-medium-term.
Matched fraction
Only 19% of UU-registered refugees were ultimately matched to a host (demand exceeded supply). This scarcity underpins the quasi-experimental identification strategy: matched vs. unmatched applicants differ only in host availability, not in refugee characteristics.

Approved by ETH Zurich (EK-2023-N-122), DeZIM (EK06/2023), and Stanford IRB (72870). Data available via controlled remote access at the DeZIM Research Data Centre (fdz.dezim-institut.de). Analysis code in Harvard Dataverse.

Core Methods

  • Selection-on-observables (quasi-experimental) design: matched vs. unmatched UU applicants are compared conditioning on the same refugee characteristics that UU itself used for matching (family size, languages, preferred municipality, etc.), under the assumption that residual selection on unobservables is negligible after conditioning.
  • Intention-to-treat (ITT) estimation: OLS regressions of IPL-12 outcomes on a binary indicator of UU matching; includes all main covariates and heteroscedasticity-robust standard errors.
  • Local average treatment effect (LATE) via 2SLS: the UU matching indicator instruments the binary "moved in with host" variable, addressing non-compliance (not all matched refugees moved in). First-stage partial F-statistics: 330–460.
  • Entropy balancing weights: applied to correct for differential non-response (matched refugees 8.5 pp more likely to respond); ensures respondent sample aligns with full UU population across all covariates.
  • Double/debiased machine learning (DML): used as a robustness check to flexibly control for covariates without parametric assumptions; results mirror the main OLS/2SLS estimates.
  • Placebo balance checks: 22 pre-determined refugee characteristics not visible to UU (e.g., citizenship, income in Ukraine, LGBTQ+ status) are regressed on the matching indicator; only 1 of 22 shows marginal imbalance, supporting the identification assumption.
  • Sensitivity analysis (hidden bias): formal Rosenbaum-style analysis shows an unobserved confounder would need to be >6× stronger than the strongest observed covariate (English/German language ability) to overturn the main effects.
  • Effect heterogeneity: subgroup-specific OLS regressions by gender, age, education, and marital status; equivalence tests to bound heterogeneity.
  • Contact mechanism test: 2SLS instrumenting contact frequency (five-point scale) with the matching indicator; higher contact frequency → larger integration gains.
  • Location mechanism test: OLS/ITT regressions using municipality characteristics as outcomes to test whether matched refugees live in observably better neighbourhoods (result: they do not).

① Map the spatial geography of civil-society hosting capacity to identify under-served refugee arrival zones

Extending this paperThe paper shows that private hosting works but treats host availability as an exogenous supply shock — it does not ask where in Germany hosting capacity is structurally high or low, nor whether proximity to host clusters affects matching probability. A natural extension is to geocode all registered UU hosts, compute a kernel density surface of host supply, overlay it against refugee arrival flows and public shelter locations, and identify "hosting deserts" where civil society capacity is structurally insufficient. This would transform the paper's policy finding into an actionable geographic targeting tool: which municipalities need government incentives to recruit hosts rather than expand shelter capacity?

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and 2SFCA accessibility framework: host supply and refugee demand become the two layers of a spatial accessibility problem, and the "hosting desert" score is a spatial accessibility score analogous to healthcare facility access. The same R2SFCA distance-decay formulation I use for primary care delineation can be adapted to model host-availability catchments, connecting refugee housing policy to my spatial accessibility methodology.

② Use explainable machine learning to decode which host–refugee match features predict integration success

Extending this paperThe paper's 2SLS design estimates a population-average LATE but explicitly acknowledges that "mechanisms are heterogeneous and the paper does not fully disentangle the quality of the host or match compatibility." A direct extension would use the rich registration data — host language skills, accommodation type, number of beds, presence of family, location — together with the IPL-12 outcomes to train a gradient-boosted regression and apply SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) decomposition to rank which match-level features most drive integration gains. This would operationalize the paper's heterogeneity hypothesis and give matching platforms like UU a data-driven quality score for pre-screening potential matches.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely within my explainable machine learning for spatial prediction agenda. Just as I use SHAP to decompose which neighbourhood features drive healthcare access disparities or housing prices, here SHAP acts on match features rather than spatial predictors. The insight is methodological: SHAP-based feature attribution works on any tabular prediction problem with policy implications, making it a transferable interpretability layer across all my facility-location and access-optimization work.

③ Test whether neighbourhood social capital amplifies the contact mechanism

Extending this paperThe paper's location analysis tests macro municipality-level indicators (AfD vote share, hate crimes, income) but finds no effect. However, it does not test finer-grained neighbourhood-level social capital — the density of civic associations, volunteerism rates, or infrastructure that brings residents together. The paper's own mechanism finding (contact drives integration) implies that the neighbourhood's social infrastructure might moderate contact quality: a refugee hosted in a high-social-capital neighbourhood may receive richer contact even with the same host. Extending the paper by linking UU hosting locations to a social capital index (e.g., derived from public transport access, community centre density, or social interaction data) would allow a three-way interaction test: hosting × contact frequency × neighbourhood social capital.

Added value to my researchThis connects directly to my work on urban agglomeration and the coupling of space-human systems. Neighbourhood social capital is itself an accessibility outcome — of civic infrastructure — and the three-way interaction model positions spatial accessibility as a moderator of individual-level social policy effects. This reframes social capital not as a background variable but as a measurable, spatially heterogeneous resource that my R2SFCA framework can quantify, linking my GeoAI methods to social cohesion outcomes.

④ Generalize the hosting platform's matching algorithm as a multi-objective facility-location problem

Extending this paperUU's matching process is described as using refugee and host registration characteristics to produce matches, but only 19% of registered refugees were matched (supply-constrained). The paper does not ask how matching could be optimized. Recasting the matching problem as a bipartite assignment with multi-objective criteria — minimize geographic distance, maximize predicted integration outcome (using the SHAP model from Idea 2), and balance load across municipalities — would directly serve UU-type platforms. The paper's own treatment effect estimates provide the outcome labels needed to train such a learned matching policy, and its sensitivity analysis (confounder would need 6× the strength of language ability) provides a natural feature-importance benchmark for the optimizer.

Added value to my researchThis is the EV-charging optimization problem in a new domain: instead of siting charging stations to balance efficiency and equity, the problem is to assign refugees to hosts balancing integration potential and geographic equity. My experience formulating bi-criteria facility-location problems (efficiency vs. equity) maps directly to this multi-objective matching task. ⑤ Track the long-term spatial trajectories of privately hosted refugees to test whether early integration gains persist

Extending this paperThe paper explicitly acknowledges that its effects are short-to-medium-term (median 14 months) and calls for longitudinal follow-up. A key open question is whether the social and psychological gains translate into durable residential choices: do privately hosted refugees cluster in the same municipalities as their former hosts, establish denser social networks in those areas, and eventually achieve stronger labour market integration? Linking UU registration data to a follow-up survey or administrative records (job registration, tax records) 3–5 years post-arrival and applying spatial econometric panel models would test whether the contact mechanism has a spatial legacy — i.e., whether proximity to the initial host location mediates long-term integration via neighbourhood network effects.

Added value to my researchThis connects to my spatiotemporal analysis of mobile phone GPS trajectories: refugee residential mobility over time can be tracked as a sequence of spatial states, and the transition probability between high-integration and low-integration neighbourhood types (defined by my accessibility scores) can be modeled as a Markov chain conditioned on initial hosting exposure. This would position GeoAI-based trajectory analysis as a tool for evaluating the spatial legacy of social policy interventions — a genuinely novel application of my methods.
