Robust chemical analysis with graphene chemosensors and machine learning

Andrew Pannone · Aditya Raj · Harikrishnan Ravichandran · Sarbashis Das · Ziheng Chen · Collin A. Price · Mahmooda Sultana · Saptarshi Das Published 2024-10-09 DOI 10.1038/s41586-024-08003-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Non-functionalized graphene-based ISFET arrays, when paired with artificial neural networks, achieve robust classification and quantification of chemical solutions — detecting food fraud, food spoilage, and PFAS contamination with high accuracy. Machine learning mitigates cycle-to-cycle, sensor-to-sensor, and chip-to-chip variability that previously limited ISFET deployment, while SHAP analysis reveals which electrical features drive each discrimination task, offering interpretable insight beyond expert intuition. This fusion of compact, energy-efficient graphene hardware with data-driven analytics signals a practical path toward commercial-scale chemosensing across environmental, food safety, and healthcare domains.

Core Methods

  • ISFET array fabrication and measurement: non-functionalized graphene transistors on a chip; transfer characteristics (drain current vs. gate voltage) collected across many cycles, chips, and chemical conditions to build large training datasets.
  • Feature extraction (figures of merit, FOMs): human-interpretable features extracted from transfer curves — Dirac-point voltage (VDirac), minimum current (IDirac), hysteresis current (Ihys), maximum/minimum currents before/after sweep, transconductance peaks — ranked by k-NN classification score and R² to identify the most discriminative features.
  • k-Nearest Neighbour (k-NN) benchmark: exhaustive evaluation over all single-feature and multivariate FOM combinations to establish a human-expertise-derived performance ceiling for each application.
  • Artificial Neural Network (ANN) classification and quantification: ANNs trained on either selected FOMs or the entire raw transfer characteristic; cross-chip and cross-batch training/testing to demonstrate generalization without recalibration.
  • SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) feature attribution: post-hoc interpretability applied to trained ANNs to identify which regions of the ISFET transfer characteristic are most informative for each analyte class, revealing sensor physics beyond human expertise.
  • Cross-chip generalization test: model trained on one chip and tested on a separately fabricated chip — showing the ANN achieves similar performance regardless of which chip was used for training, eliminating recalibration requirements.

① From chip-level generalization to federated cross-lab learning

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that an ANN trained on one graphene ISFET chip generalizes to chips fabricated subsequently — but this is still centralized, single-lab training. A natural extension is to recast the paper's chip-to-chip generalization experiment as a federated learning problem: each fabrication facility or deployment site trains locally on its own sensor data and contributes to a shared global model without exposing raw measurements. This would directly address the paper's ambition of "broader adoption in commercial applications," which requires multi-site deployment where data sharing is commercially or legally restricted.

Added value to my researchFederated learning across distributed sensing nodes maps directly onto my work with multi-source data fusion (mobile phone, social media, remote sensing): data rarely flows to a single warehouse in geospatial pipelines. A federated model of the ISFET type — where local feature extractors share compressed representations rather than raw signals — is conceptually identical to spatially-partitioned model training across cities or regions. The interpretability constraint (keep SHAP meaningful in the federated setting) is an open research problem I could port to spatial ML.

② Deploy SHAP attribution as a real-time sensor-health diagnostic

Extending this paperIn this paper, SHAP is used post-hoc to understand which electrical features distinguish chemical classes — a scientific insight tool. A direct extension: use the stability of SHAP attribution vectors over time as a real-time quality-control signal. If the SHAP profile for a pH-7 buffer drifts between measurement cycles, the sensor is degrading or contaminated before classification accuracy visibly drops. This converts an explanation tool into a predictive maintenance detector, advancing the paper's own stated goal of commercial ISFET reliability.

Added value to my researchThis idea generalizes to my explainable ML for spatial prediction work: SHAP stability over space can flag data quality issues in spatially heterogeneous datasets (e.g., where mobile phone signal degrades, census enumeration is unreliable, or remote sensing coverage changes). Treating SHAP vectors as a quality signal — not just an explanation — is a practical open-science/FAIR data contribution.

④ Replace the paper's FOM-based feature engineering with end-to-end physics-informed learning

Extending this paperThe paper's workflow extracts human-defined figures of merit (Dirac point, hysteresis, transconductance peaks) as ML inputs — a step that encodes expert physics knowledge but may miss non-obvious patterns. An extension: train a physics-informed neural network that simultaneously fits the Nernst equation governing ISFET pH response as a soft constraint while learning a richer latent representation from the full transfer characteristic. This would let the model discover new physical regimes (e.g., non-Nernstian surface effects at graphene edges) that FOM extraction discards.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly onto my physics-informed graph learning agenda: encoding known physical or spatial laws (transport equations, distance-decay, population conservation) as soft constraints in a neural network, so the model generalizes to new conditions rather than memorizing training distributions. The ISFET case is a clean testbed with well-understood governing equations — a good validation domain for my physics-informed methods before applying them to messier urban systems.

⑤ Combine graphene ISFET arrays with AI-enhanced gas spectroscopy for multimodal sensing

Extending this paperThis paper operates in the liquid phase (solutions measured on the graphene surface). An adjacent technology — AI-enhanced mid-infrared gas spectroscopy — addresses the vapor/gas phase. A direct extension of the paper: create a multimodal sensing platform that fuses liquid-phase ISFET signals with vapor-phase spectroscopic signals from the headspace above a food sample, training a joint ANN on both modalities. The paper's SHAP framework generalizes naturally to multimodal attribution, revealing which modality contributes more signal for each analyte type — an experiment the current paper's hardware already partially enables (fish freshness produces both liquid ionic changes and volatile off-gases).
