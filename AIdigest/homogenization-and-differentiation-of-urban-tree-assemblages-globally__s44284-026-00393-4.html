Homogenization and differentiation of urban tree assemblages globally

Xudong Yang · Jing Jin · Xinyi Liu · Frank A. La Sorte · Pengbo Yan · Myla F. J. Aronson · Jun Yang Published 2026-02-23 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00393-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Once you control for differences among regional species pools, there is no single global signal of urban tree homogenization — the conventional story is partly an artifact of method. Instead the effect is scale-dependent: urban tree assemblages homogenize across broad geographic distances but differentiate at short distances. Nonnative species are the main driver — sharing the same nonnatives homogenizes distant cities, while sharing different nonnatives differentiates nearby cities.

Why It Matters

  • Corrects a textbook claim. "Cities make biodiversity more similar everywhere" is one of the most repeated conclusions in urban ecology; this study shows it is not a clean global pattern once the analysis properly accounts for what regional species pools could ever have been shared.
  • Reveals a scale-dependent mechanism. Homogenization and differentiation are not opposites but coexist at different spatial scales — a finding that reframes how the impact of urbanization on biodiversity should be measured and reported.
  • Pins down the cause. The work isolates nonnative species composition (same vs. different nonnatives) as the lever, giving conservation and biosecurity policy a concrete target rather than a vague "urbanization" effect.
  • Methodological warning, broadly applicable. The "pool-controlled" framing (Fig. 6) is a transferable correction relevant to any beta-diversity comparison across regions, not just trees.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Study units
65 cities matched to 39 terrestrial ecoregions worldwide (Fig. 1). Each urban tree assemblage is paired with its surrounding ecoregion's regional species pool so that compositional dissimilarity is measured relative to what could have been shared.
Species occurrences
Tree occurrence records compiled and cleaned from large open biodiversity repositories — chiefly the Global Biodiversity Information Facility (GBIF) and the Botanical Information and Ecology Network (BIEN) — plus city/urban inventory sources, then filtered through the cleansing pipeline in Fig. 5.
Taxonomy / tree list
Species restricted to trees using GlobalTreeSearch (the global database of tree species and country distributions); names standardized for cross-source matching.
Ecoregion framework
Regional species pools delineated with the Terrestrial Ecoregions of the World map (Olson et al.), the basis for the 39-ecoregion controls.
Native / nonnative status
Each species classified as native or nonnative relative to its ecoregion, enabling the decomposition of βsim into native- vs. nonnative-driven similarity.
Core metric
Simpson's pairwise dissimilarity βsim; the analysis tracks the difference ∆βsim (observed vs. pool-expected) against geographic distance.

Core Methods

  • Occurrence cleansing pipeline (Fig. 5): harvest, deduplicate, taxonomically standardize, and quality-filter tree occurrence records from GBIF/BIEN and inventories before any comparison.
  • Species-pool control (Fig. 6): the conceptual core — compare observed urban-pair dissimilarity against the dissimilarity expected from the regional species pools, so the metric is not confounded by how different two regions' floras are to begin with.
  • Beta-diversity decomposition: compute Simpson's βsim and the deviation ∆βsim for urban–ecoregion pairs, then split contributions into native vs. nonnative components.
  • Distance-dependence analysis (Fig. 2): regress ∆βsim against geographic distance to detect the scale-dependent switch from short-distance differentiation to broad-distance homogenization.
  • Spatial / pathway diagnostics (Figs. 3–4): map the most contrasting urban–ecoregion pairs and trace plausible introduction pathways for native and nonnative species that produce the observed patterns.

① Make the scale-dependent switch a predictive, explainable model

Extending this paperThe paper documents that ∆βsim flips from differentiation at short distances to homogenization at broad distances, but the relationship in Fig. 2 stays descriptive. A direct extension: fit an explainable spatial model (e.g. gradient-boosted trees with SHAP, or MGWR) that predicts ∆βsim from distance, climate gap, economic ties, and propagule-pressure proxies, and locate the crossover distance where the sign of the effect changes. That turns a scatter pattern into a quantitative, transferable law with attributed drivers.
