Assessing MMR vaccination coverage gaps in US children with digital participatory surveillance

Eric Geng Zhou · John S. Brownstein · Benjamin Rader Published 2026-01-15 DOI 10.1038/s44360-025-00031-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Combining a digital participatory surveillance platform (OutbreaksNearMe, 22,062 parents) with multilevel small-area estimation, the study produces the first nationwide county-level map of ≥1-dose MMR coverage for US children under 5 — revealing dramatic geographic variation, including counties under 60%. Statistically significant clusters of undervaccination (West Texas, southern New Mexico, the rural Southeast) overlap closely with recent measles outbreaks, and the model estimates substantially lower coverage in Texas (62.9% vs 93.7% official) and New Mexico (61.6% vs 90.3%) — capturing children that school-entry and provider-verified systems systematically miss.

★

Why It Matters

  • Sees the populations official systems miss. Telephone surveys with provider verification and school-entry records systematically exclude homeschooled, uninsured, and foreign-born children — exactly the groups most prone to undervaccination. Parent-reported digital surveillance reaches them.
  • Granularity matters for measles. State-level figures (Texas reported 93.2% kindergarten MMR uptake) can hide local immunity gaps; this work resolves coverage to 3,109 counties and super-resolves to 29,971 ZCTAs, where outbreaks actually ignite.
  • Predicts where outbreaks emerge. Measles cases were more than twice as likely in "low–low" counties (low coverage surrounded by low-coverage neighbours), turning the map into an early-warning tool for targeted intervention.
  • Timely and scalable complement, not a replacement. Digital participatory data add timeliness, geographic resolution, and representativeness to vaccine registries and national surveys — and ship as a public dashboard (healthmap.org/measles).

Core Methods

  • Digital participatory surveillance: non-probability river sampling delivers an anonymous survey through SurveyMonkey's user pool; one eligible child randomly selected per household; sample weighted to US Census benchmarks (age, gender, race/ethnicity, education, geography).
  • Small-area estimation (MRP): spatial multilevel logistic regression with poststratification — random intercepts for county-like areas and states; individual covariates (age, gender, race/ethnicity) plus LASSO-selected county covariates; an iterative spatial aggregation algorithm merges sparse counties until ≥5 valid observations.
  • Uncertainty: Monte Carlo simulation with 1,000 replicates per county/state/subgroup to derive 95% CIs; state estimates are population-weighted averages of county estimates.
  • Spatial clustering: global Moran's I (= 0.53, P < 0.0001) plus Local Indicators of Spatial Association (LISA), queen contiguity, two-sided permutation test (499 permutations, P < 0.05 after Benjamini–Hochberg) classifying high–high / low–low / high–low / low–high counties.
  • AI super-resolution: Google PDFM embeddings (PCA components) added as auxiliary covariates to extend the MRP framework from counties to 29,971 ZCTAs.
  • Validation & benchmarking: agreement with direct survey estimates (county r = 0.84, R² = 0.71; state r = 0.88, R² = 0.77) and comparison against CDC provider-verified 36-month coverage with measles-case overlay; analyses in R, STROBE-compliant, IRB-approved.

① Fuse digital-surveillance coverage with healthcare-access supply to explain the gaps

Extending this paperThe paper maps where coverage is low but treats the cause largely through socioeconomic covariates. Extending it: layer a spatial-accessibility surface to pediatric/primary-care providers (a 2SFCA service-area model over the same counties/ZCTAs) as an additional covariate and mediator. This would separate "access deserts" (children who can't reach a provider) from "hesitancy clusters" (children whose parents decline), sharpening the intervention prescription the paper calls for — clinics vs outreach.

② Make the undervaccination map explainable, not just predictive

Extending this paperThe MRP model uses LASSO-selected covariates with global linear effects, so why a county lands in a low–low cluster is only partially legible. Extending it: refit the small-area layer with an interpretable spatial ML model (MGWR or an inherently interpretable gradient-boosting + SHAP-on-space) to expose spatially varying drivers — e.g., where income vs political vote share vs Medicaid dominates — turning a single set of coefficients into a place-specific causal narrative for targeting.

⑤ Probe and correct the access-driven bias in self-reported coverage

Extending this paperThe paper's strength — reaching children outside provider records — is also a measurement-bias risk: parent recall and who-answers-surveys both correlate with healthcare access. Extending it: run a differential-coverage / selection-bias audit comparing ONM, EHR-derived (Truveta-style), and CDC provider-verified estimates per stratum, then calibrate a fusion estimator that explicitly models access-conditioned missingness rather than assuming the survey weights remove it.
