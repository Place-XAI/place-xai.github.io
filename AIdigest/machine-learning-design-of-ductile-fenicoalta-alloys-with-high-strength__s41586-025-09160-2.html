Machine-learning design of ductile FeNiCoAlTa alloys with high strength

Yasir Sohail · Chongle Zhang · Dezhen Xue · Jinyu Zhang · Dongdong Zhang · Shaohua Gao · Yang Yang · Xiaoxuan Fan · Hang Zhang · Gang Liu · Jun Sun · En Ma Published 2025-06-18 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09160-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A domain knowledge-informed active learning pipeline navigated the ~1049-candidate composition space of multi-principal-element alloys to identify Fe35Ni29Co21Al12Ta3 — the first bulk alloy to simultaneously achieve near-2-GPa yield strength and 25% truly uniform elongation at room temperature. The secret is an extreme dual-precipitate microstructure: unusually high volume fractions of coherent L12 nanoprecipitates (~67 vol%) and deformable incoherent B2 microparticles (~15 vol%) that together sustain a work-hardening rate above 2 GPa throughout deformation, defeating the plastic instability that plagues all prior ultra-strong alloys and outperforming the best bulk steels.

★

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the 2-GPa ductility barrier. All prior bulk alloys near 2-GPa yield strength either display serrated plastic flow (Lüders/PLC bands) or severely limited uniform elongation (<4–13%). HEA05 delivers 1.75 GPa + 25% truly uniform elongation — a strength–ductility combination previously inaccessible for any bulk alloy.
  • Proves ML can design, not just predict. The 4-iteration active learning loop identified the optimal composition from ~1049 candidates using only 140 training alloys — a paradigm-shifting demonstration of data-efficient inverse design; 6 of 8 proposed alloys in each round exceeded prior training data.
  • Reveals a new work-hardening mechanism. Multicomponent B2 particles with reduced ordering energy act as deformable yet hard phases that accumulate dislocations internally, sustaining WHR above 2 GPa across all strains — overturning the conventional assumption that incoherent second phases must be brittle.
  • Tunable performance envelope. Processing alone (rolling reduction, aging temperature) traces a σy–εu ellipse from 1.5 GPa/31% to 1.95 GPa/15%, giving engineers a wide practical operating window around the same composition.
  • Transferable design strategy. The domain knowledge-constrained active learning approach and ductile-second-phase design principle are not specific to FeNiCoAlTa — they constitute a generalizable blueprint applicable to other alloy families.

Core Methods

  • Domain knowledge-informed active learning: 6-step iterative loop — training data collection → physical descriptor selection (6 of 20 features) → ML surrogate model training → domain-knowledge-constrained narrowing of virtual composition space → utility-function-guided candidate proposal → experimental validation and feedback (Supplementary Note 1).
  • Alloy fabrication and processing: arc melting under high-purity argon; homogenization at 1,250 °C / 2 h + water quench; cold rolling to ~81% thickness reduction; recrystallization at 1,100 °C / ~1 min; aging at 750 °C / 1 h (and other conditions explored). Dual-precipitate microstructure engineered by co-addition of Al (L12 and B2 stabilizer) and Ta (L12 APB energy enhancer, B2 ordering energy reducer).
  • Multi-scale microstructural characterization: STEM/HRTEM for nanoscale precipitate imaging; synchrotron SAXS for volume fraction; WAXS for interfacial mismatch strain; APT for elemental partitioning; EBSD + TKD for GND density mapping at sequential strain levels (0%, 8%, 20%).
  • Mechanical testing and work-hardening analysis: uniaxial tensile tests (engineering + true stress–strain); load–unload–reload for HDI hardening quantification; WHR (Θ) curves verified against Considère criterion to confirm truly uniform elongation.
  • Yield strength decomposition: quantitative σy contributions computed — coherent L12 precipitate hardening (~859 MPa), incoherent B2 barrier (~351 MPa), lattice friction (~277 MPa), stored dislocations (~206 MPa), grain boundaries (~167 MPa) — detailed in Supplementary Note 5.
  • First-principles / SQS calculations: chemical ordering energy and γAPB for multicomponent L12 and B2 phases computed with SQS models via RMC profile, showing Ta alloying reduces B2-γAPB by ~30% to render it deformable.

② Extend the active learning loop to predict microstructural volume fractions jointly with mechanical properties via multi-fidelity data fusion

Extending this paperThe current pipeline optimizes composition to maximize σy; the microstructural outcome (66.6% L12, 15% B2) is characterized experimentally after synthesis and not part of the ML objective. A powerful extension: add multi-fidelity surrogate targets — predicting dual-precipitate volume fractions and morphology alongside mechanical properties, using cheaper CALPHAD thermodynamics and DFT ordering energies as low-fidelity co-training signals. This would let the active learning loop directly navigate the dual-precipitate design space and propose compositions predicted to produce target phase fractions, rather than relying on post-hoc characterization to explain why a composition works.

④ Add feature attribution (SHAP / integrated gradients) to the alloy ML surrogate to extract interpretable structure–property rules

Extending this paperThe paper's ML model identifies the optimal composition but treats the surrogate as a performance-optimized black box — the 6 physical features are justified by prior domain knowledge, but which feature interactions actually dominate the σy predictions, and does the learned manifold align with the mechanistic story (APB energy, mismatch strain, B2 ductility)? Adding SHAP or integrated-gradient attribution would extract quantitative rules — e.g., "Ta > 2.5 at.% increases predicted σy via APB route, but only when Al > 10 at.%." Such rules become transferable design heuristics for other alloy families and make active learning guidance interpretable rather than oracle-like.
