Contribution of concrete nitrogenation to global NOx uptake

Ning Zhang · Georg Schiller · Hessam Azarijafari · Zhi Cao · Bin Xi · Yuli Shan · Huanyu Li · Huabo Duan · Jiakuan Yang Published 2024-06-14 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00084-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The world's enormous concrete stock can act as a passive NOx sink: by promoting nitrogenation of cementitious materials, the built environment could absorb roughly 3.4–6.9 Mt of NOx in 2021 — about 6–13% of global industry-related NOx emissions. Scaled across a global urbanization scenario to 2050, the technique could cumulatively sequester 131–384 Mt of NOx, yielding >150–160 trillion USD in economic value and avoiding ~75–260 years of disability-adjusted life-year (DALY) losses. Because carbonation and nitrogenation compete for the same alkaline reactive components, the authors find that in most regions nitrogenation is the higher-priority, more feasible option.

Why It Matters

  • Reframes a waste problem as an air-pollution solution. Billions of tons of concrete consumption and demolition waste are usually treated as a burden; this work shows the same material stock can be a distributed NOx scrubber with no new infrastructure footprint.
  • Quantifies a brand-new mitigation channel at global scale. Nitrogenation has been documented only in lab experiments; this is the first dynamic, worldwide estimate of its NOx-uptake, economic, and health potential out to 2050.
  • Couples material flows to human health. By translating NOx removal into DALYs and trillions of USD, it connects construction-material management directly to urban public-health outcomes.
  • Surfaces a real technology trade-off. Carbonation (CO2 uptake) and nitrogenation (NOx uptake) draw on the same finite alkaline reactivity; the paper gives planners a regional decision rule for which to prioritize.

Core Methods

  • Dynamic material-flow modeling: track concrete consumption, in-use stock, and demolition waste under a global urbanization scenario to 2050, defining the reactive surface available for gas uptake.
  • Reactive-uptake estimation: apply experimentally derived nitrogenation rates to the available alkaline reactive components, yielding NOx sequestration per unit material and per region.
  • Two-scenario analysis (2021): bound the economic and environmental benefits under contrasting deployment assumptions (Fig. 2), giving the 3.4–6.9 Mt range.
  • Long-term projection & valuation: integrate NOx uptake over 2021–2050 (Figs. 3–5) and convert to economic value (USD) and health benefit (DALYs avoided).
  • Carbonation vs. nitrogenation competition model: because both reactions consume the same alkaline reactivity, the paper compares their benefits region-by-region (Fig. 6) to derive a prioritization rule.
  • Uncertainty handling: results reported as ranges, with the authors explicitly noting significant remaining uncertainty in the underlying chemistry and scenarios.

① Spatialize the regional NOx sink onto a fine-grained building-stock map

Extending this paperThe paper resolves NOx uptake only to broad regions and treats concrete stock as an aggregate. That converts a region-mean estimate into a street-scale uptake surface where stock geometry and exposure co-vary, sharpening both the chemistry and the health attribution.
