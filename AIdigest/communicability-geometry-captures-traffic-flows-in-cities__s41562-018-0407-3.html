Communicability geometry captures traffic flows in cities

Meisam Akbarzadeh · Ernesto Estrada Published 2018-08-20 DOI 10.1038/s41562-018-0407-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Vehicular traffic in cities does not primarily flow along topological shortest paths. Instead, in four cities spanning four countries (Anaheim USA, Winnipeg Canada, Barcelona Spain, Isfahan Iran), communicability shortest paths — derived from a diffusion-like "all-routes" model rooted in Burgers' equation — carry significantly higher average traffic flows (up to 38.7% more in Anaheim). The explanation: shortest paths pass through highly central intersections that drivers perceive as "too central to be empty," so they collectively adopt alternative routes that resemble subdiffusive motion on the street graph.

★

Why It Matters

  • Overturns a long-standing assumption. The dominance of shortest paths is the backbone of betweenness centrality and "network efficiency" — this paper provides direct observational evidence that traffic avoids shortest paths in all four cities studied.
  • Provides a physics derivation, not just a heuristic. The communicability function is grounded in an adaptation of Burgers' equation to graph diffusion with intersection waiting times, connecting network topology to a macroscopic continuum traffic model.
  • Explains subdiffusive urban traffic. The model predicts that rush-hour traffic should be subdiffusive — consistent with independent empirical observations for Istanbul — linking city-scale behavior to statistical physics.
  • Generalizes across 13 cities on 5 continents. The centrality analysis (shortest paths are more central than communicability paths) holds universally across Anaheim, Ankara, Barcelona, Dongguan, Gold Coast, Hessen, Isfahan, Milan, Philadelphia, Quanzhou, Rome, Winnipeg, and Zhengzhou.
  • Actionable for congestion management. Communicability betweenness — not traditional betweenness — is the right tool for identifying true traffic hotspots, with direct implications for signal timing, road design, and navigation routing.

Core Methods

  • Coarse-grained fluid dynamics → graph communicability: Burgers' equation (with diffusive term) is adapted to a network graph via the Cole–Hopf transformation, yielding a heat equation on the graph Laplacian. Adding intersection waiting-time self-loops (τ ≫ kmax) makes the graph degree-regular, and the solution reduces to exp(A) — the communicability matrix.
  • Communicability distance: ξpq = √(Gpp + Gqq − 2Gpq), where G = exp(A) is the adjacency-matrix exponential. Proven to be a Euclidean distance that embeds nodes on an (n−1)-sphere, avoiding high-subgraph-centrality nodes.
  • Communicability shortest path (CSP): Dijkstra algorithm applied to the communicability-weighted directed adjacency matrix W = A ○ M′, where M′ is the matrix of communicability distances. Respects street direction; filters path pairs that differ by more than 67% of edges from shortest path.
  • Static traffic assignment: User-equilibrium flow assignment (Nash equilibrium) on O-D matrices using standard transportation engineering procedures; results validated against empirical observed flows.
  • Centrality analysis: Five measures (degree, closeness, betweenness, eigenvector, subgraph centrality) computed on undirected network versions; average centrality along each path compared across 30,073 path pairs in 13 cities.
  • Theoretical limit: Analytically proven that as congestion → 0, CSPs converge to topological shortest paths — unifying the model under both regimes.

① Replace betweenness centrality with communicability betweenness for congestion hotspot mapping

Extending this paperThe paper's central finding — that traditional betweenness centrality identifies intersections that are "too central to be empty" and therefore avoided — immediately implies that standard congestion hotspot maps built on betweenness centrality are systematically wrong. A direct extension: compute communicability betweenness on the 13-city dataset and compare which intersections are identified as hotspots versus traditional betweenness. The paper's own statistical distribution result (betweenness is higher on shortest paths in 79.4% of cases) provides the baseline to measure the gap. This reframing would sharpen the paper's policy implication from "communicability is better" to "here is the specific error made by current practice."

Replacing the kernel's decay function with a communicability-distance metric — in the spirit of this paper — would capture the true "reachability" of intersections under diffusion-like urban flow rather than shortest-path assumptions. ② Use deep learning on street-network imagery to predict communicability-path traffic without O-D surveys

Extending this paperThe paper's CSP model requires a full O-D matrix — expensive to collect in most cities — and then uses static user-equilibrium assignment as a proxy for observed flows. A natural extension: train a deep learning model on satellite/street-view imagery of the 13 cities where the paper already computed CSP-based centrality metrics, learning a direct mapping from visual urban morphology to communicability-flow distributions. This would let the model's geometry-driven traffic prediction transfer to cities lacking O-D surveys, vastly scaling the paper's reach beyond the handful of cities with transportation network data.

Extending this paperThe paper studies single-mode vehicular traffic on undirected/directed street graphs. A critical extension: apply the communicability shortest-path framework to multimodal networks (street + metro + bus + cycling layers) and test whether the "all-routes" diffusion model still outperforms shortest-path predictions when passengers can switch modes at transfer nodes. ④ Couple the communicability geometry with urban morphology to explain cross-city variation in traffic avoidance

Extending this paperThe paper reports markedly different "avoidance rates" across cities: Anaheim shows CSP dominance in 94.1% of paths vs. only 59.6% in Winnipeg. The paper attributes this variation loosely to city size and density but offers no systematic explanation. A direct extension: use the paper's communicability embedding of all 13 street networks to quantify how grid regularity, street hierarchy, and physical geographic fragmentation (natural barriers that detour routes) predict the degree of CSP-vs-SP traffic divergence. The communicability hypersphere radius itself — a summary of network topology — could serve as a single predictor of how strongly a city's traffic deviates from shortest-path assumptions.

⑤ Build a physics-informed graph neural network that encodes the communicability operator as a learnable layer

Extending this paperThe communicability matrix G = exp(A) is costly to compute for large cities (O(n³) via matrix exponentiation) and must be recomputed each time the network changes. A scalable extension: replace the exact exp(A) with a physics-informed GNN layer that approximates the matrix exponential using a truncated Chebyshev polynomial expansion on the adjacency eigenspectrum, encoding the paper's coarse-grained Burgers'-equation derivation as a soft constraint in the loss function. This would allow the communicability geometry to be applied to dynamic, evolving road networks in near-real-time — for instance as road closures or signal changes alter the adjacency matrix during events.

Added value to my researchThis is the core of my physics-informed graph learning agenda. Encoding the communicability operator — derived from first-principles traffic physics — as a GNN inductive bias would give my spatiotemporal graph models a principled structural prior beyond the standard graph convolution. Applied to healthcare facility access networks, this means the learned adjacency respects the physics of how patients actually navigate road networks under congestion, directly improving the spatial fidelity of my 2SFCA catchment delineation.
