Sources of organizational variability in fatal police shootings in the USA

Roland Neil · John M. MacDonald · Anthony A. Braga Published 2025-04-11 DOI 10.1038/s41562-025-02169-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Fatal police shooting rates are far from random across 2,727 US police agencies studied from 2015 to 2020: they are primarily explained by differences in social context — especially crime rates, 911 call rates, officers per capita, and gun prevalence — rather than by racial/ethnic composition or most organizational policies. Yet even after accounting for context, a set of persistent outlier agencies consistently deviate from predictions, and if all agencies performed like the best-performing ones the model implies a potential 40% reduction in annual fatal police shootings nationally.

Why It Matters

  • Shifts the conversation from individuals to organizations. Most prior research focuses on officer or victim characteristics; this study is among the first at national scale to partition agency-level variation into randomness, social context, and persistent organizational outliers.
  • Largest multi-agency sample to date. At 2,727 agencies and 16,362 agency-year observations (2015–2020), the study provides a nationally representative picture that far exceeds earlier multi-agency analyses.
  • Quantifies the policy leverage. Reducing 911 call rates to the lowest decile is estimated to avert 45% of shootings; if all agencies performed like the best outliers, roughly 600 people per year might avoid death — a 40% annual reduction.
  • Identifies actionable outliers. The Bayesian framework flags 50 outlier agencies (approx. 25 high, 25 low) whose performance cannot be explained by context, giving policymakers concrete targets for case-study investigation and reform.
  • Nuances the role of race. Agency-level racial composition is not strongly associated with aggregate shooting rates — which, rather than minimizing the racial dimension, underscores that reducing overall shooting levels is itself a critical mechanism for protecting Black lives (a 42% reduction would match the equity gain from eliminating racial disparity in who gets shot).

Core Methods

  • Bayesian multilevel Poisson models (5 nested): residential population as offset; models build sequentially from a naive random-distribution baseline (Model 1) through agency random intercepts (Model 2), socio-demographics (Model 3), dangerous-interaction predictors — crime, guns, 911 calls, officers per capita (Model 4), and organizational factors (Model 5).
  • Variance decomposition via random-intercept variance (τ₀₀): tracking how τ₀₀ decreases from 0.90 (Model 2) to 0.16 (Model 5) quantifies how much explained variance each covariate block adds.
  • Posterior predictive checks: 4,000 simulated distributions compared to observed data on three statistics (zero-inflation rate, maximum value, variance); calibration target near 0.5.
  • Leave-one-out cross-validation (LOOIC / ELPD): model comparison metric asymptotically equivalent to WAIC but more robust in smaller samples; Model 4 beats Model 3 by 4.5σ, confirming that dangerous-interaction predictors are key.
  • Counterfactual simulations: covariates set to their 10th percentile to estimate predicted reductions in shootings (e.g., 45% reduction if 911 call rates at minimum decile; 29.9% if gun assaults at minimum).
  • Outlier identification: agencies whose posterior random-intercept distribution falls ≥90% to one side of zero are flagged as consistent high or low outliers (50 flagged total).
  • Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (Stan in R): 4 chains × 1,000 warm-up + 1,000 sampling iterations; convergence assessed via R-hat, effective sample size (ESS), and trace plots; weakly informative priors scaled to outcome standard deviation.
  • Missing data: k-nearest-neighbours imputation (k = 5) for variables with <11% missingness.

① Spatially explicit modeling of outlier clusters with GeoAI

Extending this paperThe outlier map (Fig. 4) locates anomalous agencies but the paper's model has no spatial structure — agencies are treated as independent units. Yet the paper itself notes that some outliers sit adjacent to each other (Riverside vs. Orange County; Phoenix vs. Dallas), implying spatial clustering of unexplained residuals. A direct extension: apply spatially explicit priors (CAR/ICAR) or GeoAI methods (MGWR, graph neural networks with geographic attention) to the agency-level random intercepts, testing whether unobserved neighborhood-level factors — land-use mix, transit connectivity, socioeconomic gradients — mediate spatial autocorrelation among outliers beyond what Model 5 captures.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely within my explainable ML and MGWR agenda: modeling spatially non-stationary residuals from a hierarchical model is a regime where GWR and geographically weighted neural networks add direct value. The paper's random intercepts could serve as a spatially resolved outcome for an R2SFCA-style decomposition — framing police-service accessibility (response time, coverage per 10,000 residents) as a predictor of the outlier score and exposing spatial access inequities in policing.

② Integrate urban gun-violence scaling laws to separate context from policing culture

Extending this paperThe paper's strongest predictor, gun prevalence, is an aggregate county-level proxy that cannot distinguish the spatial concentration of guns (an urban-scaling phenomenon: gun violence scales superlinearly with city size) from the cultural willingness to confront police. Extending this paper: merge city-level gun-ecosystem data from urban scaling studies with the agency-level residuals from Model 5, testing whether agencies that are gun-dense relative to their expected urban-scaling benchmark deviate systematically from predictions. This would sharpen the causal interpretation of the gun-prevalence coefficient and explain part of the outlier variance not captured by LEMAS.

Added value to my researchUrban scaling laws provide a physics-informed prior: expected gun violence is a power-law function of city population. Incorporating this into a spatiotemporal kernel density model of gun-incident hotspots aligns with my spatiotemporal KDE methods and could generate density surfaces that feed back as context variables into the multilevel model — a full GeoAI loop from data to model to policy.

③ Replace the static LEMAS snapshot with longitudinal organizational trajectories

Extending this paperThe 2016 LEMAS survey is treated as time-invariant across 2015–2020. This is a known limitation: departments adopted body-worn cameras and shed unions at different rates during the study period, and the body-worn-camera finding (linked to ~14.8% fewer shootings) deserves temporal treatment. A natural extension: exploit multiple LEMAS waves or state-level administrative records to build a longitudinal panel of organizational change, then use a difference-in-differences or interrupted-time-series design nested within the same Bayesian Poisson framework — converting the associational claim about body-worn cameras into a closer causal approximation.

Added value to my researchFusing periodic survey snapshots with annual administrative records is the same challenge I face integrating ACS waves and mobile-phone trajectories for accessibility modeling: cross-temporal data fusion under mismatched sampling frequencies. My multi-source data-fusion pipeline (mobile phone + census) could be adapted to harmonize irregular LEMAS waves into continuous organizational trajectories, and the quasi-experimental design mirrors my interrupted-time-series work on public-facility policy impacts.

④ Model inter-agency peer influence through officer-mobility networks

Extending this paperThe paper treats agencies as independent units connected only through the grand intercept. Yet officers transfer between agencies, share regional training academies, and participate in inter-agency task forces — forming a latent organizational network. Extending it: use officer-movement records or shared-academy affiliation data to build an inter-agency network, then test whether the random intercepts from Model 5 exhibit network autocorrelation beyond geographic proximity. If a policing culture is socially transmitted through officer mobility, agencies that are network-neighbors should have correlated residuals — a hypothesis the current non-network model cannot test.

Added value to my researchNetwork community detection is one of my core methods. Identifying clusters of agencies with correlated random intercepts via network analysis is analogous to detecting job-housing or urban-regional economic communities. Coupling detected communities with physics-informed graph learning would let me build a GNN predicting an agency's shooting propensity from its position in the officer-mobility network — a GeoAI complement to the regression framework that transfers learned policing-culture representations across unseen agencies.

⑤ Test whether city-level implicit racial bias mediates the residual outlier deviation

Extending this paperThe paper deliberately excludes racial bias, noting that aggregate racial composition does not strongly predict shooting rates. But implicit racial bias — measurable at the metropolitan level via validated behavioral tasks (Project Implicit) — is a distinct construct from residential composition. Extending the paper: link city-level implicit-bias scores to the agency-level random intercepts from Model 5, testing whether agencies embedded in more biased metros have higher residual shooting rates after controlling for all of Model 5's predictors. This would provide the individual-level attitudinal mechanism the paper explicitly avoids, and clarify which part of the outlier variance reflects organizational culture vs. ambient community attitudes.

Added value to my researchBridging aggregate bias scores to spatially explicit outcomes connects to my health-geography work on racial disparities in healthcare access. The multilevel structure — city-level cultural context modulating agency-level behavior — mirrors my analysis of telehealth adoption across metros with varying digital-divide intensity. Framing cultural racism as a spatial contextual covariate (a raster or choropleth layer) opens the door to R2SFCA-style models of police-service equity weighted by community trust, combining criminology with my GeoAI accessibility framework.
