Health and climate impacts of future United States land freight modelled with global-to-urban models

Liang Liu · Taesung Hwang · Sungwon Lee · Yanfeng Ouyang · Bumsoo Lee · Steven J. Smith · Christopher W. Tessum · Julian D. Marshall · Fang Yan · Kathryn Daenzer · Tami C. Bond Published 2019-02-11 DOI 10.1038/s41893-019-0224-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using a system-of-systems approach spanning economy, technology, infrastructure, and urban form, this study projects US freight truck and rail emissions and their health and climate impacts from 2010 to 2050 under multiple policy scenarios. While emission standards will dramatically cut air-pollutant emissions and premature deaths by 2030, long-lived CO2 forcing will continue to grow if petroleum remains the dominant fuel. Among all policies tested, carbon taxation produces the largest climate benefit (24% reduction in long-lived forcing via a truck-to-rail modal shift), eliminating high-emitting trucks yields the largest mortality reduction (36%), and compact urban development offers modest but positive health co-benefits (13% reduction in urban-delivery mortalities).

Why It Matters

  • First unified triple-impact assessment for US freight. No prior study had simultaneously projected mortality from PM2.5, short-lived climate forcing, and long-lived CO2 forcing for the entire US truck-and-rail system through 2050.
  • Quantifies the urban compactness dividend for freight. Denser urban form cuts short-haul delivery distances but concentrates exposure — the paper disentangles these countervailing effects, showing net health benefits of compact cities over sprawl (13% reduction in urban delivery mortalities).
  • Reveals an emissions-health policy mismatch. Carbon taxes are best for climate but not for health; maintenance enforcement is best for health. Without this integrated view, policy would optimize one axis at the expense of others.
  • Identifies high-emitting trucks as the dominant near-term health risk. By 2050 these account for >70% of PM2.5 from short-haul freight while consuming only 5% of fuel — a striking non-linearity invisible to aggregate models.
  • Provides a replicable, open methodological scaffold. The modular system-of-systems architecture (economy → spatial → vehicle fleet → air quality → health/climate) can be adapted to other freight sectors, fuels, or countries.

Continental United States, divided into 120 Freight Analysis Zones (FAZs) defined by the US Federal Highway Administration Freight Analysis Framework (FAF3), with sub-FAZ resolution at the census-tract level in 73 metropolitan FAZs (those with >500,000 people).
Freight demand
Freight Analysis Framework 3 (FAF3) — FHWA, 2011 (ops.fhwa.dot.gov). Provides commodity flow data between FAZs. Supplemented by a general equilibrium model and shift-share model to project demand to 2050.
Vehicle fleet & emissions
SPEW-Trend vehicle fleet model — tracks vintage technologies, degradation, retirement, and technology-slip to estimate speciated pollutant emissions (PM2.5, NOx, CO, VOCs, NH3, SOx) for each scenario. Validated against EMFAC2014 (California Air Resources Board, 2015; arb.ca.gov/emfac/2014) for urban truck mileage.

InMAP (Intervention Model for Air Pollution) — reduced-form model using preprocessed WRF-Chem meteorology; variable-resolution grid (1–48 km edge length); calibrated against COBRA, APEEP/AP2, and EASIUR. Covers the contiguous US.

Background all-cause mortality from CDC 2014 county data. Concentration-response coefficient: relative risk = 1.06 per 10 µg m-3 PM2.5.
Time window
Base year 2010 (with 2007 calibration for EMFAC); projections to 2030 and 2050 under four macroeconomic and three urban development scenarios.

Core Methods

  • General equilibrium & shift-share downscaling: a national-level general equilibrium model projects economic activity by commodity; a shift-share model disaggregates national growth to the 120 FAZ level to generate freight demand.
  • Four-step freight demand forecasting: a multiregional input-output model derives regional commodity production/consumption; a four-step demand framework generates origin-destination freight flows; a modal-shift model allocates truck vs. rail based on oil price and carbon tax; traffic equilibrium assigns flows to networks.
  • Urban short-haul modelling: in 73 metropolitan FAZs, a dynamic spatial density-gradient model projects census-tract employment distributions under three urban form scenarios (trend, polycentric, compact); an asymptotic vehicle routing model estimates total short-haul delivery distance from employment locations.
  • Vehicle fleet emissions (SPEW-Trend): models vintage-technology composition, emission degradation with vehicle age, early retirement, and technology-slip (transition to high-emitting state); projects speciated emissions (PM2.5, NOx, CO, VOCs, NH3, SOx) annually from 2010 to 2050.
  • Reduced-form air quality (InMAP): combines preprocessed WRF-Chem meteorology with a variable-resolution Eulerian grid to estimate annual PM2.5 concentrations from primary and secondary precursor emissions, orders of magnitude faster than full CTMs.
  • Health impact assessment: log-linear concentration-response function (RR = 1.06 per 10 µg m-3 PM2.5) applied to grid-cell populations and county background mortality rates; results cross-validated against COBRA, APEEP/AP2, and EASIUR.
  • Climate impact assessment: radiative forcing (TW-yr) computed using forcing-per-emission literature values for both short-lived species (BC, OC, sulfate, NOx-ozone, CO-methane) and long-lived CO2 (AGWP over 100 years).

① Replace the fixed carbon-tax modal-shift model with a learned policy that adapts to city-level freight demand patterns

Extending this paperThe paper's modal-shift model assigns truck vs. rail based on oil price and a carbon tax parameter calibrated at the national level. This treats the freight system as a monolithic cost minimizer, ignoring local infrastructure constraints, shipper heterogeneity, and the spatial configuration of rail access near urban cores. A direct extension of this paper would recast modal choice as a spatially varying, data-driven policy function learned from observed Origin-Destination freight flows — allowing the scenario engine to reflect that a carbon tax has very different modal-shift elasticity in rail-rich Midwest corridors versus rail-sparse Sun Belt metros. Coupling this with the paper's existing urban-form scenarios would reveal whether compact cities with better rail access produce disproportionately larger health co-benefits than the current framework suggests.

Added value to my researchThis resonates directly with my work on spatially varying regression (MGWR/GWR): the modal-shift elasticity is a spatially non-stationary coefficient rather than a global constant. Treating it as a locally weighted function — estimated from disaggregate shipment data — would be a natural extension of my geospatial methods toolkit. It also connects to my interest in network community detection to partition the US freight network into regionally coherent markets where modal substitution is actually feasible.

② Integrate electric vehicle and grid-decarbonization pathways into the paper's health-climate trade-off surface

Extending this paperThe paper explicitly acknowledges that it excludes alternative fuels and vehicle electrification — yet these are now the dominant near-term policy levers. A key extension of this framework is to add a fourth scenario axis: electrification of short-haul delivery fleets paired with the paper's three urban-form scenarios, where health impacts depend on the carbon intensity of the local electricity grid. The paper's InMAP infrastructure and triple-impact accounting are fully compatible with this extension; one needs only to replace diesel emission factors with electricity-generation emission surrogates distributed to power-plant locations. The existing finding that urban delivery contributes 30% of population-weighted PM2.5 despite being only 6% of area-weighted concentration makes urban fleet electrification the highest-leverage intervention the paper has not yet evaluated.

③ Apply explainable machine learning to identify which spatial features most amplify the marginal health impact of urban freight emissions

Extending this paperTable 1 in the paper shows that the marginal health impact of primary PM2.5 from urban freight is 7-8 times higher than from rural freight, and that this gap grows 30-50% by 2050 with population growth. But the paper cannot explain which spatial attributes of a census tract — proximity to highways, green space, building density, commuter flow — drive this amplification beyond simple population density. A direct extension would use the paper's per-grid-cell mortality outputs as labels in a spatially explicit SHAP analysis (Shapley additive explanations on a MGWR or ensemble model) trained on tract-level built-environment variables, revealing the spatial signatures of disproportionate freight health impact.

④ Extend the paper's urban-form scenarios to quantify environmental justice — who bears the residual health burden under each policy?

Extending this paperThe paper reports aggregate mortalities and population-weighted PM2.5, but is silent on the distributional dimension: does the carbon-tax scenario reduce mortality uniformly, or does it shift burden toward lower-income census tracts near rail yards? Does the compact-city scenario increase exposure for those already living in dense, disadvantaged neighborhoods? An extension of this paper would disaggregate the per-grid-cell mortality output by census-tract socioeconomic quintile (income, race, housing tenure) to produce a distributional health-impact matrix across the policy scenarios, directly operationalizing environmental justice analysis within the paper's existing framework.

⑤ Use the paper's technology-slip finding to design a spatially targeted inspection regime via a physics-informed graph model of the truck fleet network

Extending this paperThe paper's most striking finding is that high-emitting trucks (technology-slip vehicles) account for over 70% of PM2.5 emissions from short-haul freight while representing only 5% of fuel use by 2050. The paper's no-technology-slip scenario simply removes these trucks wholesale — but a practical extension asks: where in the road network should inspections or retrofit incentives be targeted to achieve the same health gain at minimum cost? Modelling the truck fleet as a spatiotemporal network where nodes are FAZ terminals and edge weights are emission intensities would allow a physics-informed graph learning model to identify inspection chokepoints (e.g., freight corridors entering dense metro areas) that maximally reduce population-weighted PM2.5 with minimal inspection effort.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my physics-informed graph learning research line: the emission-transport relationship (how diesel exhaust disperses from a road segment to surrounding census tracts) provides a physically grounded message-passing rule for a graph neural network operating on the freight network topology. Coupling this with spatiotemporal kernel density estimation of truck routes from GPS data would make the framework empirically grounded and deployable by fleet regulators.
