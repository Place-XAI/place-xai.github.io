Potential drivers of urban green space availability in Latin American cities

Maryia Bakhtsiyarava · Mika Moran · Yang Ju · Yuquan Zhou · Daniel A. Rodriguez · Iryna Dronova · Maria de Fatima R. P. de Pina · Vanderlei Pascoal de Matos · Daniel Albert Skaba Published 2024-11-06 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00162-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 371 large cities in 11 Latin American countries, urban green space (UGS) is highly heterogeneous and inequitably distributed. Random forest analysis shows that climate zone — an essentially unmodifiable factor — is by far the strongest driver of green-space quantity (NDVI, % green area, per-capita area), while education, street-intersection density and population density dominate urban park availability. Arid cities have the least vegetation yet the most parks per capita. Because climate so strongly constrains greenness, cities have unequal capacity to deploy nature-based solutions for heat mitigation and health promotion.

Why It Matters

  • First region-wide, large-sample evidence base for an understudied region. Most Latin American UGS research has focused on a handful of mega-cities (São Paulo, Buenos Aires, Santiago); this is the first representative description spanning 371 cities and 11 countries (>247 million people).
  • Separates the modifiable from the unmodifiable. By ranking climate against built-environment and socioeconomic drivers, it tells planners where greening is feasible versus where climate sets a hard ceiling — reframing expectations for nature-based heat mitigation.
  • Distinguishes "greenness" from "parks". Vegetation-based metrics (NDVI, % green area) and recreational urban parks are driven by different forces and are not interchangeable — a methodological warning for any equity or health study that uses a single green metric.
  • Surfaces equity paradoxes. Evidence of a U-shaped greenness–GDP relationship and high greenness in some disadvantaged cities challenge the simple "deprivation amplification" narrative and warn that more green is not automatically better if it is unmanaged.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Spatial coverage
371 large cities (≥100,000 residents) in 11 Latin American countries, representing >247 million people. Unit of analysis is each city's urban extent (built-up area) within administrative boundaries, delineated by the SALURBAL project from the 12-m Global Urban Footprint via spatial clustering.
Greenness (NDVI)
MODIS Terra MOD13Q1.006 product, 250-m, 16-day; annual-maximum NDVI for 2017, spatial median per city, water masked. City NDVI ranged 0.10–0.80 (mean 0.53).
Green-space land cover & configuration
10-m Sentinel-2 TOA imagery (2017), supervised classification per city (Ju et al., overall accuracy 0.76–0.96); resampled to 30 m for landscape metrics (% green area, patch density, clumpiness, nearest-neighbor distance). Green coverage ranged 4–72% (mean 41%); ~123,220 m² green space per 1,000 residents.
Urban parks
Web-mined Google geotagged "park"-category locations, filtered with NDVI (900-m buffer) and delimited by OpenStreetMap street network; validated against official park data in 77 cities. Mean ~2,142 m² park area per 1,000 residents. Park data are not publicly available (Google terms of use).
City characteristics
Natural (climate zone, terrain, coastal/inland), built (population, population density, intersection density, area) and socioeconomic (GDP per capita, education, unemployment), compiled by SALURBAL from satellite, census and survey data; socioeconomic years vary by country (earliest 2005, Guatemala).
Time window
Greenness/land cover 2017; parks 2020; socioeconomic varies (2005 onward). Temporal misalignment noted as a limitation; NDVI change 2005–2017 was small.
Availability
All data except urban parks are public via the SALURBAL data platform; park data by data-use agreement (SALURBAL.data@drexel.edu). Analysis code at GitHub.

Core Methods

  • Multi-metric green-space quantification: three complementary measures — satellite NDVI (greenness), supervised land-cover-derived % green area and per-capita area, and web-mined urban parks — capturing distinct planetary-health vs recreational dimensions.
  • Landscape configuration metrics: patch density, clumpiness and mean nearest-neighbor distance describe fragmentation, aggregation and isolation of green space.
  • Descriptive group comparisons: green-space indicators stratified by tertiles/categories of natural, built and socioeconomic environment; ANOVA with post-hoc Tukey HSD for group-mean differences.
  • Random forest regression (variable importance): non-parametric ensemble explained 65% of NDVI, 41% of % green area, 63% of per-capita green area variance; permutation-based importance ranks climate vs built vs socioeconomic drivers; partial-dependence plots visualize relationships.
  • Sensitivity checks: adjusted multivariable linear regressions, country random-intercept models, and continuous-predictor / interaction specifications corroborate the descriptive and RF findings.

① From availability to equitable accessibility of parks

Extending this paperThe authors explicitly note their city-level metrics "do not easily generalize to smaller, within-city scales" needed to identify UGS inequalities and accessibility, and that intersection density (a proxy for reachability) often diverges from greenness. A natural extension is to push their web-mined park layer down to the neighborhood level and compute true catchment-based access — who can actually reach a green park on foot — rather than per-capita counts, separating formal from informal settlements.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct target for my 2SFCA/R2SFCA toolkit: treat parks as "supply", population grids as "demand", and OSM walking networks as the cost surface to map green-space service areas and shortages — extending my facility-access work from clinics to environmental amenities.

② Open the random forest with explainable spatial ML

Extending this paperThe paper uses permutation importance and partial-dependence plots, which capture global, average effects but hide where and for which city types a driver matters. Extending it with SHAP interaction values and geographically aware ML would reveal local, non-stationary driver effects (e.g., GDP mattering in arid but not tropical cities) and quantify the U-shaped greenness–GDP relationship they observe.

Added value to my researchSquarely my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction line: pairing tree ensembles with SHAP and MGWR to disentangle spatially varying drivers, here applied to environmental-equity outcomes instead of health-service access.

Cross-ref (corpus): Green spaces provide substantial but unequal urban cooling globally — 10.1038/s41467-024-51355-0

③ Couple green space to a health-outcome model

Extending this paperThe study deliberately stops at green-space drivers and notes it "did not include health data," repeatedly flagging heat-mortality, hypertension and obesity as downstream concerns. The obvious extension is to join its city-level UGS metrics to SALURBAL's mortality/morbidity records and estimate climate-conditioned dose–response curves — testing whether equal greenness yields unequal health benefits across climate zones.

Added value to my researchThis bridges to my health-geography agenda: a space–human coupling model in which green-space exposure becomes a covariate in spatial mental-health and chronic-disease accessibility models, supporting urban-health policy.

Cross-ref (corpus): The global burden of hypertension preventable by urban greenness — 10.1038/s44360-026-00090-5

④ Add the cooling-service layer to interpret arid-city parks

Extending this paperA central puzzle in the paper is that arid cities have the least vegetation yet the most parks per capita, and the authors speculate about purposeful investment and ecosystem (dis)services. Extending it with land-surface-temperature and humid-heat data would let one test whether those arid-city parks actually deliver cooling, turning a speculative explanation into a measured ecosystem-service outcome along a wetness gradient.

Added value to my researchFeeds my multi-source remote-sensing fusion work: stacking NDVI, LST and population to model the efficiency-vs-equity of green cooling — directly analogous to my EV-charging efficiency/equity optimization, but for nature-based solutions.

Cross-ref (corpus): Regulation of humid heat by urban green space across a climate wetness gradient — 10.1038/s44284-024-00157-y

⑤ Test green gentrification & equity dynamics over time

Extending this paperThe paper's snapshot reveals that some advantaged cities have less-fragmented, clustered green space and finds nonlinear socioeconomic gradients — but it cannot say whether greening causes displacement. Extending it longitudinally with the available 2005–2017 NDVI trend and SALURBAL socioeconomic panels would let one test green-gentrification dynamics: does new green space track, or push out, disadvantaged residents?
