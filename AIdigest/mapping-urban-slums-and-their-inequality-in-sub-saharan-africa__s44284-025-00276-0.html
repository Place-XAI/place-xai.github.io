Mapping urban slums and their inequality in sub-Saharan Africa

Chengxiu Li · Le Yu · Robert Ndugwa · Adrian J. Bailey · Xiaoling Zhang · Francis Oloo · Ellasy Gulule Chimimba · Xiangzheng Deng · Jim Wright Published 2025-08-08 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00276-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

By fusing open satellite/geospatial data with household surveys, the authors map slum prevalence and asset-based wealth across 32 sub-Saharan African countries and find that 54.6% of the urban population live in slums with markedly lower wealth than non-slum areas. Even though the proportion of slum dwellers fell over the past two decades, wealth inequality rose—especially in countries with large slum populations—revealing that aggregate progress masks deepening intra-urban divides in essential services, living conditions, and household wealth.

★

Why It Matters

  • Makes an invisible quarter of humanity measurable. Slums house nearly a quarter of the world's urban population, yet are systematically undercounted; this work delivers a fine-scale, multi-country map where official statistics are sparse or absent.
  • Separates "fewer slums" from "less inequality." The headline finding—declining slum share but rising wealth inequality—warns that the SDG framing of "proportion living in slums" can hide worsening within-city deprivation.
  • Operationalizes "leave no one behind." Sub-pixel, asset-based wealth estimates let policymakers target the poorest neighborhoods rather than treating cities as uniform.
  • Fully open and reproducible. Code plus 79 geospatial datasets on Google Earth Engine and Zenodo make the slum maps, wealth index, and population estimates auditable and extensible by others.

Core Methods

  • Slum mapping (deep learning): classify urban areas into slum / non-slum from fused Sentinel-1 + Sentinel-2 plus environmental features (land cover, LST, topography), trained against a slum sampling dataset.
  • Asset-based wealth prediction: machine-learning model maps the DHS wealth index from geospatial covariates to a fine spatial grid, enabling within-city wealth surfaces.
  • Inequality assessment: compare wealth and access to essential services between slum and non-slum areas and compute a slum-severity / SSI measure plus inequality trends.
  • Population estimation: combine slum maps with gridded population to estimate slum population shares and changes (2005–2022), benchmarked against UN estimates.
  • Accuracy assessment: validate slum classification and wealth prediction (Fig. 3), with R code provided for reproducible accuracy and trend analysis.

① Make the slum classifier explainable, not just accurate

Extending this paperThe paper validates slum mapping with an accuracy assessment (Fig. 3) but treats the deep-learning classifier largely as a black box. A direct extension: attach explainable-ML attribution (SHAP / feature importance, spatial cross-validation) to expose which Sentinel-1/2, LST, and topography signals actually define "slum-ness" in each country, and audit where the model transfers poorly across the 32 nations—turning a single accuracy number into a transparent, transferability map.

③ Fuse phone and social signals to sharpen the wealth surface

Extending this paperThe wealth index is anchored to DHS surveys, which are infrequent and spatially displaced for privacy—limiting temporal resolution between survey waves. Extending it: fuse mobile-phone CDRs and social-media footprints with the satellite features to interpolate wealth between DHS years and downscale below cluster level, then re-estimate the 2005–2022 inequality trend at finer cadence.

Added value to my researchThis is my multi-source data-fusion line: phone/social/remote-sensing fusion that I apply to mobility and health now becomes a poverty-nowcasting engine, with the paper's open DHS+satellite baseline as the validation harness for FAIR, reproducible fusion.

④ Reframe rising inequality as an urban scaling law

Extending this paperThe paper reports that wealth inequality rose fastest in countries with large slum populations but stops at description. Extending it: test whether intra-urban inequality follows a scaling relationship with city size / slum share, fitting an exponent across the 32 countries to ask whether African slum inequality obeys—or breaks—the universal urban-scaling patterns documented elsewhere in the corpus.

⑤ Fine-tune a geospatial foundation model for transferable slum detection

Extending this paperThe paper trains a bespoke classifier per the study design; performance in label-scarce countries is bounded by the slum sampling dataset. Extending it: fine-tune a geospatial foundation model (Sentinel-pretrained) so slum detection transfers to unlabeled cities and new years with few labels, encoding the paper's environmental priors (LST, water proximity, density) as soft constraints in a physics-informed graph operator over the urban grid.
