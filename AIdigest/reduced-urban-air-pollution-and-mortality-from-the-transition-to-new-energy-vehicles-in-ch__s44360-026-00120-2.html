Reduced urban air pollution and mortality from the transition to new energy vehicles in China

Qianqian Yang · Quan Yuan · Qiangqiang Yuan · Huanfeng Shen · Yuan Wang · Hongzan Jiao · Liangpei Zhang · Meng Gao Published 2026-05-13 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00120-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

By 2023, China's rapid expansion of new energy vehicles (NEVs) produced measurable air quality improvements across 150 cities: a 23.80% reduction in PM2.5 (8.97 µg m−3) and 30.67% reduction in CO (0.26 mg m−3), preventing approximately 262,000 non-accidental deaths and 75,000 all-cause deaths. However, benefits are concentrated in economically developed cities, and reductions in coarse particles (PM10) and NO2 remain low — revealing pollutant-specific disparities and socio-economic inequalities that call for targeted electrification of heavy-duty diesel vehicles and stronger NEV deployment in less-developed regions.

Why It Matters

  • First city-level quantification of NEV health benefits in China. While NEV adoption trends are widely tracked, this is among the first studies to translate vehicle electrification into averted deaths across a national-scale sample of 150 Chinese cities, using high-resolution satellite data at 60-m spatial resolution.
  • Exposes pollutant-specific blind spots. NEV growth reduces PM2.5 and CO substantially, but has limited impact on NO2 and PM10 (coarse particles) — pointing to the continued role of heavy-duty diesel and non-exhaust emissions that EVs alone cannot address.
  • Reveals socio-economic inequality in green transition benefits. Air quality improvements and avoided deaths are disproportionately concentrated in wealthier cities where NEV penetration is higher, underscoring that the green dividend is not equitably distributed.
  • Provides an interpretable causal attribution. By combining physics-informed deep learning (phyMTDNN) with Random Forest and SHAP analysis, the study isolates the NEV signal from meteorological confounders, delivering credible causal estimates rather than correlations.
  • Directly informs climate and health policy. The findings provide quantitative evidence to accelerate heavy-duty vehicle electrification, tighten diesel emission standards, and redirect NEV subsidies toward less-developed cities.

Core Methods

  • Physics-informed multi-task deep neural network (phyMTDNN): retrieves near-surface concentrations of PM2.5, NO2, CO, O3, and PM10 simultaneously from Landsat 8 imagery at 60-m resolution, validated with 10-fold cross-validation across 150 cities and years.
  • Random Forest (RF) fusion model: an ensemble of 100 RF-based regression models fuses phyMTDNN retrievals with ground-level monitoring data to produce final pollution estimates; model accuracy statistics reported per city and pollutant.
  • SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations): used for interpretable attribution of NEV contribution to pollution reduction, isolating the vehicle electrification signal from meteorological variability and other confounders.
  • Counterfactual scenario analysis: simulates a "no-NEV" baseline to quantify how much pollution levels would have differed without NEV adoption, then attributes the difference to NEV penetration at city level.
  • Health impact assessment: applies exposure-response functions (from the PubMed-cited meta-analysis) together with city-level population and baseline mortality to convert pollution reductions into avoided non-accidental and all-cause deaths.
  • Kruskal–Wallis non-parametric tests: used to assess statistical significance of differences in pollution reduction and health benefits across city groups (by development level, NEV ownership tier, etc.).

① Spatially disaggregate health burdens to sub-city level using fine-resolution population grids

Extending this paperThe paper computes avoided deaths at the city level, allocating health benefits uniformly to each city's population. However, the same 60-m satellite pollution maps the authors produce could support sub-city health impact assessment if coupled with gridded population data (e.g., WorldPop or LandScan). Extending this paper by intersecting its 60-m NEV-driven pollution-reduction surfaces with fine-grained population density grids would reveal intra-urban disparities in who captures the health dividend — likely exposing that even within cities classified as "benefiting," lower-income neighborhoods near freight corridors still bear elevated residual exposure due to heavy-duty diesel vehicles the study flags but cannot quantify at finer scale.

② Transfer the phyMTDNN retrieval framework to non-Chinese cities using geospatial foundation models

Extending this paperThe physics-informed multi-task deep neural network (phyMTDNN) is trained and validated exclusively on 150 Chinese cities. Yet the underlying input — Landsat 8 imagery plus road network features from OpenStreetMap — is globally available. A direct extension: fine-tune the phyMTDNN on non-Chinese city samples (e.g., South Asian or African megacities undergoing rapid motorization) to test whether the Chinese-trained model generalizes, and where it fails, diagnose which physical or land-use features require city-specific adaptation. This would transform the paper's pollution retrieval approach from a China-specific tool into a globally portable environmental monitoring capability.

③ Extend the counterfactual attribution to a causal graph incorporating electricity grid emissions

Extending this paperThe paper's counterfactual ("what if no NEVs?") captures only tailpipe emission reductions. In reality, NEV charging shifts emissions upstream to power plants, and China's electricity mix varies substantially by province and year. Extending the attribution model with a causal graph that couples NEV adoption → grid electricity demand → upstream SO2 / NOx / PM emissions → secondary aerosol formation would produce a full lifecycle pollution balance. This may revise both the magnitude and geographic distribution of the paper's avoided-death estimates, particularly in coal-heavy northern provinces where grid emissions partially offset tailpipe gains.

Added value to my researchThis is a natural extension of my interest in physics-informed graph learning: the causal chain from vehicle fleet composition through grid dispatch to atmospheric chemistry is structurally a directed graph with physical conservation constraints (mass balance, reaction kinetics). Encoding these priors into a graph neural network causal model would enable me to simultaneously attribute pollution sources and propagate uncertainty across the energy–transport–health system.

④ Couple the SHAP attribution with spatiotemporal kernel density to map "NEV benefit hotspots"

Extending this paperThe paper's SHAP analysis (Fig. 3) attributes feature importance at the model level, revealing that NEV penetration is a significant driver of pollution reduction. However, SHAP values are reported in aggregate rather than mapped spatially. Extending this paper by computing spatially explicit SHAP values at 60-m resolution — one SHAP attribution map per city per year — and then summarizing their concentration patterns with spatiotemporal kernel density estimation would identify persistent "NEV benefit hotspots" (road corridors where cleaner vehicles produce the largest pollution reduction) and underscore where heavy-duty diesel substitution would yield the next largest gains.

Added value to my researchSpatiotemporal KDE is a core method in my toolkit, and this extension would demonstrate its applicability to environmental attribution rather than purely point-process analysis. More broadly, this produces a spatially explicit explainability layer — the kind of local, interpretable output that my GeoAI-for-public-health agenda seeks: understanding not just "that" NEVs help, but "where" along the transport network they help most.

⑤ Use the paper's city-level disparity findings to design equitable NEV subsidy allocation

Extending this paperThe paper's finding that health benefits concentrate in economically developed cities is primarily diagnostic — it calls for accelerated NEV deployment in less-developed regions but does not propose a mechanism. A natural extension: formulate a multi-objective optimization of NEV subsidy allocation across Chinese cities that balances (a) maximizing total avoided deaths (efficiency), (b) minimizing the coefficient of variation in per-capita health gains across cities (equity), and (c) respecting budget constraints and city-level charging infrastructure capacity. The paper's own regression models relating NEV ownership to pollution reduction provide the response function needed to parameterize this optimization.

Added value to my researchThis directly parallels my work on EV charging-station efficiency-vs-equity optimization: I can adapt the same bi-objective or Pareto-frontier framework I use for charging infrastructure placement to the subsidy-allocation problem, treating city-level health gain as the welfare function and city-level NEV penetration as the decision variable. Combined with my explainable ML work, SHAP values from this paper's models could identify which city characteristics most constrain equitable benefit distribution — translating environmental epidemiology into actionable urban policy.
