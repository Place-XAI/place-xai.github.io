Matching clinicians with clinical trials using AI

Junyi Gao · Cao Xiao · Lucas M. Glass · Ewen M. Harrison · Jimeng Sun Published 2026-03-06 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00073-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

DocTr, a cross-modal deep learning framework, dramatically improves clinical trial site selection by jointly embedding unstructured trial documents, patient encounter claims (ICD-10 codes) and historical clinician–trial payment relationships. Evaluated on 24,984 US clinicians and 5,210 trials, DocTr achieves 58% higher match similarity than the best baseline for unseen trials. A genetic algorithm post-processing step further enhances racial and ethnic fairness entropy by up to 25% compared with ground-truth enrolment while reducing competing trial conflicts per clinician to near zero — offering a principled, data-driven alternative to manual site selection that is simultaneously more accurate, more fair, and financially transparent.

★

Why It Matters

  • Fixes a broken process at scale. Conventional site selection causes 37% of trial sites to under-enrol and 11% to enrol nobody; 80% of trials miss timelines at costs of $600k–$8 million per day. DocTr converts this manual, biased search into a reproducible, scalable ranking task.
  • First to fuse three heterogeneous data modalities for clinician ranking. Combining free-text trial criteria (ClinicalBERT), structured patient encounter vectors (1,718 ICD-10 codes, 5-year averages) and a payment-derived enrolment graph (OpenPayments) goes beyond any prior site-selection or patient–trial matching approach.
  • Fairness built in, not bolted on. A genetic optimization layer explicitly maximises racial, ethnic and geographic diversity in the final recommendation list, producing more equitable enrolment than the ground truth itself — 25% higher ethnicity fairness entropy.
  • Operational and financial utility. Cost and enrolment estimation via embedding similarity (CCC = 0.83 for trial cost) gives trial managers actionable budget forecasts alongside the ranked site list.
  • Fully open. Code and processed data are released via GitHub (github.com/v1xerunt/DocTr) and Zenodo, lowering the barrier for adoption and replication.

Core Methods

  • Cross-modal semantic encoding: ClinicalBERT with mean pooling encodes trial summary text and inclusion criteria into dense embeddings; a separate encoder compresses 1,718-dimensional ICD-10 patient encounter vectors into a shared latent space.
  • Heterogeneous graph neural network (LightGCN): a bipartite clinician–trial graph built from OpenPayments payment records is processed by a heterogeneous LightGCN to produce structurally enriched embeddings that capture latent enrolment patterns beyond semantic similarity.
  • Enrolment-history augmentation: each clinician's embedding is further enriched by aggregating (mean-pooling) the trial summary and criteria embeddings of their historically enrolled trials, bridging the semantic and structural branches.
  • Matching score and BPR loss: a combined matching score (dot product of semantic + structural embeddings, weighted by a learnable α) is optimized using Bayesian Personalized Ranking (BPR) loss over positive/negative clinician pairs per trial.
  • Genetic algorithm post-processing: a multi-objective genetic algorithm refines the ranked list by maximizing a fitness function that jointly accounts for matching score, fairness entropy (race, ethnicity, geography) and the number of competing trials per clinician — each weight (β) is tunable.
  • Cost/enrolment estimation: for a target trial or clinician, the top-3 nearest neighbors in embedding space provide cost/enrolment estimates via cosine-distance weighted averaging; accuracy evaluated with the Concordance Correlation Coefficient (CCC).
  • Evaluation: composite similarity (CS@K, K = 5/10/20/GT) measures profile overlap between recommended and actually enrolled clinicians; temporal split (8:1:1 by trial start date) tests generalization to entirely unseen future trials including 27.3% new clinicians.

① Replace the census zip-code fairness proxy with realized patient-mobility demographic flows

Extending this paperDocTr approximates a clinician's patient demographics by the census distribution of their practice zip code — acknowledged in the paper as a "notable simplification." A direct extension: replace this static proxy with mobile phone or hospital-visit mobility data that reveals the actual catchment area and demographic composition of a clinician's patient panel. This is not merely a data swap; it rewrites the fairness entropy calculation in the genetic algorithm with spatially realized demand rather than ambient area demographics, making the fairness signal both more accurate and more geographically nuanced.

Added value to my researchMobile phone data enabling clinician-level patient-flow catchment areas directly mirrors my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and 2SFCA/R2SFCA service-area delineation work. The DocTr use case would let me apply my catchment-area methods to the supply side of clinical research, creating a bridge between GeoAI health geography and clinical trial operations — and giving mobility-derived accessibility a new downstream application.

② Integrate a multi-modal graph foundation model to replace the hand-crafted ClinicalBERT + LightGCN pipeline

Extending this paperDocTr's architecture manually stitches together three encoders (ClinicalBERT, ICD-10 MLP, LightGCN) with a learnable α to combine them. A natural successor: replace this composite with a pretrained multi-modal graph foundation model that ingests text, tabular code vectors, and a relational graph jointly within a single unified architecture, learning cross-modal interactions end-to-end. Such a model would remove the need to manually balance semantic versus structural signals (the α parameter) and could be fine-tuned on different countries' trial registries and payment systems as DocTr's geographic scope expands beyond the USA.

Added value to my researchFine-tuning geospatial foundation models is one of my core research directions. The DocTr extension maps directly onto this: just as I fine-tune models on remote sensing or mobility data to adapt a pretrained spatial representation, the same paradigm applied to biomedical graph+text data could produce a transferable clinician–trial embedding space. This opens a methodological transfer from my GeoAI toolkit into biomedical informatics.

Cross-ref (corpus): Multimodal learning with graphs — 10.1038/s42256-023-00624-6

③ Extend the genetic fairness algorithm to a reinforcement learning policy that learns the fairness–accuracy Pareto frontier

Extending this paperThe genetic algorithm in DocTr is a heuristic search over a fixed linear fitness function with manually tuned β weights. This approach cannot guarantee Pareto optimality, struggles when fairness attributes conflict (e.g., geographic diversity vs. racial diversity), and must be re-run from scratch for every trial. A direct extension: reformulate the post-ranking optimization as a multi-objective reinforcement learning problem where the agent learns a policy that dynamically trades off match quality, each fairness dimension, and competing-trial minimization — producing a deployable policy that generalizes across trials without re-optimizing.

Added value to my researchI already work on efficiency–equity optimization for EV charging stations and facility-location problems. The DocTr RL extension would let me apply that same multi-objective optimization apparatus — which I currently use in physical urban infrastructure — to the informational infrastructure of clinical research, expanding my scope while reusing my methodological toolkit.

Cross-ref (corpus): Algorithmic fairness and bias mitigation for clinical machine learning with deep reinforcement learning — 10.1038/s42256-023-00697-3

④ Diagnose how access disparities in the underlying claims data propagate into DocTr's recommendations

Extending this paperDocTr's limitation section notes that ICD-10 codes are billing codes potentially shaped by reimbursement incentives, and that cost-constrained patients may be systematically under-represented in claims data. An important extension: quantify how healthcare access disparities embedded in the IQVIA claims corpus — for example, underrepresentation of uninsured or rural patients — distort the learned clinician embeddings and thus systematically bias recommendations toward urban, well-insured patient panels. This would require an "access audit" of the training data, inspired by work showing that EHR reliability depends on access, before the model is used to improve trial diversity.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly onto my spatial accessibility and health geography work, specifically the digital divides and access-to-care themes. I study how geographic disparities in primary care access shape health outcomes; applying the same diagnostic lens to training data in a clinical AI pipeline is a natural methodological extension and a publishable "fairness audit" contribution.

Cross-ref (corpus): Access to care affects electronic health record reliability and AI-driven disease prediction — 10.1038/s44360-026-00054-9

⑤ Add a city-level spatial layer to DocTr to optimize geographic equity across the US urban hierarchy

Extending this paperDocTr reports that geographic fairness entropy shows "no significant change" after genetic optimization, noting this may be because the candidate pool is already nationally distributed. But this national distribution masks the well-documented city-level inequality in specialized healthcare provision — large academic medical centers in a handful of metropolitan hubs dominate trial enrolment, leaving smaller or rural cities chronically underrepresented. Extending DocTr with a city-level spatial optimization layer, informed by the intercity healthcare hierarchy, would target geographic equity at the level that actually matters for patient access: which cities host trial sites, not just which zip codes.

Added value to my researchThis is the intersection of my urban analytics work (intercity healthcare networks, 15-minute neighborhood access, regional collaboration) with clinical trial operations. Applying network community detection or my intercity healthcare system analysis to the OpenPayments clinician–trial graph would reveal the cities and regions structurally excluded from trial participation — a new GeoAI health-geography application combining spatial network analysis with clinical AI.
