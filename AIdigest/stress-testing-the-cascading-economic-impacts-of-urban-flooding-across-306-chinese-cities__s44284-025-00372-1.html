Stress-testing the cascading economic impacts of urban flooding across 306 Chinese cities

Delin Fang · Fei Xu · Xuanyi Jin · Changqing Song · Peichao Gao · Laixiang Sun · Daoping Wang · Kuishuang Feng Published 2026-01-19 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00372-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Treating cities as isolated nodes badly understates flood losses. By coupling fluvial flood hazards with a risk-extended multiregional input–output (MRIO) model for 306 Chinese cities across 6 return periods, the study shows losses rise nonlinearly with severity and shift from direct capital damage in frequent, low-intensity floods to local-indirect (supply-chain) losses in rare, high-intensity floods. Wealthier cities bear larger absolute losses but lower loss-to-GDP ratios, while poorer cities suffer higher proportional losses, especially via labor. Spillovers concentrate in major hubs, and a Yangtze River Delta co-shock amplifies damage far beyond the sum of single-city stress tests.

★

Why It Matters

  • Networks, not islands. Conventional flood damage accounting stops at the wet city's direct losses. This work traces how the shock ripples through inter-city supply chains, revealing that aggregating isolated city stress tests gives only a conservative lower bound.
  • A new spillover lens. It introduces a spillover indicator for the passive losses absorbed by cities that were never flooded — quantifying systemic risk that current risk maps make invisible.
  • Exposes a development-equity fault line. The same flood is regressive: poorer cities lose a larger share of GDP (heavily through labor disruption), so a one-size-fits-all adaptation budget would entrench inequality.
  • Reframes adaptation planning. Because the loss mix flips from capital to supply-chain with severity, optimal interventions are sector- and region-specific and must protect critical hubs (e.g., transport equipment, transportation) whose failure propagates widely.

Core Methods

  • Hazard–exposure overlay: intersect JRC flood-depth maps with land use, 3D buildings, population and elevation to estimate direct capital and labor losses per city per return period.
  • Risk-extended MRIO: embed direct shocks into a multiregional input–output model to propagate disruptions through inter-city supply chains and estimate indirect losses.
  • Loss decomposition: separate direct capital, labor, local-indirect (within the flooded city) and ripple (other cities) losses, plus economic-recovery dynamics.
  • Spillover indicator: a new metric for passive losses borne by non-flooded cities, mapped in absolute value and as a share of each city's GDP.
  • Severity & equity analysis: trace the nonlinear loss–severity curve and the regressive loss-to-GDP gradient across rich vs. poor cities and economic sectors.
  • Multi-city co-shock scenario: a Yangtze River Delta simultaneous-flood stress test showing amplification beyond aggregated single-city tests.

① Learn the supply-chain cascade as a graph operator instead of solving the MRIO per scenario

Extending this paperThe risk-extended MRIO is re-solved for every city × every return period, and the inter-city economic web is treated as a fixed linear coupling. Extending the paper: cast the 306-city MRIO as a weighted economic graph and train a physics-informed graph neural operator that maps (flood-depth field, sectoral exposure) → (direct + local-indirect + ripple + spillover) losses, with input–output balance encoded as a soft constraint. This would let the framework extrapolate to new co-shock combinations (not just the Yangtze River Delta) without re-running the full solver.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my physics-informed graph-learning agenda: a conservation-aware GNN surrogate that transfers across regions, reusable as a fast accessibility/loss propagator and pairing naturally with my spatiotemporal prediction stack.

② Attribute the regressive loss-to-GDP gradient with explainable ML

Extending this paperThe paper documents that poorer cities lose a larger GDP share, especially via labor, but the drivers are described rather than disentangled. ③ Couple the economic cascade to a public-health cascade

Extending this paperThe framework stops at capital and labor losses; it never propagates the shock into health-system function. Extending it: overlay flood depth and the labor-disruption layer onto hospital/clinic locations and treat care access as another downstream node — so that a flooded transport sector also degrades spatial accessibility to primary and emergency care, giving a health-economic loss term alongside GDP.
