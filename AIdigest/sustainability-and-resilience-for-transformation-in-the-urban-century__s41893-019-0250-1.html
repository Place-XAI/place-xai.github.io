Sustainability and resilience for transformation in the urban century

Thomas Elmqvist · Erik Andersson · Niki Frantzeskaki · Timon McPhearson · Per Olsson · Owen Gaffney · Kazuhiko Takeuchi · Carl Folke Published 2019-04-09 DOI 10.1038/s41893-019-0250-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Urban sustainability and resilience are not interchangeable: sustainability is normative (it defines desirable goals), while resilience is non-normative (it describes a system's capacity to maintain function, whether desired or not). Conflating the two in global policy — as the UN's New Urban Agenda does — produces planning contradictions, including efficiency gains that erode the redundancy essential for resilience. A new framework reconceives urban systems as having multiple development trajectories and treats resilience management as the lever for enabling or blocking transformation toward more sustainable pathways — providing a clear, actionable distinction for urban policy in the Anthropocene.

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a costly conceptual confusion in global policy. The New Urban Agenda (2017) mentions "resilience" 17 times and "sustainability" 11 times, conflating them in 8 sentences — this paper exposes why that leads to contradictory planning outcomes and offers clear definitions for both.
  • Identifies a hidden planning trap: efficiency vs. redundancy. Optimizing for sustainability (resource efficiency) systematically erodes resilience by removing the redundancy that keeps complex urban systems functional under shocks — the energy sector and transport networks are concrete examples.
  • Reframes "lock-in" as an active governance target. Poverty, car-dependent urbanism, and high-carbon energy systems are "undesired resilient" states — the framework tells planners to deliberately reduce resilience of those sub-systems to unlock transformation.
  • Introduces scale-explicit resilience thinking. Resilience is not a property of a single city at one scale; the paper argues it must be addressed across household, neighborhood, city, and regional scales simultaneously to avoid perverse outcomes (e.g., flood walls protecting wealthy enclaves while increasing risk elsewhere).
  • Connects concepts to the Anthropocene urgency. With over 4 billion urban dwellers (projected to reach 60% of global population by 2030), the conceptual clarity this framework provides has direct planetary-scale stakes for SDG 11 and beyond.

① Operationalize "desired vs. undesired resilience" as a spatial indicator computed from mobility and infrastructure data

Extending this paperThe paper defines desired and undesired resilience conceptually but offers no metric to detect them in a real city. A direct extension: treat undesired resilience as the persistence of low-accessibility, high-poverty neighborhoods despite repeated urban interventions — and measure it using longitudinal mobility data as a revealed-preference signal of lock-in. Mobility trajectories that remain constrained despite policy investment would identify areas where the framework's "reduce resilience" prescription is most urgent, transforming the paper's qualitative logic into a spatially explicit diagnostic.

Added value to my researchThis aligns with my interest in GPS trajectories and mobile-phone records as data for urban analytics. Detecting spatial lock-in from mobility patterns is a natural application of spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and agent-based modeling, and the resulting "resilience-of-what" map would directly inform 2SFCA service-area analyses by flagging which low-accessibility zones are structurally entrenched versus temporarily underserved.

② Build a multi-scale resilience index that operationalizes the paper's cross-scale critique

Extending this paperThe paper's strongest methodological contribution is the argument that resilience cannot be measured at a single urban scale — yet it provides no quantitative test of this claim. Extending it: construct a nested resilience index (household → neighborhood → city → region) using openly available transport, green-space, and economic data, then test whether optimizing resilience at the city scale reliably degrades it at the neighborhood scale (the flood-wall analogy). This would empirically validate — or challenge — the paper's core multi-scale thesis across a diverse sample of global cities.

Added value to my researchThis extends my work on urban agglomeration and regional collaboration: the paper's cross-scale dynamics map directly onto my interest in how city-region networks (job-housing, transport) create hidden interdependencies. A nested resilience index built with MGWR/GWR would quantify where non-stationarity in resilience-environment relationships is most scale-sensitive — adding an ecological dimension to my spatial regression toolkit.

Cross-ref (corpus): Interconnectedness enhances network resilience of multimodal public transportation systems for Safe-to-Fail urban mobility — 10.1038/s41467-023-39999-w

③ Use nature-based solutions as empirical test-cases for the directed-transformation pathway

Extending this paperThe paper's directed-transformation concept (proactive, path-preserving) is illustrated by the High Line analogy but never tested systematically. A natural extension is to use the recent global inventory of urban nature-based solutions (NbS) as a cross-city dataset: classify each NbS intervention as directed vs. abrupt transformation, measure its effect on both sustainability indicators (carbon, biodiversity, health) and resilience indicators (flood buffering, heat mitigation, social cohesion), and test whether the paper's predicted trade-offs between efficiency and resilience hold empirically at scale across hundreds of cities.

Added value to my researchGreen infrastructure accessibility is an understudied dimension of my health geography work. Combining NbS location data with 2SFCA-style spatial accessibility would let me quantify who is within reach of directed-transformation interventions — and whether nature-based resilience investments reproduce or reduce existing spatial health inequities. This bridges the paper's governance framework to a concrete public-health policy lever.

Cross-ref (corpus): Global mapping of urban nature-based solutions for climate change adaptation — 10.1038/s41893-022-01036-x

④ Encode the sustainability–resilience tension as an explainable ML constraint to predict policy misalignment

Extending this paperThe paper argues that efficiency-focused sustainability policies often inadvertently reduce resilience — but this claim has never been tested predictively. A data-driven extension: build a multi-output predictive model that takes city-level policy packages (energy, transport, land use) as inputs and predicts simultaneous sustainability scores and resilience indicators as outputs, then use SHAP values to reveal which policy features drive the two metrics in opposite directions. This would give planners an explainability tool to anticipate efficiency–resilience misalignments before implementation, grounding the paper's conceptual warnings in actionable foresight.

Added value to my researchThis is precisely my explainable machine learning agenda applied to urban governance: using ML ensembles + SHAP not just to predict outcomes but to surface the mechanisms driving trade-offs. The sustainability–resilience tension becomes a two-headed target variable, and the spatial heterogeneity of trade-off magnitudes across city types is a natural extension to spatial ML (MGWR-informed feature weighting).

⑤ Translate the paper's trajectory-tunnel framework into an agent-based simulation for urban governance scenario planning

Extending this paperThe paper's conceptual "trajectory + tunnel" model in Fig. 2 is richly suggestive but remains a schematic. A direct computational extension: implement it as an agent-based model (ABM) where agents represent urban actors (planners, developers, communities, NGOs) whose decisions widen or narrow the resilience tunnel around a development trajectory. Parameterize lock-in strength and transformation capacity from empirical data on cities that have successfully executed abrupt transformations (e.g., Medellín's cable-car urbanism, Seoul's Cheonggyecheon stream restoration), and use the ABM to generate transition-risk maps that policy makers can query for specific sustainability targets.
