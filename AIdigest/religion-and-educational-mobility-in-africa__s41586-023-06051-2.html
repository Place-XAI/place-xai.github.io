Religion and educational mobility in Africa

Alberto Alesina · Sebastian Hohmann · Stelios Michalopoulos · Elias Papaioannou Published 2023-05-17 DOI 10.1038/s41586-023-06051-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 21 African countries and 2,286 districts, Christians consistently achieve higher intergenerational educational mobility than Muslims and Traditionalists, even when controlling for household structure, parental occupation, and district fixed effects. The gap is largest in religiously segregated, predominantly Muslim areas — where Muslims also emigrate least — revealing a compounding spatial trap: segregation suppresses both educational opportunity and the willingness to migrate toward better opportunities.

★

Why It Matters

  • First continent-scale, religion-specific mobility atlas. Using IPUMS census data on more than 7 million children, the study constructs district-level upward and downward educational mobility measures for three faiths — a dataset that did not previously exist at this scale.
  • Separates spatial sorting from causal exposure. A quasi-experimental movers' design (leveraging age-at-move variation across 13 countries) shows both Christians and Muslims gain equally from early relocation to high-mobility districts — but Muslims move far less often, accentuating their disadvantage.
  • Identifies religious segregation as a first-order driver. The Christian–Muslim mobility gap is strongly correlated with the Muslim population share of a district; Muslim children underperform most precisely where they are numerically dominant, mirroring US evidence on racial segregation and educational underinvestment.
  • Policy relevance for education programmes. As African governments and international institutions scale school-construction and fee-abolition programmes, the findings highlight that blanket interventions may miss faith-specific barriers rooted in segregated geography and differential internal migration costs.
  • Open data and code. All newly constructed faith-specific mobility statistics and the replication code (R + Python) are openly available, enabling replication and extension.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Primary source
IPUMS International — harmonized census microdata. Records religious affiliation in 45 censuses from 20 African countries plus Nigeria (Nigeria data from household surveys 2006–2010). Publicly available at international.ipums.org; free, registration required.
Sample coverage
21 countries, 2,286 districts (administrative level-2/3). The 21 countries hosted approximately 750 million of Africa's 1.35 billion people in 2020. North Africa represented only by Egypt.
Individual sample
Base sample: 7,188,717 children aged 14–18 cohabiting with an older generation member. Extended sample (ages 14–25): 13,018,904 individuals with ~75% cohabitation rates. Movers' design subsample: 276,686 individuals from 13 countries with age-of-move data.
Faith categories
Muslims, Christians (Orthodox/Catholic/Protestant aggregated), Traditionalists/Animists, plus auxiliary Other and No Religion categories. Conversion rates between faiths below 3% in virtually all countries.
Supplementary data
Afrobarometer Surveys (religiosity, living conditions); colonial-era railroad/road maps; missionary location records; population density and urbanization proxies at independence — used as district-level covariates. Sources documented in Supplementary Section F.
Replication code
R v4.2.2 and Python v3.11; code and data on github.com/imreligionafrica/imreligionafrica.
Time period
Birth cohorts primarily from the 1950s–1990s; primary school completion used as the educational milestone (secondary/tertiary enrolment rose mainly post-2000).

Core Methods

  • Absolute intergenerational mobility (IM) construction: upward IM = probability that a child aged 14–18 with uneducated parents completes primary school; downward IM = probability that a child with educated parents fails to complete primary school. Computed separately by faith group for each district–cohort cell.
  • OLS regressions with progressive controls (Fig. 2): individual-level linear probability models sequentially adding country × cohort fixed effects, household structure (size, family organization, age at marriage), parental occupation/industry/urban-rural status, district-level prior literacy of the older coreligionist generation, and district × urban/rural fixed effects. Weighted by 1980 country populations to adjust for differential IPUMS coverage.
  • Quasi-experimental movers' design (Fig. 3): exploits variation in the age at which children move across districts to separate regional childhood exposure effects from spatial sorting. The key assumption — that timing of moves is unrelated to children's ability — is supported by prior work. Semiparametric and parametric age-of-move slope estimates computed for Christians and Muslims separately.
  • District-level correlate analysis (Figs. 4–5): standardized OLS regression of Christian–Muslim and Christian–Traditionalist IM gaps on four categories of regional characteristics (pre-independence development, geography/location, colonial history, religious composition), with country fixed effects. Heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors clustered at the country level.
  • Internal migration analysis (Figs. 6–7): classification of migrants as individuals residing outside their birth district at census time; demeaned-at-origin statistics to control for interreligious residence differences; bootstrap confidence bands (1,000 and 10,000 iterations) for binned scatterplots.
  • Steady-state educational share formula: analytical derivation of the long-run equilibrium share of completed primary education as a function of upward and downward IM rates, translating observed mobility differences into projected steady-state educational achievement gaps.

① Map the paper's district mobility scores onto school and health-facility accessibility to quantify the supply-side of the religious gap

Extending this paperThe paper attributes the Christian–Muslim mobility gap partly to Muslims residing in "more remote areas with limited infrastructure," but this is measured indirectly through colonial-era proxies. A direct extension would overlay the paper's district-level faith-specific IM scores onto georeferenced school facility counts, walk/drive times from residential clusters to the nearest primary school, and pupil–teacher ratios, testing whether the Christian–Muslim upward IM gap is mediated by measurable present-day accessibility deficits — rather than historical inertia alone. The paper's openly shared district statistics and IPUMS geographic shapefiles make this linkage tractable. Datasets from the sub-Saharan Africa infrastructure and slum literature provide compatible spatial units.

Added value to my researchThis is the natural intersection of the paper with my 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility toolkit. Applying an enhanced floating-catchment-area model to primary schools in religiously heterogeneous African districts — with separate service-area delineations per faith community — would test whether spatial accessibility deficits explain the residual IM gap that the paper's district fixed effects do not absorb. The exercise also pilots R2SFCA in a low-income global-south context, extending my existing work on healthcare and facility access beyond North American and Chinese settings.

② Use GeoAI on satellite imagery and mobile phone data to nowcast faith-specific mobility without waiting for the next census

Extending this paperThe paper's IM statistics are necessarily backward-looking: they require completed schooling for 14–18-year-olds observed at census time, producing estimates anchored to 1980–1990 birth cohorts. A key extension would build a nowcasting pipeline — training an explainable ML model on the paper's district IM scores and their established correlates (nightlight intensity, road density, mosque/church density from OpenStreetMap, mobile network coverage) to infer updated faith-disaggregated mobility estimates for post-2010 cohorts before the next census round. The paper's supplementary geographic and historical covariates supply a rich feature set.

Added value to my researchThis aligns with my work on multi-source data fusion (mobile phone, remote sensing, POIs) and fine-tuning geospatial foundation models. Specifically, it pilots a spatiotemporal GeoAI workflow where a fine-tuned satellite-imagery encoder predicts socioeconomic opportunity at sub-district scale, with SHAP/LIME attribution diagnosing which built-environment features most explain the Christian–Muslim gap — translating the paper's econometric findings into an operational GeoAI monitoring product.

③ Model internal migration as a network flow and identify which district-level interventions unlock Muslim mobility

Extending this paperThe paper documents that Muslims migrate less — and that the correlation between own-group population share and migration propensity is three times steeper for Muslims than Christians — but treats migration as an aggregate rate rather than a directed flow. Extending it: reconstruct origin–destination migration matrices from the IPUMS birthplace × residence data, represent them as a weighted directed network, and apply community-detection and centrality analysis to identify which districts act as "gateway hubs" for Muslim inter-regional mobility. Counterfactual simulations could then quantify the IM gain if migration propensity in high-segregation nodes were raised to the cross-faith median.

Added value to my researchThis mirrors my job-housing network and urban agglomeration work, extended to educational-mobility flows. The network community-detection step (e.g., Louvain or InfoMap on the migration graph) maps directly onto my spatial network analysis toolkit, while the counterfactual simulation aligns with my agent-based and Monte Carlo modeling methods. The result would be a flow-based complement to the paper's regression evidence — a spatial network view of the segregation-mobility trap.

④ Extend the movers' design to quantify heterogeneous neighbourhood effects by religious-community size

Extending this paperThe paper's movers' design (Fig. 3) estimates a single average childhood exposure effect of ~2 percentage points per year of exposure for both Christians and Muslims. However, the paper also shows that Muslim children underperform most sharply in high-Muslim-share districts. Extending it: interact the age-of-move exposure effect with the destination district's Muslim population share — testing whether the 2% exposure gain is itself heterogeneous, potentially lower or even negative when a Muslim child moves into a high-Muslim segregated district. This would reframe the neighbourhood-effect estimate as a function of the religious composition of the destination, directly connecting the movers and segregation literatures within the same paper's framework.

Added value to my researchThe heterogeneous neighbourhood-effects specification is formally equivalent to a spatially varying coefficient model — the same family of methods as my MGWR/GWR work. The exercise would demonstrate how a geographically weighted regression over destination religious composition can recover locally varying exposure gradients, piloting MGWR in an intergenerational-mobility context and linking spatial econometrics to development economics.

⑤ Translate the paper's steady-state IM formula into a district-level educational equity index to guide targeted school investment

Extending this paperThe paper derives an elegant analytical formula: the long-run steady-state share of completed primary education equals upward IM / (upward IM + downward IM). Using the paper's open district × religion IM dataset, one can compute the implied steady-state educational gap for every district and overlay it with current government and NGO school-investment geographies to identify districts where the faith-specific steady-state gap is large and where interventions are sparse — a spatial targeting mismatch map. Combining this with the paper's finding that segregation correlates strongly with the gap would allow simulation of how much the Muslim steady-state share would improve under a migration-facilitation or school-redistribution scenario.

Added value to my researchThis bridges the paper directly into my facility-location optimization and public-facility accessibility work. The steady-state formula provides a principled objective function: maximizing the population-weighted Muslim steady-state educational share subject to a budget constraint on new school placements. Combined with my EV-charging efficiency–equity optimization framework — recast for schools — and spatial entropy analysis to measure diversity of access, this produces an actionable GeoAI policy tool that is directly grounded in the paper's empirical estimates rather than assumed parameters.
