Extending atomic decomposition and many-body representation with a chemistry-motivated approach to machine learning potentials

Qi Yu · Ruitao Ma · Chen Qu · Riccardo Conte · Apurba Nandi · Priyanka Pandey · Paul L. Houston · Dong H. Zhang · Joel M. Bowman Published 2025-04-14 DOI 10.1038/s43588-025-00790-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

MB-PIPNet is a new machine learning potential framework that decomposes total potential energy into chemically meaningful monomer energies — not arbitrary atomic local energies — using one-body and two-body permutationally invariant polynomial (PIP) descriptors as inputs to feed-forward neural networks. Applied to liquid water (256 molecules, 75,874 training configurations), water trimer, methane–water clusters, and liquid CO2, MB-PIPNet achieves CCSD(T)-level accuracy while reaching the speed of conventional force fields on a single CPU core — a balance that has not previously been demonstrated for atomistic MLPs such as DeePMD. The monomeric representation also unlocks direct, interpretable per-molecule energy decomposition, a capability absent in all standard atom-centered architectures.

Why It Matters

  • Bridges accuracy and efficiency. Existing high-accuracy MLPs (MB-pol, q-AQUA) are too slow for large-scale MD; fast force fields sacrifice accuracy. MB-PIPNet achieves both in a single framework, enabling first-principles-quality simulations at force-field cost.
  • Restores chemical interpretability. Atom-centered NNs assign arbitrary local energies to atoms — physically undefined and hard to interpret. MB-PIPNet's monomer energies map onto the well-understood concept of molecular orbital energy vs. atomic orbital energy, allowing chemically meaningful energy decomposition.
  • Favorable computational scaling. Cost scales with number of molecules rather than atoms, and the use of PIPs (vs. heavy message-passing operations) keeps the prefactor low — outperforming DeePMD several-fold on a single CPU core and matching TTM3-F polarizable force field performance at large system sizes.
  • Broad transferability. Demonstrated across gas-phase clusters, liquid water, mixed methane–water systems, and liquid CO2; backed by a PIP library covering more than 100 different molecular systems.
  • Open science. All training data, source code, and examples are publicly released on figshare (figshare) and Zenodo/GitHub (zenodo), making the framework immediately reproducible.

Core Methods

  • Monomer-centered energy decomposition: total potential energy Etotal = ∑ Ei over N monomers. Each Ei is the "perturbed monomer energy," analogous to molecular orbital energy — physically interpretable and distinct from arbitrary atomic local energies.
  • Permutationally invariant polynomial (PIP) descriptors: one-body PIPs (sixth-order) encode the monomer's own geometry (G(self)); two-body PIPs (fourth-order) encode intermolecular environment within a cut-off Rc (G(env)) using a smooth switching function. Together these form 188-dimensional inputs for water models.
  • Feed-forward neural network: two hidden layers per monomer; neuron structures [30, 60] for water trimer, [15, 30] for liquid water, [10, 20] for CH4–water, [10, 30] for CO2. Training via Levenberg–Marquardt algorithm until learning rate < 10−5.
  • Cut-off and scaling: monomeric environment cut-off Rc = 9.0 Å (or 6 Å in speed tests); computational cost scales linearly with molecule count rather than atom count, improving prefactor significantly over atom-centered methods.
  • Validation suite: (i) energy/force RMSE on held-out test sets; (ii) harmonic normal-mode analysis; (iii) diffusion Monte Carlo (DMC) for anharmonic zero-point energy; (iv) classical MD simulations (NVT, 256 water molecules, 1 ns) benchmarked against experimental OO RDF, OOO angular distribution, and self-diffusion coefficient D(T).
  • Benchmarking: compared against BPNN, EANN, REANN, NequIP, MACE, DeePMD, TTM3-F (polarizable FF), q-TIP4P/F (non-polarizable FF) and q-AQUA many-body expansion.

① Incorporate message-passing to handle long-range interactions the paper explicitly identifies as a limitation

Extending this paperThe Discussion explicitly flags the absence of long-range electrostatics (Ewald summation) as a limitation of the current cut-off-based environment descriptor G(env). A natural extension: graft a graph message-passing layer onto the MB-PIPNet monomer nodes so that each monomer's descriptor incorporates iterated neighbor messages beyond Rc, without sacrificing the PIP-enforced permutational invariance. This would let MB-PIPNet handle charged or polar condensed phases (e.g., ionic liquids, protein–water interfaces) while retaining its chemistry-interpretable energy decomposition. The corpus paper on uncertainty-driven active learning for interatomic potentials (Vandermause et al.) offers a complementary strategy: use uncertainty signals from the current two-body descriptors to identify which long-range configurations are underrepresented and trigger targeted data collection.

The message-passing extension proposed here maps onto extending my physics-informed GNN models with multi-hop propagation: just as MB-PIPNet needs a message-passing layer for charged systems, spatial models for health-access or epidemic diffusion need explicit long-range graph paths. The parallelism helps motivate bandwidth-adaptive graph convolution designs.

② Transfer the monomer-energy interpretability principle to spatially decomposed prediction models

Extending this paperMB-PIPNet's key insight is that decomposing Etotal into chemically meaningful monomer sub-energies (not arbitrary atomic units) produces a model that is simultaneously more accurate and more interpretable. The paper benchmarks this through monomer energy scatter plots (Fig. 2b) where the physical meaning of each molecule's contribution is traceable. Extending this: apply the same fragment-based decomposition principle to reactive systems — where reactive and non-reactive zones could use different descriptors (atomistic for the reactive core, MB-PIPNet for the solvent shell), as the Discussion proposes for CO2 + water → carbonic acid. The corpus paper on a universal graph deep learning interatomic potential (M3GNet) provides a complementary architecture that spans the periodic table, which could serve as the atomistic "reactive core" layer in such a hybrid.

⑤ Extend the paper's many-body hierarchy to spatiotemporal interaction modeling

Extending this paperMB-PIPNet's theoretical core is that two-body PIP descriptors are sufficient to represent three-body and higher-order interactions (verified by the water trimer DMC benchmark). This is a non-trivial finding: it says that pairwise environmental features carry enough information to reconstruct collective effects. A direct extension: test the minimum-order hypothesis more rigorously across a wider range of molecular systems and interaction types (hydrogen bonding, dispersion, charge transfer) to establish when two-body sufficiency breaks down and a three-body PIP layer becomes necessary. The corpus paper on the unified deep learning framework for many-body quantum chemistry via Green's functions (Zhu et al.) provides a complementary formalism for rigorously capturing many-body correlations that could serve as an exact reference for this stress test.

Added value to my researchThe "pairwise sufficiency for higher-order effects" principle has a direct spatiotemporal analogy: in my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and epidemic SIR modeling, do pairwise spatial interaction kernels (two-point correlations: origin-destination flows, contact matrices) suffice to predict higher-order outbreak clusters? MB-PIPNet's finding suggests that if the right basis functions are chosen (analogous to PIPs), pairwise features may be sufficient — reducing the combinatorial complexity of multi-body spatial interaction models. This is a testable hypothesis in my mobility-flow-based disease transmission work.
