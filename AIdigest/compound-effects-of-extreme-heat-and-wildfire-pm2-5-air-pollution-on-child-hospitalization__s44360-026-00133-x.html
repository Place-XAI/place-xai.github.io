Compound effects of extreme heat and wildfire-PM2.5 air pollution on child hospitalizations in multiple countries

Yiwen Zhang · Rongbin Xu · Yao Wu · Wenzhong Huang · Zhengyu Yang · Bo Wen · Yanming Liu · Zhihu Xu · Zhaoyuan Li · Wenhua Yu · Gongbo Chen · Shuang Zhou · Tingting Ye · Eric Lavigne · Micheline S. Z. S. Coelho · Paulo H. N. Saldiva · Patricia Matus · Kraichat Tantrakarnapa · Jiangning Song · Yuming Guo · Shanshan Li Published 2026-06-08 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00133-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 954 communities in five countries (Australia, Brazil, Canada, Chile, Thailand, 2000–2019), days when an extreme heat event and a high-level wildfire-PM2.5 pollution day co-occurred carried a 3.8% higher risk of all-cause child hospitalization (RR = 1.038, 1.029–1.048) — greater than either hazard alone. The interaction was synergistic (positive RERI) for respiratory, renal and diabetes admissions, and consistently stronger than the heat × non-fire-PM2.5 combination, signalling that wildfire smoke is a distinctly hazardous co-exposure for children and adolescents under a warming climate.

★

Why It Matters

  • Names the hazard that climate change is manufacturing. Heatwaves and wildfire smoke increasingly arrive together; this is among the first multi-country quantifications of their compound effect specifically on children — a population usually studied for single exposures only.
  • Distinguishes fire smoke from ordinary pollution. By separating wildfire-sourced PM2.5 (HFPP) from non-fire PM2.5 (HNPP), it shows wildfire co-exposure is more harmful, challenging policies that treat all PM2.5 as equivalent.
  • Moves beyond the respiratory cliché. Significant synergy for renal and diabetes hospitalizations broadens the recognised footprint of compound climate hazards into metabolic and renal pediatric disease.
  • Identifies who is most vulnerable. Stratification by sex, age, community income and urbanisation gives targeted adaptation handles for paediatric public-health planning rather than a single national average.

Core Methods

  • First-stage time-series model: community-specific quasi-Poisson regression relating daily child hospitalizations to EHE, HFPP/HNPP and their co-occurrence, over lag 0–7 days (headline effects at lag 0–1).
  • Second-stage pooling: random-effects meta-analysis across the 954 communities to produce country-level and overall pooled relative risks.
  • Interaction quantification: additive synergy measured by the Relative Excess Risk due to Interaction (RERI), with uncertainty from 1,000 Monte-Carlo samples.
  • Effect modification: random-effects meta-regression (maximum-likelihood) testing differences by sex, age, community income and urbanisation level.
  • Comparative design: wildfire-PM2.5 (HFPP) co-exposure contrasted against non-fire-PM2.5 (HNPP) co-exposure to isolate the wildfire-specific compound hazard.
