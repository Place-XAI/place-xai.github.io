Learning on topological surface and geometric structure for 3D molecular generation

Odin Zhang · Tianyue Wang · Gaoqi Weng · Dejun Jiang · Ning Wang · Xiaorui Wang · Huifeng Zhao · Jialu Wu · Ercheng Wang · Guangyong Chen · Yafeng Deng · Peichen Pan · Yu Kang · Chang-Yu Hsieh · Tingjun Hou Published 2023-10-09 DOI 10.1038/s43588-023-00530-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

SurfGen, a structure-based 3D molecular generative model, encodes protein-pocket topology via two equivariant graph neural networks — Geodesic-GNN and Geoattn-GNN — that jointly learn topological surface interactions and spatial ligand–pocket geometry, mimicking the lock-and-key principle. SurfGen outperforms state-of-the-art deep learning methods on both the CrossDock2020 benchmark and a curated real-world therapeutic-target set in binding affinity and 3D shape similarity. Critically, its sensitivity to pocket geometry makes it capable of generating mutation-adapted drug candidates for drug-resistant protein variants — an otherwise unsolved challenge for existing approaches.

Why It Matters

  • Closes the gap between chemical-space search and target-specific design. Most prior generative models explore ligand chemical space without faithfully modeling pocket topology; SurfGen makes the pocket surface itself a first-class geometric object, yielding molecules that are geometrically and energetically complementary to the target.
  • Demonstrates 3D rationality as a prerequisite for affinity claims. By introducing the scoring-energy metric (in-situ evaluation without conformational re-search), the paper exposes that models with good docking scores can generate thermodynamically unstable, distorted molecules — a methodological contribution to benchmark design.
  • Directly addresses mutation-induced drug resistance. SurfGen is sensitive enough to pocket structural changes caused by single amino-acid substitutions, enabling generation of hit candidates tailored to mutated, drug-resistant targets — a first for end-to-end 3D generative models.
  • Provides a practical open resource. Code and training data (CrossDock2020) are publicly available; real-world COVID-19 (3CL protease, PDB 6LU7) examples are included as ready-to-run discovery starting points.

Core Methods

  • Protein surface representation: the protein pocket is modeled as a triangulated topological surface (mesh of surface nodes) rather than as a point cloud or graph of residue atoms, capturing the geometric shape complementarity of the binding site.
  • Geodesic-GNN: an equivariant graph neural network that performs message passing along geodesic distances on the curved pocket surface, using Gaussian curvature, mean curvature, shape index, Poisson–Boltzmann charge, hydrogen-bond donors, and hydropathy as node features — ensuring the receptive field respects the topology of the surface (no "leakage" through the pocket wall).
  • Geoattn-GNN: an equivariant geometric-attention graph neural network that models spatial interactions between ligand atoms and pocket surface nodes via Euclidean distances, enabling the model to learn van der Waals and electrostatic interaction patterns.
  • Geometric vector perceptron (GVP): used throughout to jointly process scalar and vector features while enforcing rotation-equivariance (Eq. 1) and invariance (Eq. 2).
  • Autoregressive 3D generation: atoms are placed sequentially into the pocket; each step predicts (i) a focal atom / starting surface node, (ii) the next atom's 3D coordinates via a Gaussian mixture model (NLL loss), and (iii) the atom type and bond order via cross-entropy with kNN aggregation and triangle attention.
  • Evaluation metrics: scoring affinity (in-situ Vina energy without conformational re-search), docking affinity (with conformational re-search), RMSD, 3D shape similarity (SP), Tanimoto topological similarity, SA, QED, and internal diversity.
  • Mutation sensitivity experiment: single- and multi-point alanine mutations on shikimate kinase (1ZYU) used to verify that SurfGen generates geometrically adapted molecules in response to pocket volume and interaction changes.

① Transfer SurfGen's geodesic surface learning to spatial accessibility delineation

Extending this paperSurfGen models the protein pocket as a topological surface with geodesic distances, ensuring that the receptive field of each node respects the curved geometry rather than cutting through structural barriers. This is a direct analogy to the urban street-network problem: pedestrian catchment areas for a healthcare facility are bounded by actual network distances, not Euclidean radii, yet most GNN-based spatial models use flat Euclidean adjacency. Extending SurfGen's Geodesic-GNN block — replacing the protein surface mesh with a city's road-network planar graph and surface node features with POI density, land use, and population — would yield a topology-respecting catchment delineator for 2SFCA/R2SFCA. The training objective would shift from affinity to realized service utilization rates.

Added value to my researchMy 2SFCA and R2SFCA work defines service areas by fixed radii or gravity decay, both agnostic to network topology. Importing SurfGen's geodesic message-passing as the backbone of a learned catchment delineator would allow the service-area boundary to emerge from the network structure and demand distribution rather than from a tuned parameter — a concrete GeoAI upgrade to spatial accessibility modeling directly aligned with my health geography agenda.

② Encode mutation sensitivity as a spatial heterogeneity detector

Extending this paperSurfGen's mutation experiment demonstrates that the model is sensitive to single residue substitutions — small structural perturbations produce measurably different generated outputs. This pocket-level perturbation sensitivity is an architectural property of the geodesic and geometric-attention design, not a post-hoc analysis. Extending the paper: systematically benchmark this sensitivity against an information-theoretic measure (e.g., mutual information between pocket perturbation magnitude and output distribution shift) to quantify how much topological change is detectable, and compare across model classes. This would position SurfGen as a molecular sensitivity analysis tool for allosteric drug-resistance studies.

③ Replace the CrossDock training objective with a multi-objective equity–efficacy criterion

Extending this paperSurfGen is trained end-to-end on binding affinity (implicitly, via learned interaction patterns) and evaluated on a single-target affinity plus drug-likeness. However, real drug-discovery campaigns require molecules that are potent and selective (not toxic to off-targets) and synthesizable. Extending the paper: incorporate a multi-objective training signal — binding affinity to primary target, predicted off-target penalty, and a synthesizability score (SA) — into SurfGen's NLL + cross-entropy loss, balancing efficacy against selectivity and accessibility to synthesis. MISATO's quantum-mechanical features on protein–ligand complexes would enrich the training representation.

④ Apply SurfGen's autoregressive atom-placement logic to sequential spatial entity generation

Extending this paperSurfGen's autoregressive generation places atoms one at a time, each conditioned on the current pocket state and the already-placed atoms, using a focal-selection step followed by coordinate prediction (Gaussian mixture) and topology assignment (atom type + bond). This sequential pocket-filling logic is structurally identical to sequential facility placement: place each new facility conditioned on existing facilities and local demand. Extending the paper: replace atoms with facility types, bonds with service-area overlaps, and the pocket surface with a city's demand density surface, and retrain SurfGen's generation backbone on facility-placement trajectories — yielding an end-to-end learned facility-location generator.

⑤ Ground SurfGen's surface energy features in physics-informed spatial kernels

Extending this paperSurfGen attaches six physics-derived features (Gaussian curvature, mean curvature, shape index, Poisson–Boltzmann charge, H-bond donors, hydropathy) to pocket surface nodes, encoding known biophysical priors as soft constraints on the learned representations. This is physics-informed representation learning, but the constraints are only in the feature engineering step, not in the loss. Extending the paper: encode these biophysical priors as physics-informed loss terms (e.g., penalize generated conformations that violate Lennard-Jones exclusion volumes) so the model is architecturally constrained to obey physics in addition to being feature-informed — improving generalization to unseen target families and reducing the rate of thermodynamically implausible outputs.

Added value to my researchPhysics-informed graph learning is a core methodological theme in my research. SurfGen's architecture offers a concrete blueprint: my spatiotemporal GNNs for urban health prediction could similarly encode physical-law constraints (e.g., conservation of population flows, spatial autocorrelation decay) as architectural priors, making predictions robust to data-sparse regions — analogous to how SurfGen's equivariance prevents implausible 3D poses in data-sparse pocket environments.
