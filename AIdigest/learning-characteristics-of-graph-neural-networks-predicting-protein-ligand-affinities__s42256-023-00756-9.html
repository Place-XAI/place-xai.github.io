Learning characteristics of graph neural networks predicting protein–ligand affinities

Andrea Mastropietro · Giuseppe Pasculli · Jürgen Bajorath Published 2023-11-13 DOI 10.1038/s42256-023-00756-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

GNNs predicting protein–ligand binding affinity from interaction graphs do not primarily learn protein–ligand interactions but instead rely heavily on ligand memorization — recalling affinity values for structurally similar training compounds. However, the picture is nuanced: some architectures (especially GC-GNN) increasingly prioritize interaction-edge information when predicting high-affinity compounds, revealing an architecture-dependent balance between memorization and genuine interaction learning revealed through EdgeSHAPer and GNNExplainer explainability analysis.

★

Why It Matters

  • Settles a contested question in computational drug design. The community has debated whether GNNs truly "learn" binding physics or merely memorize training data. This study provides the first systematic, multi-architecture XAI-based answer: ligand memorization dominates, but it is not the whole story.
  • Reframes apparent predictive accuracy. High benchmark correlations do not indicate that GNNs capture thermodynamic reality; they partly reflect the chemical similarity structure of training sets — an artificial feature that limits prospective applicability.
  • Reveals architecture-dependent learning differentiation. For GC-GNN, interaction edges account for more than half of the most important edges in high-affinity predictions, suggesting that not all GNNs are equivalent and that architectural choices meaningfully steer what is learned.
  • Opens a design path forward. The finding motivates tailored interaction graphs that amplify interaction edges and suppress ligand-memorization signals — a concrete engineering direction for future graph-based drug-design models.

Core Methods

  • Six GNN architectures evaluated: GCN, GAT, GIN, GINE (edge-weight variant of GIN), GraphSAGE, and GC-GNN (GraphConv operator with max aggregator) — all implemented in PyTorch Geometric with 256 hidden units, trained for 100 epochs with Adam optimizer (lr = 1e-3), evaluated by root mean square error (r.m.s.e.) against experimental pKi.
  • Protein–ligand interaction graphs: nodes represent ligand pseudo-atoms (intramolecular interactions) and protein pseudo-atoms (amino acid residues); edges encode three types: ligand–ligand (intramolecular), protein–protein (intramolecular), and protein–ligand (intermolecular interaction) — weighted by scaled distance.
  • EdgeSHAPer (primary XAI): a model-agnostic, Shapley-value-based method that quantifies the contribution of individual edges in a GNN's prediction via Monte Carlo sampling (100 steps); ranks edges by absolute SHAP value; top-k edges (k = 5, 10, 15, 20, 25) tracked as ligand, protein, or interaction edges.
  • GNNExplainer (secondary XAI): learns a mask on the graph maximizing mutual information between prediction and subgraph distribution — used as a methodologically independent cross-check of EdgeSHAPer findings.
  • Affinity-stratified analysis: edge importance profiles compared separately across low-, medium-, and high-affinity prediction subranges to detect whether GNN learning mode changes with potency level.
  • Structural mapping: top-25 edges from interaction graphs mapped back to 3D protein–ligand structures to visually verify memorization vs. genuine interaction-learning for exemplary complexes.

① From edge-SHAP attribution to physics-constrained graph re-design

Extending this paperThe paper diagnoses what GNNs currently learn — predominantly ligand memorization — and explicitly calls for "new types of interaction graph that specifically emphasize interaction patterns while including only fuzzy ligand components." The logical next step is to close this loop: use the EdgeSHAPer attributions as a supervision signal to automatically re-weight or prune edges in the interaction graph so that the graph topology encodes more physics (interaction edges) and less memorization (intramolecular ligand edges). This transforms the paper's diagnostic XAI into a generative, physics-guided graph construction step — extending the study from analysis to active improvement.

Added value to my researchThis translates directly into my physics-informed graph learning agenda: encoding domain constraints (e.g., spatial decay, network topology) as hard graph priors rather than soft regularization. Just as interaction edges encode binding physics, my spatiotemporal graphs can encode geographic contiguity or accessibility decay as structural graph properties — and XAI attribution can verify whether the model actually uses those priors.

② Memorization vs. generalization as a universal GNN audit framework

Extending this paperThe ligand-memorization finding rests on a conceptually general test: partition edges by whether they carry specific interaction information vs. recurrent structural identity, then measure which class drives predictions. This audit framework is not specific to protein–ligand graphs. Extending the paper: apply it to out-of-distribution (OOD) molecular benchmarks — if ligand memorization is the dominant learning mode, GNNs trained on one chemical scaffold should fail badly when tested on a structurally distinct one. The paper's EdgeSHAPer framework, combined with recent OOD benchmark work, could establish a universal "memorization index" that predicts generalization failure before deployment.

Added value to my researchThe memorization audit maps onto a critical problem in GeoAI model evaluation: spatial autocorrelation means that geospatial models often "memorize" local neighborhood patterns rather than learning generalizable laws. The edge-attribution logic — distinguishing structural-identity edges from interaction edges — is analogous to distinguishing spatial proximity edges from causal mechanism edges in my spatiotemporal graphs. I can adapt EdgeSHAPer's SHAP-on-edges approach to audit whether my physics-informed spatial GNNs genuinely use physical constraints or just exploit spatial clustering.

Cross-ref (corpus): Navigating molecular OOD-ness — 10.1038/s42256-026-01251-7

③ Affinity-stratified XAI as a template for task-difficulty-stratified model explanation

Extending this paperOne of the paper's most illuminating insights is that GNNs change their learning strategy depending on the difficulty of the prediction target: low-affinity compounds are predicted via memorization, high-affinity compounds increasingly via interaction learning. Extending this: test whether the same affinity-stratified XAI strategy reveals a phase transition in GNN behavior as a function of training data size — i.e., does more data shift the balance from memorization to interaction learning? If so, the paper's finding implies that current benchmark sizes are insufficient to train genuine interaction-learning and that there is a data-volume threshold at which GNNs could in principle transcend memorization.

Added value to my researchTask-difficulty stratification is a neglected axis in my explainable ML for spatial prediction work. In accessibility modeling, hard-to-predict locations (e.g., peri-urban fringe areas with sparse data) may rely on entirely different features than easy-to-predict urban cores. Applying SHAP-on-edges stratified by prediction difficulty to my spatial GNNs could identify where the model relies on meaningful spatial signals vs. local memorization of dense training clusters — a direct quality-control tool for my GeoAI pipelines.

④ Contrastive pre-training to de-bias ligand memorization in GNN affinity models

Extending this paperThe paper shows that ligand memorization dominates because similar ligands share training-set affinity values. A natural remedy: contrastive pre-training that explicitly teaches the GNN to distinguish structurally similar ligands that have different affinities (active cliffs) — directly targeting the memorization regime. The paper's EdgeSHAPer framework provides an objective criterion to evaluate whether contrastive pre-training actually shifts the edge-attribution profile from ligand-dominated to interaction-dominated, turning XAI attribution into a training objective validator.

Added value to my researchContrastive learning for de-biasing memorization connects to my interest in multi-source data fusion and fine-tuning geospatial foundation models. Spatial foundation models pre-trained on dense urban areas may similarly "memorize" dense-area patterns and fail in sparse rural areas. Contrastive fine-tuning that explicitly pairs dense-area and sparse-area instances with contrasting outcome labels — analogous to activity cliffs — could de-bias the foundation model toward learning generalizable spatial laws.

⑤ From protein–ligand graph explainability to spatial-interaction graph explainability for health geography

Extending this paperThe paper's central methodological move — represent a complex interaction system as a typed, edge-weighted bipartite graph (ligand nodes + protein nodes + interaction edges) and then use SHAP-on-edges to determine which edge type drives predictions — is a transferable graph XAI template. Extending it: apply the same typed-edge-SHAP design to supply–demand accessibility graphs in which nodes represent facilities and population units, edges encode supply flow (interaction edges), and intramolecular edges encode facility capacity or population vulnerability. EdgeSHAPer-style analysis would reveal whether an accessibility GNN learns genuine supply–demand matching or memorizes the spatial distribution of training facilities.

Added value to my researchThis idea is directly actionable for my 2SFCA/R2SFCA spatial accessibility work. I can model the facility–demand system as a typed bipartite graph identical in structure to a protein–ligand graph, then apply EdgeSHAPer to audit whether my GNN captures meaningful catchment-area interactions or merely memorizes the high-accessibility training neighborhoods. The audited edge profiles would also feed into my explainable ML for health geography papers, providing a new type of interpretability evidence — edge-attribution maps rather than feature-importance bar charts — that reviewers and planners can directly link to spatial accessibility mechanisms.
