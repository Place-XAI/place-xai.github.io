Decoupling geographical constraints from human mobility

Louis Boucherie · Benjamin F. Maier · Sune Lehmann Published 2025-09-01 DOI 10.1038/s41562-025-02282-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

By normalizing the observed moving-distance distribution by the pair distribution function — a tool from statistical mechanics that encodes the geometry of all possible location pairs — the authors reveal a power law spanning five orders of magnitude (10 m to 500 km) with exponent ≈ 1 that is consistent across residential mobility in Denmark and France and day-to-day mobility in Houston, San Francisco, and Singapore. Geography does not produce a universal law by itself; only the geography-corrected intrinsic distance attractiveness, π(r) = 1/r, is universal. This framework bridges and generalizes both the gravity model and the radiation model of human mobility into a single continuous, geometry-independent formulation.

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a long-standing tension in human mobility research. Distance-based (gravity) and opportunity-based (radiation) models have remained competing paradigms for decades. This work shows both emerge from the same geometry-corrected power law, unifying the two schools.
  • Reveals that observed mobility patterns conflate two distinct signals. Raw distance distributions confound geography with human choice; the pair distribution function cleanly separates them, giving researchers an unbiased baseline for any sub-population analysis.
  • Delivers unprecedented empirical grounding. The Danish dataset — 39 million residential moves over 36 years pinpointed to ±2 m — is far more complete and precise than GPS or mobile-phone data, virtually eliminating selection bias.
  • Opens a new application for condensed-matter physics in urban science. Modelling buildings as interacting particles under a confining potential reproduces the multi-scale fractal structure of cities, providing a physics-grounded theory of the built environment.
  • Provides a null model for population sub-group studies. Deviations from π(r) = 1/r — across demographics, socioeconomic strata, or over time — directly quantify how specific factors shape mobility above and beyond geography.

Core Methods

  • Pair distribution function (PDF): imported from condensed-matter physics; computes p(r) = the expected number of location pairs at distance r, fully encoding the geometry (landmass shape, city positions, building layout) of a given geography.
  • Normalization / decoupling: π(r) = f(r) / p(r) separates the geometry-induced signal from the human movement propensity, yielding the intrinsic distance attractiveness independent of geography.
  • Maximum-likelihood estimation of power laws: following Clauset et al. and the bounded-MLE method of Hanel et al. (for exponents < 1.5); Kolmogorov-Smirnov goodness-of-fit tests; Akaike information criterion for model comparison.
  • Statistical physics modelling of urban structure: (i) ideal-gas model with linear confining potential → Erlang radial density → generalized gamma pair distribution, used to define city radius without assuming a city centre; (ii) Lennard-Jones and attractive hard-disc simulations to reproduce micro-scale oscillatory structure of building placement; (iii) fractal and independent-patch models to explain the meso-scale power-law p(r) ∝ r0.67.
  • Piecewise power-law fitting per city: for each of Denmark's ~1,400 cities, a city-level pair distribution is computed and the intrinsic distance attractiveness is fit to a piecewise power law (exponents ≈ 0.60 within cities, ≈ 2.0 across cities), revealing the transition radius that defines a "mobility city radius."
  • Molecular dynamics simulation: velocity-Verlet integration with Berendsen thermostat to generate equilibrium building configurations for comparison with empirical pair distributions.
  • Continuous gravity-law derivation: shows analytically that the discrete gravity model (P ∝ N₁N₂/d) emerges as a limiting case of the pair distribution framework in the limit of well-separated cities.

① Use the pair distribution framework to define geometry-corrected service-area catchments in 2SFCA

Extending this paperThis paper decouples observed mobility from geography to uncover π(r) = 1/r. A direct extension is to apply the same normalization to healthcare utilization trips: compute the pair distribution between residential addresses and facility locations, divide observed patient flows by p(r), and recover the intrinsic demand-distance decay function for each facility type and city. Instead of assuming a Gaussian or power-law decay — as 2SFCA family methods do — the decay function would be derived from data, specific to facility type, and corrected for the local built-environment geometry rather than borrowed from trip-distance distributions that conflate geography and human preference.

Extending this paperThe paper establishes π(r) = 1/r as a null model and explicitly highlights that deviations across demographic groups (age, gender, socioeconomic status) could reveal mobility inequity. ④ Incorporate the fractal pair distribution model as a physics-informed prior in spatiotemporal graph learning

Extending this paperThe paper derives that the meso-scale pair distribution follows p(r) ∝ r0.67 from fractal building arrangements with packing fraction θ, and provides an analytic formula α = 1 − 2 ln θ / ln(θ/b) that ties the scaling exponent to urban structure parameters. This physics-derived scaling is not merely descriptive — it is a structural prior about how location density grows with distance in any city. Extending the paper: embed this fractal scaling as a learnable but physics-constrained kernel in a graph neural network, so the GNN's spatial attention weights are initialized from and regularized toward the pair distribution prior rather than learned from scratch. This yields a transferable urban-mobility GNN whose inductive bias is grounded in the geometry of the built environment.

Added value to my researchMy physics-informed graph learning agenda — building GNNs with conservation-law and scaling priors — can directly incorporate the pair distribution as a structural constraint. Rather than learning edge weights purely from mobility records, the model would encode the expectation that transition probabilities between locations scale as 1/r after pair-distribution correction, with the fractal exponent as a city-specific learnable parameter. This would improve generalization to cities with sparse mobility data by leveraging the geometry-derived prior.

⑤ Benchmark Deep Gravity and learned mobility models against the pair distribution null model

Extending this paperThe paper provides a geometry-corrected null model, π(r) = 1/r, that is both simple and empirically grounded. This is a powerful benchmark that machine-learning mobility models have never been evaluated against: instead of measuring accuracy relative to observed flows (which conflate geography with behavior), one could measure how well a learned model recovers π(r) after pair-distribution normalization. If a Deep Gravity model or closed-form gravity-like model trained on raw trip data reproduces the observed f(r) but not the intrinsic π(r), it has merely re-learned the geometry — not the underlying behavioral law. This paper's framework thus provides a model-agnostic diagnostic for whether a learned mobility model has captured human decision-making versus geographic structure.

Added value to my researchFor my data science and reproducibility work — including multi-source data fusion and geospatial foundation model fine-tuning — this diagnostic matters enormously. Any mobility foundation model I fine-tune on city-specific GPS or mobile-phone data could be evaluated on whether its learned distance-decay aligns with the geometry-corrected π(r) = 1/r baseline, using the pair distribution computed from the same city's address data. This would distinguish genuine behavioral learning from geography overfitting, improving model interpretability and cross-city transferability.
