Complex economic activities concentrate in large cities

Pierre-Alexandre Balland · Cristian Jara-Figueroa · Sergio G. Petralia · Mathieu P. A. Steijn · David L. Rigby · César A. Hidalgo Published 2020-01-13 DOI 10.1038/s41562-019-0803-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The spatial concentration of productive activities increases with their complexity: complex economic activities such as biotechnology, neurobiology and semiconductors disproportionately concentrate in a handful of large cities, while simple activities like apparel manufacturing distribute more evenly. Across 353 US metropolitan areas, complexity explains 40–80% of the variance in urban concentration of occupations, industries, scientific fields and technologies. Historical patent data extending back to 1850 show that the spatial concentration of cutting-edge technologies has increased continuously over 150 years, suggesting a self-reinforcing cycle between complexity and urbanization that may be a key driver of growing spatial inequality.

Why It Matters

  • Unifies two major research traditions. By connecting economic complexity theory (Hidalgo & Hausmann) with urban scaling laws, the paper provides a single explanatory variable — complexity — that reconciles why some activities super-scale with city size while others barely do.
  • Explains the rise of superstar cities. The finding that complexity drives agglomeration offers a mechanistic account of why knowledge-intensive hubs (San Francisco, Boston, New York) have pulled away from the rest, and why digital communications have not equalized the playing field.
  • Links economic evolution to spatial inequality. The 150-year historical lens shows concentration increasing with each industrial revolution, directly connecting the growth in activity complexity to rising geographic inequality between large and small cities.
  • Has urgent policy implications. If complex activities cannot be decentralized without losing the coordination benefits of density, then regional policy must grapple with structural limits — not just funding gaps — when trying to spread growth beyond a few megacities.

Core Methods

  • Urban scaling analysis: fit power-law relationships y ≈ xβ between MSA population (x) and output (y = patents, papers, employment, GDP) using OLS on log-log scale; the scaling exponent β captures the degree of spatial concentration (superlinear = β > 1 means larger cities have disproportionately more).
  • Complexity measurement (activity-side): four independent proxies to avoid circularity with spatial information: (1) average team size for scientific papers; (2) average year of appearance of combined patent subclasses (recency proxy for knowledge complexity); (3) NK complexity (Fleming & Sorensen) and average inventor count per patent; (4) average years of education of workers in occupations/industries.
  • Complexity-concentration regression: correlate the per-activity scaling exponent β with each complexity proxy (Pearson r reported), and confirm with multivariate regressions including interaction terms between city population and complexity measures.
  • Historical trend analysis: track the scaling exponent decade-by-decade from 1850 to 2010 for the top-25% and bottom-25% most complex patent technologies, as well as for six NBER main technology categories, using HistPat + USPTO data.
  • Robustness checks: alternative city definitions, inclusion of natural-resource categories, alternative complexity metrics (NK, inventor count), Brazilian mesoregions, and separate supplementary figures for every activity category.

① Extend the complexity-scaling framework to healthcare facility specialization

Extending this paperThis paper shows that knowledge-complex activities (biotechnology, neurobiology) concentrate superlinearly in large cities. The same logic applies to healthcare specialization: a neurosurgery center requires a much deeper division of medical knowledge than a general practitioner's office, and thus should exhibit a higher urban scaling exponent. Extending the paper by replacing patents/papers with healthcare facility types (primary care, specialist clinics, tertiary hospitals) and using physician skill-mix or patient case-mix complexity as the complexity proxy would test whether the complexity-concentration law is universal across public services. The corpus paper on technology transfer in US cities used analogous scaling methods to map how knowledge transfer scales, demonstrating the transferability of this exact framework to new domains.

Added value to my researchMy 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility work currently treats all healthcare facilities as equivalent supply points. If healthcare facilities follow the paper's complexity-concentration law, the supply density of complex facilities (e.g., mental-health specialists, oncologists) will be systematically lower in smaller cities — a structural spatial inequality that 2SFCA measures but cannot explain. Incorporating a complexity score per facility type would let me decompose accessibility gaps into structural (complexity-driven) and policy-remediable components, directly enriching my health geography research agenda.

② Use mobility data to reveal the intra-city knowledge-mixing that drives scaling

Extending this paperThe paper argues that cities enable knowledge spillovers through mixing and matching, but it only tests this at the city (aggregate) level; the within-city mechanisms remain a black box. A natural extension is to instrument the paper's scaling exponent with intra-city mobility intensity — measured from GPS traces or mobile-phone CDRs — to show that cities where people mix more across neighborhoods exhibit stronger superlinear scaling for complex activities. The corpus paper "Infrequent activities predict economic outcomes" demonstrates that rare, exploratory trips (not routine commutes) predict economic vitality, which is a direct behavioral correlate of the coordination benefit the paper posits.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal kernel density estimation methods and mobile-phone data pipelines are well-positioned to operationalize this. Constructing a cross-neighborhood mixing index from CDR data for Chinese or US metro areas would let me test whether the complexity-concentration relationship holds cross-nationally, while producing a spatial mixing covariate directly usable in my urban health and job-housing network analyses.

③ Derive a closed-form scaling law linking economic complexity to within-city inequality

Extending this paperThis paper shows that complex activities concentrate between cities (inter-city inequality), but does not examine what happens within cities — do complex-activity cities also have steeper intra-urban income gradients? The corpus paper "Urban scaling laws arise from within-city inequalities" proposes that aggregate superlinear scaling is itself generated by within-city heavy-tailed productivity distributions. Combining these: extending the paper by decomposing the scaling exponent β into an inter-city and an intra-city component, and testing whether higher complexity amplifies both, would produce a unified multi-scale inequality theory grounded in this paper's empirical backbone.
