Training data composition determines machine learning generalization and biological rule discovery

Eugen Ursu · Aygul Minnegalieva · Puneet Rawat · Maria Chernigovskaya · Robi Tacutu · Geir Kjetil Sandve · Philippe A. Robert · Victor Greiff Published 2025-08-20 DOI 10.1038/s42256-025-01089-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The composition of the negative training class — not just positive examples — critically determines both the in-distribution accuracy and out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization of ML models for antibody–antigen binding prediction. Counterintuitively, models trained on harder negatives (sequences similar to positives in affinity space) achieve lower in-distribution accuracy but superior OOD generalization and recovery of biological binding rules. This finding, validated on both synthetic (Absolut!) and experimental HER2 datasets, generalizes as a principle across biological ML tasks: closer negative–positive boundaries in training foster more nuanced, transferable and biology-aware models.

Why It Matters

  • Challenges a default assumption in ML benchmarking. Standard practice equates high in-distribution accuracy with model quality; this paper demonstrates that ID accuracy can be negatively predictive of real-world generalization — a critical warning for immunotherapeutic ML pipelines.
  • Connects negative sampling strategy to biological rule recovery. Not only do negatives affect accuracy: they determine whether the model learns genuine biological binding rules (affinity-correlated logits, residue-level attribution) or superficial discriminative shortcuts.
  • Provides actionable guidance for dataset design. The paper recommends collecting negative examples that are close in binding affinity to positives (e.g., weak binders, deep mutational scanning data), translating directly into experimental design criteria for therapeutic antibody discovery.
  • Broadly generalizable principle. The authors extend the insight to small-molecule drug discovery and transcriptomics-based phenotyping, suggesting that "hard negatives improve OOD robustness" is a domain-agnostic principle in biological classification tasks.
  • Leverages synthetic data to isolate causal effects. By using the Absolut! simulation framework (ground-truth binding energies for ~700k sequences over 10 antigens), the work uniquely controls for confounds impossible to eliminate in purely experimental datasets.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Synthetic dataset
Absolut! framework (greifflab.org/Absolut/) — lattice-based 3D docking of CDRH3 sequences against antigen structures from the Protein Data Bank. Covers 10 antigens (PDB IDs: 3VRL, 1NSN, 3RAJ, 5E94, 1H0D, 1WEJ, 1ADQ, 1FBI, 2YPV, 1OB1). Per antigen: avg. ~51,815 high-affinity binders (top 1%), ~205,069 weak binders (1–5%), ~391,499 non-binders (top 95%). Total CDRH3 sequences: approximately 700,000. Sequences are 11-mer CDRH3s from mouse repertoires.
Experimental dataset
Porebski dataset (HER2mllib library) — high-throughput ribosome display + next-generation sequencing screening of anti-HER2 scFv antibodies. After filtering for ≥12 replicates and clean sequences: 24,790 CDRH3 sequences (length 21 aa), classified as binders (10%), weak binders (15%), non-binders (75%) using fluorescence intensity (intensity 8) thresholds. Available via Zenodo: 10.5281/zenodo.8241732.
Training tasks
For each of the 10 antigens, 4 binary classification tasks varying in negative class definition: vs non-binder (bottom 95%), vs weak (1–5%), vs 1 (top 1% binders to 1 other antigen), vs 9 (top 1% binders to any of 9 other antigens). Each task: 30,000 training sequences (15k pos + 15k neg) and 10,000 testing sequences.
Processed data & models
All processed datasets, attribution values and trained models deposited on Zenodo: 10.5281/zenodo.11191740. Code on GitHub: csi-greifflab/negative-class-optimization.
Replication strategy
6 train–test splits per task; 4 random seed initializations per split; resulting in up to 81 technical replicates per OOD test scenario for robust estimation.

Core Methods

  • Binary classification framework: supervised models trained on one-hot-encoded 11-mer CDRH3 sequences (220-dim input) over four qualitatively different negative class definitions, with balanced datasets (20k positive + 20k negative, 10k test each).
  • SN10 neural network: small fully connected network (220 input → 10 hidden ReLU neurons → 1 sigmoid output), chosen for interpretability parity. Trained with Adam optimizer, binary cross-entropy, batch size 64, lr = 0.001, 50 epochs, stochastic weight averaging (after epoch 3).
  • Baselines: logistic regression (LR) as an additive model; transformer encoder (4 heads, dim 24); protein language model embeddings (ESM2b 1,280-dim; AntiBERTa2 1,024-dim) fed into the SN10 architecture — neither improved over SN10.
  • Jensen–Shannon Distance (JSD): quantifies positive–negative dissimilarity via position weight matrices (PWMs) of CDRH3 sequences, enabling correlation of sequence divergence with ID/OOD accuracy differences.
  • OOD evaluation: models trained on one negative type are tested on all other negative-type test sets, producing a full cross-task generalization matrix (4×4 per antigen × 10 antigens).
  • DeepLIFT attribution: per-residue feature importance scores computed using 10 shuffled baselines per sequence (Captum v0.5.0), then correlated against ground-truth per-amino-acid Miyazawa–Jernigan binding energies from Absolut!.
  • Affinity-partition sweep: 26 negative datasets with progressively increasing affinity gap from positives, enabling a continuous analysis of how positive–negative separation affects ID and OOD performance simultaneously.

① Diagnose dataset composition bias in geospatial ML training sets the same way this paper diagnoses biological ML

Extending this paperThis paper operationalizes "negative class similarity" as Jensen–Shannon Distance between position weight matrices of CDRH3 sequences — a principled, computable measure of how distinct training negatives are from positives. The paper stops at biological sequences, but the framework is fully general: any binary classification task with a continuous measure of feature proximity can be subjected to the same JSD-sweep / affinity-partition analysis. An immediate extension is to apply the same logic to spatial negative sampling in geospatial binary classifiers — e.g., presence-only species distribution models, facility-accessibility binary labels, or spatial anomaly detection — where the distribution of "negative" background locations is typically defined arbitrarily (random, spatially stratified, or absence-proxy) rather than by principled proximity to positive instances.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal accessibility and GeoAI pipelines routinely train binary or ordinal classifiers (e.g., accessible vs. inaccessible zones, high vs. low health-risk cells) where the negative set is defined by spatial sampling heuristics rather than theoretical grounding. Applying this paper's JSD-sweep to spatial negative definitions — using kernel density or distance-in-feature-space as the proximity metric — would reveal whether my models achieve only ID accuracy while failing OOD (e.g., across different cities, populations, or time periods). This directly addresses my FAIR/open-science reproducibility agenda: documenting negative-set construction as a first-class methodological choice.

② Use OOD accuracy as a benchmark metric for explainability methods in geospatial prediction

Extending this paperA key finding is that OOD performance — not ID accuracy — is the reliable proxy for whether a model has learned genuine biological binding rules. The paper proposes OOD accuracy as an "orthogonal metric for benchmarking rule recovery methods." This suggests a general evaluation protocol: for any explainability method (SHAP, LIME, DeepLIFT, integrated gradients), compute its rule-discovery quality not against a held-in test set but against OOD test performance. Extending the paper: apply this OOD-as-explainability-benchmark framework to compare attribution methods across task types — does DeepLIFT systematically outperform SHAP at identifying binding-relevant residues when OOD accuracy is the criterion?

Added value to my researchIn my explainable ML work for spatial prediction (e.g., MGWR-informed feature attribution for health geography), I currently validate explanations against holdout samples from the same spatial distribution — a form of ID evaluation. This paper's logic suggests I should instead validate feature attribution by testing whether models trained on attributions-selected features generalize to spatially disjoint test regions (OOD by geography). This would transform explainability from a qualitative interpretation step into a quantitative, OOD-validated signal quality metric for my GeoAI pipelines.

③ Extend the affinity-partition sweep to continuous negative-difficulty gradients in healthcare accessibility datasets

Extending this paperThe paper's most mechanistically revealing experiment is the 26-partition affinity sweep (Fig. 2f), which continuously varies positive–negative separation and shows a smooth transition from OOD-favorable (hard negatives, low JSD) to ID-favorable (easy negatives, high JSD) regimes. This sweep reveals the Pareto-like trade-off between ID and OOD without requiring arbitrary category boundaries. Extending the paper: apply the same continuous-difficulty sweep to other biological classification problems where a continuous feature-space distance can be defined — e.g., protein–ligand binding affinity, genomic sequence edit distance, or structural RMSD — and test whether the crossover point between ID-favoring and OOD-favoring negative regimes is task-specific or universal.

Added value to my researchIn healthcare facility accessibility modeling, the "negative" class — census tracts labeled as inaccessible — can range from barely-inaccessible (travel time slightly above threshold) to deeply inaccessible (very remote). Applying the partition-sweep logic means systematically constructing training sets where negatives vary from hard (near-threshold travel times) to easy (far-above-threshold), measuring how this affects R2SFCA model OOD generalization across cities. This would generate the first empirical accessibility–ML composition curve, enabling optimal negative-set design for spatial access models deployed across diverse urban contexts.

④ Apply synthetic-data-enabled controlled study design to spatiotemporal simulation benchmarks

Extending this paperA key methodological strength of the paper is that synthetic Absolut! data provide ground-truth binding energies, which allow direct comparison between model explanations and true causal quantities — something impossible with real experimental data alone. The paper uses this to demonstrate that feature attribution methods can recover ground-truth per-residue energies when the negative class is properly chosen. Extending this: build a synthetic spatiotemporal accessibility benchmark analogous to Absolut! — generate synthetic urban environments with known accessibility ground truth, construct varied negative-set definitions (spatially random vs. near-threshold), and use the synthetic ground truth to validate whether SHAP-based spatial feature attributions recover true accessibility drivers.

⑤ Transfer the "hard-negative promotes OOD robustness" principle to multi-source data fusion for spatiotemporal models

Extending this paperThe paper's conclusion — models trained on more similar positive–negative pairs learn more transferable rules — has a direct analog in multi-source data fusion: when training on a mixture of easy negatives (from a source domain with large distributional gap, e.g., a completely different city) and hard negatives (from a source domain with small gap, e.g., a nearby city), the resulting model's OOD performance will depend on the mixture composition. Extending the paper: formulate a "negative source mixing" experiment where the ratio of same-distribution to cross-distribution negative examples is varied, mapping out an ID–OOD trade-off surface over mixture proportions — extending the binary partition sweep to a 2D negative-composition simplex.

Added value to my researchMy multi-source data fusion work (mobile phone, social media, remote sensing, POI) constructs training batches that mix records from multiple spatial contexts — which is precisely a "mixture of easy and hard negatives" in disguise. This paper's framework gives me a principled way to audit my fusion pipeline: compute the JSD-analog (e.g., feature distributional distance) for each data-source pair, predict which mixing ratios will yield the best OOD generalization across unseen cities or time periods, and verify this empirically. This would make my fusion strategy principled rather than empirically tuned, advancing my data-science and reproducibility research agenda.
