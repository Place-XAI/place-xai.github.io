Global mapping of urban nature-based solutions for climate change adaptation

Sean Goodwin · Marta Olazabal · Antonio J. Castro · Unai Pascual Published 2023-01-30 DOI 10.1038/s41893-022-01036-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A systematic global mapping of 216 urban nature-based solution (NbS) interventions across 130 cities in 55 countries reveals that current urban NbS practice is largely incremental and reformistic — not transformative. Practice is heavily biased toward the Global North and addresses only narrow slices of the climate–biodiversity–society (CBS) nexus, particularly underrepresenting social justice dimensions (recognition and distributional equity), private-sector engagement, and the climate hazards most acute in the Global South (drought, coastal hazards). Fulfilling the transformative potential of NbS requires deliberate expansion in geography, governance models, and social equity frameworks.

★

Why It Matters

  • First systematic, comparable global database of implemented urban NbS. By screening 823 records from nine international databases and coding 216 interventions across a unified framework, the study produces the first cross-regional, empirical picture of what cities are actually doing with NbS — not what they plan or model.
  • Diagnoses a North–South knowledge gap with policy urgency. 63% of mapped interventions are in Europe; the Global South — which faces the most acute climate hazards (drought, coastal flooding) and rapid urbanization — is structurally under-represented, making this a call to action for funders and researchers alike.
  • Links governance deficiencies to transformative failure. The analysis shows that shallow social engagement (procedural-only justice), dependence on constrained local public budgets, and absence of legally binding urban planning mechanisms collectively explain why most NbS remain incremental.
  • Identifies actionable "bright spots." Cases in Addu City (Maldives), Durban, São Paulo, and Hamburg exemplify what transformative social, ecological, and technical NbS can look like — providing concrete reference points for replication.
  • Provides an open, Zenodo-archived dataset (doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.7059923) enabling further research, meta-analyses, and comparative studies.

Core Methods

  • Systematic mapping protocol following Collaboration for Environmental Evidence standards: defined Subjects (urban areas per UN Population Division), Interventions (implemented NbS per IUCN definition), and Outcomes (climate change adaptation) — a SIO framework guiding the entire search–screen–code pipeline.
  • Multi-database search: nine grey-literature databases spanning intergovernmental funders, global city-level sharing platforms, and national funding organizations; database-specific search strategies (filter tags, web scraping via rvest) compiled into a single master spreadsheet.
  • Two-step manual screening: Step 1 used platform tags and automated web-scraping prefilling; Step 2 involved full document review plus targeted internet searches for ambiguous cases. Language bias mitigated using Google Translate and DeepL.
  • Structured coding of ten CBS features per intervention (binary and categorical), partially semi-automated via keyword scraping from intervention URLs, always verified manually. Coded from project descriptions, M&E reports, linked peer-reviewed literature, and internet searches.
  • Transformative capacity scoring per social–ecological–technical (SET) city dimension: each intervention classified as incremental / reformistic / transformative based on interpretive coding of available documentation against established transformation theory.
  • Descriptive statistics and visualization: R-based analysis; Sankey diagrams (networkD3) for funding–actor–goal flows (Fig. 4); radar/circular bar charts (ggplot2, Fig. 2) for regional feature distributions; QGIS for geographic city-level examples (Fig. 3).

① Quantify NbS co-benefit accessibility: extend the 216-intervention database with spatial equity metrics

Extending this paperThe paper codes 216 NbS interventions with categorical features (hazard type, ecosystem type, actors) but never computes who can actually reach each NbS intervention and at what travel cost. A direct extension: geo-locate the 130 cities and their NbS projects (already in the Zenodo dataset) and apply a 2SFCA-style spatial accessibility analysis — treating NbS interventions as "supply" and vulnerable urban populations as "demand" — to measure whether the communities that face the highest climate exposure (as the paper argues) are also those within walkable or transit-accessible distance. This would reframe the paper's governance findings (e.g., that Global South NbS are underfunded) through a spatial equity lens, showing precisely which neighborhoods receive co-benefits and which remain underserved. Linking to the Urban Nature Atlas and ClimateADAPT geometries could scale this analysis across the European subset immediately.

Added value to my researchThis is a natural application of my R2SFCA and 2SFCA toolkit to a new supply type: green/blue infrastructure as a health-relevant resource. Climate-adaptive NbS reduce heat, flooding, and mental-health burdens — all outcomes central to my health-geography agenda. Mapping NbS accessibility across the 130-city dataset would produce a GeoAI training corpus linking sociodemographic vulnerability, NbS type, and realized spatial accessibility — directly feeding explainable ML models that predict which neighborhood-level factors determine NbS equity outcomes.

② Map the Global South gap with machine-learning-assisted systematic discovery of under-reported NbS

Extending this paperThe paper's central limitation — 63% of interventions are in Europe — stems from the structural bias of the nine databases it drew from. A key extension of the paper's own agenda: apply supervised ML (NLP classifiers + geospatial filtering) to a broader corpus of grey literature (municipal reports, NGO assessments, climate fund evaluations in Portuguese, Spanish, French, Swahili, Arabic) to surface NbS interventions in the Global South that simply did not appear in the original nine databases. The paper's own coding framework (a IUCN-aligned 10-feature schema) provides the labeled training data. This mirrors the approach taken by recent systematic urban climate stocktakes — using ML to classify tens of thousands of documents — but oriented toward discovering real implementations rather than academic publications.

Added value to my researchMy multi-source data fusion agenda (mobile-phone records, social media, remote sensing) maps directly onto this challenge: NbS interventions in the Global South often leave stronger footprints in social media and satellite imagery (vegetation cover changes, reduced urban heat island signatures) than in structured databases. Fine-tuned geospatial foundation models (e.g., adapted remote sensing transformers) could detect and date vegetation-based NbS deployments from Sentinel imagery — providing an independent, spatially explicit audit of the Grey-literature-derived database.

③ Model green gentrification risk as a spatial spillover from NbS locations

Extending this paperThe paper flags that only 20% of its interventions address distributional social justice — and explicitly cites green gentrification as a key risk. Yet it cannot quantify this risk: it codes only whether an intervention intends to address distributional justice, not whether it produces displacement or housing-price effects. A direct extension: link the Zenodo intervention database to property-price panel data and census poverty indices for the 130 cities (where available), and model whether NbS interventions create measurable rent increases in surrounding neighborhoods using MGWR or spatial difference-in-differences, treating NbS installation date and location as the treatment. Heterogeneous treatment effects across cities at different income levels (Global North vs. South) would address the paper's call for context-sensitive governance.

Added value to my researchThis connects to my public-facility accessibility and housing prices research thread. I can apply my MGWR/GWR toolkit to model spatially varying rent gradients around NbS installations, building a generalized "green amenity spillover" model that is explicitly governance-conditioned — distinguishing whether interventions with procedural+distributional justice coding actually suppress displacement relative to procedural-only ones. This is both a methods contribution (spatial causal inference at global scale) and a policy tool for urban planners designing just NbS.

Cross-ref (corpus): Green gentrification in European and North American cities — 10.1038/s41467-022-31572-1

④ Build a CBS-nexus score and optimize NbS placement to maximize multi-hazard co-benefits

Extending this paperThe paper's ten-feature coding framework implicitly defines a CBS-nexus "completeness score" — how many of climate, biodiversity, and social challenges does each NbS simultaneously address? Currently this is analyzed only descriptively (52% address only one vulnerability aspect). A direct extension: formalize a composite CBS-nexus index per intervention and per city, then use it as the objective function in a multi-objective facility-location optimization that jointly maximizes climate-hazard reduction, biodiversity value (ecosystem type), and social equity (recognition + distributional justice coding). The Zenodo database already contains the input features; the optimization would guide future NbS siting decisions, operationalizing the paper's recommendations into a planning tool.

Added value to my researchThis generalizes my EV charging-station efficiency-vs-equity optimization framework to a more complex, multi-criteria setting. Replacing a two-objective (efficiency + equity) with a three-objective (climate resilience + biodiversity + social justice) formulation, and using the SET transformative-capacity score as a post-hoc validation metric, extends my facility-location methods to nature-based urban infrastructure. The CBS-nexus index itself — a structured multi-attribute value function over categorical coding — would be a reusable GeoAI feature set for urban-health predictive models.

⑤ Use community detection and network analysis to reveal knowledge-transfer pathways across the 130-city NbS network

Extending this paperThe paper calls for better knowledge transfer among cities, especially from transformative "bright spots" to cities with weaker NbS practice. The Zenodo dataset contains intervention metadata including funding source, implementing actor, and city — enough to construct a bipartite city–database network (which cities share funding sources, actor types, or NbS approaches). Applying network community detection (Louvain / spectral clustering) to this graph would reveal which cities form natural knowledge-exchange communities and which are structurally isolated. Overlaying the SET transformative-capacity scores on the network would identify whether high-transformative "bright spot" cities are network hubs (and thus potential diffusion nodes) or peripheral — a crucial finding for directing knowledge-transfer policy.

Added value to my researchNetwork community detection is one of my core methods (applied previously to job-housing networks and urban agglomeration). Applying it here to a multi-layer city network (layer 1: shared funding sources; layer 2: shared actor types; layer 3: shared CBS challenges) would extend my spatiotemporal network toolkit to a governance context. The resulting community structure could be spatially mapped and analyzed with my spatiotemporal kernel density methods — producing a GeoAI model of NbS knowledge diffusion that generalizes to other urban governance transfer problems (e.g., 15-minute city policy adoption, telehealth deployment).
