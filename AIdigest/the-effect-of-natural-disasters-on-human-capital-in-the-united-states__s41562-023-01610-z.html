The effect of natural disasters on human capital in the United States

Isaac M. Opper · R. Jisung Park · Lucas Husted Published 2023-06-01 DOI 10.1038/s41562-023-01610-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using the universe of Presidential Disaster Declarations across the United States (1998–2018), this study shows that natural disasters inflict persistent, scarring damage to human capital — reducing standardized test scores by up to 0.023 standard deviations and post-secondary enrolment by ~2.5% after the largest disasters. Crucially, the present discounted value of these human capital losses rivals assessed physical property damage in magnitude: disasters causing more than $500 per-capita property damage reduce a county's human capital by an estimated $268 per capita ($1,300–$1,500 per student). Current disaster-relief policy, which focuses almost exclusively on physical damages, fails to account for this invisible but equally consequential cost.

★

Why It Matters

  • Fills a critical policy blind spot. Most disaster relief — including FEMA's Public Assistance and Individual Assistance grants — is allocated entirely on the basis of physical damage thresholds, ignoring the potentially equal-magnitude costs to human capital. This paper makes those costs visible and quantifiable.
  • Evidence at national scale with quasi-experimental design. By exploiting within-county, over-time variation in disaster timing and intensity (rather than comparing disaster-prone vs. non-prone regions), the study provides the most credible causal estimates to date across all disaster types nationwide.
  • Scarring effects, not transitory shocks. Test scores do not recover in the years after a disaster, and post-secondary enrolment does not rebound within 3 years — meaning communities that received no targeted educational assistance have permanently lower human capital stocks.
  • Links disaster exposure to long-run inequality. Because disasters are unevenly distributed across and within countries, these educational effects compound pre-existing socioeconomic disparities and may be an underappreciated driver of regional economic divergence.
  • Informs the social cost of carbon. As the authors note, if climate change increases disaster frequency and intensity, these human capital losses are a direct climate externality that should enter estimates of the social cost of carbon — broadening how we price climate risk.

Core Methods

  • Quasi-experimental panel design: exploit within-county, year-to-year variation in disaster frequency and intensity to estimate causal effects, removing time-invariant county characteristics and controlling for year fixed effects — avoiding the confound that disaster-prone counties differ systematically in student achievement.
  • Binned disaster intensity: categorize per-capita property damage into four bins (small: $1–$10; medium: $10–$100; large: $100–$500; very large: >$500 per capita) to flexibly estimate a dose-response relationship without imposing a log-linear or linear functional form.
  • Year-on-year change as outcome: the dependent variable is the year-to-year change in test scores, enrolment, or graduation rate, which differences out level differences across counties and isolates the within-unit shock of a disaster.
  • Pre-disaster placebo test: run the same regression using changes in the year before the disaster as the outcome (red dashed line in Fig. 3); the absence of pre-trends confirms the causal interpretation.
  • Dynamic lags specification: estimate effects at multiple post-disaster years to assess persistence; findings show no reversion to pre-disaster test scores or enrolment for any disaster size class.
  • Migration robustness: use IRS county-level migration flows to rule out that out-migration of high-achieving students is driving observed test-score declines; confirms effects persist in low-migration subsamples and when holding school racial composition fixed.
  • Human capital monetization (Jorgenson-Fraumeni method): translate estimated achievement and attainment losses into present discounted values of lifetime earnings using published estimates of the causal earnings returns to test scores and to years of schooling, enabling comparison with physical property damage on a common dollar scale.

① Spatially explicit disaster-to-human-capital pathways via GeoAI and mobility data

Extending this paperThe paper's quasi-experimental design uses county-year aggregates, which cannot distinguish where within a county the learning disruption is most severe — a critical limitation for targeting remedial educational investment. A direct extension would decompose county-level effects to the sub-county level using fine-grained mobile-phone mobility data: measure school-level attendance disruption trajectories post-disaster and link them to neighborhood-level socioeconomic vulnerability. Integrating neighborhood inequality metrics (e.g., educational attainment of surrounding population) would reveal which community types bear the greatest human capital cost from the same disaster intensity, making the paper's aggregate estimates spatially actionable.

Added value to my researchMy multi-source data fusion pipeline (mobile phone records, remote sensing, POIs) is directly applicable: disaster-disrupted mobility traces from GPS/phone data can serve as a near-real-time early signal of school-attendance collapse, enabling a spatiotemporal kernel density approach to map the human capital "shock footprint" far before SEDA-style administrative data become available. This extends my spatiotemporal analysis agenda to disaster-resilience monitoring at the neighborhood scale.

② Socioeconomic vulnerability as a moderator: intersecting disaster exposure with neighborhood inequality

Extending this paperThe paper documents aggregate effects and notes unequal disaster exposure across counties, but does not formally test whether the human capital damage is larger in poorer or racially marginalized neighborhoods within the affected counties. An extension would link the county-level damage estimates from SHELDUS to neighborhood-level socioeconomic data (census tract income, educational attainment, race) to produce an interaction model — essentially asking whether the same $500 per-capita disaster causes twice the test-score loss in high-poverty versus low-poverty neighborhoods. The paper's own racial-composition robustness check hints at this heterogeneity but does not exploit it as a primary finding.

Added value to my researchThis aligns with my health geography agenda: the same socioeconomic vulnerability dimensions that predict healthcare accessibility gaps (low income, minority status, digital divide) are precisely the moderators the paper leaves unmeasured. An explainable ML (SHAP/XGBoost) model trained to predict disaster-induced human capital loss from neighborhood covariates would yield policy-relevant vulnerability maps — a GeoAI version of the paper's aggregate causal estimates.

③ Integrating heat-induced learning loss with disaster-induced learning loss into a unified climate-education model

Extending this paperThe paper explicitly benchmarks its disaster effects against the learning losses caused by hot school days (Park, 2020 — cited as ref. 6), noting that "very large" disasters reduce learning by an amount comparable to 40 hot days above 80°F. This creates a natural integration opportunity: build a county-level panel model that jointly estimates disaster shocks and chronic heat exposure as two distinct but potentially interacting climate-education pathways. The paper currently treats them separately; a unified model would reveal whether counties simultaneously exposed to heat stress and high disaster frequency suffer compounding (super-additive) human capital losses — a mechanism with direct implications for climate-change adaptation planning.

Combined with my agent-based modeling capabilities, I could simulate climate-scenario pathways for human capital stocks under different warming trajectories — linking GeoAI predictions to long-run economic inequality forecasts.

④ Cascading economic spillovers: from county-level human capital loss to regional economic networks

Extending this paperThe paper estimates human capital losses at the county level and compares them to physical damages, but treats each county as an isolated unit. In reality, human capital — especially post-secondary workers and students — flows across county boundaries via migration and commuting. Extending the paper with a network-based spillover model would trace how a disaster in county A reduces skilled-labor supply in connected counties B, C, D (via depressed college enrolment that propagates to regional labor markets), thereby amplifying the aggregate economic cost estimated by the paper's conservative local-county calculation. The paper's own IRS migration data and its human capital monetization framework provide the inputs needed for such a spatial propagation model.

Added value to my researchMy work on urban agglomeration, job-housing networks, and network community detection positions me to model the regional spillover graph. Specifically, a spatial network approach — with commuting zones or metro areas as nodes and inter-county migration as edges — would quantify the shadow human capital loss that disaster-affected counties externalize onto their neighbors. This is a natural extension of my regional-collaboration and urban-agglomeration research theme.

Cross-ref (corpus): Intercity economic ties amplify natural disasters spillovers in China — 10.1038/s44284-026-00399-y

⑤ Displacement and educational access: linking post-disaster migration to school-facility accessibility

Extending this paperThe paper establishes that very large disasters (>$500 per-capita damage) generate modest but significant out-migration of approximately 37 households per 100,000 residents and that this migration is associated with reduced post-secondary enrolment. However, it cannot determine whether displaced students drop out entirely or are forced to attend lower-quality or more distant schools. A richer extension would combine the paper's IRS migration flows with a spatial accessibility model of educational facilities — measuring whether counties receiving disaster-induced migrants have sufficient college capacity to absorb them, and whether migrant students face longer travel times or worse facility-to-student ratios at destination counties.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my 2SFCA/R2SFCA spatial accessibility methodology: post-disaster counties can be characterized by their disaster-adjusted educational-supply-to-demand ratio, revealing whether the geographic concentration of higher-education facilities exacerbates human capital losses from disaster-induced displacement. My EV-charging efficiency-vs-equity work also transfers: the same optimization framework can be used to site temporary educational facilities in disaster-affected areas to minimize post-disaster access disparities.
