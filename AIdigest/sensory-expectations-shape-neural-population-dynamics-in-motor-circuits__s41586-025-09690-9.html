Sensory expectations shape neural population dynamics in motor circuits

Jonathan A. Michaels · Mehrdad Kashefi · Jack Zheng · Olivier Codol · Jeffrey Weiler · Rhonda Kersten · Jonathan C. Lau · Paul L. Gribble · Jörn Diedrichsen · J. Andrew Pruszynski Published 2025-10-29 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09690-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Motor circuits do not merely prepare for self-initiated movements — they also pre-configure themselves for expected sensory disturbances. When humans or monkeys receive probabilistic cues about upcoming mechanical perturbations, sensory expectations are encoded widely across the brain (including motor cortex) as a simple, linearly scaled neural geometry. After perturbation onset, a condition-independent signal shifts the neural state, enabling rapid corrective responses that initially reflect prior expectations rather than actual perturbation direction. Recurrent neural network models coupled to a biomechanical arm confirm that this geometry emerges only when a non-directional perturbation signal gates the resolution of sensory uncertainty.

★

Why It Matters

  • Unifies two previously separate motor-control frameworks. Movement preparation has been studied almost exclusively for self-initiated actions; this work shows the same preparatory machinery also encodes sensory expectations, bridging voluntary and reactive motor control.
  • Reveals a simple, probabilistic neural geometry. Sensory expectation signals scale linearly with perturbation probability — a striking organizing principle found across six brain areas including primary motor cortex, premotor cortex, and thalamus, but notably absent from sensory thalamus (VPL).
  • Identifies a condition-independent gating signal. A perturbation-detection signal that is direction-agnostic shifts the neural state before directional information is available, explaining the speed and probability-modulation of long-latency reflexes.
  • Computational model reproduces and explains the geometry. RNN experiments with a biomechanical arm model show that both a realistic feedback-timing delay and the condition-independent signal are necessary for sensory expectations to emerge, providing a constructive mechanistic theory.
  • Open data deposit. All primate electrophysiology data are openly deposited in Dryad, enabling community reanalysis and model benchmarking.

Core Methods

  • Probabilistic perturbation paradigm: discrete probability cues (0%, 25%, 50%, 75%, 100%) signal the likelihood of elbow flexion or extension perturbation, enabling parametric manipulation of sensory expectation.
  • High-density population decoding: SVM classifiers (5-fold cross-validated) applied to population activity to decode perturbation direction over time and detect perturbation onset independent of direction; classifier velocity used to timestamp information emergence.
  • Representational dissimilarity matrix (RDM) + Shapley-value decomposition: neural geometry compared to four model RDMs (Probability, Perturbation, Prediction Error, Unsigned Prediction Error) using non-negative least-squares + Shapley values to estimate each model's unique contribution to explaining the neural population structure.
  • RNN–biomechanical arm model: 32 RNN instances trained end-to-end to perform reaching with realistic muscle dynamics and feedback delays; ablation experiments isolate which architectural features are necessary for sensory-expectation geometry to emerge.
  • EMG epoch analysis: short-latency reflex (SLR, 20–50 ms), long-latency reflex (LLR, 50–100 ms), and voluntary epoch (100–150 ms) compared across probability conditions via repeated-measures ANOVA (F-tests reported for brachioradialis and medial triceps).

① Use a foundation model of neural activity to benchmark and generalize the paper's RDM geometry

Extending this paperThe paper's Shapley-value RDM analysis characterizes sensory-expectation geometry within a single perturbation paradigm in two monkeys. A direct extension is to test whether this geometry is task-specific or a general organizing principle: applying a large-scale foundation model of neural activity — pre-trained on diverse electrophysiology recordings — to predict responses in the paper's six areas would test transferability of the probability-scaling axis without task-specific fitting, and could identify which latent dimensions correspond to it.

Added value to my researchFoundation-model thinking maps onto my agenda of fine-tuning geospatial foundation models: just as a neural foundation model generalizes across brain areas and tasks, a geospatial foundation model generalizes spatiotemporal prediction across cities and health-system contexts. ② Interpret the paper's condition-independent gating signal as an attractor-state transition

Extending this paperThe paper identifies a condition-independent perturbation signal that shifts the neural population state before directional information is resolved. This is the signature of a low-dimensional attractor transition: the system moves along an attractor manifold before reading out the direction dimension. Extending the analysis by fitting attractor geometry to the population trajectory in the perturbation window would test whether the gating signal marks the entry point into an attractor that holds transient sensory uncertainty, and how its geometry relates to known motor-state attractors.

Added value to my researchThe attractor-state metaphor transfers to spatiotemporal graph learning: urban systems exhibit quasi-stable regimes (rush-hour, off-peak, event-driven) with rapid transitions analogous to condition-independent shifts. Encoding these regime transitions as graph-level attractor states could improve physics-informed GNNs for traffic or accessibility prediction.

③ Use minimal interpretable RNNs to discriminate the cognitive computation behind probability scaling

Extending this paperThe paper's RNN models are trained end-to-end on a motor-control objective and analyzed post-hoc for geometry. An extension is to use tiny, interpretable RNNs designed specifically to compare competing computational hypotheses — Bayesian integration, drift-diffusion, value-based updating — and test which best accounts for the probability-scaling axis identified in real neural data. This would move the paper's RNN from a "plausibility demonstration" to a theory-discriminating tool.

Added value to my researchMinimal, interpretable RNNs are a methodological model for my use of explainable machine learning for spatial prediction: the same philosophy — train small models, dissect what computation they perform — applies to building interpretable GeoAI models for health-access prediction where policy transparency is mandatory.

④ Test whether arousal modulates the amplitude of the paper's expectation geometry

Extending this paperThe paper treats preparatory neural geometry as driven purely by explicit probability cues, but arousal state (pupil dilation, cortical excitability) is known to modulate brain-wide dynamics. A direct extension: augment recording sessions with continuous arousal monitoring and include it as a covariate in the Shapley-value RDM analysis to test whether the amplitude of the probability-scaling geometry is modulated by arousal — distinguishing attentional amplification from pure Bayesian prior encoding.

⑤ Encode the paper's feedback-timing constraint as a physics prior in a compact neural network model

Extending this paperThe paper's key modeling insight is that feedback delay is necessary for sensory-expectation geometry to emerge: without a realistic timing gap, networks do not develop probability-scaled preparatory states. This is a physics constraint (biological propagation delay) enforced implicitly in training. An extension: reformulate it as an explicit architectural prior — a delay-aware physics-informed RNN with hard-coded minimum feedback latency — and test whether compact models with this prior outperform unconstrained large models on held-out perturbation stimuli, directly testing the paper's mechanistic claim with minimal parameters.

Added value to my researchThis is the bridge to my physics-informed graph learning agenda: just as biological delay is a physics constraint that shapes neural geometry, travel-time constraints (network routing delays, service catchment physics) can be hard-coded as graph-level priors in GNN-based accessibility models, making them sample-efficient and transferable across cities — exactly the challenge in my spatial accessibility work.
