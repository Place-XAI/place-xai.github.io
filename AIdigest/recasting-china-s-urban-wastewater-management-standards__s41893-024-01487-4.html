Recasting China's urban wastewater management standards

Wen-Wei Li · Han-Qing Yu Published 2024-11-22 DOI 10.1038/s41893-024-01487-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

China's current urban wastewater discharge standards are overly uniform, ecologically disconnected, and temporally rigid, and are likely to hinder rather than advance urban water sustainability. The authors argue for a shift toward holistic, localized, ecologically based, and dynamically adaptive pollutant discharge standards — covering emerging contaminants, nutrient criteria calibrated to local eutrophication risk, carbon-aware treatment incentives, and robust regulations for reclaimed water reuse — drawing on experience from the US, EU, Australia, and Israel.

★

Why It Matters

  • China is the world's largest producer and user of chemicals, and its wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs) handle an enormous and chemically heterogeneous load — getting the discharge standards wrong propagates aquatic ecosystem damage at national scale.
  • Current standards are ecologically blind. Existing national and regional standards focus on total nutrients and a limited set of toxicants, ignoring eutrophication sensitivity, self-purification capacity, and the actual biodiversity risks in receiving water bodies.
  • Emerging pollutants — pharmaceuticals, personal care products, antimicrobial resistance genes — are not regulated at all under current Chinese discharge law, yet China's consumption profile makes these a leading aquatic ecological risk.
  • Wastewater reuse is under-governed. China has ambitious national reuse targets but nearly no quality standards for reclaimed water (except a rudimentary irrigation standard), creating both safety gaps and barriers to adoption.
  • Lessons from the US, EU, Australia, and Israel are mapped onto China's regulatory gaps, offering a concrete, comparative policy roadmap that is directly transferable to other rapidly urbanizing nations.

Core Methods

  • Comparative regulatory analysis: structured comparison of China's national and regional wastewater discharge standards against the US NPDES pretreatment program, EU Urban Wastewater Treatment Directive, Australian marine discharge rules, and Israel's reclaimed water irrigation standards — identifying specific gaps and reform opportunities.
  • Contaminant-class diagnosis: categorizing China's regulatory blind spots by pollutant class — biodegradable organics, nutrients (N/P), emerging contaminants (pharmaceuticals, personal care products), antimicrobial resistance genes, combined sewer overflow (CSO) — and proposing treatment/monitoring responses for each.
  • Ecological framing: applying eutrophication sensitivity and water body self-purification capacity as primary axes for calibrating local discharge criteria, informed by nutrient modelling approaches used in the US and EU.
  • Policy instrument mapping: cataloguing enabling instruments — pollutant discharge taxes (polluter pays), tradable discharge permits, technology mandates (quaternary treatment, advanced oxidation, UV disinfection), AI/remote-sensing-assisted monitoring, and demonstration-region pilots.
  • Stakeholder framework: placing regulatory reform within a three-actor model (policymakers, technology innovators, market instruments) to identify implementation barriers and concerted-action prerequisites.
