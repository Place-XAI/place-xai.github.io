Learning produces an orthogonalized state machine in the hippocampus

Sun, Weinan · Winnubst, Johan · Natrajan, Maanasa · Lai, Chongxi · Kajikawa, Koichiro · Bast, Arco · Michaelos, Michalis · Gattoni, Rachel · Stringer, Carsen · Flickinger, Daniel · Fitzgerald, James E. · Spruston, Nelson Published 2025-02-12 DOI 10.1038/s41586-024-08548-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

As mice learn a memory-guided virtual-reality task, hippocampal CA1 neurons progressively decorrelate their activity for visually identical but task-distinct states, ultimately forming an orthogonalized state machine (OSM) that encodes the task's latent structure rather than its sensory surface. Of the computational models tested — including LSTMs, transformers, and vanilla RNNs — only the clone-structured causal graph (CSCG), a hidden Markov model variant, reproduces both the final orthogonalized representations and the step-by-step learning trajectory, pointing to hidden state inference as a fundamental algorithmic principle of hippocampal cognitive map formation.

Why It Matters

  • First longitudinal window into cognitive-map formation at scale. Two-photon random access mesoscope imaging tracked thousands of CA1 neurons per mouse across many weeks — providing an empirical trajectory of learning, not just a before/after snapshot.
  • Constrains the algorithm, not just the anatomy. The paper moves beyond "where" cognitive maps live to show "how" they are built: through sequential latent state inference implemented via progressive decorrelation steps.
  • Rules out dominant AI architectures. LSTMs and transformers fail to reproduce hippocampal dynamics unless their cost function is explicitly modified, motivating a rethink of how AI systems form internal world models.
  • Identifies flexible reuse as a core property. Once the OSM is formed, mice learn new cue sets roughly 3x faster; neural representations flexibly incorporate novel states, matching how intelligent agents generalize efficiently.
  • Bridges neuroscience and AI design. The CSCG's success and the Hebbian-RNN's partial success directly suggest which architectural choices — soft winner-take-all, discrete latent states, expectation-maximization — deserve more attention in AI.

Core Methods

  • Longitudinal two-photon calcium imaging: GCaMP6f fluorescence acquired with a random access mesoscope; Suite2P-style cell segmentation and cross-session neuron registration to track thousands of individual neurons over weeks.
  • Population vector (PV) cross-correlation analysis: near-trial vs. far-trial population vectors computed at each track position per session, producing a correlation matrix that tracks progressive decorrelation and orthogonalization over learning.
  • UMAP dimensionality reduction: all sessions embedded jointly in 3D UMAP space; topological evolution (unstructured → hub-and-spoke → ring → split-shank ring) visualizes the OSM formation trajectory.
  • Single-cell tuning characterization: difference score (|Anear − Afar| / max) vs. near–far correlation coefficient maps cells onto a continuous 2D phenotype space spanning place cells through splitter cells to remapping splitter cells.
  • Coefficient of partial determination (CPD): four behavioural strategies used as regressors; CPD isolates each strategy's unique contribution, documenting sequential emergence over learning sessions.
  • Clone-structured causal graph (CSCG): HMM variant with deterministic emission and clone states; trained via Baum–Welch expectation-maximization on the task's sensory observation sequence.
  • RNN and LSTM / transformer comparison: multiple architectures trained with backpropagation through time for next-sensory-input prediction; final cross-correlation matrices and decorrelation timelines compared to CA1 data.
  • Hebbian-RNN: spiking RNN with soft winner-take-all feedback inhibition and local timing-based Hebbian plasticity; trained without any global cost function or end-to-end supervision.

① Transfer the CSCG latent-state architecture to spatiotemporal sequence modeling of urban mobility

Extending this paperThe paper's key contribution is identifying the CSCG — a HMM that infers hidden latent states from ambiguous sensory sequences — as the uniquely successful model of hippocampal learning. The paper validates this on a short, two-alternative VR task. A direct extension is to test the CSCG at greater task complexity: longer sequences, more latent states, or multi-environment settings where the same cues recur in different contexts. Pairing CSCG with graph neural network encoders that represent the relational structure of the environment (rather than a flat sensory sequence) would move the model from laboratory scale toward real-world sequence complexity.

Added value to my researchGPS trajectories and mobile-phone mobility records present exactly the same challenge the 2ACDC task poses: visually similar locations (e.g., two identical subway stations) must be differentiated using context and sequential history rather than raw coordinates. Adopting the CSCG's clone-state approach for urban mobility sequence modeling would enable latent trip-purpose inference (commute vs. leisure vs. errand) from indistinguishable traces — a direct enhancement to my spatiotemporal kernel density and mobile-phone data fusion work. The OSM concept also maps naturally onto origin–destination state inference in activity-based travel demand models.

② Use the paper's decorrelation-sequence signature as an evaluation criterion for geospatial foundation models

Extending this paperThe paper introduces a powerful quantitative learning signature: the ordered sequence in which cross-correlations drop (off-diagonal first, then pre-R2, then pre-R1). This ordering is specific enough to discriminate all seven computational models tested. A productive extension of the paper would be to use this decorrelation trajectory as a benchmark criterion rather than only as a finding: given any new architecture or learning rule, one could ask whether it reproduces the biological decorrelation sequence. This would turn an empirical observation into a rigorous evaluation metric for "hippocampus-like" internal representations in AI systems.

Added value to my researchGeospatial foundation models (pre-trained on satellite imagery or street-view data) currently lack principled diagnostics for whether their internal embeddings have captured functional spatial structure versus surface texture. The paper's framework suggests a concrete evaluation protocol: after fine-tuning, measure whether spatially similar but functionally distinct locations (e.g., two parks with very different accessibility scores) are orthogonalized in the model's embedding space. This would directly strengthen my work on fine-tuning geospatial foundation models for accessibility and health-geography prediction.

③ Apply local Hebbian plasticity as a privacy-compatible learning rule for federated health-geography models

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that a spiking RNN with soft winner-take-all dynamics and local Hebbian plasticity — no global cost function, no backpropagation — builds OSM-like representations. An important extension is to quantify the performance gap between the Hebbian-RNN and the CSCG as task complexity grows, and to test whether augmenting Hebbian-RNN with a local reward signal (rather than purely unsupervised Hebbian updates) closes this gap. The paper's biologically plausible model opens a path to understanding how hippocampal circuits could build world models without global error signals.

Added value to my researchIn federated health analytics, data cannot leave individual hospitals or clinics. Local Hebbian-style plasticity — where each node updates using only local activity, not gradients from a central server — is architecturally aligned with federated learning constraints. The paper's proof-of-concept that local learning builds high-quality latent representations motivates applying sWTA-regularized local learning rules in my spatial primary-care accessibility models, enabling each geographic unit (census tract or health region) to contribute to a shared spatial representation without centralizing patient records.

④ Map the OSM's tuning-phenotype continuum onto a functional typology of urban spatial units

Extending this paperA notable methodological contribution of the paper is the 2D feature space (difference score vs. correlation coefficient) that reveals a continuum of CA1 response phenotypes rather than discrete cell types — from pure place cells through splitter cells to remapping splitter cells. An extension of the paper would quantify how this phenotype distribution shifts as a function of task complexity, environmental familiarity, or pharmacological manipulation, and test whether the proportion of splitter cells at intermediate training stages predicts final OSM quality across individual mice.

Added value to my researchUrban spatial units show an analogous duality: some areas are functionally unique (a hospital zone, a transit hub with no spatial neighbor) while others are visually similar but contextually distinct (two residential blocks differing markedly in 2SFCA accessibility). Applying the paper's 2D tuning-space logic to GeoAI embeddings of urban units would produce a data-driven "splitter-unit score" — distinguishing spatial units whose functional identity is robustly encoded from those whose representation is ambiguous and context-dependent. This is directly applicable to my explainable ML work on MGWR and spatial prediction models.

⑤ Connect OSM flexible reuse to cross-city transfer learning in spatial planning models

Extending this paperFig. 5 of the paper shows that once an OSM is established, mice reuse it for novel indicator cues roughly 3x faster than original learning, with new cue-specific states plugged into the existing scaffold. An important extension would test whether the structure of the pre-learned OSM (number of states, depth of decorrelation, proportion of splitter cells) predicts transfer speed to a qualitatively different task — moving from reusing states to generalizing the OSM's topology to a novel environment with a different layout entirely. This would link the paper's transfer finding to the broader AI literature on meta-learning and fast adaptation.

Added value to my researchThe OSM reuse finding provides a biological justification for cross-city transfer in urban spatial models: a model pre-trained on one set of cities should rapidly adapt to new cities by plugging in city-specific "indicator states" (local demographics, policy context, built-environment features) without re-learning the underlying spatial structure. This directly motivates my geospatial foundation model fine-tuning agenda and suggests a concrete architecture — a frozen backbone (the OSM scaffold) combined with lightweight city-specific adapters (the new indicator states) — evaluated against R2SFCA service-area models to measure how much the spatial-accessibility scaffold transfers across city types.
