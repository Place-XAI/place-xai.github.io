Predicting bone regeneration from machine learning

Zhiyong Li Published 2021-08-20 DOI 10.1038/s43588-021-00116-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A machine learning-based multiscale framework using three coupled neural networks replaces computationally prohibitive finite-element homogenization in tissue scaffolds, enabling efficient and accurate prediction of bone formation across thousands of representative volume elements. The method inversely identifies mechanobiological remodelling parameters from in vivo imaging data and validates predictions against a 12-month longitudinal sheep study, substantially outperforming conventional approaches in both speed and realism.

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the computational wall in scaffold design. Classic multilevel finite-element analysis for a scaffold with ~2,330 unit cells is computationally intractable for design optimization; the ML surrogate makes iterative scaffold evaluation feasible.
  • Closes the in vivo – in silico loop. Neural network NN-3 correlates machine-learning outputs with real animal imaging data, enabling inverse identification of remodelling parameters — coupling data-driven learning directly to experimental validation.
  • Opens the door to patient-specific scaffold design. By encoding subject-specific biomechanical environments, the framework lays groundwork for personalized treatment of critical-sized bone defects, moving tissue engineering from bench to clinic.
  • A reusable template beyond bone. The commentary notes that the ML-multiscale strategy anticipates extension to vascular, cartilage, and other tissue regeneration models where multi-physics complexity similarly overwhelms FE solvers.

Core Methods

  • Three-network neural architecture: NN-1 performs homogenization — predicting effective elastic tensors from RVE microstructure, replacing microscopic FE runs; NN-2 predicts strain energy density (SED) for each RVE from bone density inputs; NN-3 performs inverse parameter identification by outputting Pearson correlation coefficients between in vivo and in silico bone density distributions.
  • Wolff's law mechanobiological remodelling: bone density is iteratively updated using the SED as a mechanical stimulus within a classical Wolff's law model, but with ML-derived parameters rather than manually tuned ones.
  • Multilevel (macroscopic–microscopic) coupling: homogenized properties from NN-1 feed a macroscopic scaffold-bone-host FE model, which in turn provides boundary conditions for microscopic RVE analyses handled by NN-2 — ML acts as the bridge between scales.
  • Longitudinal validation: the first 9 months of in vivo sheep data train the inverse model; month-12 bone ingrowth distribution is predicted and compared against experimental observations as a hold-out test.
  • Computational efficiency comparison: runtime against conventional multilevel FE is benchmarked on the 2,330-unit-cell scaffold to quantify the speedup — demonstrating feasibility of iterative scaffold optimization.

① Replace the paper's three-NN pipeline with a differentiable end-to-end solver

Extending this paperThe framework chains three separate neural networks (NN-1 → NN-2 → NN-3) trained independently. Each handoff propagates approximation error without the earlier networks knowing the downstream objective. A direct extension: reformulate the entire multiscale pipeline as a single differentiable graph where gradients flow end-to-end from the inverse-identification loss back through the SED prediction and homogenization stages. This would eliminate error accumulation across stages and allow joint optimization — sharpening both the homogenization accuracy of NN-1 and the remodelling parameter quality of NN-3 simultaneously.

③ Add a Bayesian uncertainty layer to the three neural networks for scaffold design under uncertainty

Extending this paperThe current NN-1/NN-2/NN-3 pipeline produces point predictions with no quantification of uncertainty — critical for clinical translation where a scaffold that performs well on average but fails in a subset of patients is unacceptable. Extending the paper: replace each deterministic NN with a Bayesian neural network or Monte Carlo Dropout variant, propagating uncertainty from homogenization (NN-1) through SED prediction (NN-2) and into inverse-identified parameters (NN-3). This would yield credible intervals on bone ingrowth predictions and allow scaffold designs that are robust to worst-case biological variability.
