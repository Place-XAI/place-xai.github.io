Physics-based machine learning for subcellular segmentation in living cells

Arif Ahmed Sekh · Ida S. Opstad · Gustav Godtliebsen · Åsa Birna Birgisdottir · Balpreet Singh Ahluwalia · Krishna Agarwal · Dilip K. Prasad Published 2021-12-15 DOI 10.1038/s42256-021-00420-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Subcellular structure segmentation (mitochondria, vesicles) in fluorescence microscopy is a ground-truth-deficient problem that manual annotation cannot reliably solve. The authors introduce a simulation-supervision paradigm in which a physics-based simulator generates both training images and unambiguous ground truth, enabling a U-Net deep learning model to outperform all existing methods by 7–18% in mean intersection-over-union across nine living- and fixed-cell datasets. Transfer learning across different microscope types makes the approach broadly adoptable without re-acquiring large annotated datasets, and the framework unlocks automated morphological analytics and temporal tracking of individual organelles at scale.

Why It Matters

  • Solves a fundamental bottleneck in cell biology. Manual annotation of subcellular structures is extremely tedious and subjective; even 12 expert scientists could not agree on a reliable consensus ground truth. The physics-based approach bypasses human annotation entirely for training data generation.
  • Bridges physics and deep learning in a principled way. Rather than treating the microscope's physics as noise to ignore, the method embeds the 3D point spread function, fluorophore photokinetics, and noise models directly into simulation — so the network learns to compensate for known physical limitations.
  • 7–18% accuracy gain over all baselines. The method achieves mIOU of 74–76% on live-cell data, outperforming classical image processing (Otsu, adaptive threshold), semi-automatic tools, and even networks trained on manually annotated data.
  • Enables population-scale biological discovery. By segmenting hundreds of organelles automatically across large cell populations, the framework supports statistical morphology analysis and real-time tracking of mitochondria–vesicle interactions — tasks previously impossible at scale.
  • Open data and code. Both the simulation datasets and the trained models are publicly deposited (DataverseNO DOI 10.18710/IDCUCI; code at Zenodo DOI 10.5281/zenodo.5017066), enabling direct reuse and extension.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Imaging data
Nine fluorescence microscopy datasets spanning living and fixed cells of rat cardiomyoblasts (H9C2) and human cancer cells (MCC13). Datasets include mitochondria and vesicular structures imaged under 3 distinct cell-growth conditions (normal, hypoxia, hypoxia+ADM) and drug treatment (CCCP). 30 cells are analyzed in the main live-cell dataset (LiveEpi1RatMitoRed); exact per-dataset cell counts are in Supplementary Fig. 4.
Microscopes used
Three fluorescence microscopes: Epi 1 (GE DeltaVision Elite, epifluorescence), Epi 2 (Zeiss CellDiscoverer 7, epifluorescence), and Airy 1 (Zeiss LSM 880 ELYRA Airyscan). Pixel sizes ~80–120 nm; optical resolution limit ~200–300 nm; imaging rate 50 fps (10 ms exposure) for live-cell datasets.
Simulation datasets
Six physics-based simulation datasets generated by the custom engine: two large datasets of 7,000 image pairs each (SimEpi1Mito, SimEpi1Vesi) and four smaller datasets of 3,000 image pairs each for transfer-learning experiments. Image size: 256×256 pixels (assembled from 128×128 tiles).
Ground truth strategy
Physics-based GT derived from fluorescent molecule emitter positions projected onto the image plane — no manual annotation required for training. Four GT strategies evaluated (Otsu, erosion, noise-level thresholding, physics-based projection); physics-based outperforms all including expert human annotation.
Signal-to-noise ratio
SNR of 2–4 in live-cell imaging — an extremely challenging low-light regime at the limit of optical detection.

Core Methods

  • Physics-based simulation engine (6-step pipeline): (1) compute 3D structure geometry; (2) place fluorescent molecules and model photokinetics; (3–4) apply the microscope's 3D point spread function (PSF) to generate synthetic images; (5) add realistic camera noise; (6) compute unambiguous GT from noise-free emitter positions. All steps are grounded in optics and photophysics.
  • Physics-based ground truth: project emitter positions onto the image plane, max-pool by pixel size, and binarize — producing GT independent of the microscope PSF and noise. Outperforms all conventional GT strategies including expert human annotation and Otsu-based thresholding on noise-free images.
  • U-Net segmentation with EfficientNet-B3 backbone: five encoder backbones evaluated (ResNet50, ResNet100, VGG16, Inception-V3, EfficientNet-B3); EfficientNet-B3 selected as best-performing. Input/output: 256×256. Training: 60/20/20 split; early stopping and learning-rate reduction on mIOU plateau; data augmentation (flip, rotation). Hardware: NVIDIA Quadro RTX 6000 (24 GB).
  • Transfer learning across microscopes: a model pre-trained on 7,000 simulation pairs from Epi 1 is fine-tuned on 3,000 simulation pairs from the target microscope (Epi 2 or Airy 1). Generalizes across both epifluorescence types and from epifluorescence to Airyscan, without requiring any real labeled data from the target setup.
  • Morphological analytics pipeline: segmentation masks → skeleton extraction → graph construction (nodes = endpoints or junctions, edges = branches) → rule-based classification into dot/rod/network (mitochondria, by area and max node degree) or small/large (vesicles, by fitted circle radius ≤/> 150 nm).
  • Tracking: Kalman filter combined with the Hungarian algorithm for identity assignment across frames; enables temporal analysis of organelle movement, morphology changes, and mitochondria–vesicle interaction events.
  • Evaluation metric: mean intersection over union (mIOU = TP/(TP+FP+FN)) and F1 score, benchmarked against Otsu, adaptive thresholding, MorphoLibJ, manual thresholding, and iterative backpropagation.

Expanded Ideas — this paper's extensibility & added value to my research

① Extend physics-based GT to self-supervised learning without any real-image labels

Extending this paperThe paper's simulation engine already produces noise-free "oracle" images alongside noisy training images, but still trains in a fully supervised manner using simulated pairs. A direct extension: borrow the self-supervised physics-consistency principle — require the model to predict a physically consistent noise-free image from a noisy input, using the simulation engine's own forward model as the self-supervision signal. This eliminates any residual dependence on accurate simulator parameterization and could close the simulation-to-real gap. The paper already demonstrates the PSF and noise models are accurate enough to transfer across microscopes — the leap to a physics-consistency loss is small.

Added value to my researchThe same paradigm maps to geospatial AI: a model fine-tuned with physics-consistency constraints (e.g., land-surface energy-balance equations applied to satellite radiance) would not need large labeled geospatial datasets for remote sensing tasks. The lesson — physics as a label-free supervisor — directly enables fine-tuning geospatial foundation models with no annotation cost, relevant to my multi-source data fusion work.

② Replace per-microscope fine-tuning with a physics-conditioned meta-learner

Extending this paperThe paper's transfer learning approach requires generating a new simulation dataset each time a researcher uses a different microscope (different PSF, pixel size, noise model), then fine-tuning the network on 3,000 simulated pairs. A stronger extension: encode the microscope parameters (PSF width, pixel size, SNR) as a conditioning vector fed to the network, so a single model can be queried for any microscope configuration without retraining. The paper's own Supplementary Table 2 already catalogs these instrument parameters per microscope — they are ready to serve as conditioning inputs. This would turn an N-microscope fine-tuning pipeline into a single universal model.

Added value to my researchConditioning on instrument/context parameters generalizes to spatiotemporal modeling: if urban morphology parameters (population density, street-network type, land-use mix) are encoded as conditioning inputs, a single spatial prediction model could generalize across city types without city-specific recalibration — a physics-conditioned spatial meta-learner directly applicable to my R2SFCA accessibility work across heterogeneous urban environments.

③ Pretrain a multi-task foundation model on simulated subcellular image data

Extending this paperThe paper trains separate models for mitochondria and vesicles and notes that the simulation engine is extensible to other structures (endoplasmic reticulum, Golgi bodies). With simulation data freely generatable at scale, the natural extension is to pretrain a multi-task foundation model on a large diverse set of simulated subcellular structures, then fine-tune on any target structure with a small real dataset. The paper's own simulation engine becomes an unlimited pretraining data factory — avoiding the bottleneck of scarce real-world annotations that plagues biomedical imaging AI across all modalities.

Added value to my researchThis "simulation-pretraining + task fine-tuning" architecture mirrors the challenge in geospatial AI: large-scale pretraining on globally available synthetic data (e.g., simulated urban textures from OSM + building morphology models), then fine-tuning on small city-specific labeled datasets for urban health or accessibility prediction. The cross-domain lesson is clear: physics simulators are pretraining data generators — applicable to both microscopy and satellite imagery.

Cross-ref (corpus): A multi-task learning strategy to pretrain models for medical image analysis — 10.1038/s43588-024-00666-9

④ Add explainability to the segmentation model to reveal which physical features drive decisions

Extending this paperThe paper's deep learning model is validated quantitatively but remains a black box: it is unclear why EfficientNet-B3 outperforms other backbones, or which spatial frequency components of the PSF-blurred image are most diagnostic for differentiating in-focus from out-of-focus organelles. An explainability extension using gradient-based attribution maps (Grad-CAM or integrated gradients) applied to the simulation-supervised model would reveal which physical image features the network relies on — connecting the learned representation back to the physics encoded in the simulator. This could also expose where the simulation-to-real gap is largest, guiding simulator improvement.

Added value to my researchI work extensively with explainable ML for spatial prediction (MGWR, SHAP on ML ensembles, XAI for GeoAI). Applying the same attribution framework to physics-informed models is a key open question: do physics-guided networks encode physically meaningful spatial features in their attention maps? Answering this in the cell imaging domain provides a method template directly transferable to explainable GeoAI for urban analytics and health geography.

Cross-ref (corpus): Interpretability and implicit model semantics in biomedicine and deep learning — 10.1038/s42256-026-01177-0

⑤ Map the morphological analytics pipeline to a hierarchical spatiotemporal graph for cross-scale analysis

Extending this paperThe paper's morphological analytics (Figs. 4–5) produces skeleton-based graphs of individual mitochondria at the single-cell level but treats each cell independently. An extension: construct a hierarchical spatiotemporal graph with three levels — organelle (skeleton node), cell, and cell population — and learn graph neural network message-passing rules that propagate morphological signals upward to population statistics. This would allow the segmentation + tracking pipeline to answer population-level biological questions (e.g., what fraction of cells show fragmented mitochondria under hypoxia) in a single unified model, rather than post-hoc averaging.

Added value to my researchThis multi-scale graph construction is the methodological bridge to my physics-informed graph learning for urban systems: the three levels (organelle → cell → population) map exactly to (facility → neighborhood → city). The hierarchical message-passing GNN trained on morphological graphs provides a design blueprint for modeling how healthcare facility accessibility propagates to neighborhood health outcomes — a direct cross-domain application of this paper's graph-based analytics architecture.
