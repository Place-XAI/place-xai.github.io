Changing occupational structures and residential segregation in New York, London and Tokyo

Maarten van Ham · Masaya Uesugi · Tiit Tammaru · David Manley · Heleen Janssen Published 2020-08-17 DOI 10.1038/s41562-020-0927-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

All three global cities — New York, London and Tokyo — show a consistent trend of workforce professionalization (growing high-income occupations, shrinking low-income share) from 1980 to 2010–2011, directly contradicting Sassen's social polarization thesis. Despite profound changes in occupational structure, city-wide segregation indices changed only modestly, while the underlying social geography shifted dramatically: gentrification of inner-city neighbourhoods and suburbanization of poverty are the dominant megatrends, with Tokyo remaining the least segregated and most equal of the three cities throughout.

Why It Matters

  • Settles a decades-long debate with longitudinal data. The paper directly tests Sassen's globally influential social polarization thesis using four census waves (1980–2010) across three major global cities, providing the most rigorous longitudinal evidence to date — and finds professionalization, not polarization, as the dominant story.
  • Uncovers the segregation paradox. City-wide dissimilarity indices (ID, MDI) changed little, yet the spatial organization of income groups shifted profoundly — revealing that aggregate segregation measures can mask large-scale geographic restructuring of who lives where.
  • Identifies two global urban megatrends. Gentrification of inner-city areas and suburbanization of poverty appear across all three cities despite vastly different policy contexts (US liberal welfare, UK mixed model, Japanese developmental state), suggesting these are structural features of professionalization-led urban change.
  • Highlights the role of national context. Tokyo's combination of strict immigration controls, active urban renewal policy, low income inequality (Gini 0.33 vs. 0.55 for New York) and vertical segregation patterns shows that outcomes of globalization-driven occupational change are strongly shaped by national institutions — there is no single global city model.
  • Opens the door for 2020 census validation. The paper explicitly notes that the small 2000–2010 increase in low-income occupations in New York and London hints at incipient polarization; the 2020 census round will be decisive for confirming or refuting this signal.

Core Methods

  • Income-based occupational classification: each census year, occupations are ranked by income from matched wage surveys (rather than occupational status alone) and assigned to high-, middle- or low-income groups. This corrects for the time-varying and cross-city income structure of occupational categories.
  • Index of Dissimilarity (ID): measures the share of a group (high-income or low-income) that would need to change neighbourhood to achieve an even distribution across the city; ranges 0–1. Computed for each city and census year.
  • Multi-group Dissimilarity Index (MDI): extends the ID to three groups (high, middle, low) simultaneously, capturing overall multi-class segregation.
  • Quartile concentration mapping: neighbourhoods ranked by the absolute count of each occupational group and classified into Q1–Q4 quartiles, then mapped to visualize spatial concentration and its change over time.
  • Consistent geographies: enumeration districts for 1981 and 1991 London are spatially interpolated to consistent LSOA boundaries by overlapping area; Tokyo uses overlapping grid/Cho dual-series to extend the time horizon.
  • Robustness check: occupations-only classification (without income adjustment) run in parallel (Supplementary Fig. 3) to verify that findings are not artefacts of income-ranking decisions.

① Replace static dissimilarity indices with dynamic, mobility-based experienced segregation

Extending this paperThe paper's segregation measures (ID, MDI) are purely residential and static: they capture where people sleep, not where they encounter each other during daily urban life. A direct extension would overlay the paper's four-decade residential quartile maps with GPS-derived mobility flows to compute experienced income segregation — capturing how professionalization reshapes not just neighbourhood composition but the daily mixing (or non-mixing) of income groups across offices, shops, transit and parks in New York, London and Tokyo.

Extending this paperThe paper's conclusions rest on three handpicked "global cities" from the 1980s Sassen canon; a key limitation acknowledged by the authors is that contextual factors drive divergent trajectories. A corpus-compatible extension is to replace the three-city case-study design with a broader cross-section of cities undergoing digitally-driven professionalization — specifically focusing on the growth of tech workers, which provides a measurable, comparable occupational shock across cities of different welfare regimes. ⑤ Build a physics-informed graph model of the segregation paradox: occupational change without proportional residential sorting

Extending this paperThe paper's central puzzle — large occupational-structure changes but small changes in aggregate segregation indices — constitutes a regularity that calls for a mechanistic model. A physics-inspired extension: represent the city as a bipartite graph (households × neighbourhoods) with edge weights encoding housing market friction (rent, tenure, moving costs). Encode the paper's empirical constraint that professionalization reduces ID as a soft conservation law, and learn the friction parameters from the three cities' four-wave panel. The model would then predict whether professionalization in a new city would increase or decrease segregation, given its friction profile.
