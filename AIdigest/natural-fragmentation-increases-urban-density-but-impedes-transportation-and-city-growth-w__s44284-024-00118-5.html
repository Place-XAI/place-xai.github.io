Natural fragmentation increases urban density but impedes transportation and city growth worldwide

Luyao Wang · Albert Saiz · Weipeng Li Published 2024-09-04 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00118-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using three new geographic-barrier indexes — share of natural barriers, nonconvexity (a measure of natural fragmentation) and average road detour — computed around cities worldwide, the authors show that natural constraints produce more complex, longer transportation routes and are associated with higher urban densities, smaller footprints, taller buildings and less pollution, but also with lower incomes and smaller populations. The clear policy message: cross-city comparisons of environmental, economic and social outcomes must control for surrounding physical geography, or they will misattribute effects of terrain to policy.

★

Why It Matters

  • Quantifies a long-asserted but rarely measured force. Physical geography has always been "known" to shape cities; this is a comprehensive, global, quantitative treatment of how natural barriers actually constrain urban form and movement.
  • Introduces transferable metrics. The share of barriers, nonconvexity and road detour indexes give planners and economists reusable, comparable yardsticks for the "fragmentation" of a city's surrounding terrain.
  • Reveals a development trade-off. Fragmentation pushes cities to be denser, taller and cleaner — but at the cost of smaller populations, smaller footprints and lower incomes, exposing tension between compactness and growth.
  • Corrects causal inference in urban science. Because terrain is correlated with so many outcomes, comparative urban studies that ignore geography risk confounding; this work supplies the control variable.
  • Open and reproducible. All derived indexes, Python computation code and Stata regression code are released on GitHub.

Core Methods

  • Share of natural barriers: fraction of the area in/around a city occupied by natural obstacles (water, steep terrain) derived from DEM + land/water layers.
  • Nonconvexity (fragmentation): a geometric index measuring how much the buildable/urbanizable region departs from convexity — capturing how natural features fragment the urban canvas.
  • Average road detour: ratio of network travel distance to straight-line distance across the road graph, quantifying how barriers force longer, more complex routes.
  • Four city-boundary definitions: all three indexes are recomputed under multiple independent global delineations to test robustness of the geography–outcome relationships.
  • Cross-city regression: Stata regressions associate the barrier indexes with density, footprint, building height, pollution, income and population — establishing the directional effects of fragmentation.

① Turn the detour index into an accessibility correction

Extending this paperThe paper's average road detour index quantifies how natural barriers inflate network distance beyond straight-line distance, but it stops at characterizing transportation "complexity." A direct extension: feed the detour field back as a per-cell impedance correction to standard service-accessibility metrics, so that a city's measured access to jobs, clinics or schools is automatically discounted by its terrain-induced detour — converting a descriptive geography index into a corrective layer for accessibility studies worldwide.

Added value to my researchThis is exactly the missing ingredient for my 2SFCA/R2SFCA work: instead of Euclidean or generic network distance, the catchment-defining travel cost is corrected by a terrain-fragmentation factor, yielding geographically honest spatial-accessibility scores for primary care and mental-health services in hilly or water-fragmented regions.

② Make nonconvexity a control variable in urban-scaling laws

Extending this paperThe paper shows barriers correlate with population, income and density — the very quantities that urban-scaling laws relate to city size. Extending it: insert nonconvexity and share of barriers as covariates inside scaling regressions to test whether terrain fragmentation biases the scaling exponents themselves, distinguishing genuine size effects from confounded geography.

Added value to my researchFor my urban-analytics line (agglomeration, regional collaboration, healthy cities), this offers a principled geographic confounder to add to MGWR/GWR models, so spatially-varying coefficients reflect human/policy processes rather than uncontrolled terrain.

③ Learn the barrier indexes with an explainable graph model

Extending this paperThe three indexes are hand-engineered geometric statistics. Extending the paper: train an explainable graph/ML model that takes the raw DEM + road graph and predicts urban outcomes (density, height, pollution), then use feature attribution to check whether the model rediscovers — or improves upon — share-of-barriers, nonconvexity and detour, revealing higher-order terrain signatures the closed-form indexes miss.

Added value to my researchThis pairs directly with my explainable machine learning for spatial prediction agenda: SHAP/attribution over a terrain-aware model would tell us which geographic features drive an outcome, turning the paper's indexes into learned, interpretable predictors.

④ Couple fragmentation with sprawl, water and service cost

Extending this paperThe paper links barriers to density and footprint but does not connect terrain fragmentation to the cost of delivering networked services. Extending it: overlay the barrier indexes with per-capita costs of water/sanitation and other infrastructure to test whether fragmented cities, while denser, pay more per connection because barriers force longer, branchier networks — a fragmentation-cost curve.

Added value to my researchThis feeds my public-facility-accessibility and EV-charging efficiency-vs-equity optimization: terrain fragmentation becomes an explicit cost term, so siting decisions trade off equitable coverage against the higher network cost barriers impose.

Cross-ref (corpus): Urban sprawl is associated with reduced access and increased costs of water and sanitation — 10.1038/s44284-025-00338-3

⑤ Add a physics-informed terrain operator for transportation networks

Extending this paperThe detour index treats terrain as a static input to existing road graphs. Extending it: build a physics-informed graph operator that, given the DEM and barrier field, predicts the efficient transportation network and its detour — turning fragmentation from a measured outcome into a generative model of how barriers shape optimal infrastructure, transferable to cities with no detailed road data yet.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my physics-informed graph learning direction: encoding terrain/conservation priors into a GNN so an accessibility-and-routing solver trained on well-mapped cities extrapolates to data-poor regions — a reusable GeoAI operator for space–human coupling.
