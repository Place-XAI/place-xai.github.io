Big mobility data reveals hyperlocal air pollution exposure disparities in the Bronx, New York

Iacopo Testi · An Wang · Sanjana Paul · Simone Mora · Erica Walker · Marguerite Nyhan · Fábio Duarte · Paolo Santi · Carlo Ratti Published 2024-07-29 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00093-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Combining the daily mobility of over 500,000 anonymized users with hyperlocal PM2.5 mapped to 100 × 100-m grid cells, the study moves air-pollution exposure assessment from individual tracking to a population-representative scale in the Bronx. Personal exposure and exposure disparity show distinct spatial patterns: people from Hispanic-majority and low-income neighborhoods are the most severely and disproportionately exposed. Crucially, race and ethnicity are stronger predictors of disparity than income, and within-group variation contributes a major share of total disparity — implying that generic air-quality improvement must be paired with granular, individual-targeted mitigation.

Why It Matters

  • Fixes the residence-based blind spot. Most exposure studies assign pollution to where people live. By using real trajectories, this work captures exposure where people actually spend their day, correcting a systematic bias in environmental-justice accounting.
  • Scales individual exposure to a population. Earlier mobility-based exposure relied on small wearable-sensor cohorts; here a half-million-user daily sample makes the estimate population-representative rather than anecdotal.
  • Reframes the equity diagnosis. Showing that race/ethnicity outweighs income, and that within-group variation dominates, reframes the policy target from neighborhood averages to specific high-exposure individuals.
  • Open, reusable outputs. Air-quality predictions, street-segment exposure, individual-trajectory exposure, and disparity measures are released on Zenodo, enabling replication and reuse.

Core Methods

  • Mobility-based exposure modeling: intersect each anonymized user's trajectory with the hyperlocal PM2.5 grid to compute time-weighted personal exposure where people actually go, not just where they reside.
  • Hyperlocal air-quality surface: PM2.5 predicted at 100 × 100-m resolution (machine-learning / land-use-regression style mapping), giving fine spatial granularity across the borough.
  • Mobility- vs residence-based comparison: contrast trajectory-derived exposure against the conventional home-location assignment to quantify the bias residence-based methods introduce (Fig. 3).
  • Disparity quantification: compute absolute and relative exposure disparities by income and by race/ethnicity, testing which dimension dominates (finding: race/ethnicity > income).
  • Variance decomposition: partition disparity into between-group vs within-group components, showing within-group variation is a major contributor — motivating individual-targeted mitigation.
