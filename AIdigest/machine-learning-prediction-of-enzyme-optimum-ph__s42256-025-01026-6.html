Machine learning prediction of enzyme optimum pH

Japheth E. Gado · Matthew Knotts · Ada Y. Shaw · Debora Marks · Nicholas P. Gauthier · Chris Sander · Gregg T. Beckham Published 2025-04-29 DOI 10.1038/s42256-025-01026-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

EpHod, an ensemble of a residual light-attention neural network and a support vector regressor both built on frozen ESM-1v protein language model embeddings, predicts enzyme optimum pH (pHopt) with an RMSE of 1.25 pH units on a held-out test set of 1,971 sequences — markedly outperforming all methods that rely on hand-engineered features or structural biophysics. Trained on 9,855 experimentally characterized enzymes from BRENDA and pre-trained on 1.9 million secreted bacterial proteins mapped to environmental pH, EpHod learns structurally interpretable features — catalytic-site proximity and solvent-exposed charged residues — that are mechanistically linked to pH adaptation, and generalizes robustly to sequences with less than 20% identity to the training set.

Why It Matters

  • Closes a key gap in enzyme biotechnology. Industrial enzymes routinely operate far from their pHopt, causing major activity losses. A sequence-only predictor lets researchers rapidly screen millions of sequences in genome and metagenome databases to identify candidates with favorable pH tolerance — without slow, costly experimentation.
  • Demonstrates that PLM embeddings transfer to small biochemical datasets. With fewer than 10,000 labeled examples, ESM-1v embeddings enable models to outperform deep neural networks trained on raw sequences, establishing a practical blueprint for property prediction where wet-lab data are scarce.
  • Interpretability bridges sequence and structure. EpHod's attention weights recover known biophysical mechanisms — higher focus on catalytic-site residues and solvent-exposed charged residues — providing mechanistic evidence that the model has learned real pH-relevant chemistry, not spurious correlations.
  • Addresses label imbalance systematically. Nearly 75% of known pHopt values cluster near pH 7, yet the most industrially valuable enzymes operate at extremes. EpHod's sample-reweighted loss improves F1 score for alkaline enzymes by 250%, a methodological lesson applicable across imbalanced biochemical datasets.
  • Publicly released model and data. The model weights, training data, and code are openly shared via Zenodo and GitHub, enabling immediate community use and reproducible benchmarking.

Core Methods

  • Sequence representation benchmark: one-hot encoding, amino acid composition (AAC), 5,494 hand-engineered features from iFeature (with PCA and recursive feature elimination), and embeddings from 6 protein language models (ESM-1b/1v, ProtT5, Tranception, ProGen2, CARP).
  • ML model zoo: Ridge regression, k-nearest-neighbour (KNR), support vector regression (SVR), random forest (RForest), XGBoost; feed-forward (FNN), convolutional (CNN), dilated CNN (DCNN), recurrent (RNN/GRU) neural networks; light attention (LAT) and residual light attention (RLAT) models for per-residue embeddings.
  • Hyperparameter optimization: grid or random search over up to 200 instances per method combination, totaling 11,550 model instances; selection via validation-set RMSE.
  • Transfer learning: RLAT pre-trained on 1.71 million pHenv sequences (environment pH as proxy), then fine-tuned on 7,124 pHopt sequences; ESM-1v encoder kept frozen throughout.
  • Label-imbalance reweighting: five strategies (bin-inverse, LDS-inverse, bin sqrt, LDS sqrt, LDS extreme) applied as sample-weighted loss during training; bin-inverse selected as optimal for most methods.
  • EpHod ensemble: ensemble of ESM-1v–SVR (mean-pooled embeddings) and ESM-1v–RLATtr (per-residue, transfer-learned), achieving R2 = 0.662 on the low-identity test subset.
  • Interpretability: softmax attention weights per residue correlated with solvent-accessible surface area (SASA; Shrake–Rupley algorithm) and Euclidean distance to annotated active-site residues (from AlphaFold structures); statistical comparison between acidic and alkaline enzyme cohorts via Welch's t-test.
  • Biophysical baselines: pI and PROPKA3 (ΔΔGpHopt) compared against EpHod via ROC-AUC and Pearson/Spearman correlation.

① Replace EpHod's static attention with a graph-neural-network structural encoder

Extending this paperEpHod keeps the ESM-1v encoder frozen and adds only a lightweight attention head on top of per-residue embeddings. The paper's own interpretability analysis shows that proximity to the catalytic centre and solvent exposure are the dominant structural signals, but these 3D cues are not explicitly modeled — they are only post-hoc correlated with attention weights. A direct extension: build a cross-attention graph neural network that simultaneously takes ESM-1v embeddings and AlphaFold-predicted structural graphs as input, letting the model explicitly reason over spatial neighborhoods around active-site residues. This would integrate structural information into training rather than only into interpretation, likely reducing the RMSE further for extreme-pH enzymes where surface-charge geometry is decisive.

Added value to my researchThe cross-attention GNN architecture maps directly to my physics-informed graph learning agenda: encoding domain-known spatial relationships (residue-to-active-site proximity) as inductive biases inside the graph operator is the same principle as encoding spatial adjacency or travel-time decay constraints in geospatial GNNs. EpHod's interpretability pipeline — correlating learned attention with known physical features — is a clean template for validating that my GeoAI models learn spatially meaningful rather than spurious associations.

② Adapt EpHod's label-reweighting framework to spatially imbalanced geospatial targets

Extending this paperThe paper's most transferable methodological contribution is its treatment of label imbalance: because 94% of known pHopt values fall between 5 and 9, a naive model collapses to predicting neutral pH and fails for industrially critical extremes. EpHod's solution — bin-inverse and label distribution smoothing (LDS) reweighting of the loss, with the reweighting strategy itself chosen via hyperparameter optimization — improved alkaline-enzyme F1 by 250%. This recipe is directly extensible to any biochemical property with a similarly skewed distribution (e.g., thermostability, substrate specificity), but within the EpHod framework itself the next step would be to combine reweighting with active-learning sampling that prioritizes wet-lab validation of predicted extreme-pH candidates to close the data-scarcity loop.

Added value to my researchSpatial data in health geography suffer the same imbalance structure: rare events (disease hotspots, medically underserved areas) are swamped by the majority distribution. EpHod's LDS reweighting is a drop-in improvement for my explainable ML models for spatial prediction (e.g., predicting healthcare access deprivation scores where near-zero scores are abundant and severe deprivation is rare). Applying bin-inverse or LDS to spatial regression loss directly addresses the over-prediction of moderate values and under-prediction of extreme deprivation that plagues current geospatial ML.

③ Use EpHod's transfer-learning template to bootstrap property prediction from environmental metadata

Extending this paperEpHod's two-stage strategy — pre-train on a large proxy signal (organism environment pH, 1.9M sequences) then fine-tune on a small direct label (enzyme pHopt, 9,855 sequences) — demonstrated modest but consistent gain (5% R2 improvement). The paper notes that parameter-efficient fine-tuning (LoRA, adapters) and multitask learning were not explored. Extending EpHod: implement LoRA fine-tuning of ESM-1v itself (rather than keeping it frozen) on the pHenv→pHopt pipeline, and jointly train on multiple correlated biochemical properties (thermostability, substrate turnover rate) as auxiliary tasks to regularize the scarce pHopt signal.

Added value to my researchThe proxy-signal idea has a geospatial analogue: in my fine-tuning of geospatial foundation models, large remote-sensing or mobility datasets serve as the "pHenv" proxy, and small ground-truth public-health outcomes (clinic visits, mental-health access rates) serve as the "pHopt." EpHod's quantitative comparison of frozen-encoder vs. fine-tuned-encoder pipelines provides a principled experimental template I can replicate to decide whether to freeze or adapt geospatial foundation model weights when labeled health outcomes are scarce.

④ Extend EpHod from prediction to conditional generative design via a latent pH scaffold

Extending this paperThe paper explicitly anticipates incorporating EpHod into conditional protein generative models that produce novel sequences with a desired pHopt. The natural next step is to use EpHod's final hidden-layer embeddings (which the paper already shows capture pH information; Supplementary Fig. 12) as a latent pH scaffold: train a diffusion or flow-matching model in this pH-enriched latent space so that sampling a target pH value steers the generator toward valid enzyme sequences. This goes beyond filtering (run generator → screen with EpHod → discard) to a tighter feedback where pH conditioning is baked into generation.

⑤ Build a multi-property enzyme atlas by combining EpHod with structure-based affinity predictors

Extending this paperEpHod predicts only pHopt; industrial enzyme selection requires simultaneous optimization of pH tolerance, thermostability, and substrate specificity. Extending the paper: combine EpHod with structure-based protein–ligand affinity predictors (e.g., graph-neural-network models that learn from 3D binding-pocket geometry) into a multi-property Pareto screening pipeline. EpHod screens a large sequence database for pH compatibility, then the affinity model shortlists candidates with the right substrate specificity, yielding a manageable set for experimental validation. The interpretability machinery from both models — EpHod's attention weights on sequence and the affinity model's learned pocket features — could be aggregated into a unified multi-property saliency map on the protein structure, guiding site-directed mutagenesis.

Added value to my researchThe multi-objective Pareto screening logic is directly analogous to my EV charging-station efficiency-vs-equity optimization: just as no single candidate enzyme is optimal on all biochemical axes, no single charging-station placement is Pareto-dominant on both coverage equity and grid efficiency.
