Global urban visual perception varies across demographics and personalities

Matias Quintana · Youlong Gu · Xiucheng Liang · Yujun Hou · Koichi Ito · Yihan Zhu · Mahmoud Abdelrahman · Filip Biljecki Published 2025-10-22 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00330-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

How people perceive a streetscape is not universal: judgments of how safe, lively, wealthy, beautiful, boring or depressing a place looks — plus four new indicators (live nearby, walk, cycle, green) — vary systematically with the viewer's gender, age, income, education, race/ethnicity and personality traits, and are further shaped by location-based sentiment. Critically, machine-learning models trained on existing global perception datasets are biased: they over-predict positive indicators and under-predict negative ones relative to real human responses, showing that pooling multi-cultural ratings obscures demographic difference and that local context is essential.

★

Why It Matters

  • Names a hidden bias in a whole research field. Urban-perception studies routinely pool ratings from diverse populations into one "average" score; this work shows that average erases real demographic and personality differences and can amplify the preferences of whoever happened to be sampled.
  • Audits the models planners increasingly trust. It directly benchmarks ML perception models against demographically balanced human responses and finds a systematic optimism bias — a warning for any tool that scores streets for safety, beauty or walkability at scale.
  • Extends the vocabulary of perception. Beyond the six classic Place-Pulse-style indicators it adds four behavior-relevant ones (would you live nearby, walk, cycle, and perceived greenery), tying perception more tightly to actual urban behaviour.
  • Ships an open, equity-aware dataset. The SPECS dataset (survey responses + participant demographics, 1,000 balanced participants from 5 countries / 45 nationalities) is released openly, enabling fairness-aware re-training of perception models.

Core Methods

  • Balanced large-scale perception survey: recruit 1,000 demographically balanced participants and elicit perceptions of global streetscapes through pairwise image comparisons, converted to per-image perception Q scores.
  • Demographic & personality stratification: compute Q scores within nested demographic groups (e.g. gender × age × income) and by personality trait, then test for differences with Games–Howell post-hoc tests at p < 0.05.
  • New indicator design & validation: introduce four behaviour-linked indicators (live nearby, walk, cycle, green) and validate them against the six classic ones via Pearson correlation and polynomial (linear/quadratic/cubic) regression.
  • Location-based sentiment analysis: examine how perceptions shift with the geographic/cultural context of both the imagery and the rater.
  • Human-vs-model bias audit: compare human ratings with ML perception models trained on existing global datasets, quantifying systematic over-/under-prediction of positive vs. negative indicators.

① Recalibrate perception models with a demographic-conditioned fairness layer

Extending this paperThe paper diagnoses that ML perception models trained on pooled global data are systematically optimistic but stops at the diagnosis. A direct extension: use the SPECS demographics to fit a causal/post-hoc debiasing layer that re-maps a model's predicted Q score to a target demographic's expected response, treating "who is rating" as a confounder rather than noise — turning the paper's bias finding into a deployable correction.

Added value to my researchThis is the same machinery I need to fairness-audit my explainable ML spatial predictors of health access and housing price: a reusable recipe for detecting and correcting when a GeoAI model encodes the preferences of an over-represented subgroup.

Cross-ref (corpus): A causal perspective on dataset bias in machine learning for medical imaging — 10.1038/s42256-024-00797-8

② Link perceived "live nearby / walk / cycle / green" to measured accessibility

Extending this paperThe paper's four new indicators are perceptual — would you live here, walk, cycle? — yet never confronted with objective accessibility. Extending it: pair each rated street with its real walkable service catchment (e.g. distance to groceries/amenities) and test where perceived walkability diverges from measured proximity, and for whom, mapping a perception–reality gap across demographics.

Added value to my researchThis directly enriches my 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility work: perceived walkability becomes a demographic-specific weighting on the distance-decay function, so a "15-minute" catchment is calibrated to how groups actually experience the street, not just network distance.

Cross-ref (corpus): Hundreds of grocery outlets needed across the United States to achieve walkable cities — 10.1038/s41467-025-61454-1

③ Fuse perception with the physical greenery signal it claims to capture

Extending this paperThe paper adds a perceived "green" indicator but does not anchor it to measured streetscape greenery (green-view index from semantic segmentation). Extending it: regress perceived-green against segmented vegetation fraction per image and per demographic, quantifying whose perception of greenness is most decoupled from the physical signal — a perception-vs-pixels calibration of the new indicator.

Added value to my researchThis is multi-source data fusion in my wheelhouse: combining remote-sensing/segmentation-derived environmental exposure with survey perception lets me build perception-aware environmental exposure metrics for urban-health models.

Cross-ref (corpus): Visualizing greener cities — 10.1038/s41893-024-01277-y

④ Connect perceived safety to objective surveillance & demographic context

Extending this paperThe paper finds perceived "safe" varies by race/ethnicity and personality, but does not relate it to the built infrastructure of surveillance on the same streets. Extending it: overlay SPECS safety Q scores with measured surveillance-camera density and neighbourhood racial composition, testing whether perceived safety tracks actual surveillance — and whether that link itself differs across rater demographics.

Added value to my researchThis feeds my space–human coupling for public-health policy: perceived safety is a known mediator of physical-activity and mental-health outcomes, and disentangling it from infrastructure helps target equitable interventions rather than mere camera deployment.

Cross-ref (corpus): Surveillance camera prevalence and racial diversity in ten US cities — 10.1038/s44284-025-00274-2

⑤ Treat the perception dataset itself as a geographically biased cultural artifact

Extending this paperThe paper warns against pooling multi-cultural ratings, which raises a deeper question it leaves open: are the images themselves geographically skewed (which cities, which streets are even shown)? Extending it: audit SPECS/Global Streetscapes for spatial representation bias and re-weight perception estimates by an inverse-coverage / spatial-entropy correction so under-sampled regions are not silently averaged away.

Added value to my researchThis squarely advances my open-science/FAIR and reproducibility agenda and uses my spatial-entropy toolkit: a documented protocol for quantifying representativeness of crowd-sourced geospatial corpora before they train foundation models.
