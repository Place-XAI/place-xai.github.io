Machine learning to guide the use of adjuvant therapies for breast cancer

Ahmed M. Alaa · Deepti Gurdasani · Adrian L. Harris · Jem Rashbass · Mihaela van der Schaar Published 2021-06-24 DOI 10.1038/s42256-021-00353-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Adjutorium, an automated and interpretable ML model built with AutoPrognosis on nearly one million breast cancer patients from UK (NCRAS, n = 395,862) and US (SEER, n = 571,635) cancer registries, significantly outperforms PREDICT v2.1 — the current clinical standard — in predicting all-cause and breast cancer-specific survival at 3, 5, and 10 years. Gains are largest in underserved subgroups (elderly patients, ER-negative/HER2-negative cancer), and Adjutorium's transparent risk equation could improve adjuvant therapy decisions for an estimated 25% of patients relative to multidisciplinary-team (MDT) decisions.

Why It Matters

  • Largest ML prognostication study of its kind. Nearly one million patients from two nationally representative registries spanning the UK and US give Adjutorium statistical power and demographic diversity that dwarfs prior tools, enabling subgroup robustness that has been elusive.
  • Transparent by construction. AutoPrognosis converts an optimized ML ensemble into a symbolic risk equation — a closed-form mathematical expression interpretable to clinicians — breaking the black-box barrier that has stalled clinical adoption of ML.
  • Immediate clinical pathway. Adjutorium is already deployed as a publicly accessible web tool, bridging the gap between research and practice for patients and oncologists worldwide.
  • Equity-oriented accuracy gains. Subgroups historically under-served by PREDICT v2.1 — older women, ER-negative/HER2-negative cancers — show the greatest accuracy improvements, directly targeting health-outcome equity.
  • Reusable infrastructure. The open-source AutoPrognosis framework can be re-fit as new data or therapies emerge without restarting the modelling process from scratch, embodying reproducible and updatable clinical science.

Core Methods

  • AutoPrognosis (AutoML pipeline): automated Bayesian optimization over an ensemble of ML models (random forest, gradient boosting, neural network, AdaBoost) to find the optimal pipeline and hyperparameters for a given clinical dataset.
  • Symbolic regression: converts the fitted ML ensemble into a closed-form mathematical risk equation (log-odds form with Meijer G-function basis), enabling transparent, clinician-readable predictions without sacrificing accuracy.
  • Competing-risks survival modelling: 10 binary classification models (one per year horizon, 1–10 years) for breast-cancer-specific mortality (Pbc) and other-cause mortality (Pnbc); combined into all-cause survival as Pbc × Pnbc.
  • Counterfactual untreated survival: observed survival times are adjusted using EBCTCG hazard ratios to reconstruct counterfactual untreated survival curves for training.
  • Missing data imputation: MICE with 10 imputed datasets; predictions pooled via Rubin's rule — enabling robust performance in real-world settings where data are often incomplete.
  • Validation metrics: time-dependent AUC-ROC, Harrell's C-index, Uno's C-index; all evaluated at 3, 5, and 10 years; confidence intervals via bootstrapped resampling.
  • Calibration assessment: Kaplan–Meier-based calibration curves across 10 predicted-risk quantiles, for both internal and external cohorts.
  • Subgroup and sensitivity analyses: stratification by age, ER status, HER2 status, tumour grade, and diagnosis year; missingness robustness tests.

① Incorporate causal treatment-effect estimation to replace the EBCTCG hazard-ratio plug-in

Extending this paperAdjutorium's treatment-benefit predictions borrow hazard ratios from external EBCTCG meta-analyses rather than estimating individual treatment effects from the data itself. The authors acknowledge this as a limitation. A direct extension: replace the HR plug-in with a counterfactual ML estimator (e.g., causal forest or TARNet) trained on the sub-cohort patients who did or did not receive each therapy, estimating individualized treatment effects (ITEs) entirely from the registry data. This would allow Adjutorium to discover heterogeneous subgroup responses beyond the average HR, and would be technically feasible given the large NCRAS sample.

Added value to my researchCounterfactual reasoning maps directly onto my GeoAI work on spatial health disparities: estimating the individualized effect of a healthcare intervention (e.g., a new telehealth service or a newly opened clinic) on a specific subpopulation mirrors the ITE problem. Framing 2SFCA catchment expansion as a causal treatment — "what would utilization look like if travel time decreased by X minutes for this census tract?" — would give a principled counterfactual backbone to my accessibility analyses.

② Spatialize Adjutorium: link registry outcomes to place-based risk factors

Extending this paperBoth NCRAS and SEER carry geo-coded residence data (at postcode or county level) that Adjutorium does not exploit. Extending the paper: augment each patient record with area-level socio-economic deprivation, green-space exposure, access to cancer care, and urbanicity, and allow the symbolic risk equation to include place-based terms. This would test whether the large subgroup disparities (elderly, ER-negative) partly reflect where patients live — and would reveal whether Adjutorium's gains over PREDICT v2.1 differ systematically by geography.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct extension of my health geography and spatial accessibility agenda: mapping breast cancer treatment benefit as a spatially varying function using MGWR, and comparing it against cancer-care travel times derived from my R2SFCA frameworks. The joint model would let me quantify the share of outcome variance attributable to spatial access barriers versus patient-level biology — a key policy question for NHS geography planning.

③ Apply explainable morphological profiling to discover new subgroups beyond ER/HER2/grade

Extending this paperAdjutorium's symbolic risk equation reveals interactions between grade, ER status, and other variables — essentially discovering data-driven subtypes through the modelling process. A direct extension: pre-process whole-slide pathology images from NCRAS with an explainable morphological ML model to generate image-derived features, then feed these into AutoPrognosis alongside the standard clinicopathological inputs. This would test whether morphological features capture residual prognostic signal beyond the existing variables, potentially revealing new clinically actionable subtypes.

Added value to my researchThe idea of explainable feature discovery at the stratum level translates to my work on explainable ML for spatial prediction: just as SHAP values in morphological ML reveal which cellular regions drive a prediction, spatial SHAP decompositions in my MGWR models can reveal which local context features drive health outcome heterogeneity across census tracts — a shared methodological architecture for interpretable subgroup discovery.

④ Build a foundation model for clinical cancer prognostication using Adjutorium as a pre-training anchor

Extending this paperAutoPrognosis retrains from scratch for every dataset and cancer type. A transformative extension: use the ~1 million NCRAS+SEER patient records as pre-training data for a foundation model for cancer prognostication, with Adjutorium's risk equations serving as interpretability-preserving supervision signals. The model could then be fine-tuned to other cancer sites (lung, colorectal) and other countries with small local cohorts — analogous to how imaging foundation models transfer across modalities. This would realize Adjutorium's "automated update" aspiration at scale.

Added value to my researchThis mirrors my work on fine-tuning geospatial foundation models: just as a geospatial foundation pre-trained on global remote sensing can be adapted to a specific city with local GPS traces, a clinical foundation model pre-trained on NCRAS+SEER could be adapted to a specific regional NHS trust with limited local data. The transfer-learning recipe — pre-train broadly, fine-tune locally, preserve interpretability — is shared across both domains.

⑤ Generalize Adjutorium's stable-learning objective to reduce spurious correlations across registry shifts

Extending this paperThe paper shows that Adjutorium generalizes better than PREDICT v2.1 from NCRAS to SEER — two registries with different demographic structure and coding practices. However, this cross-registry generalization is demonstrated empirically, not enforced methodologically. Extending the paper: adopt a stable-learning or invariant-risk-minimization objective inside AutoPrognosis so that the optimized ensemble explicitly minimizes prediction instability across environment partitions (e.g., diagnosis year, registry, ethnicity stratum). This would provide a principled guarantee of generalization rather than a retrospective demonstration.

Added value to my researchStable learning is directly relevant to my multi-source data fusion work: when combining mobile-phone records, social-media signals, and remote-sensing layers from different cities, spurious correlations between data sources and outcomes can drive overfitting to particular cities. An invariant-learning layer in my GeoAI pipelines would make spatiotemporal models more robustly transferable across urban contexts — exactly the generalizability challenge Adjutorium faces across registries.
