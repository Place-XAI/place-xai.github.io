Morphological and molecular breast cancer profiling through explainable machine learning

Alexander Binder · Michael Bockmayr · Miriam Hägele · Stephan Wienert · Daniel Heim · Katharina Hellweg · Masaru Ishii · Albrecht Stenzinger · Andreas Hocke · Carsten Denkert · Klaus-Robert Müller · Frederick Klauschen Published 2021-03-08 DOI 10.1038/s42256-021-00303-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

An explainable machine-learning pipeline combining layer-wise relevance propagation (LRP) with kernel SVMs can simultaneously detect cancer cells and tumour-infiltrating lymphocytes from histological images with over 90% accuracy, and predict multiple molecular features (DNA methylation, gene expression, copy number variations, somatic mutations, proteins) directly from H&E slides — reaching balanced accuracies up to 78% overall and over 95% for identifiable subgroups. Crucially, the approach generates spatial heatmaps linking morphological regions to predicted molecular properties, turning black-box predictions into interpretable, experimentally validated morphomolecular maps that bridge imaging and omics for precision oncology.

★

Why It Matters

  • Bridges the spatial-molecular gap in cancer biology. Molecular omics profiling lacks spatial resolution; microscopy lacks throughput for multi-omics. This is the first integrated XAI framework to computationally assign molecular profiles to spatial tissue regions using only standard H&E stains.
  • Turns predictions into testable hypotheses. LRP-generated heatmaps do not just say what is predicted — they say where the evidence is. This enables biologically meaningful, spatially explicit hypothesis generation validated here against immunohistochemistry for p53, E-cadherin, and MSH2.
  • Opens precision medicine for resource-limited settings. Predicting expensive molecular assays (methylation panels, RPPA protein arrays) from cheap H&E whole-slide images could dramatically reduce the cost of personalized cancer diagnostics globally.
  • Establishes clinical stratification potential. By identifying high-confidence "tail" prediction subgroups, the method can flag patients for whom histology-derived molecular estimates are already clinically actionable (>95% accuracy achievable for on average 8.2% of cases for the top 50 proteins).
  • Ships an open annotated image database. The Berlin Cancer Image Base (B-CIB, >200,000 manually annotated patches) and all analysis code are released for non-commercial use, serving as a benchmark resource for the computational pathology community.

Core Methods

  • Layer-wise Relevance Propagation (LRP): a backward-propagation XAI technique that assigns a pixel-wise relevance score to every input pixel, explaining which image regions drove a given classification or regression output. Adapted here from the original Bach et al. 2015 formulation.
  • Bag-of-Words (BoW) feature extraction: local SIFT descriptors combined with quantiles of gradient norms and colour intensities are soft-mapped via rank-weighted aggregation onto visual vocabularies (510 visual words) obtained by k-means clustering, yielding a fixed-length feature vector per image tile.
  • Kernel SVMs: sum-of-χ² kernels (cancer detection), sum-of-histogram-intersection kernels (lymphocyte detection), and histogram-intersection kernels (molecular prediction) normalized to unit Hilbert-space variance. High-vs-low binary classification for each molecular target with threshold chosen from nine equally-spaced quantiles.
  • Nested cross-validation: 10-fold outer / 9-fold inner CV on the TCGA cohort (~565 patients); prevents data leakage and accounts for small sample size. Regularization swept over C ∈ {10k/2, k=−2…2}.
  • Statistical evaluation: Hoeffding's inequality-based significance test for each molecular target; Benjamini–Hochberg (BH) FDR correction at α = 0.05 across all >60,000 features; complementary Monte Carlo FDR estimation via random label permutation.
  • Tail accuracy analysis: SVM decision scores are used to identify high-confidence subsets of cases, enabling high-accuracy sub-population stratification without changing the model.
  • Quadrat test (geospatial statistics): borrowed from spatial point-process analysis to quantify and test the statistical significance of co-localization between morphological predictions and IHC staining patterns in image patches — avoiding pixel-level misregistration artefacts between consecutive tissue sections.
  • Synthetic validation: geometric shapes with assigned molecular properties used to confirm that morphomolecular heatmaps are non-random and biologically specific.

① Replace BoW+SVM with a spatial foundation model for whole-slide images

Extending this paperThe paper's morphological and molecular predictors use Bag-of-Words features and kernel SVMs — an architecture chosen carefully to avoid overfitting in the small TCGA cohort (~565 cases). A direct extension of the paper: replace BoW+SVM with a pretrained pathology foundation model (trained on millions of WSIs) as the encoder, then fine-tune only the classification head for each molecular target. This would inherit richer spatial features while preserving the paper's XAI layer — LRP can be applied to transformer attention maps as well as to SVM decision planes. The paper itself notes that deep nets challenged by small samples at the time; foundation models solve precisely this problem.

Added value to my researchThe same transfer-then-explain pattern maps directly onto my geospatial foundation model fine-tuning agenda: a pretrained spatial encoder (e.g., on remote-sensing imagery) + task-specific head + LRP-style backward attribution lets me explain what spatial features in a satellite image drive predicted health outcomes — a cross-domain methodological transfer from pathology XAI to GeoAI.

② Spatially resolved gene expression prediction at spot resolution

Extending this paperThe paper predicts bulk molecular profiles from histology — it does not attempt spatially resolved molecular prediction within each slide, acknowledging that "bulk" omics are non-spatial by nature. A direct extension: couple the paper's LRP heatmaps with spatial transcriptomics data (Visium/MERFISH) as supervision signals, so the model learns to predict gene expression at spot resolution (<55 µm grid) rather than slide-level averages. This would validate whether the paper's morphomolecular associations actually reflect subcellular-level spatial biology, not just slide-level correlations.

Added value to my researchSpatial transcriptomics prediction from H&E mirrors my interest in predicting unobserved spatial attributes from cheap proxy data — analogous to predicting health service utilization from street-view imagery or mobile-phone activity. The method of using spatial co-registration between a high-resolution spatial assay and a cheap image to train a spatially explicit predictor is transferable across domains.

Cross-ref (corpus): HESpotEx: a dual-stream deep learning framework for spot-level gene expression prediction from histological images — 10.1038/s43588-026-00992-0

③ Extend heatmap calibration with uncertainty quantification for clinical triage

Extending this paperThe paper's "tail accuracy" approach is a practical but informal way to express model confidence — patients far from the SVM hyperplane are predicted with higher accuracy. A more principled extension: replace the point-estimate SVM with a conformal prediction or calibrated Bayesian classifier that outputs coverage-guaranteed confidence sets for each molecular target and patient. This would turn the paper's current binary high/low-confidence split into a continuous uncertainty map overlaid on the LRP heatmap — directly informative for clinical routing decisions (which patients to send to molecular testing vs. which to diagnose from histology alone).

Added value to my researchUncertainty-calibrated predictions are essential in my 2SFCA accessibility modelling: catchment-level demand estimates inherit uncertainty from population projections and travel-time models. Integrating conformal prediction into spatial accessibility scores would give planners explicit coverage guarantees — "this sub-district's unmet demand estimate is within ±X% with 95% coverage" — matching the clinical triage logic above.

④ Multi-cancer transfer: test whether morphomolecular associations generalize across tumour types

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates its approach on breast cancer and mentions applicability to other cancer types (gastrointestinal, bladder, lung cited in related work), but all of its quantitative results are breast-specific. A natural extension: train a pan-cancer morphomolecular model on TCGA's multi-tumour cohorts (BRCA + LUAD + CRC + BLCA) using a shared LRP backbone, and explicitly test whether morphomolecular associations transfer (e.g., does TiL–cyclin-B1 correlation hold across lung and breast cancer?). This would determine whether the paper's discovered associations reflect general cancer biology or breast-specific artefacts.

Added value to my researchCross-domain transfer testing is the core of my geospatial foundation model fine-tuning work: a model pre-trained on one city's mobile-phone patterns should fine-tune to a new city with minimal data. The methodological question — how much of what is learned in one context transfers to another — is identical whether the domain is cancer histology or urban health data. The evaluation framework for pan-cancer transfer (held-out cancer types, per-feature AUC) directly parallels held-out city evaluation in GeoAI.

⑤ From morphomolecular maps to a multi-omics representation-learning framework

Extending this paperThe paper treats each molecular target independently — a separate classifier is trained for each of the >60,000 features. A fundamental extension: learn a joint latent embedding of histological patches and molecular profiles (e.g., via a contrastive or variational autoencoder) so that the morphomolecular space is modelled as a single manifold rather than thousands of independent binary problems. LRP heatmaps applied to the shared embedding would then reveal which morphological directions in image space correspond to which molecular axes — a richer interpretability than per-feature classification. This would also address the multiple-testing burden inherent in the current approach.

Added value to my researchLearning joint latent spaces across heterogeneous data modalities is central to my multi-source data fusion work (mobile-phone, social media, remote sensing, POIs). A contrastive embedding between spatial images and tabular health/activity data would let me ask: "which spatial visual features co-vary with which health-outcome dimensions?" — directly analogous to the morphomolecular embedding above, but for urban health geography.
