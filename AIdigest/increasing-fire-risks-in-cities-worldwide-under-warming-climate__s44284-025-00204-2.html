Increasing fire risks in cities worldwide under warming climate

Long Shi · Jinhui Wang · Guohui Li · Michael Yit Lin Chew · Heping Zhang · Guomin Zhang · Bogdan Z. Dlugogorski Published 2025-03-03 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00204-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A warming climate reshapes urban fire risk by fire type, not uniformly. Across 2,847 cities, building fires follow a quadratic (U-shaped) relationship with air temperature, bottoming out at the thermal-comfort point of ~24.0 °C, whereas vehicle and outdoor fires rise linearly — by (2.5 ± 0.8)% and (4.7 ± 2.2)% per +1 °C. Under SSP5-8.5 by 2100, building fires could fall (4.6 ± 1.1)% while vehicle and outdoor fires could surge (11.6 ± 3.7)% and (22.2 ± 10.2)%, giving fire services a quantitative basis to reallocate budgets across cities.

★

Why It Matters

  • Opens a blind spot in climate-impact science. The climate–fire literature is dominated by vegetation/wildland fires; this is the first global quantification of how warming shifts urban fire frequency across distinct incident types (building, vehicle, vegetation, outdoor).
  • Reveals that warming is not monotonically protective indoors. The U-shaped building-fire response — minimized near thermal comfort and rising on both cold and hot sides — means heating-driven cold-side fires fall but the gains are partly offset, a nuance that a simple linear story would miss.
  • Quantifies fast-growing exterior risks. Outdoor fires rising ~22% and vehicle fires ~12% by 2100 under high emissions flags where future fire-service demand will concentrate.
  • Directly actionable for fire-service budgeting. The per-degree elasticities (FFV) and country-level projections translate into concrete, quantitative advice for allocating and reallocating national fire-service budgets across cities and climates.

Core Methods

  • Fixed-effect panel regression: a city fixed-effect linear model (Eq. 5) relates monthly fire frequency to Tmax, separating within-city temperature response from cross-city heterogeneity (different intercepts per city).
  • Piecewise / quadratic specification: building fires fitted with a quadratic form around a turning-point temperature (Tv ≈ 24.0 °C); regressions estimated separately below and above Tv (Extended Data Fig. 2) to capture the U-shape.
  • Fire-frequency variation per °C (FFV): the key elasticity — % change in fire frequency per +1 °C — estimated per fire type, per city and per country (Eqs. 7, 9), then aggregated to a world average.
  • Mechanistic "fire-triangle" model: a conceptual model (heat/fuel/oxygen pathways) explains why building, vehicle, vegetation and outdoor fires respond differently to warming, linking statistical results to physical ignition mechanisms.
  • SSP-based projection: fitted elasticities propagated through IPCC SSP scenarios (esp. SSP5-8.5) to project 2020–2100 fire frequency and cumulative fire deaths, normalized to 2020 baselines.

① Replace the global fixed-effect regression with explainable, spatially varying models

Extending this paperThe paper estimates a single global FFV per fire type from a fixed-effect linear/quadratic model, allowing only city-specific intercepts. Yet Fig. 2 already shows the per-degree sensitivity varies strongly by country. A direct extension: refit the temperature–fire relationship with geographically weighted regression (MGWR) or an explainable ML ensemble (SHAP) so the elasticity becomes a continuous spatial surface, exposing which urban/climatic covariates (building stock, density, income) modulate the response — turning one global coefficient into an interpretable map.

② Couple urban fire risk with the wildland–urban interface

Extending this paperThe paper treats "vegetation" and "outdoor" fires as urban incident categories but stops at the city boundary. Linking its per-city FFV to wildland–urban interface (WUI) geography would let outdoor/vegetation projections be conditioned on adjacency to flammable landscapes — explaining the wide outdoor-fire confidence band (±10.2%) as a WUI-exposure effect rather than pure noise.
