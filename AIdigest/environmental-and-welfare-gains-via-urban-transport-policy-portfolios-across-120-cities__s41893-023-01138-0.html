Environmental and welfare gains via urban transport policy portfolios across 120 cities

Charlotte Liotta · Vincent Viguié · Felix Creutzig Published 2023-05-29 DOI 10.1038/s41893-023-01138-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using a spatially explicit monocentric urban economic model calibrated on 120 cities worldwide, this study shows that a portfolio of four representative transport policies (fuel tax, bus rapid transit, restrictive land-use regulations, fuel efficiency mandate) can reduce urban transport GHG emissions by 31% in 15 years — but the same policy has radically different outcomes across cities. A tailored welfare-increasing policy portfolio, applied city by city, still achieves a 22% emission reduction globally while improving average welfare, demonstrating that there is no one-size-fits-all solution but that context-specific strategies can deliver large simultaneous environmental and welfare gains.

★

Why It Matters

  • First systematic global urban-scale policy simulation. Unlike integrated assessment models (IAMs) that operate at national or sector scale, this is one of the first spatially explicit, city-by-city modelling exercises covering 120 cities on five continents — bringing global GHG accounting down to the street-level land-use/transport interaction.
  • Quantifies the joint emission-welfare trade-off. The paper goes beyond emissions alone to rigorously price health co-benefits (air pollution, noise, accidents, active-mode exercise) and housing-transport costs together, showing that welfare-positive portfolios can capture two-thirds of the full mitigation potential.
  • Destroys the "one-size-fits-all" myth with data. The same fuel tax is welfare-enhancing in dense European cities but harmful in sprawled low-income cities with little public transport — a finding with direct implications for national climate policy design and just-transition concerns.
  • Actionable for city planners and NDC design. The paper directly helps cities identify which policy mix to adopt given local income, population growth, density and public transport availability, and provides a reproducible open-code framework for future scenario testing.

Core Methods

  • Spatially explicit monocentric urban economic model (NEDUM): combines a transport mode-choice model with an Alonso–Muth–Mills residential location model; simulates population density, housing supply, transport choices and rents as a function of employment locations, transport costs and land-use constraints — calibrated individually for each of the 120 cities.
  • Four policy scenarios: (1) local fuel tax (+30% fuel price); (2) bus rapid transit (BRT) network investment; (3) restrictive land-use regulations promoting density near public transport; (4) fuel efficiency mandate (−3.7% annual fuel consumption of new vehicles). Each is applied in all 120 cities and compared with a business-as-usual (BAU) baseline.
  • Welfare accounting: total social welfare = sum of household utilities (housing + composite goods consumption) plus four monetised health co-benefits — air pollution, noise, car accidents (negative) and active-transport mortality reduction via WHO's HEAT model (positive).
  • Welfare-maximising portfolio optimisation: for each city, selects the policy mix that maximises emission mitigation subject to the constraint that aggregate welfare must be non-negative, capturing synergies (e.g., restrictive land-use regulations become welfare-positive when combined with BRT).
  • Scenario drivers: BAU uses SSP2 income growth (IMACLIM-R IAM) and UN city-level population projections to 2035; new vehicle fuel consumption declines 1%/year; no new public transport infrastructure in BAU.
  • Validation: within-city density and rent correlations (median r = 0.63 for density); city-level modal shares compared against EPOMM, CDP and Deloitte (Pearson r = 0.63 for public transport modal share); transport emissions compared against Moran et al., Nangini et al. and Kona et al.
  • Robustness checks: alternative policy magnitudes and BRT routing via simplified north–south/east–west lines; qualitative results confirmed.

① Replace the city-by-city calibration with a multi-city transfer model

Extending this paperThe paper calibrates NEDUM independently for each of its 120 cities, requiring dense local data (population density, rents, transport costs at 1 km resolution). This limits applicability to data-rich cities and prevents knowledge sharing across cities. A direct extension: recast calibration as a meta-learning or transfer-learning problem where city-type embeddings (income level, density gradient, public-transport share) condition shared model parameters, so the framework can be applied to data-sparse or African cities currently excluded. The paper itself anticipates this gap ("current and continuous increase in available urban data … may enable this limit to be overcome"), making the extension explicitly in-scope.

Added value to my researchThis maps onto my agenda of fine-tuning geospatial foundation models: the monocentric model structure becomes a physics-informed prior layer inside a geospatial LLM that can zero-shot calibrate in cities lacking ground-truth rent surfaces — connecting urban economic modelling to geospatial foundation model research.

② Learn the welfare–emission Pareto surface with an explainable ML surrogate

Extending this paperThe paper's core finding — that welfare-increasing portfolios still achieve 22% emission reduction — is derived from a single constrained optimisation per city. However, the relationship between city characteristics and optimal policy mix remains largely opaque (R² = 0.24–0.54 in linear regressions, ~50% variance unexplained). An extension: train a gradient-boosted surrogate on the paper's own 120-city simulation output to predict both emission reduction and welfare change jointly, then apply SHAP to attribute prediction to city features (density, income, modal share). This turns the paper's simulation database into a policy-recommendation engine that planners can query without running NEDUM.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my explainable machine learning for spatial prediction work: the SHAP attribution gives policy-makers spatially explicit "why" explanations — which city features drive the welfare–emission trade-off in their specific context — mirroring my use of SHAP/MGWR to make spatial model outputs interpretable for health-geography decision support.

③ Extend the health co-benefit component with spatially explicit disease burden modelling

Extending this paperThe paper monetises air-pollution and accident health co-benefits using city-average linear factors (marginal cost per vehicle-km), discarding within-city spatial variation in exposure — a known simplification the authors acknowledge. An extension: replace the linear co-benefit term with a spatially explicit health impact model that links the NEDUM-simulated population redistribution to local exposure fields (PM2.5, road proximity) using fine-resolution dispersion, producing co-benefit maps rather than a city-average scalar. This would allow the paper to differentiate policies by who benefits, not just how much benefit accrues on aggregate.

Added value to my researchThis directly extends my health geography and accessibility agenda: spatially disaggregated co-benefit maps produce the kind of health-access surface I study with 2SFCA/R2SFCA methods — allowing the paper's transport policy scenarios to be evaluated as accessibility equity interventions, not just efficiency outcomes, linking transport GHG policy to primary-care and mental-health access disparities.

④ Make the BRT design data-driven using real network topology and demand flows

Extending this paperThe paper's BRT scenario assumes a single fixed network design (OpenStreetMap-routed lines) identical in structure across all 120 cities. In reality, BRT value depends critically on where lines run — connecting employment centres to residential zones. An extension: integrate the paper's NEDUM residential-choice model with a network flow optimiser that co-designs BRT route layout and residential density simultaneously, so the BRT scenario becomes a true planning co-optimisation rather than an exogenous shock. The paper explicitly names this gap ("bicycle lane networks … promotion of mixed land use … could not be evaluated here").

Added value to my researchThis is an instance of my facility-location and network community detection research: the BRT route becomes a public-facility location problem where the "catchment" of each route stop (job-housing flows) is learned from mobile phone or GPS trajectory data — exactly the multi-source data fusion and spatiotemporal kernel density estimation approach I apply to EV charging-station efficiency-equity optimisation.

⑤ Couple the model's land-use dynamics to real-time mobility data for near-real-time policy evaluation

Extending this paperNEDUM is calibrated once on 2015 cross-sectional data and projected forward with fixed assumptions (no congestion dynamics, no intra-city income heterogeneity, monocentric employment). An extension: replace the static 2015 snapshot with an annually updated data pipeline using mobile phone records and GPS traces to re-estimate mode shares, trip distances and density gradients each year, effectively making NEDUM a continuously recalibrated policy evaluation engine. This converts a research model into an operational urban observatory.
