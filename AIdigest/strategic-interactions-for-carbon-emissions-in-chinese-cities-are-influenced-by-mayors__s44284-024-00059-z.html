Strategic interactions for carbon emissions in Chinese cities are influenced by mayors

Bei Zhu · Chu Wei Published 2024-04-10 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00059-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Chinese cities do not cut carbon in isolation — their mayors strategically imitate one another. Using a spatial Durbin model on city panel data from 2000–2019, the authors find a strong positive interaction: a 1% drop in a neighbor's carbon intensity is associated with a 0.792% drop in the reference city. The effect is conditioned on who the mayor is — those with higher education, younger age, science-related majors, and local (hometown) ties perform better — and the dominant dynamic is an "imitation competition" pattern in which mayors emulate neighboring cities' carbon-regulation practices.

★

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the "city as island" assumption. Most city decarbonization studies treat each city as an independent entity; this work shows carbon-intensity reduction is a networked, peer-driven outcome — neglecting interactions biases policy evaluation.
  • Puts the decision-maker back in the model. By linking carbon performance to individual mayor attributes (education, age, major, hometown), it turns an abstract "city" into the people who actually set policy — a behavioral micro-foundation for macro emissions.
  • Identifies the mechanism, not just the correlation. Distinguishing "imitation competition" from other spillover patterns tells central planners how low-carbon behavior diffuses across the urban hierarchy, suggesting that seeding leader cities can cascade through neighbors.
  • Direct policy leverage for China's dual-carbon goals. If peer imitation is real, targeted demonstration cities and the right cadre profiles could amplify nationwide carbon-intensity reductions at low cost.

Core Methods

  • Spatial Durbin Model (SDM): the workhorse — jointly estimates a city's own effects plus spatially lagged outcomes and covariates of neighboring cities, capturing direct and indirect (spillover) effects of mayors' interaction on carbon intensity.
  • Strategic-interaction estimation: recovers the peer-response coefficient (the 0.792% elasticity), quantifying how much a city moves when its neighbors move.
  • Heterogeneity analysis: spatio-temporal heterogeneity (Fig. 1) and across-city heterogeneity by mayor attributes (Fig. 2) — education, age, science major, hometown ties — to identify which mayors interact most effectively.
  • Interaction-pattern classification: distinguishes the "imitation competition" pattern (emulating neighbors) from alternative spillover regimes (Fig. 3), using rank/turnover and pilot-policy analyses (MATLAB).
  • Robustness battery: baseline + robustness tests and temporal/spatial heterogeneity checks (Stata .do), with low-carbon city pilot policy as a corroborating institutional lens.

① Replace the fixed spatial weight matrix with a learned interaction graph

Extending this paperThe SDM hard-codes a single geographic neighbor matrix, so the 0.792% peer elasticity is conditional on an assumed adjacency. Mayor imitation, however, flows along administrative rank, economic ties, and cadre-exchange channels, not just shared borders. A direct extension: re-estimate the interaction over multiple candidate graphs (economic-linkage, personnel-exchange, same-province) and let the data — or a graph-learning layer — choose the edges that actually carry the carbon-imitation signal.

② Separate "policy copying" from "official transfer" as competing diffusion channels

Extending this paperThe paper's "imitation competition" lumps all peer emulation together, but emulation can happen by transplanting written policies or by moving the people who run them. Extending it: decompose the spillover into a policy-text-similarity channel and a cadre-mobility channel, testing whether mayors who personally rotate between cities carry the low-carbon practice more effectively than copied documents — turning the paper's single pattern into an identified mechanism.
