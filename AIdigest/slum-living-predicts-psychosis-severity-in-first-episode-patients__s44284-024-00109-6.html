Slum living predicts psychosis severity in first-episode patients

Fernando Malinowski · Carolina Ziebold · Cristiano Noto · Daniel Cavalcante · Síntia Belangero · Rodrigo Bressan · Ary Gadelha Published 2024-08-16 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00109-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Among 190 antipsychotic-naive first-episode psychosis patients in São Paulo, Brazil, living in a slum (favela) independently predicts greater initial psychosis severity: higher total PANSS score (p = 0.029, B = 9.882) and worse negative (p = 0.044) and disorganization (p = 0.032) symptoms. Lower income and education are also tied to higher PANSS, and longer duration of untreated psychosis is associated with brown or Black race. The work argues that place-based social exclusion shapes the core clinical presentation of psychotic disorders, not just their incidence.

★

Why It Matters

  • Moves psychiatry from "where you get sick" to "how sick you present." Most neighbourhood-and-psychosis research targets incidence; this study shows the residential environment also scales the severity of the very first episode — a clinically actionable signal at the point of entry to care.
  • Centers the Global South. The slum/favela is the dominant urban form for a quarter of the world's city dwellers, yet the psychosis literature is overwhelmingly North-American and European; a São Paulo cohort fills a major geographic blind spot.
  • Connects spatial deprivation, race and treatment delay. By linking slum residence, low income/education and the brown/Black race gap in duration of untreated psychosis, it frames mental-health inequity as a compound place-and-identity effect.
  • Implies geography as a triage variable. If address-level deprivation predicts severity, then early-intervention services can be spatially targeted to the highest-burden neighbourhoods.

Core Methods

  • Cross-sectional clinical study: antipsychotic-naive first-episode patients assessed at presentation, removing medication-history as a confounder of baseline severity.
  • Exposure classification: residential addresses geocoded and labelled as slum vs. non-slum using official São Paulo/IBGE settlement boundaries.
  • Regression modelling: associations between slum residence and PANSS dimensions estimated with covariate adjustment (income, education, age, sex, race), reporting B coefficients and p-values.
  • Multi-outcome testing: separate models for total, negative, positive and disorganization PANSS scores, plus duration of untreated psychosis.
  • Social-determinant framing: income, education and race treated as explanatory social-exclusion variables rather than mere controls, interpreting the slum effect within a deprivation pathway.

① Replace the binary slum label with a continuous, satellite-derived deprivation surface

Extending this paperThe exposure here is a binary slum / non-slum flag tied to a single census-era boundary. Slums are internally heterogeneous and rapidly changing, so a coarse label likely attenuates the true dose–response. A direct extension: rebuild the exposure as a continuous deprivation index from remote sensing + morphology (built density, roof material, street connectivity) at each patient's address, then re-fit the PANSS models against the gradient rather than the dichotomy — turning the paper's "slum effect" into a calibrated deprivation–severity curve.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my multi-source data-fusion line: fusing satellite morphology, POIs and census into a fine-grained deprivation raster gives me an exposure layer I can plug straight into health-accessibility models, and a reusable GeoAI slum-mapping pipeline for other megacities.

② Add a spatial-accessibility pathway to explain the duration-of-untreated-psychosis gap

Extending this paperThe paper reports that slum residence and brown/Black race track longer duration of untreated psychosis (DUP), but treats DUP as an outcome without modelling why care is delayed. A natural extension: layer in spatial access to mental-health services from each residence — travel time and supply-to-demand ratios to the nearest CAPS/early-intervention clinic — and test whether access mediates the slum→DUP→severity chain. That converts a correlational finding into a service-geography mechanism.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my 2SFCA/R2SFCA work to mental-health care: building a São Paulo mental-health accessibility surface and using it as a mediator is exactly the health-geography / digital-divide analysis I do, now extended to psychiatric early intervention.

Cross-ref (corpus): Mobile phone data show spatial and socioeconomic inequalities in hospital utilization — 10.1038/s44360-026-00100-6

③ Make the social-determinant model explainable and spatially non-stationary

Extending this paperThe analysis uses global linear regression, implicitly assuming the slum/income/education effects on PANSS are constant across the city. But the deprivation–severity relationship may be stronger in some districts than others. Extending it with MGWR and an explainable ML ensemble (SHAP) would map where deprivation bites hardest and rank the relative contribution of income vs. education vs. race vs. slum morphology — a spatially-resolved, interpretable upgrade of the paper's coefficients.

Added value to my researchThis is my explainable-spatial-prediction agenda end to end: MGWR for non-stationarity plus SHAP-attributed ensembles, applied to a real clinical outcome, gives a methods showcase that bridges GeoAI and public-mental-health policy.

Cross-ref (corpus): Genetics and the geography of health, behaviour and attainment — 10.1038/s41562-019-0562-1

④ Reframe slum residence as experienced segregation from mobility, not just home address

Extending this paperThe paper fixes a person to their residential slum status. Yet social defeat and exclusion accrue through daily exposure across the whole city, not only at home. Extending it: use GPS/mobile-phone trajectories to compute each patient's experienced socioeconomic segregation (where they actually spend time) and test whether this dynamic exposure predicts PANSS better than a static home label — sharpening the social-exclusion mechanism the paper invokes.

Added value to my researchThis marries my GPS-trajectory and spatiotemporal-KDE methods to a clinical endpoint: an "experienced-exclusion" exposure metric is a transferable construct for my space–human coupling work, reusable across health and accessibility studies.

Cross-ref (corpus): US cities are defined by rings and pockets with limited socioeconomic mixing — 10.1038/s44284-025-00350-7

⑤ Turn the finding into a spatial intervention-targeting and optimization problem

Extending this paperThe paper ends at association; the obvious policy question is where to deploy early-intervention and crisis resources to cut severity and DUP most. Extending it: combine the deprivation–severity surface with a facility-siting / co-responder optimization that trades off efficiency against equity, simulating how many additional outreach teams or clinics, placed where, would shrink the slum severity gap.
