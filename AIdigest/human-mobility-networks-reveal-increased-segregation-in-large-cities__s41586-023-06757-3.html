Human mobility networks reveal increased segregation in large cities

Hamed Nilforoshan · Wenli Looi · Emma Pierson · Blanca Villanueva · Nic Fishman · Yiling Chen · John Sholar · Beth Redbird · David Grusky · Jure Leskovec Published 2023-11-29 DOI 10.1038/s41586-023-06757-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Contrary to the long-standing "cosmopolitan mixing hypothesis," exposure segregation is 67% higher in the ten largest US metropolitan areas than in small MSAs — large cities are more segregated, not less, because they offer residents a greater choice of socioeconomically differentiated spaces. This segregative effect can be substantially countered when commercial hubs (shopping centres, plazas) are positioned to bridge diverse neighbourhoods: MSAs with the highest bridging index are 53% less segregated than those with the lowest, independent of population size, income inequality, or race.

Why It Matters

  • Refutes a century-old conjecture in human geography. The "cosmopolitan mixing hypothesis" — that big cities reduce socioeconomic segregation through density and diversity — is empirically falsified for the first time at a nationwide scale using real behavioural data, not residential proxies.
  • Introduces exposure segregation as a richer measure. The paper's dynamic, GPS-based measure captures who people actually encounter at work, leisure, and en route — 38% lower than static residential segregation — revealing that conventional census-tract indices systematically overestimate social sorting.
  • Identifies a tractable urban-design lever. The bridging index translates an abstract sociological finding into a concrete, actionable metric: hub (shopping centre) placement predicts segregation more accurately than population size, inequality, or race, pointing planners toward specific zoning interventions.
  • Links mobility-derived segregation to downstream inequality. Exposure segregation predicts upward economic mobility (Spearman = −0.37) more strongly than the conventional neighbourhood sorting index (Spearman = −0.12), grounding the metric in real-world consequence.
  • Nation-scale infrastructure for future research. The resulting dataset — 1.6 billion exposure events among 9.6 million people across 382 MSAs and 2,829 US counties — is publicly shared at segregation.stanford.edu and GitHub, enabling follow-on studies.

Core Methods

  • Home inference and SES imputation: each of the 9.6 million mobile phone users has their home location inferred from stationary night-time GPS pings (18:00–09:00), then linked via CoreLogic addresses to Zillow monthly rent estimates as a continuous SES proxy — more accurate than tract-median census imputation.
  • Dynamic exposure network construction: two users are defined as "exposed" when their GPS pings are within D = 50 m and T = 5 minutes of each other. A k-d tree implementation parallelised across 288 HPC cores identifies 1.57 billion exposure dyads in under a week. Each edge carries a timestamp and geolocation.
  • Exposure segregation estimation via linear mixed-effects model: the key metric is the Pearson correlation between each person's SES and the mean SES of all individuals they encounter. A mixed-effects model (R lme4, REML) corrects the downward bias that naive sample Pearson correlations exhibit in data-sparse areas — a generalisation of the neighbourhood sorting index.
  • Decomposition by activity type and tie strength: exposures are annotated by fine-grained POI polygon (SafeGraph Places) and TIGER road/rail, enabling segregation decomposition by location type (restaurant, stadium, golf course, religious organisation) and tie strength (1 vs. 5+ co-occurrences).
  • Bridging index: a new, mobility-data-free metric. Homes are clustered by nearest commercial centre (hub); within-hub SES diversity (Gini index), normalised by city-wide SES diversity, yields a 0–1 score capturing whether hubs are positioned to bridge diverse neighbourhoods. Validated via regression controlling for population, inequality, race, and walkability.
  • Statistical validation: bootstrap hypothesis tests (10,000 replications); Spearman correlation tests (two-sided Student's t); Steiger's Z-test to compare predictor strength; robustness confirmed across multiple D/T thresholds, alternative SES measures, and varying tie-strength criteria.

① Adapt the bridging index as a spatial accessibility equity metric for health facility siting

Extending this paperThe paper's bridging index measures how well commercial hubs bridge socioeconomically diverse neighbourhoods, and it already demonstrates that hub placement is a stronger predictor of social integration than population size, income inequality, or race. A direct extension: reframe the bridging index for public health facility siting — replacing commercial centres with primary care clinics, mental health services, or vaccination sites. By clustering residential census block groups around their nearest facility and computing within-cluster SES diversity, planners could identify whether existing facility networks bridge or reinforce residential segregation. The paper's GPS-based validation pipeline (exposure network correlated with Facebook social connectedness and economic mobility) provides a ready-made external validation template.

Added value to my researchThis is a natural synthesis with my 2SFCA/R2SFCA health accessibility work. The bridging index gives R2SFCA a supply-side complement: while 2SFCA measures how easily patients reach facilities, the bridging index measures whether the facility network mixes or segregates patients by SES — adding an equity dimension that distance-decay metrics alone cannot capture. Applied to my telehealth/digital-divide work, it could show whether broadband access points bridge or reinforce the digital divide across income strata.

② From GPS-inferred SES to multi-source SES: fusing mobile phone, remote sensing, and POI data

Extending this paperThe paper's SES measure is anchored exclusively to housing rent (Zillow) via an inferred home address — a one-dimensional proxy that the authors themselves flag as a limitation. Extending the paper: replace or supplement the Zillow rent with a multi-source SES index that fuses nighttime light intensity from satellite imagery, POI density patterns, census income data, and mobility-derived consumption proxies (e.g., types of restaurants visited). Such an index would generalise beyond US cities where Zillow data are unavailable, enabling the paper's exposure segregation methodology to be applied in Global South cities where rental databases are absent. The paper's mixed-effects estimation framework accommodates any continuous SES variable, making this substitution technically straightforward.

Added value to my researchMulti-source data fusion — mobile phone records, social media, remote sensing, POIs — is a core theme of my research. This extension would produce a transferable geospatial foundation model for SES estimation that I could fine-tune for health geography applications: estimating neighbourhood deprivation in cities without census coverage, improving the supply-side inputs to R2SFCA models, or feeding socioeconomic layers into spatiotemporal kernel density estimation of health outcomes.

③ Decompose exposure segregation by time of day to identify activity-specific segregation windows

Extending this paperThe paper decomposes segregation by tie strength and POI category but only briefly touches on temporal decomposition (Supplementary Fig. 21). A focused extension: compute exposure segregation in 3-hour windows across the full day for each MSA, then model whether peak-segregation windows (e.g., lunch hours at differentiated restaurants vs. evening commute crossings) vary systematically with city size, land-use zoning, and transit accessibility. The paper already has the timestamped edges (1.57 billion exposures each with t, lat, lon) to support this — it is a matter of stratified re-estimation with the same mixed-effects pipeline. This would identify when urban design interventions are most needed, not just where.

④ Predict the bridging index from street morphology using explainable spatial ML

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that the bridging index strongly predicts exposure segregation (Spearman = −0.78) and that it is determined by three built-environment factors — hub location, hub count, and residential SES distribution. But the paper does not predict the bridging index itself from observable urban morphology features. A targeted extension: build an explainable ML model (gradient boosting + SHAP) that predicts the bridging index for any MSA using only street network geometry (connectivity, intersection density), zoning classifications, transit stop density, and census residential SES — features available without mobility data. This would make the bridging index computable for cities where SafeGraph-style data do not exist, and SHAP attributions would identify which morphological features most strongly determine integration potential.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my explainable machine learning for spatial prediction agenda. The bridging index becomes a new labelled target variable for GeoAI: a city-scale equity outcome derived from GPS data that can be learned from open street/census features. MGWR/GWR extensions of the predictor model would reveal whether the morphology-bridging relationship is spatially stationary or varies by region — a question central to my geospatial methods research on spatial non-stationarity.

⑤ Apply the exposure network framework to quantify health-service encounter segregation

Extending this paperThe paper defines exposure segregation as the correlation between a person's SES and the mean SES of whom they encounter in everyday space — currently measured only in commercial and leisure contexts. A substantive extension: apply the identical framework to healthcare encounters. Using GPS mobility traces (or claims-based visit records), construct a bipartite network of patients visiting health facilities, annotate each patient with SES (via rent or income), and measure whether high-SES and low-SES patients visit different facilities or the same ones. The resulting "healthcare encounter segregation" score would directly complement residential healthcare accessibility metrics and test whether geographic proximity to a facility translates into shared usage across income groups — or whether differentiated facility types (private vs. public, specialist vs. primary care) recreate the same homophily-generating mechanism the paper documents in restaurants and golf clubs.

Added value to my researchThis is the most direct linkage to my health geography and accessibility research. A healthcare encounter segregation measure would tell me whether the facilities identified as "accessible" by R2SFCA are actually serving diverse income groups in practice — a behavioural validity check that static supply-demand models cannot provide. It would also enrich my mental health accessibility studies: if mental health clinics in large cities serve narrowly defined SES groups (analogous to the Michelin-star restaurant pattern), spatial proximity may be a necessary but insufficient condition for equitable access.
