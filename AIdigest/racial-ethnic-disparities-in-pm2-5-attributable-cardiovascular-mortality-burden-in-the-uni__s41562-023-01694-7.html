Racial/ethnic disparities in PM2.5-attributable cardiovascular mortality burden in the United States

Yiqun Ma · Emma Zang · Ijeoma Opara · Yuan Lu · Harlan M. Krumholz · Kai Chen Published 2023-08-31 DOI 10.1038/s41562-023-01694-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 3,103 US counties from 2001 to 2016, a 1 µg m−3 increase in long-term PM2.5 was associated with 4× more cardiovascular deaths per capita in non-Hispanic Black people than in non-Hispanic white people — attributable to both greater exposure (environmental racism) and greater vulnerability (structural racism). Although the absolute Black–white disparity in PM2.5-attributable CVD mortality fell by 44% over this period, in 2016 the per-capita burden remained 3.47 times higher for non-Hispanic Black people and 1.45 times higher for Hispanic people, underscoring that improvements in air quality alone will not close the racial health gap without policies that also tackle differential vulnerability.

Why It Matters

  • Disentangles exposure from vulnerability. Prior work measured who breathes the most pollution; this study separately estimates race-/ethnicity-specific exposure–response functions, showing that Black people face a biologically and socially amplified health toll beyond the extra pollution they inhale.
  • Quantifies the hidden cost of a uniform ERF. Using a single average exposure–response function underestimates total PM2.5-attributable CVD deaths by roughly 19,000 deaths per year (69,675 vs 88,786) — a systematic bias in every previous burden assessment.
  • Tracks 16 years of longitudinal change. The 2001–2016 panel across all 3,103 contiguous counties allows causal inference with interactive fixed effects while documenting a dramatic 34% decline in total attributable deaths — and showing the disparity has persisted in relative terms.
  • Directly actionable for environmental policy. The findings call for pollution standards that go beyond reducing average concentrations and instead prioritize communities with elevated biological and social vulnerability — a reframing with direct regulatory consequence.
  • Open data and code. R code is publicly available and supporting data deposited at Zenodo, enabling replication and extension across new geographies and time windows.

Core Methods

  • Interactive fixed effects (IFE) panel model: extends the classical two-way fixed effects (TWFE) model by decomposing unmeasured time-varying county-specific confounders into heterogeneous impacts of common latent trends — relaxing the "no time-varying unobserved confounders" assumption that TWFE requires and that was detected to be violated (P < 0.001) in this data.
  • Race-/ethnicity-specific exposure–response functions (ERF): separate IFE models fitted for non-Hispanic white, non-Hispanic Black, and Hispanic populations to estimate race-specific PM2.5–CVD-mortality slopes (β), with between-group differences tested by CI overlap and interaction terms in expanded nested models.
  • Attributable mortality burden calculation: ANi,t = β × PM2.5 i,t × Populationi,t, with reference concentration set to 0 µg m−3 (no safe level); sensitivity check used the 10th percentile (4.58 µg m−3) as an alternative reference.
  • Age-adjustment: direct standardization using the 2000 US Census population; all mortality rates age-adjusted to ensure comparability across counties and time.
  • Lag structure analysis: 12-month moving average PM2.5 as the primary exposure measure; robustness tested with 24-, 36-, 48-, and 60-month moving averages to capture longer-term lag effects.
  • Spatial and temporal placebo tests: PM2.5 concentrations randomly permuted 2,000 times (spatially and temporally) to confirm that the observed association is not an artifact of spatial/temporal dependence or model misspecification.
  • Urban/rural stratification: NCHS urban–rural classification applied to test effect modification by urbanization level.
  • Software: R v4.1.3, phtt package for IFE estimation; all tests two-sided.

① Resolve within-county exposure heterogeneity using mobility-weighted PM2.5

Extending this paperThe authors acknowledge their principal limitation: the study is county-level and cannot capture within-county variation in PM2.5 exposure across racial/ethnic groups. A direct extension is to replace the area-weighted county-mean PM2.5 with a mobility-weighted personal exposure estimate — integrating GPS-derived activity patterns of each racial/ethnic group with the 1 km gridded pollution surface. This would resolve the ecological fallacy at the heart of the paper and likely increase the estimated racial disparity (consistent with the paper's Supplementary Fig. 7 showing a larger disparity when race-specific population-weighted PM2.5 is used).

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and mobile-phone trajectory methods provide exactly the infrastructure for this extension: constructing individual-level exposure by convolving mobility trajectories with the 1 km PM2.5 raster produces a dynamic, population-stratified exposure surface that can feed directly into the IFE model. This enriches my health-geography agenda by linking fine-grained mobility analytics to causal health burden estimation.

② Decompose the Black–white vulnerability gap into social-determinant pathways with explainable ML

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that the Black–white difference in ERF exists but does not explain which structural factors drive elevated vulnerability — healthcare access deficits, chronic stress, comorbidity prevalence, neighborhood greenness deficits, or housing quality. An extension using the paper's county-month panel as the outcome and attaching a rich set of social-determinant covariates (neighborhood deprivation index, healthcare facility access scores, green space area) could decompose the residual disparity into attributable pathways with SHAP-based feature attribution, converting a "black box" vulnerability gap into an actionable target list for policymakers.

Using MGWR or GWR to spatially stratify the SHAP attributions would reveal whether healthcare-access deficits or green-space deficits dominate vulnerability in different regions — a multi-layer geospatial explanation that my methods are uniquely suited to provide.

③ Test whether green-infrastructure interventions differentially reduce the disparity versus the average burden

Extending this paperThe paper's policy call is to reduce both exposure and vulnerability for racial/ethnic minorities. A key candidate intervention is green infrastructure (trees, parks), which reduces local PM2.5 concentrations. The corpus contains direct evidence that green infrastructure has race-unequal placement. An extension of this paper's IFE framework would add a green-space-change interaction term — testing whether counties with growing canopy cover showed faster convergence in the racial ERF gap — giving empirical traction to a class of environmental-justice interventions.

Added value to my researchMy spatial accessibility and 2SFCA methods, applied to parks and green space as "facilities," can quantify the equity of green-infrastructure distribution at county level and over time, providing the exposure variable for this extension. This extends my healthy-cities research from service-facility access to environmental amenity access, with a direct cardiovascular health outcome.

④ Extend to sub-regulatory concentrations and causal identification with double negative controls

Extending this paperThe paper's sensitivity analysis shows a near-linear dose–response above the 10th percentile of PM2.5 (4.58 µg m−3), but most of the US now sits at concentrations below the current NAAQS standard (12 µg m−3). The race-stratified ERF has never been estimated specifically in the sub-regulatory concentration range. An extension replacing the IFE model with a double negative control approach — which uses two negative-control variables (one for the outcome, one for exposure) to identify causal effects under unmeasured confounding — would produce causal estimates at low concentrations across racial groups, directly informing whether lowering the standard would close or merely shift the disparity.

Added value to my researchThe double negative control framework is a transferable causal-inference tool applicable across my health-geography work: it could replace propensity-score matching in my telehealth/digital-divide studies where experimental variation is absent but negative-control outcomes are identifiable. Learning this methodology as a bridge between epidemiology and GeoAI is high-leverage for my research portfolio.

⑤ Build a global PM2.5-burden atlas with race-analogous socioeconomic stratification

Extending this paperThe paper covers only the contiguous US and only three racial/ethnic groups (non-Hispanic white, Black, Hispanic). A global extension would use the paper's IFE methodology but substitute race/ethnicity — unavailable in most countries — with a socioeconomic vulnerability index (poverty rate, educational attainment, healthcare access score) to proxy differential vulnerability across nations. This transforms the paper's US-specific findings into a generalizable global environmental-justice burden assessment, bridging it with SDG 3.9 monitoring.
