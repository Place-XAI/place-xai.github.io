Urban food delivery services as extreme heat adaptation

Yunke Zhang · Daoping Wang · Yu Liu · Kerui Du · Peng Lu · Pan He · Yong Li Published 2025-01-02 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00172-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Urban residents use food delivery as an immediate behavioral adaptation to extreme heat: across 100 Chinese cities (2017–2023), lunchtime orders rise more than 12.6% as temperature climbs from 20 °C to 35 °C and 21.4% at 40 °C. The benefit — avoided personal heat exposure — accrues unevenly, more strongly to female, high-income and older consumers, while heat exposure is transferred from consumers to delivery riders. The technology is both adaptation and inequity, demanding policy that protects the workers who absorb the displaced risk.

★

Why It Matters

  • Quantifies a new, market-mediated adaptation channel. Most heat-adaptation research studies infrastructure (green space, cool roofs) or physiology; this is among the first to measure a digital-service behavior — on-demand food delivery — as a real-time coping response to temperature.
  • Reveals a hidden distributional transfer. The same service that reduces consumers' heat exposure redistributes that exposure onto gig riders, surfacing an environmental-justice problem invisible in aggregate order statistics.
  • Maps who benefits. Heterogeneous responses by gender, income and age turn a convenience story into an equity story, identifying which groups can and cannot afford to buy their way out of heat.
  • Gives policy a concrete lever. By pricing the "gains and pains" of the platform economy under climate stress, it argues for targeted protections (rider heat standards, scheduling, compensation) rather than treating delivery as purely beneficial.

Core Methods

  • Temperature–response estimation: regress lunchtime order volume on daily temperature to recover the heat-induced surge curve (panel fixed-effects models via the R fixest package, controlling for city/time effects).
  • Heterogeneity analysis: stratify the response by gender, income and age to quantify who adapts most strongly and who benefits least.
  • Avoided-exposure quantification: translate substituted outdoor trips into reduced consumer heat exposure, monetizing the protective gain of delivery.
  • Exposure-transfer accounting: reallocate the avoided consumer exposure onto delivery riders, measuring workload and experienced heat to expose the consumer→worker transfer.
  • Future projection: couple estimated elasticities with CMIP6 warming scenarios to project adaptation demand and the growing exposure burden on riders.

① Make the consumer→rider exposure transfer spatially explicit

Extending this paperThe paper establishes that delivery shifts heat exposure from consumers to riders but treats the transfer at the city aggregate. A direct extension: route the surge orders over the actual street network and the urban heat island field, so the displaced exposure is resolved at the street segment level — revealing which neighborhoods generate the most rider heat-debt and whether high-income demand pushes exposure into already-hot, low-shade corridors.

② Turn the order-surge curve into a heat-vulnerability accessibility index

Extending this paperThe paper's temperature–order curve is a behavioral signal of who can afford to adapt. Extending it: invert the elasticity into a heat-adaptive-accessibility measure — the capacity of a population to substitute delivery for outdoor exposure — and contrast it against physical access to cooling (green space, cool buildings) to find populations doubly exposed (low affordability and low cooling access).

⑤ Optimize rider dispatch as an efficiency-vs-equity heat-protection problem

Extending this paperThe paper diagnoses the rider exposure transfer but proposes no mechanism to manage it.
