Urban transitions toward sufficiency-oriented circular post-consumer textile economies

Katia Vladimirova · Yassie Samie · Irene Maldini · Samira Iran · Kirsi Laitala · Claudia E. Henninger · Sarah Ibrahim Alosaimi · Kelly Drennan · Hannah Lam · Ana-Luisa Teixeira · Iva Jestratijevic · Sabine Weber Published 2024-10-23 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00140-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across nine wealthy OECD cities, the way unwanted clothing is managed is strikingly similar: cities lean on collection, charity resale and — above all — export of used textiles abroad. This export pathway makes the growing pile of post-consumer textile waste, itself a direct product of fashion overproduction and overconsumption, effectively invisible to the public and to municipalities. The authors argue cities must shift from end-of-pipe waste handling to upstream sufficiency (consuming less), and offer a concrete list of actionable policy recommendations for circular, sufficient urban textile systems.

Why It Matters

  • Fills a near-empty evidence gap. What actually happens to clothes after consumers discard them is poorly documented at the city level; this is one of the first cross-city, comparative accounts of post-consumer textile flows.
  • Names the "invisibility" mechanism. By routing unwanted textiles toward exports, wealthy cities externalize their waste problem — keeping it off municipal balance sheets and out of public view, which suppresses the political pressure needed for action.
  • Reframes the lever from recycling to sufficiency. The paper argues that circularity alone cannot absorb the volume produced by overproduction/overconsumption; consuming less (sufficiency) is the missing policy axis.
  • Gives city governments a to-do list. It translates analysis into concrete, actionable policy recommendations that municipalities — not just nations or brands — can adopt.

Core Methods

  • Comparative case-study design: nine wealthy OECD cities chosen as comparable high-consumption hubs, enabling cross-city pattern detection.
  • Policy-document analysis: systematic reading of municipal/regional/national circular-economy, waste and textile strategies to map how each city governs post-consumer textile flows.
  • Expert interviews: qualitative interviews with stakeholders across the collection–resale–recycling–export chain to capture practice on the ground (data kept confidential).
  • Literature triangulation: scientific and gray literature plus statistical sources (EEA, EPA, Textile Exchange) used to corroborate and quantify the export-driven "invisibility" of textile waste.
  • Synthesis to policy: findings distilled into a sufficiency-oriented framework and a list of actionable recommendations for city governments.

① Turn the qualitative export "invisibility" into a quantified material-flow map

Extending this paperThe paper's central claim — that exports make textile waste invisible — is established qualitatively from policy documents and interviews. A direct extension: build a spatially explicit post-consumer textile material-flow account for the nine cities, fusing UN Comtrade used-textile trade flows, EEA/EPA recovery statistics, and charity-collection records into an origin–destination flow graph, so the "invisible" export volumes become an auditable, comparable number per city.

② From a uniform OECD picture to a density- and equity-stratified one

Extending this paperThe paper reports similarity across nine wealthy cities but treats each city as monolithic. Extending it: descend to the neighborhood scale and test whether collection infrastructure (donation bins, drop-off points, repair/resale outlets) is equitably distributed across income and density gradients within each city — converting an inter-city comparison into an intra-city equity diagnosis.

Added value to my researchThis maps cleanly onto my 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility toolkit: treat textile-collection facilities as "supply" and population as "demand," producing a spatial-accessibility-to-circular-infrastructure index — a 15-minute-circularity metric analogous to my healthcare-access work.

Extending it: model the full coupled system — wealthy-city outflow linked to importing-region absorption capacity, informal resale markets and landfilling — so "waste colonialism" is represented as a two-sided spatial process rather than an offstage sink.
