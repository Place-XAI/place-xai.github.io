Strong demand for clean air policies in Majority World cities

E. Keith Smith · Ella Henninger · Camille Fournier de Lauriere · Vally Koubi · Raeesa Moolla · Liam F. Beiser-McGrath · Thomas Bernauer Published 2026-05-28 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00451-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across four severely polluted Majority-World cities — Accra, Delhi, Jakarta and Johannesburg — residents do not treat dirty air as an acceptable price of development. In a survey of 11,417 adults, air quality was a highly prioritized concern and majorities backed all six mitigation proposals, including cost-salient ones (stricter industrial limits, household electrification, expanded public transport). Concern, trust in government, and low perceived behavioral control were the strongest correlates of support — challenging the view of weak environmental demand in highly exposed, economically developing settings.

★

Why It Matters

  • Overturns a policy assumption. The "pollute now, clean up later" / limited-environmentalism narrative for poorer economies is contradicted by direct evidence: people in these cities want government action even at economic cost.
  • Fills a measurement gap. Cross-national evidence on policy support (not just diffuse concern) in the Majority World was scarce; this delivers comparable, pre-registered measures across four continents-worth of megacities.
  • Identifies actionable levers. Because concern, institutional trust and behavioral control dominate the explained variance, interventions that build trust and a sense of agency may matter more than income alone.
  • Speaks to the most exposed populations. These cities sit among the world's worst for PM2.5 (IQAir 2024 rankings); demonstrating public mandate here strengthens the case for ambitious urban clean-air policy where the health burden is highest.

Core Methods

  • Pre-registered cross-national survey: harmonized instrument fielded in four cities, enabling comparable measures of concern and policy support rather than diffuse attitudes.
  • Descriptive prioritization & support analysis: most-important-problem rankings and majority-support shares across the six mitigation policies, per city and pooled.
  • Correlates of support: linear models of a composite average policy-support scale on individual covariates; variance decomposed by Shapley value (LMG metric, relaimpo in R) to rank relative importance of predictors per city.
  • Trade-off simulations: city-specific logistic regressions of preferring pollution reduction vs. economic growth, with predicted-probability changes as each predictor moves min→max (95% CIs).
  • Robustness: outcomes reported by city and across policies (Figs. 5–8), with full replication code archived for reproducibility.

① Geolocate support and couple it to measured exposure surfaces

Extending this paperThe paper treats each city as a unit and uses respondent-level perceptions of air quality. A direct extension: tie every respondent to a neighborhood-level PM2.5 surface (satellite/low-cost-sensor blended) and test whether policy support tracks objective exposure gradients within a city, not just perceived ones — turning the four-city contrast into an intra-urban dose–response of demand. The Bronx hyperlocal-exposure work shows how mobility-weighted exposure can be reconstructed at fine scale.

Added value to my researchThis is exactly my multi-source-fusion + spatial-accessibility agenda: I can join survey demand to remote-sensing and POI exposure layers, then run MGWR to map where the perception–exposure gap is widest — a spatial diagnostic for targeting clean-air outreach.

② Replace the variance-decomposition with explainable spatial ML

Extending this paperThe paper ranks predictors with Shapley/LMG on linear models per city. Extending it: fit a single pooled gradient-boosted / explainable-ML model with model-agnostic SHAP, allowing nonlinear and interaction effects (e.g., trust × concern, behavioral-control × income) and testing whether the importance ranking is stable across the four cities or context-dependent — a sharper test of the paper's "concern–trust–control" claim.

Added value to my researchMethodologically identical to my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction line: SHAP-based attribution over fused individual + place covariates lets me separate compositional (who lives there) from contextual (where they live) drivers of demand — a template I reuse for telehealth-adoption and care-access modeling.

Cross-ref (corpus): Exploring the variances of climate change opinions in Germany at a fine-grained local scale — 10.1038/s41467-024-45930-8

③ Link the demand mandate to where the health burden actually falls

Extending this paperThe paper establishes that people want action but stops short of connecting demand to the distribution of avoidable harm. Extending it: overlay the support surface on poverty/exposure coupling so that the mandate is read together with who bears the mortality and economic burden — testing whether the strongest demand coincides with the worst exposure-poverty cells, which would make the equity case for prioritization explicit.

Added value to my researchThis bridges to my health-geography work: demand + exposure + service access can be fused into a single prioritization index for clean-air and clinic siting — an efficiency-vs-equity optimization analogous to my EV-charging and 2SFCA service-area framing.

Cross-ref (corpus): Global air pollution exposure and poverty — 10.1038/s41467-023-39797-4

④ Pair stated support with a low-cost message intervention

Extending this paperThe survey measures baseline support but cannot say what shifts it. A natural extension is to embed a randomized framing experiment (cost-salience, health framing, trust-building cues, AI-rendered visuals of cleaner streets) inside the same multi-city design, turning correlational drivers (concern, trust, control) into manipulable levers — and measuring whether the effect generalizes across the four contexts.

⑤ Scale the four-city probe toward a transferable demand model

Extending this paperFour cities establish a striking counter-narrative but cannot yet predict demand elsewhere. Extending it: treat the harmonized items as training signal and build a transferable model that predicts city-level support from city covariates (exposure, governance/trust indices, electrification, transport supply) — so the paper's finding becomes an out-of-sample forecast for the many Majority-World megacities not surveyed, while flagging where national climate action may help or hurt local equity.
