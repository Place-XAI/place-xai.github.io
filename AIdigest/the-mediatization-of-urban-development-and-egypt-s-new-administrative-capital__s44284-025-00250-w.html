The mediatization of urban development and Egypt's New Administrative Capital

Mennatullah Hendawy · R. R. Riad · S. H. Elgredly Published 2025-10-03 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00250-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Analyzing 111 Al-Ahram newspaper headlines (2015–2019) on Egypt's New Administrative Capital (NAC), the study shows that national press coverage did not merely report the megaproject — it helped construct it. Coverage shifted from framing the NAC as an investment opportunity and a solution to Cairo's urban problems (2015–2017) toward showcasing spectacular physical landmarks (2017–2019), privileging visual enticement and short-term financial returns while affordability and accessibility for the urban majority faded from view. Media and planning mutually reinforce one another, allowing neoliberal, exclusive urban growth to advance through virtual, visual and discursive means.

  • Names media as an active force in city-making, not a passive mirror. The paper operationalizes mediatization to show how text-based news exerts virtual, visual and discursive power that recreates urban futures rather than just describing them.
  • Documents a measurable narrative drift. A short five-year window reveals a fast turnover of visions — from responsive ("solve overpopulation, congestion, jobs") to physical-commercial ("highest tower in Africa, largest mosque/cathedral") — coinciding with the 2017 launch of the first gated compound, Midtown.

  • Travels beyond Egypt. New-capital and megaproject playbooks (Naypyidaw, Putrajaya, Sejong, Nusantara, Brasilia) share the same media logic; the framework is offered as transferable to other cities and global contexts.

Core Methods

  • Qualitative exploratory media analysis: national press treated as an apparatus reflecting and promoting urban visions, following a descriptive media-analysis design.
  • Thematic analysis (layer 1): 111 headlines inductively classified into five themes — Project's Details, Positive Opinions & Slogans, Project's Partners, Project's Execution, and Criticism (Fig. 1 timeline).
  • Discourse analysis (layer 2): abstracts the themes into three messaging patterns — the project as (1) an investment, (2) a solution to urban problems, (3) a landmark — linked to the Egyptian social/political/economic context (Fig. 2).
  • Mediatization framework: interprets the patterns as three urban-planning visions (commercial, responsive, physical) and traces their temporal shift (commercialism 2015–17 → responsive 2015–17 → physical 2017–19).
  • Triangulation: headline coding supplemented by full-text scanning, prior literature, and expert interpretation; multidisciplinary author team for rigor.

① Scale 111 hand-coded headlines into a reproducible NLP discourse pipeline

Extending this paperThe paper's thematic + discourse coding rests on 111 manually translated headlines coded by one author — robust qualitatively but hard to reproduce or scale across years, outlets, or social media. A direct extension: build a multilingual (Arabic/English) topic-modeling and sentiment pipeline over the full Al-Ahram run plus Facebook/YouTube, validate the five themes/three patterns against an LLM-assisted coder, and quantify exactly when the responsive-to-physical drift occurs — turning a qualitative claim into a dated, reproducible signal.
