Individual and city-level variations in heat-related road traffic deaths in Latin America

Cheng-Kai Hsu · D. Alex Quistberg · Brisa N. Sánchez · Josiah L. Kephart · Usama Bilal · Nelson Gouveia · Carolina Pérez Ferrer · Waleska T. Caiaffa · Amélia Augusta de Lima Friche · Ignacio Yannone · Daniel A. Rodríguez Published 2025-09-15 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00279-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 272 cities in six Latin American countries (2000–2019, >1.9 million city-days), road traffic mortality risk rose monotonically with ambient temperature, with significantly elevated risk on extremely hot days (95th and 99th temperature percentiles). The heat penalty was concentrated among younger people (≤19), males, motorcyclists and bicyclists, and in cities with hotter climates, longer commutes and more extended street segments — pointing to vulnerable road users in heat-exposed, informal, non-climate-controlled peripheral transport as the priority for protection.

Why It Matters

  • Two converging crises, finally quantified together. Latin America carries some of the world's highest road-traffic mortality and intensifying extreme heat; this is one of the first multi-city studies to measure how the two interact in the region.
  • Names who is at risk, not just that there is risk. By resolving the effect down to age, sex and road-user type, it shows the heat burden falls disproportionately on young people, males, and unprotected riders (motorcyclists, bicyclists).
  • Connects body-level vulnerability to city form. Risk is amplified in hotter cities with longer commutes and more extended street networks — making urban form and transport planning, not only individual behavior, the lever for intervention.
  • Speaks directly to the tropical Global South. It reframes climate adaptation around peripheral residents enduring long, heat-exposed commutes in informal, non-air-conditioned transport — a population usually invisible in heat-health research.

Core Methods

  • Time-stratified case-crossover design: each road-traffic death serves as its own control by comparing the death day to referent days in the same month/year/day-of-week, inherently controlling for time-invariant individual confounders and seasonality.
  • Distributed lag non-linear modeling: conditional logistic / distributed-lag framework relates daily ambient temperature (as city-specific percentiles) to mortality risk, recovering the monotonic, non-linear exposure–response curve (Fig. 1).
  • Temperature clustering: cities grouped into temperature clusters (Extended Data) so association curves can be compared across climate regimes (Fig. 2).
  • Subgroup / effect-modification analysis: stratification by age, sex, and road-user type (Fig. 3) and by city characteristics — climate, commute length, street-segment extent (Fig. 4).
  • Multi-city pooling: city-level estimates pooled (meta-analytic / hierarchical) to produce regional dose–response relationships; pipeline covers data processing, modeling, pooling, prediction reconstruction, EDF calculation and plotting (public code repository).

① Replace coarse ambient temperature with morphology-resolved heat exposure

Extending this paperThe paper exposes each death to a single city-level ERA5-Land temperature, yet the crash actually happens on a specific street under specific shade, canopy and surface conditions. A direct extension: couple the case-crossover outcome to morphology-resolved urban-heat fields — modeling temperature from building density, street geometry and land cover — so the exposure varies within the city along the very commute corridors the paper implicates. This would test whether the "extended street segment" effect modifier is really a proxy for hotter microclimates.

Added value to my researchThis feeds my spatiotemporal-KDE and explainable-ML pipelines: I can build a fine-grained, street-level heat-exposure surface from remote sensing and urban-form features and attribute crash risk to interpretable morphology drivers — a transferable GeoAI exposure layer for health geography.

② Turn the static exposure into demand-weighted, mobility-aware risk

Extending this paperThe design counts deaths but cannot see who was on the road, where, and for how long on a hot day. Heat may raise risk partly by changing exposure (more cyclists/motorcyclists out) and partly by changing per-trip hazard. Extending the paper with mobility data (GPS traces, mobile-phone records) as a time-varying denominator would separate "more exposure" from "more dangerous exposure" and sharpen the vulnerable-road-user story.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my multi-source data-fusion line: combining GPS/mobile-phone flows with health outcomes lets me build a demand-aware, heat-conditioned accessibility/exposure metric — the same machinery that powers my 2SFCA work, repurposed for risk rather than service supply.

Cross-ref (corpus): Global expansion of the ecological impact of extra-urban road traffic — 10.1038/s41893-025-01637-2

③ Couple heat-crash risk to commute accessibility and the job–housing periphery

Extending this paperThe paper's strongest city-level finding — longer commutes amplify heat risk — is exactly an accessibility problem. Extending it: map each city's job–housing accessibility and commute-burden gradient and test whether peripheral, low-accessibility neighborhoods carry the excess heat-related mortality, converting a city-average modifier into an intra-urban equity map.

Added value to my researchThis unites my accessibility (2SFCA/R2SFCA), job–housing-network, and 15-minute-neighborhood threads with heat-health: a poor accessibility landscape becomes a measurable health hazard, giving my service-area work a direct mortality endpoint.

④ From association to intervention: shade, cooling and protection as an optimization

Extending this paperThe paper ends with a policy call (protect peripheral vulnerable road users) but no allocation plan. Extending it: pose where to place shade, cooling stops and protected lanes along heat-exposed corridors as an efficiency-vs-equity facility-location problem, using the paper's own dose–response curve as the objective to minimize predicted excess deaths under a fixed budget.

⑤ A physics-informed, transferable heat–mortality operator across cities

Extending this paperThe paper pools 272 cities but still estimates each curve largely independently before pooling. Extending it: learn a single physics-informed spatial model that maps (temperature regime, urban form, mobility, demographics) → exposure–response curve, with the observed monotonic-in-heat shape encoded as a soft constraint, so the framework extrapolates to data-poor Global-South cities not in SALURBAL.
