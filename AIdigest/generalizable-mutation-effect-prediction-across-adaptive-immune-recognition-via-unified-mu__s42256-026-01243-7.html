Generalizable mutation-effect prediction across adaptive immune recognition via unified multimodal framework

Rong Han · Yumeng Zhang · Xiaohong Liu · Lei Fu · Tong Pan · Jing Xu · Xiaoyu Wang · Peidong Zhang · Xuanzhong Chen · Jiesi Lei · Wuyang Lan · Changwei Ji · Shuguang Cui · Song Wu · Jiangning Song · Ting Chen · Guangyu Wang Published 2026-05-27 DOI 10.1038/s42256-026-01243-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

UniAIR is a unified, modular, multimodal framework that accurately predicts how amino-acid mutations reshape adaptive immune recognition — covering antibody maturation, antigen escape, and TCR–pHLA binding — across both extracellular and intracellular scenarios. By fusing evolutionary sequence information with geometric structural representations via an interface-centric sequence–structure fusion transformer, UniAIR achieves state-of-the-art performance on the SKEMPIv2 benchmark and multiple independent test sets, delivers robust predictions with minimal task-specific tuning, and successfully performs multi-round peptide optimization under sparse experimental feedback — establishing a generalizable computational foundation for mapping immune mutation landscapes and accelerating immunotherapeutic design.

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the task-specific silo problem. Previous computational models for immune mutation effects are built for single modalities (sequence only or structure only) or single tasks (antibody or TCR). UniAIR is the first framework that generalizes across the full heterogeneous landscape of adaptive immune recognition, substantially reducing the re-engineering cost for each new immunological setting.
  • Tackles a major clinical bottleneck. Designing antibodies with stronger maturation, predicting viral antigen escape, and optimizing TCR–pHLA binding are central to vaccine development, cancer immunotherapy, and pandemic preparedness. A generalizable predictor accelerates all three simultaneously.
  • Works without experimental structures. By adapting to AlphaFold/ESMFold-predicted structures, UniAIR removes the bottleneck of requiring expensive crystallography data, extending applicability to proteins with no solved experimental structures.
  • Demonstrates real-world utility under sparse feedback. UniAIR's multi-round peptide optimization of a TCR–pHLA complex with minimal experimental labels exemplifies a closed-loop AI-guided wet-lab cycle — the key paradigm for next-generation drug discovery.
  • Open and reproducible. Code, model weights, and all benchmark datasets are publicly released on GitHub, Zenodo, and HuggingFace, enabling community adoption and reproducibility.

Core Methods

  • Interface-centric sequence–structure fusion transformer: the core model attends specifically to the protein–protein interface residues, fusing evolutionary information (from protein language model embeddings) with geometric 3D representations — enabling it to capture both conservation signals and structural context for mutation-effect prediction.
  • Modular multimodal data pipeline: a standardized preprocessing pipeline normalizes heterogeneous inputs (antibody–antigen, TCR–pMHC, TCR–pHLA) into a common representation, enabling a single model to ingest multiple immunological task types without task-specific preprocessing.
  • Multi-expert consensus ensemble: a suite of expert modules contributes to a consensus prediction, improving robustness across diverse immune recognition tasks and reducing task-specific fine-tuning requirements.
  • Predicted-structure adaptation: an extension module adapts the framework to use AlphaFold/ESMFold-predicted structures rather than experimental crystal structures, maintaining prediction quality when experimental structures are unavailable.
  • Few-shot fine-tuning with FEP integration: for TCR–pHLA peptide optimization, UniAIR couples its mutation predictor with Free Energy Perturbation (FEP) in multi-round loops under sparse experimental annotations, demonstrating closed-loop optimization capability.
  • Large-scale benchmarking: systematic evaluation on SKEMPIv2, TCRen benchmark, HER2, ATLAS, LASSA, and SARS-CoV-2 escape sets, with zero-shot and few-shot regimes explicitly separated to characterize model generalization.

① Transfer UniAIR's interface-centric fusion to spatial interaction modeling

Extending this paperUniAIR's core innovation is the interface-centric sequence–structure fusion transformer: rather than encoding the whole protein, it focuses attention on residues at the molecular binding interface, fusing evolutionary context (sequence) with geometric context (3D structure). The paper demonstrates this focus dramatically improves prediction at the precise site of functional change. A direct extension would be to ask whether this interface-centering principle also applies to multi-chain immune complexes beyond antibody–antigen and TCR–pHLA, such as cytokine–receptor interfaces or co-stimulatory checkpoint interactions, using newly available structural databases (e.g., updated PDB entries for PD-1/PD-L1 or CAR-T constructs). Coupling with the conformational flexibility modeling from the corpus would further extend UniAIR to dynamic rather than static interfaces.

Added value to my researchThe interface-centric attention mechanism is a direct analogy for how I model spatial interaction zones — e.g., the "catchment interface" between facility supply areas and residential demand in 2SFCA/R2SFCA. Just as UniAIR focuses on residues at the binding interface rather than encoding the whole protein, a spatial accessibility model could focus attention on the boundary zone between service catchments rather than the full service area polygon. This could yield a more precise, geometry-aware delineation of healthcare access zones, especially in complex urban geometries with overlapping catchments.

② Generalizable multi-task pretraining as a template for geospatial foundation models

Extending this paperUniAIR's key contribution is moving from task-specific models to a unified pretrained backbone that adapts to multiple immunological scenarios with minimal task-specific tuning. The paper benchmarks this across four distinct downstream tasks (antibody maturation, antigen escape, TCR–pMHC, TCR–pHLA) — all sharing the same molecular recognition physics. A natural extension would be to test whether UniAIR's multi-expert consensus module can be scaled to an even larger pretraining corpus encompassing all published protein–protein interface mutations, not just immune-related ones, using PDB-wide mutation scanning databases. This would test whether the framework's generalization reflects true universal learning of binding-interface physics or is still partially domain-specific.

Added value to my researchThe multi-task pretraining + minimal fine-tuning strategy is the blueprint for geospatial foundation models: pretrain on diverse spatial tasks (accessibility, mobility, land use, health geography) with a shared backbone, then fine-tune per city with minimal labeled data. This aligns with my research on fine-tuning geospatial foundation models — UniAIR's zero-shot and few-shot evaluation protocol provides a concrete benchmark design I can adapt to measure how well a spatial foundation model transfers across cities and health access contexts.

③ Explainability of mutation landscapes as a model for explainable spatial prediction

Extending this paperUniAIR maps full mutation landscapes via zero-shot prediction (Fig. 3) — producing per-residue scores across the entire interface, not just at a handful of labeled mutations. This landscape view is essentially a spatial attribution map over the protein surface. The paper could be extended by coupling this landscape output with mechanistic interpretation: which geometric or evolutionary features (buried vs. exposed, conserved vs. variable) most strongly drive the predicted binding-change direction? An explainability layer (e.g., attention rollout or gradient-based attribution) applied to the interface-centric transformer would make UniAIR's predictions not just accurate but interpretable to structural immunologists, enabling hypothesis generation beyond the training distribution.

The paper's zero-shot landscape generation (no labeled mutations needed) is a compelling parallel to my work on SHAP-based spatial attribution in GWR/MGWR settings. The key transfer: can I design a spatial model that generates a zero-shot healthcare-access landscape — predicting accessibility scores at unvisited locations using only structural spatial features, analogous to UniAIR predicting mutation effects at unobserved residues?

④ Few-shot closed-loop optimization as a design for adaptive health facility siting

Extending this paperUniAIR's most striking demonstration is the multi-round peptide optimization of a TCR–pHLA complex under sparse experimental feedback (Fig. 5) — a Bayesian-style closed loop where each round of in-silico prediction selects which mutations to synthesize and test, feeding results back to refine the next prediction. The paper currently validates this for a single complex; a natural extension is to test this loop across a panel of diverse TCR–pHLA pairs spanning different cancer neoantigens, measuring how many rounds are needed to reach a given affinity threshold as a function of initial data availability. Coupling with structure prediction updates after each round (predicted structures from ESMFold) would also test robustness to structural uncertainty.

Added value to my researchThis closed-loop, sparse-feedback optimization paradigm directly maps onto adaptive facility siting for healthcare access: instead of solving the facility-location problem once from fixed data, a closed-loop approach selects candidate clinic locations, collects accessibility and utilization feedback, and iteratively refines the siting plan. This is the operational model for my EV-charging station efficiency–equity optimization and primary care accessibility work — UniAIR's sparse-feedback loop provides both a methodological template and a benchmark protocol for measuring convergence speed and equity of adaptive siting strategies.

⑤ Multimodal sequence–structure fusion as architecture for multi-source spatiotemporal prediction

Extending this paperUniAIR fuses two complementary data modalities — evolutionary sequence information (what residues are conserved across evolution, carrying functional constraints) and geometric structure (how residues are positioned in 3D space, determining physical contacts). The fusion is not naive concatenation but an interface-centric attention mechanism that learns when to weight sequence vs. structure for each residue position. The paper could be extended by investigating whether modality-specific dropout during pretraining (randomly masking either sequence or structure features) improves robustness to missing structural data, which is common in newly sequenced immune receptors without solved structures.

Added value to my researchUniAIR's sequence–structure fusion is a direct architectural blueprint for my multi-source spatiotemporal data fusion work: remote sensing (spatial structure, analogous to protein structure) fused with mobile phone trajectories or social media (temporal/behavioral sequences, analogous to evolutionary sequence). The paper's insight — that interface-centering the attention outperforms global fusion — translates to focusing spatiotemporal attention on the interaction zone between human behavior and built environment (e.g., the micro-geography around healthcare facilities) rather than on whole-city averages, potentially improving urban health prediction models substantially.
