Periodicity-aware deep learning for polymers

Wu, Yuhui · Wang, Cong · Shen, Xintian · Zhang, Tianyi · Zhang, Peng · Ji, Jian Published 2025-11-20 DOI 10.1038/s43588-025-00903-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

PerioGT, a periodicity-aware graph transformer, explicitly encodes the repeating-unit periodicity of polymers into a self-supervised pre-training framework via contrastive learning, achieving state-of-the-art performance on 16 downstream polymer property prediction tasks. Wet-lab validation confirmed real-world applicability: PerioGT screened 25 antimicrobial polymer candidates from a 30-candidate validation set (83% hit rate), including two leads with potent activity against methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) at an MIC of 8 µg ml−1. The results demonstrate that incorporating a chemical knowledge-driven periodicity prior substantially enhances the generalizability and robustness of polymer representations across diverse architectures.

★

Why It Matters

  • Solves a long-standing representation gap in polymer science. Unlike small molecules, polymers have no universally accepted deep learning framework owing to their structural complexity. PerioGT is the first unified SSL model to explicitly encode polymer periodicity, addressing the core bottleneck preventing cross-task transfer.
  • Unlocks low-data polymer discovery. Labeled polymer datasets are severely scarce. By pre-training on approximately 1 million unlabeled virtual polymers and fine-tuning efficiently, PerioGT makes property prediction feasible where experimental data collection is prohibitively expensive.
  • Demonstrates real-world drug-discovery impact with wet-lab confirmation. PerioGT's 83% hit rate in antimicrobial polymer screening (versus 57% and 20% for competing SSL models) reflects actual synthesis and MIC testing of 30 candidates — not just a benchmark number. Two lead polymers outperform vancomycin in MRSA colony-count assays by three to six orders of magnitude.
  • Introduces a modular PolymerGraph architecture that flexibly integrates additional conditions — molecular weight, crosslinking degree, copolymer composition — via virtual nodes, enabling a single model to generalize across homopolymers, and random, alternating, and block copolymers.

Core Methods

  • Periodicity Augmentation (PA): a chemical-knowledge-driven sampling module that slides a window of N times the backbone atom count along the polymer chain to generate structurally equivalent fragments (different MRU orders and lengths) as a periodicity prior. Provides positive pairs for contrastive pre-training.
  • PolymerGraph construction: converts the SMILES of a repeating unit into a molecular line graph via RDKit and DGL. Additional conditions (molecular weight, crosslinking degree, copolymer composition) are incorporated as virtual nodes connecting to all real nodes via special edges, enabling one unified graph to represent homo-, random, alternating, and block copolymers.
  • PA-based Contrastive Learning (PACL): pre-training uses InfoNCE loss to maximize agreement between PA-derived fragments from the same polymer (positives) and push apart fragments from different polymers (negatives). Combined with a masked node modeling (MNM) objective — 50% node masking on both physical and virtual nodes — for hierarchical self-supervised learning.
  • Graph Transformer encoder (LiGhT): a graph transformer with path encoding (PE) and distance encoding (DE) in attention to capture long-range dependencies and mitigate over-smoothing. Adopted over GraphGPS based on ablation results; base configuration of approximately 100 million parameters trained on 3× NVIDIA RTX4090 GPUs for 100,000 steps.
  • Periodicity Prompt-Guided Fine-Tuning (PGFT): at fine-tuning time, PA generates equivalent motifs whose pre-trained representations are aggregated via multi-head attention with a learnable [PROMPT] token and injected into initial node features via a learnable weight α, guiding the model to leverage periodicity knowledge from pre-training.
  • Ablation study: six ablated models tested across 10 tasks to isolate contributions of virtual nodes, masked node modeling, contrastive learning, PA, linear probing, and prompt tuning. PA-based contrastive learning is the most impactful component.
  • Wet-lab validation: Michael addition combinatorial synthesis, MRSA MIC broth microdilution assay, colony-forming unit counting, membrane potential monitoring (DiSC3(5) fluorescence probe), live-dead staining, and transmission electron microscopy for mechanistic characterization.

① Transfer PerioGT's periodicity-prior paradigm to spatiotemporal data with structural repetition

Extending this paperPerioGT's key innovation is encoding a domain-specific structural prior — polymer periodicity — into contrastive pre-training via PA. The paper is currently limited to linear polymers; the Discussion acknowledges branched, cross-linked, and topologically diverse polymer systems as future work requiring topology-aware virtual nodes. A concrete extension: introduce branching-degree and crosslinking-density virtual nodes into PolymerGraph and define PA augmentations that traverse branching points rather than just backbone windows. This would test whether the periodicity prior generalizes from linear to hierarchically branched macromolecular periodicity — an empirically testable architectural extension using the existing PerioGT codebase.

Added value to my researchThe principle that a domain-specific structural prior improves SSL generalization maps directly to geospatial pre-training. Urban street networks exhibit quasi-periodic topological structure (grid blocks, ring roads, hierarchical road types). Encoding such spatial priors as contrastive augmentations in a graph transformer pre-trained on OpenStreetMap — analogous to PA sliding over a polymer backbone — could yield a stronger initialization for downstream GeoAI tasks including spatial accessibility prediction and healthcare facility-location optimization, directly extending my physics-informed graph learning agenda.

② Couple PerioGT's rich latent space with active learning for label-efficient polymer discovery

Extending this paperPerioGT is fine-tuned on fixed 72:8:20 random splits inherited from prior benchmarks, and the antimicrobial screening selected the top 30 candidates from a static one-shot prediction. The paper does not address which labeled examples would most efficiently improve the model. A direct extension: couple PerioGT's representation space with an active learning acquisition function (uncertainty sampling or diversity-based batch selection) to iteratively guide which polymers to synthesize and label next. Given wet-lab synthesis is the bottleneck, an active loop would allow the 624-candidate combinatorial library to be navigated progressively rather than requiring the full 150-polymer labeled subset before prediction.

Added value to my researchThe same active-sampling logic applies to health geography, where labeled data such as fine-resolution healthcare utilization rates are expensive to collect. An active spatial sampling strategy — selecting which census tracts to survey next based on model uncertainty in an R2SFCA or MGWR framework — could dramatically reduce field data collection costs while improving model fidelity. PerioGT's demonstration that a pre-trained SSL model produces a sufficiently rich embedding space for active learning selection is a direct methodological template for GeoAI data acquisition.

③ Attribute PerioGT's predictions with graph-level explainability to extract structure-property rules

Extending this paperThe Discussion explicitly names interpretability as a current limitation: "PerioGT's internal decision process is not directly interpretable." The model achieves state-of-the-art prediction but provides chemists no actionable guidance on which substructures drive a high glass-transition temperature or antimicrobial activity. A well-defined extension: apply attention rollout or gradient-based attribution (e.g., GNNExplainer or integrated gradients on the LiGhT line graph) to identify which atoms, bonds, or virtual-node conditions most influence each property prediction, then validate attribution patterns against known polymer structure-property relationships (e.g., backbone rigidity → high Tg). This moves PerioGT from a screening tool to a design guide.

Adapting PerioGT's graph-level attribution to spatial prediction graphs — identifying which spatial features (POI density, road network centrality, demographic virtual nodes) drive health-outcome or accessibility predictions — is technically analogous. The explainability module I would build for polymer graph transformers directly generalizes to my explainable spatiotemporal kernel work, where understanding why a neighborhood has low healthcare accessibility is as policy-critical as predicting the outcome.

④ Adapt PolymerGraph's virtual-node hierarchy as a multi-scale conditioning mechanism for spatial graphs

Extending this paperPerioGT's PolymerGraph cleanly separates intrinsic polymer structure from extrinsic conditions via a three-level virtual-node hierarchy: physical atom nodes, local component virtual nodes per monomer, and a global sample virtual node encoding copolymer type and molecular weight. This modular design is explicitly noted as flexible and condition-agnostic. Extending it to more complex topologies — star polymers, hyperbranched networks — requires only adding topology-type virtual nodes that encode branching degree at the global level and branch-point environments at the local level, an architectural extension directly supported by PolymerGraph's modular construction rules.

Added value to my researchThe virtual-node hierarchy elegantly solves a problem I face in spatial graphs: encoding multi-scale contextual information (individual building, city block, neighborhood, city region) into a single graph model without losing spatial resolution. Adopting PerioGT's virtual-node pattern in my spatiotemporal GNN for urban accessibility — with local virtual nodes encoding census-block demographics and global virtual nodes encoding city-level transit supply — would provide a principled, modular multi-scale conditioning architecture replacing current ad hoc feature concatenation in my models.

⑤ Couple PerioGT's discriminative pre-training with a generative model for de novo polymer design

Extending this paperPerioGT is a discriminative model: given a polymer structure, it predicts properties. The antimicrobial application screens a predefined combinatorial library of 624 candidates rather than generating novel candidates beyond the library. A natural next step: use PerioGT's periodicity-aware encoder as a reward or discriminator signal within a generative model (flow-based, variational autoencoder, or diffusion model over SMILES/graph space) to directly propose novel polymer sequences with target property profiles. The PA augmentation already defines a structure-aware traversal of polymer space that could seed a generative traversal — and the PGFT prompt mechanism suggests that property-conditioning prompts could steer generation.

Added value to my researchThe discriminative-to-generative upgrade mirrors a direction in GeoAI: moving from spatial prediction (where is healthcare accessibility low?) to spatial design (where should new facilities be sited to optimally raise accessibility?). Coupling a trained accessibility predictor with a generative spatial optimizer — structurally analogous to coupling PerioGT with a polymer generator — is the formal basis of my facility-location optimization work. PerioGT's demonstration that domain priors can be injected as fine-tuning prompts also suggests that geographic constraints (zoning laws, road network topology, equity targets) could be encoded as prompt conditions steering a spatial design model.
