Towards quantum enhanced adversarial robustness in machine learning

Maxwell T. West · Shu-Lok Tsang · Jia S. Low · Charles D. Hill · Christopher Leckie · Lloyd C. L. Hollenberg · Sarah M. Erfani · Muhammad Usman Published 2023-05-25 DOI 10.1038/s42256-023-00661-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Quantum machine learning (QML) faces a dual reality: quantum classifiers are theoretically highly vulnerable to adversarial attacks due to the concentration of measure in Hilbert space, yet the unique properties of quantum information — including quantum noise and quantum hypothesis testing — offer novel defensive strategies unavailable classically. The nascent field of quantum adversarial machine learning (QAML) has seen rapid early progress, but achieving practical robustness will require advances in data encoding, circuit architecture, noise mitigation, and fault-tolerant hardware as quantum devices scale up beyond the NISQ era.

Why It Matters

  • Security stakes are rising for AI-critical systems. ML algorithms increasingly underpin autonomous systems, facial recognition, military surveillance, and self-driving cars — where adversarial vulnerabilities are not merely inconvenient but potentially catastrophic.
  • Quantum computing is converging with ML. As quantum hardware scales (IBM roadmap: >4,000 qubits by 2025, >10,000 beyond 2026), QML will be deployed in high-stakes tasks, making it imperative to characterize and address adversarial risks before they are trusted in production.
  • Quantum noise can be a defense, not just a liability. Depolarization noise induces quantum differential privacy, offering certifiable robustness bounds — a conceptual inversion that reframes a hardware limitation as a security asset.
  • Quantum-to-classical attack transferability is a new threat vector. Recent findings show QAML adversarial perturbations target robust features and successfully transfer to classical networks, suggesting quantum adversaries could compromise classical AI infrastructure.
  • QAML provides a structured research roadmap. The six-block framework (Data, Encoding, Architecture, Attack, Noise, Measurement) systematically maps which aspects of the QML pipeline require hardening, guiding both academia and industry toward practical robustness.

Core Methods

  • Quantum variational classifiers (QVCs): parameterized quantum circuits composed of data-encoding layers, trainable rotation gates, entangling controlled-Z gates, and a final measurement; analogous to classical neural networks with quantum weights.
  • Adversarial attack methods: classical attacks (PGD, FGSM, BIM) adapted to quantum input spaces; attacks operate by iteratively perturbing inputs to cross the classifier's decision boundary.
  • Concentration of measure (COMP) analysis: theoretical framework showing that Hilbert-space points cluster exponentially close to decision boundaries as qubit number grows, implying adversarial vulnerability scaling as O(2−nqubits).
  • Quantum differential privacy via depolarization noise: theoretical connection showing that depolarization noise induces differential privacy, providing a certifiable upper bound on the adversarial influence any perturbation can exert.
  • Quantum hypothesis testing (QHT) for certifiable robustness: bounds on adversarial robustness derived from the fundamental impossibility of perfectly distinguishing two non-orthogonal quantum states — a robustness guarantee without classical analog.
  • Adversarial training (quantum setting): adversarial examples generated at training time and included in the training set; shown to substantially restore accuracy of attacked QVCs.
  • Surface code fault-tolerant resource estimation: scaling analysis of physical qubit requirements, gate depth, and error rate thresholds needed to implement QVC50/100/200 on logical qubits using surface codes.

① Certifiable robustness for geospatial ML via quantum hypothesis testing principles

Extending this paperThe paper introduces quantum hypothesis testing (QHT) as a source of certifiable robustness: because two non-orthogonal quantum states cannot be perfectly distinguished, any classifier limited by quantum measurements inherits provable adversarial bounds. This result is currently derived only for quantum classifiers with simple binary outputs. A direct extension of the paper: generalize the QHT robustness certificates to multi-class quantum classifiers on structured spatial data (e.g., geospatial feature maps encoded via amplitude or phase encoding), and derive how the spatial correlation structure of inputs affects the tightness of the bound. This would bridge the paper's theoretical framework to real geographically distributed datasets.

Added value to my researchMy explainable ML pipelines for spatial prediction (MGWR, ensemble models for health geography) face the same certification gap: predictions can be manipulated by adversarially perturbed POI counts or accessibility scores. The QHT-inspired certification principle — that distinguishability constraints bound adversarial influence — can be adapted classically via Lipschitz-bounded spatial encoders that certify how much any local perturbation (e.g., a manipulated healthcare facility count) can shift the model output, producing certifiably robust spatial accessibility scores.

② Quantum differential privacy as a blueprint for federated GeoAI

Extending this paperThe paper's most operationally grounded defensive mechanism — depolarization noise inducing quantum differential privacy (QDP) — currently exists as a theoretical result with no implementation path for near-term hardware. Extending the paper: design a practical QDP protocol using tunable noise injection on NISQ devices (e.g., by deliberately inserting parameterized depolarizing channels), calibrate the privacy–accuracy trade-off curve empirically on MNIST and FMNIST, and compare against classical randomized smoothing baselines. This would transform a theoretical concept into an engineering-ready defense.

Added value to my researchDifferential privacy is a natural desideratum for multi-source geospatial fusion involving sensitive mobile phone trajectories and social media data. The QDP analogy provides a conceptual template for designing noise-calibrated federated GeoAI: instead of quantum depolarization, inject spatially correlated Laplace noise proportional to local population density, inheriting the certifiable robustness guarantee while maintaining spatial accuracy for health-geography predictions.

③ Exploit quantum-to-classical attack transferability to stress-test classical GeoAI

Extending this paperThe paper reports a striking finding: adversarial perturbations generated by attacking quantum classifiers target robust features and successfully transfer to classical networks, creating a new class of powerful classical attacks. The paper acknowledges this but does not yet systematically characterize which dataset properties amplify or suppress cross-architecture transferability. Extending it: conduct a controlled study varying dataset complexity (MNIST vs. structured graph/tabular data), encoding strategy (amplitude vs. phase), and classifier architecture depth, producing a transferability map that tells practitioners when quantum-generated attacks are a credible threat to their classical models.

Added value to my researchMy multi-source geospatial models (remote sensing + mobile phone + POI fusion) are classical ensemble models. The transferability finding suggests that if a quantum adversary attacks a QVC trained on the same feature space, the resulting perturbations may fool my classical accessibility or health-prediction models. This motivates stress-testing my ensemble spatial models with robust-feature-targeted perturbations (classically simulated via adversarially trained surrogate networks), closing a currently unexamined attack surface in spatiotemporal prediction pipelines.

④ Replace fixed data encoding with a learned, robustness-aware encoding for spatial features

Extending this paperThe paper identifies data encoding as "one of the biggest challenges towards the practical implementation of QAML" — the choice of amplitude vs. phase vs. interleaved encoding directly shapes both adversarial robustness and the class of decision boundaries learnable. The paper discusses trade-offs qualitatively but does not propose a principled method for choosing or optimizing encodings. Extending it: frame encoding selection as a bi-level optimization — an outer loop maximizing certifiable robustness (e.g., the QHT bound of Weber et al.) and an inner loop maximizing classification accuracy — jointly learning the encoding map and the variational circuit weights.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly onto my work on geospatial foundation model fine-tuning: the spatial patch embedding (analogous to the quantum encoding circuit) determines which geographic features the model can disentangle. Learning the embedding jointly under a robustness objective would yield encoding-hardened geospatial foundation models that resist adversarial location spoofing (e.g., manipulated GPS trajectories or synthetic POI injections), a critical need for trustworthy GeoAI in public health applications.

⑤ Leverage quantum error mitigation techniques to improve noisy spatiotemporal ML

Extending this paperThe paper argues that quantum error mitigation (QEM) — short of full fault tolerance — may enable practical QAML in the NISQ era by suppressing the effects of hardware noise on classifier outputs. The paper's resource estimation (Fig. 4) focuses on fault tolerance but acknowledges QEM as a near-term on-ramp. Extending it: systematically benchmark zero-noise extrapolation, probabilistic error cancellation, and symmetry verification as QEM techniques applied specifically to QVCs under adversarial attack conditions — asking whether QEM simultaneously mitigates hardware noise and adversarial perturbations or whether the two interact adversarially.

Added value to my researchThe conceptual parallel to spatiotemporal ML is direct: my models are trained on noisy, heterogeneous multi-source data (mobile phone dropout, GPS signal loss, social media selection bias). Error mitigation techniques from QAML — particularly zero-noise extrapolation adapted to missing spatial data — offer a structured framework for uncertainty-aware spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and SIR epidemic modeling, where measurement noise and adversarial data corruption (e.g., bot-inflated social media signals) must be jointly handled.
