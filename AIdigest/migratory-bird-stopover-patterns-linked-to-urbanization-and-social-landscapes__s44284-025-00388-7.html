Migratory bird stopover patterns linked to urbanization and social landscapes

Miguel F. Jimenez · Hanna M. McCaslin · Maria C. T. D. Belotti · Kyle G. Horton Published 2026-02-20 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00388-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using weather-surveillance-radar estimates of migratory bird stopover across the continental USA, the authors show that cities punch far above their weight as migration habitat: nearly half of stopover hotspots fall inside Metropolitan Statistical Areas (spring 48%, fall 44%) despite their small land area. The urbanization–stopover relationship is regionally contingent — negative in eastern flyways, positive in western ones — and stopover is positively correlated with household income on average, yet this link is weak or reversed in many cities. The work establishes radar aeroecology as a tool for macroscale urban biodiversity science linked to social landscapes.

★

Why It Matters

  • Breaks urban ecology out of the single-city trap. Most urban-ecology findings come from one city at a time; this study analyzes 2,130 parks across 88 urban areas over the whole continental USA, enabling the intercity comparison the field has lacked.
  • Reframes cities as continental-scale habitat. Showing that ~half of migration hotspots sit inside MSAs makes urban green space a hemispheric conservation priority, not just an amenity.
  • Reveals that "more city = less birds" is too simple. The urbanization–stopover sign flips between eastern and western flyways, so conservation prescriptions cannot be copy-pasted across regions.
  • Connects biodiversity to environmental justice. Linking stopover density to neighborhood income and the share of low-income residents within walking distance of parks surfaces a potential inequitable distribution of access to nature.
  • Demonstrates a reusable sensing pipeline. Repurposing the existing NEXRAD weather-radar network for biodiversity monitoring is a low-cost, continuously updating instrument for tracking urban ecological change.

Core Methods

  • Radar aeroecology: convert WSR-88D reflectivity into spatially explicit migratory stopover density estimates, identifying high-density stopover hotspots for spring and fall.
  • Hotspot–urban overlap analysis: quantify the share of migration hotspots that fall within MSAs and alternative urban-area definitions (Fig. 2), benchmarked against urban land area.
  • Generalized linear models (Bayesian): regress stopover density on urbanization across the CONUS and summarize posterior regression coefficients (Fig. 3), allowing the urbanization–stopover relationship to vary regionally / by flyway.
  • Hierarchical model: estimate the relationship between mean stopover density in urban parks and the proportion of nearby low-income residents (Fig. 4), partially pooling across cities to capture heterogeneous, sometimes opposite, city-level slopes.
  • Cross-scale design: combine remote sensing, administrative boundaries, and socioeconomic data to link biodiversity patterns with urbanization and social landscapes at macroscale.

① Turn "income correlation" into an access-equity accessibility model

Extending this paperFig. 4 correlates stopover density with the share of low-income residents within walking distance, but the walking-distance buffer is a crude proximity proxy. Extend the paper by recasting nature contact as a formal two-step floating catchment (2SFCA/R2SFCA) problem: treat high-stopover urban parks as the "supply" of migratory-biodiversity experience and demographic blocks as "demand," yielding a distance-decay-weighted biodiversity-access score per neighborhood. That converts a single correlation into a spatially explicit map of who can actually reach high-quality stopover habitat.

② Explain the flyway sign-flip with spatially varying, interpretable models

Extending this paperThe headline result — urbanization correlates negatively with stopover in eastern flyways but positively in western ones — is reported via region-pooled GLM posteriors (Fig. 3), which fix the regional split a priori. Extend it with MGWR/GWR or spatially explicit explainable ML (e.g., SHAP over a geographic GBM) so the coefficient surface is estimated continuously and the drivers of the flip (aridity, riparian-corridor concentration, park configuration) are attributed rather than assumed.

③ Disentangle income from historical redlining as competing causes

Extending this paperThe paper finds stopover positively correlated with household income on average but weak/reversed in many cities — a pattern that may be confounded by historical redlining and legacy tree canopy rather than contemporary income. Extend it by adding HOLC redlining grades and canopy-history covariates to the hierarchical model, then test whether legacy or contemporary inequality better explains the residual city-level slopes.
