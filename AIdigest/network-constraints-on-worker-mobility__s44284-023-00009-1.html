Network constraints on worker mobility

Morgan R. Frank · Esteban Moro · Tobin South · Alex Rutherford · Alex Pentland · Bledi Taska · Iyad Rahwan Published 2024-01-11 DOI 10.1038/s44284-023-00009-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Career pathways can be modeled as a city-specific occupation network whose edges encode the skill similarity between jobs. Skill similarity predicts occupation-to-occupation transition rates, and predictions sharpen as the skill data becomes more granular. A worker's network embeddedness (a measure of skill specialization) governs their dynamics: job changes that decrease embeddedness tend to raise wages, and workers drift toward lower embeddedness over their careers. At the city scale, pairs of cities with dissimilar embeddedness show greater census migration and more enplaned-passenger flow — directly linking workplace skills to both career and spatial mobility.

Why It Matters

  • Unifies career mobility and spatial mobility under one mechanism. Skill similarity between occupations explains both how workers move between jobs and how people and flights move between cities — a rare bridge from micro skill structure to macro urbanization.
  • Gives "skill specialization" an operational, predictive metric. Network embeddedness turns a fuzzy idea into a position in a graph that forecasts future wage and career dynamics.
  • Validates a model across four independent labor datasets. A nationally representative survey plus three large resume datasets show the result is not an artifact of one data vendor — and that finer skill data improves prediction.
  • Speaks directly to policy on reskilling and labor-market access. If de-specialization raises wages, then training and city-design choices that broaden a worker's reachable neighborhood in the occupation network have measurable economic value.

Core Methods

  • Occupation network construction: for each US city, build a network where nodes are occupations and edge weights are the skill similarity of their requirements (from O*NET, at several granularities).
  • Transition prediction: test whether skill similarity predicts observed occupation-to-occupation transition rates, and show prediction quality rises with finer skill data across the survey and three resume datasets.
  • Embeddedness metric: define an employment-weighted embeddedness ec,i capturing a worker's skill specialization via their position in the city's occupation network.
  • Career dynamics analysis: relate changes in embeddedness to wage changes and track embeddedness trajectories over careers (Fig. 2).
  • Spatial mobility linkage: regress census migration and enplaned-passenger flows on city-pair embeddedness dissimilarity, and test how average embeddedness moderates the city-size–mobility relationship (Fig. 3).

① Make the occupation network spatial: couple embeddedness with the geography of access

Extending this paperThe paper builds a separate occupation network per city but treats each city as an aggregate point when relating embeddedness to migration. A direct extension: embed the occupation network in physical space — overlay it with commuting/job-housing geography so that a worker's embeddedness is conditioned not only on skills but on which jobs are reachable from their home. This turns "skill specialization" into a joint skill-and-access measure and tests whether spatially constrained access, not skills alone, drives the embeddedness–wage gradient.

Extending it: fit a deep / closed-form gravity model where the deterrence and attraction terms are functions of embeddedness dissimilarity alongside distance and size — yielding a generative, predictive map of skill-driven intercity flows rather than an association.

Extending this paperThe paper stops at wages and mobility; it never asks who sits in highly embedded versus de-specialized positions.
