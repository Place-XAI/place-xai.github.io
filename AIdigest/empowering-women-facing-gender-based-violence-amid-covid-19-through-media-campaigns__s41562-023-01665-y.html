Empowering women facing gender-based violence amid COVID-19 through media campaigns

Fotini Christia · Horacio Larreguy · Elizabeth Parker-Magyar · Manuel Quintero Published 2023-08-07 DOI 10.1038/s41562-023-01665-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A pre-registered randomized experiment in Egypt (n = 5,618 female Facebook users) finds that social media (WhatsApp) and traditional TV can both effectively deliver an edutainment intervention on gender-based violence during COVID-19, with WhatsApp outperforming Facebook in content delivery. The campaign increased women's knowledge of support resources and their hypothetical and reported use of those resources, but produced no credible evidence of attitude change regarding gender norms or the justifiability of violence — underscoring the stickiness of entrenched norms even under repeated media exposure.

★

Why It Matters

  • First randomized evidence on social media vs. TV for GBV edutainment. Prior experiments used in-person community screenings or TV alone; this is among the first to directly compare WhatsApp groups, WhatsApp individual, Facebook, and satellite TV within the same pre-registered RCT.
  • Separates knowledge from attitude change. The campaign moved women's factual knowledge of support organizations and their willingness to contact those organizations, while leaving gender-norm attitudes largely unchanged — a nuanced behavioral finding with direct implications for how NGOs invest in media-based programming.
  • COVID-19 crisis context with broader generalizability. Fielded during Egypt's 2020 mobility restrictions (national mobility ~20% below pre-pandemic baseline per Google Mobility data), the study demonstrates that digital outreach can substitute for in-person programming in public health crises without sacrificing reach.
  • Platform comparison via natural experiment. A difference-in-differences design exploiting a mid-study technical switch from Facebook to WhatsApp delivery establishes that WhatsApp generates significantly more content views than Facebook (0.126 more visits per assigned user, p = 0.022).
  • Open data and code for replication. All de-identified survey data, server visit logs, YouTube analytics, and R analysis code are deposited in Harvard Dataverse, enabling extension to other LMIC contexts.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Survey sample
5,618 Egyptian women recruited via 76 Facebook advertisements covering all governorates and age brackets in Egypt. Baseline survey via Qualtrics (9,431 valid responses); endline survey (75% response rate = 4,165 participants), conducted 10 Sep – 11 Oct 2020.
Intervention period
Eight weeks: 18 July – 10 September 2020. Five arms: WhatsApp Individual, WhatsApp Group, Facebook Individual (switched to WhatsApp after 4 videos), TV reminder, control.
Treatment content
Two content sets from ECWR / Aboul Qomsan: (1) 10 TV show episodes (~25–30 min each) on satellite channel Al Kahera Wa Al Nas; (2) 13 social media videos (5–9 min each) hosted on YouTube and delivered via WhatsApp/Facebook links.
Platform analytics
Server-visit logs and YouTube channel analytics tracking unique IPs, visits, and average view time by treatment arm. Approximately 45% of social media arm participants visited the hosting site; mean visitor watched 2–3 videos (Extended Data Tables 4–5).
Mobility context
Google Mobility Reports used to characterize Egypt's pandemic environment — national mobility remained ~20% below pre-pandemic baseline throughout the study; data cover 6 venue categories (retail, grocery, parks, transit, workplaces, residential).
Benchmark survey
Arab Barometer (2016 and 2018 waves, nationally representative) used to assess representativeness of the experimental sample relative to Egyptian female internet users.
Preregistration
EGAP/OSF: osf.io/tekyr (14 April 2021).

Core Methods

  • Randomized controlled trial (RCT) with block randomization: 5,618 women assigned to five arms using governorate-by-age-bracket blocks to ensure covariate balance; verified via balance tables (Supplementary Tables 1–9).
  • Intent-to-treat (ITT) specification — weighted generalized least squares (WGLS): inverse-probability-of-treatment weights; outcome indices z-scored within families; one-sided tests for pre-hypothesized directional effects, two-sided otherwise.
  • LASSO covariate selection: preferred specification (panel a) uses LASSO to select baseline covariates from the outcome family, reducing overfitting and improving estimate stability.
  • Difference-in-differences (DiD) for platform comparison: exploits the mid-experiment technical switch of the Facebook arm to WhatsApp delivery to estimate the causal effect of platform on video views using server-visit data.
  • Standardized index construction: each outcome domain (gender/marital equality, sexual violence attitudes, resource use, future outlook) aggregated into z-score indices; Bayes factors (BF) used to evaluate evidence for null results against the alternative.
  • Heterogeneous effects analysis: treatment effects examined by baseline demographics (age, education, marital status) and attitudes; no credible heterogeneity found, supporting external validity to the broader population of Egyptian female internet users.
  • Demand-effects / social desirability audit: placebo outcomes (pre-COVID resource use, reported violence before intervention) confirmed to be null, ruling out response bias as a driver of positive findings.

① Map the spatial digital divide that determines who the campaign can reach

Extending this paperThe paper's sample is drawn from Egyptian governorates via Facebook advertising, but geographic participation rates vary dramatically (Cairo accounts for 2,341 of ~5,618 respondents; Matrouh had only 1). The paper never spatially models why coverage is uneven or who is structurally excluded by internet access. A direct extension: combine the governorate-level recruitment data with subnational internet penetration and mobile-phone subscription records to build a spatial reachability model for social media GBV campaigns — identifying which women are systematically unreachable via digital channels versus TV, and quantifying what a spatially targeted supplement strategy would cost.

Mobile phone subscription data (a core input in my work) provides the internet-penetration surface at fine granularity.

② Use mobility-restriction severity as a continuous treatment moderator

Extending this paperThe paper relies on Google Mobility data to characterize Egypt's pandemic context but treats the intervention as a single block (~20% below baseline mobility). A richer extension: leverage the governorate-by-day granularity of Google Mobility to construct a time-varying restriction index and interact it with treatment assignment in a difference-in-differences specification. This would test whether the campaign was more effective during stricter lockdowns (when women were more homebound and screen time was higher) versus partial reopening — directly addressing the paper's open question about whether COVID-19 context inflates the results or whether findings generalize to non-pandemic settings.

③ Apply a misinformation-inoculation front-end to break through norm stickiness

Extending this paperThe paper finds that eight weeks of edutainment content shifted knowledge and resource use, but left gender-norm attitudes intact — consistent with the stickiness of patriarchal norms reinforced by religion, culture, and economic structures. A theoretically motivated extension: borrow the prebunking / inoculation design from misinformation research — deliver brief, targeted counter-arguments against the most prevalent pro-violence myths before the main edutainment content — and test whether this cognitive front-loading reduces resistance to attitude change. The ECWR infrastructure and WhatsApp delivery channel are already in place; an inoculation arm requires only adding 2–3 short pre-videos to the existing design.

Added value to my researchMulti-country inoculation experiments generate large behavioral datasets (e.g., 34,286 persons across 16 countries) linkable to spatial covariates — internet penetration, urbanization, socioeconomic status, prior norm-survey scores. My explainable-ML approach (SHAP on spatial features) can map where prebunking is most tractable, producing a GeoAI-based spatial prioritization of intervention intensity that is directly analogous to my work on spatially heterogeneous health-service access.

④ Quantify how lockdown stringency shifted demand for digital GBV support resources

Extending this paperThe paper documents that mobility restrictions increased GBV exposure and drove women to search for online resources, but it does not causally quantify how much restriction severity shifted baseline demand for digital GBV support. A cross-country extension: link OxCGRT lockdown-stringency indices to country-level search volume for GBV hotlines, NGO websites, and domestic-violence resources (via Google Trends) in an event-study design across dozens of countries. This anchors the paper's Egypt-specific finding in a generalizable causal framework and identifies which restriction types (curfew, school closure, movement restriction) drive the largest shifts in digital help-seeking.

⑤ Train an explainable spatial model for NGO campaign targeting across governorates

Extending this paperThe paper randomizes delivery mode but not geographic targeting of the campaign. Yet the finding that group WhatsApp failed to shift future outlook while individual messages succeeded implies that the most cost-effective campaign would tailor platform choice and content intensity to local context. A direct extension: train a SHAP-interpretable ML model on the paper's governorate-level treatment-effect estimates combined with subnational covariates (internet penetration, urbanization, women's labor-force participation, prior GBV prevalence from the Egypt DHS) to predict where digital versus TV campaigns will have the highest marginal return on investment — producing a spatial resource-allocation map for NGOs scaling beyond Egypt.
