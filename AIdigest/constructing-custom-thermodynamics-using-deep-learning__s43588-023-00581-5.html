Constructing custom thermodynamics using deep learning

Xiaoli Chen · Beatrice W. Soh · Zi-En Ooi · Eleonore Vissol-Gaudin · Haijun Yu · Kostya S. Novoselov · Kedar Hippalgaonkar · Qianxiao Li Published 2023-12-29 DOI 10.1038/s43588-023-00581-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

S-OnsagerNet — a deep learning framework grounded in a generalized stochastic Onsager principle — automatically discovers interpretable, low-dimensional thermodynamic coordinates and governing equations directly from high-dimensional microscopic trajectory data. Applied to polymer chain stretching (300-bead, 900-degree-of-freedom systems), it recovers three macroscopic coordinates with physically meaningful interpretations, reveals a free-energy landscape with stable states and saddle points, and enables data-driven control of stretching rate — outperforming human labeling and validating against single-molecule DNA experiments.

Why It Matters

  • Automates thermodynamic description for arbitrary complex systems. Previously, constructing macroscopic equations of state for non-equilibrium systems required significant expert intuition; this framework does it from trajectory data alone, opening pathways in polymer science, biophysics, and soft matter.
  • Simultaneously discovers coordinates and dynamics. Unlike previous dimensionality-reduction methods, S-OnsagerNet solves the harder closure problem — finding the minimal set of coordinates and their closed governing equations at the same time.
  • Handles stochasticity explicitly. The framework captures thermal fluctuations central to non-equilibrium processes, explaining heterogeneous behavior (why identical polymer chains unfold at vastly different rates) that deterministic models cannot.
  • Delivers actionable control. By learning the free-energy landscape and its saddle points, the method yields a physics-based control protocol that modifies polymer stretching rates using simple flow switching — reducing mean unfolding time by ~14% or increasing it by ~15% without any reinforcement learning exploration.
  • Validated experimentally. Predictions from simulation-trained models transfer to single-molecule DNA stretching experiments in a microfluidic cross-slot device, distinguishing fast versus slow trajectories beyond human-labeling capability.

Core Methods

  • Generalized stochastic Onsager principle: the evolution law is parameterized as dZ = −[M(Z) + W(Z)]∇V(Z)dt + σ(Z)dB(t), where M is symmetric positive-semidefinite (dissipation), W is antisymmetric (conservative forces), V is a learned generalized potential, and σ captures thermal noise. The structure encodes Onsager's reciprocal relations and thermodynamic stability by architectural design.
  • PCA-ResNet encoder: a residual network with approximate PCA orthogonality regularization maps high-dimensional microscopic coordinates X to low-dimensional closure variables Z-hat. Dimensionality of Z-hat is selected empirically (3 chosen for polymer system).
  • End-to-end training: encoder and dynamics network are jointly optimized on microscopic trajectory data by minimizing trajectory prediction error while maintaining physics constraints throughout.
  • Automatic differentiation for interpretability: directional perturbations computed via automatic differentiation of the encoder reveal physical meaning of coordinates: Z2 correlates with end-to-end distance; Z3 with degree of chain foldedness.
  • Free-energy landscape analysis: critical points (stable states and saddle points) of V(Z) found via BFGS optimization; Taylor expansion around critical points yields local equations of state describing fluctuation correlations.
  • Data-driven control: saddle-point geometry from the learned landscape guides a flow-switching protocol that aligns or misaligns polymer reduced coordinates with the unstable manifold of the saddle, speeding or slowing unfolding without reinforcement learning.
  • Theoretical guarantees: Theorem 2 proves long-time stability (energy dissipation up to thermal fluctuation order); the model encompasses Langevin and generalized Poisson dynamics as special cases.

① Learn transition pathways in the energy landscape without pre-specified collective variables

Extending this paperS-OnsagerNet identifies stable and saddle points of V(Z) via BFGS optimization but does not discover minimum-energy paths connecting them. A direct extension is to integrate the learned free-energy landscape with committor-based path learning — automatically discovering transition pathways between folded and stretched polymer states without requiring human-specified reaction coordinates. This would also provide a more principled criterion for selecting the closure variable dimension (choose the dimensionality that yields committor-consistent paths) rather than the current trial-and-error procedure.

Added value to my researchCommittor-consistent path discovery maps onto spatiotemporal tipping-point detection in my GeoAI work. Just as polymer unfolding has saddle points on a learned energy surface, urban systems (epidemic onset, gentrification, service-demand surge) have spatial tipping points. Learning committor functions on spatial state spaces derived from mobile-phone or POI data would let me identify which urban configurations are on the verge of a phase transition — a novel framing for early-warning indicators in public health geography.

② Replace the PCA-ResNet encoder with a graph neural network for structured collective variable discovery

Extending this paperThe paper's PCA-ResNet encoder maps bead-rod polymer coordinates to macroscopic variables through a residual network with approximate orthogonality, effective but agnostic to the polymer's bond topology. Replacing the encoder with a geometric GNN that operates on the molecular graph (beads as nodes, bonds as edges, SE(3)-equivariant features) would leverage structural symmetry, discover more physically grounded closure coordinates without the PCA approximation, and generalize to molecular topologies the original network was not trained on — branched polymers, ring polymers, or protein backbones.

Added value to my researchIn urban spatial analysis, cities are graphs (census units as nodes, adjacency/mobility as edges). Borrowing the GNN encoder idea from this extension, I can learn graph-structured latent coordinates of urban systems: a GNN encoder compresses a spatiotemporal mobility or POI graph into a small set of interpretable macroscopic variables (regional hub centrality, accessibility gradient, connectivity assortativity), and S-OnsagerNet-style dynamics on those coordinates model how cities evolve. This is a principled bridge from molecular dynamics to physics-informed graph learning for urban analytics.

③ Extract conservation laws from the learned antisymmetric component of S-OnsagerNet dynamics

Extending this paperThe antisymmetric matrix W(Z) in the learned dynamics encodes conservative (non-dissipative) forces. Since conservative dynamics preserve certain quantities, the learned W could be analyzed — via manifold learning and optimal transport on trajectory data — to identify conserved quantities embedded in the complex polymer dynamics that are not known a priori. This extends S-OnsagerNet's interpretability beyond stable states and saddle points: the user would also know what physical invariants constrain the motion, providing a second layer of scientific insight alongside the free-energy landscape.

Added value to my researchIdentifying conserved or near-invariant quantities in spatial systems is an underexplored dimension of GeoAI. Applied to urban mobility data, a conservation-law discovery step could reveal invariants such as total daily travel demand being approximately conserved under network perturbations, or spatial entropy remaining stable across temporal slices. These theoretically grounded invariants would provide principled features for my explainable ML models of urban health outcomes, replacing ad-hoc spatial indicators with physics-discovered quantities.

④ Drive the closure-problem framework with real mobility data to discover macroscopic epidemic coordinates

Extending this paperThe paper applies S-OnsagerNet to a synthetic spatial SIR model using idealized diffusion on a spatial grid, discovering a single closure coordinate (spatial overlap of infective and susceptible individuals). A more powerful extension would feed real mobile-phone mobility trajectories as microscopic inputs — replacing the idealized diffusion kernel with observed human movement patterns — and apply S-OnsagerNet to discover macroscopic epidemic coordinates that naturally encode superspreader locations, commuter corridors, and socioeconomic heterogeneity in transmission. This would also test whether the learned closure variable generalizes across epidemic waves or geographic regions.

⑤ Use the automatic differentiation interpretability probe as a standard for evaluating geospatial encoder quality

Extending this paperThe paper's most underrated contribution is its interpretability protocol: compute ∂Zi/∂X via automatic differentiation, perturb the input in those directions, and identify what physical structure each latent coordinate encodes. The paper uses this ad hoc to interpret Z2 and Z3 for polymer chains. A systematic extension would turn this into a standard evaluation metric for latent representations: given any encoder of a physical system, the gradient-perturbation test checks whether each latent dimension has a clean physical interpretation, and ranks encoders accordingly — providing a principled alternative to dimension selection by predictive accuracy alone.

Added value to my researchWhen fine-tuning geospatial foundation models, choosing which latent layer or representation to use for downstream spatial prediction is currently done by validation loss. Adopting the gradient-perturbation probe, I can evaluate whether each latent dimension of a geospatial encoder corresponds to interpretable spatial features (terrain, density gradient, network centrality). This upgrades spatial deep learning interpretability from post-hoc attribution (SHAP/LIME applied after training) to intrinsic gradient probing during architecture design — a methodological contribution to explainable GeoAI.
