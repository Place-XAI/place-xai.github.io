Global wood harvest is sufficient for climate-friendly transitions to timber cities

Alperen Yayla · Adam R. Mason · Junyang Wang · Stijn van Ewijk · Rupert J. Myers Published 2025-07-30 DOI 10.1038/s41893-025-01605-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Transitioning 90% of the new global urban population to engineered timber buildings does not require harvesting more wood: redirecting 40% of current wood fuel to industrial use ("shift" pathway) or maximizing circular use ("circular" pathway) can meet timber city demand within the current harvest volume. These pathways reduce cumulative CO2-eq. emissions by 40.8 Gt by 2100 versus business as usual — roughly 22% of the remaining 1.5 °C carbon budget — while the "harvest" pathway (expanding plantation forests 128%) is far more damaging environmentally. The most critical policy actions are reducing virgin wood combustion for bioenergy and scaling wood-waste recycling globally, not expanding plantations.

★

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a key decarbonization dilemma. "Timber cities" are widely promoted for climate mitigation, but the supply-side question — where does all the wood come from without destroying forests? — was unanswered. This study proves current harvest is enough if used smarter.
  • Quantifies the full global wood cycle for the first time. Using Bayesian material flow analysis across 12 MAgPIE world regions, it maps every flow from harvesting through processing, use, and waste treatment for 2021 — the most comprehensive global wood-cycle dataset produced to date.
  • Exposes the bioenergy bottleneck. Bioenergy consumes 67% of all wood end use (1,876 Modmt; 35.6 EJ/yr, 6% of global energy). This single sector is the largest barrier to the wood transition and the largest source of biogenic CO2-eq., dwarfing construction savings by ~10x.
  • Reveals dangerous regional disparities. Developing regions (India, Sub-Saharan Africa, Middle East Asia) have both the highest future timber demand and the lowest circular/shift potential, pointing squarely to the need for inter-regional wood trade and renewable-energy investment.
  • Delivers open data infrastructure. Full input/output datasets, BaMFA code, and interactive Sankey diagrams are publicly available (Zenodo + GitHub), enabling future regional and national wood-transition studies.

Core Methods

  • Wood-based product classification: FAO-aligned taxonomy extended to separate "engineered structural timber" (glulam, CLT) from "builder's joinery," and to include illegal-logging stocks and six end-of-life treatment categories (recycling, energy recovery, non-energy recovery, incineration, landfill, open dump).
  • Bayesian material flow analysis (BaMFA): probabilistic mass-balance model using PyMC3 / No-U-Turn-Sampler; reconciles multiple uncertain data sources by combining prior distributions with observed data, propagating uncertainty into 95% credible intervals on all flows and stocks.
  • Four scenario pathways: (1) Business as usual (BAU); (2) Shift — redirect 40% of wood fuel harvesting to industrial roundwood; (3) Harvest — increase industrial roundwood harvesting 40%; (4) Circular — raise wood-waste recycling rate from 30% to 57%. Three hybrid pathways (Shift+Harvest, Shift+Circular, Circular+Harvest) are also evaluated.
  • Demand quantification: engineered structural timber demand derived from SSP2 urban population growth (2021–2100) at 30 m2/capita floor area (range: 9.20–79.10 m2), scaled linearly as an annual average.
  • GHG & energy accounting: ecoinvent v3.10 life-cycle emission factors scaled linearly from process to global level; biogenic CO2 from combustion calculated per IPCC guidelines; forest regrowth sequestration modeled as a Gaussian distribution by species rotation period.
  • Regional case study: EUR-to-SSA inter-regional timber export case study to quantify trade-off between transport emissions and deforestation avoidance.
  • Visualization: Sankey diagrams for all 12 regions + global; publicly available interactively at alperenyayla.github.io/globalwoodcycle.

① Spatially explicit urban-demand mapping to close the regional gap

Extending this paperThe paper quantifies engineered timber demand at the regional (MAgPIE) scale using SSP2 urban population growth, but within-region spatial heterogeneity is invisible: Sub-Saharan Africa's 87 Modmt demand is aggregated even though demand peaks in a handful of rapidly urbanizing megacities. Extending it: disaggregate annual urban population growth to city or district level using gridded population datasets (e.g., WorldPop, GHS-POP), overlay with current plantation forest and secondary forest coverage, and compute city-to-forest supply-chain distances. This would produce a spatially explicit timber-demand map that identifies exactly which cities face the highest import dependence and deforestation risk — directly actionable for the inter-regional transport analysis the paper calls for.

Combining WorldPop gridded population with GHS urban extents to derive annual urbanization hotspots, then pairing with FAOSTAT plantation-area data, would create a novel wood-transition accessibility surface — analogous to my R2SFCA service-area delineation but applied to timber supply chains. The resulting supply-demand gap maps could directly feed urban construction policy planning.

② Explainable ML to identify which regional variables drive recycling-rate heterogeneity

Extending this paperThe paper shows that the "circular" pathway fails in SSA and MEA because recycling rates are too low (current global rate: 30%; circular target: 57%), but the paper does not explain why recycling rates differ across regions — economic development, infrastructure, cultural preferences, and institutional capacity all co-vary. Extending it: assemble a cross-national panel linking BaMFA-derived regional recycling rates to socioeconomic and governance covariates, and apply gradient-boosted trees with SHAP explanations to identify the top drivers. This would turn the paper's regional constraint observation into a ranked policy lever list, telling policymakers where targeted investment buys the most circular-rate improvement.

③ Coupling the wood-transition model to urban building-energy demand for integrated carbon budgeting

Extending this paperThe paper focuses on the supply side of the wood transition (harvesting, processing, waste), but assumes a fixed energy-demand profile for bioenergy replacement. In reality, the buildings that house the new urban population also consume energy throughout their operational life. Extending it: couple the paper's BaMFA scenario outputs with a city-level building-energy model that distinguishes timber from concrete/steel structures across climate zones and SSP2 urbanization trajectories. This would produce an integrated embodied-plus-operational carbon budget for timber cities, capturing the full life-cycle benefit (or cost) of the transition in each region.

④ Monte Carlo stress-testing of the Shift pathway against renewable energy transition uncertainty

Extending this paperThe paper's "shift" pathway assumes that the 4.5 EJ of bioenergy displaced by redirecting wood fuel to construction can be replaced according to current global primary energy fuel percentages (30.9% oil, 26.8% coal, 23.2% natural gas, 19.1% other). This is a deterministic, static substitution that ignores the enormous uncertainty in how fast renewable energy will penetrate different regions by 2100. Extending it: replace the fixed substitution factor with a Monte Carlo ensemble drawing from IEA, IRENA, and SSP scenario ranges for renewable energy deployment rates per region, propagating the resulting emission uncertainty through the BaMFA posterior. This would produce credible-interval estimates of the shift pathway's net GHG benefit conditional on the renewable energy transition pace — making the policy recommendation robust to decarbonization uncertainty.

Added value to my researchMonte Carlo simulation is already in my methodological toolkit (applied to SIR epidemic models and spatial accessibility uncertainty). Embedding it into BaMFA's probabilistic framework and coupling it with scenario databases would be a methodological contribution in itself, extending physics-informed uncertainty propagation from epidemiological to environmental material-flow systems — exactly the cross-domain transfer my research program is exploring.

⑤ Network community detection to design optimal inter-regional wood trade corridors

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates in a EUR-to-SSA case study that inter-regional timber export is climate-positive (transport emissions are outweighed by avoided deforestation), and calls for future studies on wood trade-flow analysis. Extending it: construct a global bipartite network of (timber-surplus, timber-deficit) regions weighted by supply gap, transport distance, emission cost, and deforestation risk, then apply community detection (e.g., Louvain, modularity maximization) to identify optimal multi-hop trade corridors and self-sufficient regional clusters. This would turn the paper's single case study into a systematic global timber trade architecture recommendation.
