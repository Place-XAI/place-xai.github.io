A multi-agent framework combining large language models with medical flowcharts for self-triage

Yujia Liu · Sophia Yu · Hongyue Jin · Jessica Wen · Alexander Qian · Terrence Lee · Mattheus Ramsis · Gi Won Choi · Lianhui Qin · Xin Liu · Edward Jay Wang Published 2026-04-20 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00112-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Free-text LLM chat is flexible but unreliable for health advice; rigid clinical flowcharts are auditable but inflexible. This paper fuses the two: a three-agent system (retrieval → decision → conversation) lets an LLM converse naturally while being constrained to walk 100 American Medical Association self-triage flowcharts. On large synthetic evaluations it reaches 84.1% flowchart-retrieval accuracy (N = 2,000) and 99.1% flowchart-navigation accuracy (N = 37,200), demonstrating that a structured, transparent and auditable protocol layer can make conversational AI self-triage both accurate and trustworthy.

Why It Matters

  • Tackles the core failure mode of medical LLMs head-on. Pure LLM chat hallucinates, is opaque, and cannot be audited — exactly the properties that block safe deployment. Grounding every step in a published AMA flowchart makes each recommendation traceable to a clinical protocol.
  • Separates "what to talk about" from "what to decide." By splitting retrieval, decision and conversation into distinct agents, clinical logic lives in the auditable flowchart, while the LLM only handles language — a clean way to keep the model from improvising medicine.
  • Evaluated at a scale clinical NLP rarely reaches. 37,200 simulated navigation conversations and 2,000 retrieval cases across varied conversational styles stress-test robustness far beyond hand-curated vignettes.
  • Targets a real bottleneck: triage and resource utilization. Reliable at-home self-triage could route patients to the right level of care, easing pressure on overstretched primary and emergency services.
  • Open-source and reproducible by design. Code is released on GitHub, with prompts and dataset samples in the Supplementary Information.

Core Methods

  • Multi-agent decomposition: three specialized LLM agents — a retrieval agent (pick the most relevant flowchart from the opening statement), a decision agent (interpret the patient's free-text reply and choose the next flowchart branch), and a conversation agent (turn protocol state into a patient-friendly, personalized message).
  • Flowchart grounding: clinical logic is externalized into the 100 AMA flowcharts; the LLM never invents triage steps — it only maps natural language onto a fixed, auditable graph of decision nodes.
  • Retrieval over a structured protocol library: matching a free-text symptom description to the correct flowchart among 100 candidates (the dominant error source, at 84.1%).
  • Constrained navigation: step-by-step traversal of the chosen flowchart, with the decision agent resolving each node from conversational input (99.1%).
  • Synthetic-conversation evaluation at scale: LLM-generated simulated patients with varied conversational styles/conditions provide a controlled, repeatable benchmark for retrieval and navigation accuracy.
  • Transparency by construction: because every recommendation is the endpoint of a traceable flowchart path, the system output is inherently auditable against a clinical standard.

① Geolocate the triage endpoint — from "what level of care" to "which reachable facility"

Extending this paperThe system stops at a care-level recommendation (e.g., self-care, primary care, emergency) but is blind to where the patient can actually obtain that care. A direct extension: add a fourth, spatially-aware agent that, given the flowchart's acuity verdict and the user's location, returns the nearest reachable, open, appropriate-tier facility — closing the loop from triage decision to actionable referral and making the AMA protocol spatially grounded.

Added value to my researchThis is exactly my spatial accessibility of primary care agenda: the triage acuity becomes the "demand weight" feeding an R2SFCA service-area model, so the referral respects real catchments, travel time and supply capacity rather than naive nearest-distance.

② Audit the flowchart system for socio-demographic and digital-divide bias

Extending this paperFlowchart grounding constrains the decision logic, but the conversation and retrieval agents still parse free text via an LLM, which can encode bias. The paper's all-synthetic evaluation cannot surface this. Extending it: stratify the synthetic patients by inferred age, gender, ethnicity and health-literacy/conversational fluency, then test whether retrieval and navigation accuracy — and the final acuity recommendation — drift across groups, the way LLM pain-management advice has been shown to.

Added value to my researchDirectly feeds my telehealth / digital-divide line: free-text triage may systematically underserve low-literacy or non-native-speaker populations, and pairing equity audits with the spatial distribution of those populations links algorithmic bias to geographic health inequity.

Cross-ref (corpus): Socio-demographic gaps in pain management guided by large language models — 10.1038/s44360-025-00017-6

③ Move from in-silico accuracy to real-world safety and trust

Extending this paperHeadline accuracies (84.1% / 99.1%) are on synthetic conversations — a clean benchmark, but not deployment evidence. The natural extension is a prospective, randomized evaluation with real users, measuring not just accuracy but safety (missed high-acuity cases), over-triage, and user trust/comprehension, mirroring how community-codesigned and African-clinic LLM systems were validated.

Added value to my researchSupports my open-science / FAIR stance: a transparent, flowchart-auditable system is the ideal substrate for a reproducible safety study, and the trust dimension connects to my interest in how digital health literacy mediates uptake across populations.

④ Make the retrieval bottleneck explainable — and learn from where it fails

Extending this paperThe 84.1% retrieval accuracy is the system's weakest link: ~16% of users get routed to the wrong flowchart from the start. Extending it: apply explainable analysis of retrieval errors — which symptom phrasings, comorbidities or overlapping flowcharts cause confusion — and feed those failure clusters back as targeted training/contrastive examples, turning a black-box retriever into a diagnosable, improvable component.

Added value to my researchMirrors my explainable machine learning for spatial prediction work: the same error-attribution discipline (which features/regions drive misclassification) transfers from spatial models to clinical retrieval, and the failure taxonomy is itself a research artifact.

⑤ Generalize the architecture into a reliability pattern for protocol-grounded agents

Extending this paperThe deepest contribution is a design pattern: externalize the decision logic into an auditable graph and let LLMs handle only language. Extending it: formalize this as a reliability framework and apply it beyond triage — to any standardized guideline (screening intervals, medication titration, public-health advisories) — and quantify the reliability gain versus an unconstrained LLM under the "rethinking reliability" lens for healthcare foundation models.
