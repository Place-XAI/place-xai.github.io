Origins and spread of formal ceremonial complexes in the Olmec and Maya regions revealed by airborne lidar

Takeshi Inomata · Juan Carlos Fernandez-Diaz · Daniela Triadan · Miguel García Mollinedo · Flory Pinzón · Melina García Hernández · Atasta Flores · Ashley Sharpe · Timothy Beach · Gregory W. L. Hodgins · Juan Javier Durón Díaz · Antonio Guerra Luna · Luis Guerrero Chávez · María de Lourdes Hernández Jiménez · Manuel Moreno Díaz Published 2021-10-25 DOI 10.1038/s41562-021-01218-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A large-scale airborne lidar survey of 84,516 km² across southern Mexico and western Guatemala identified 478 formal rectangular and square ceremonial complexes dating 1,050–400 BC, revealing a previously unknown pan-regional tradition of standardized civic space. Reprocessed lidar of San Lorenzo — the earliest Gulf Olmec centre (apogee 1,400–1,150 BC) — shows a rectangular plaza defined by 20 edge platforms, plausibly the spatial template from which all later Mesoamerican ceremonial formats descended. After San Lorenzo's decline, this template was formalized and diffused through intensive inter-regional interaction, ultimately encoding calendrical and directional cosmologies that became hallmarks of Mesoamerican civilization.

Why It Matters

  • Rewrites the origin story of Mesoamerican urbanism. By identifying 478 formal complexes invisible to ground survey, the study demonstrates that standardized civic space appeared centuries earlier and spread far more widely than textbook accounts assumed.
  • Establishes San Lorenzo as the spatial template. The reprocessed lidar reveals the previously unrecognized rectangular plaza and 20-platform arrangement at San Lorenzo, directly linking the earliest Gulf Olmec centre to the later MFU, MFC, and MFG traditions.
  • Demonstrates that low-cost national lidar can do archaeological science at continental scale. The study shows that Mexico's publicly available INEGI lidar (density ~0.03 pulses m−2) — designed for topographic mapping — can identify large ceremonial complexes with 80–100% accuracy in deforested terrain.
  • Encodes cosmological science in built form. The recurrent 20-platform layout and consistent astronomical orientations (tied to solar zenith-passage intervals) constitute evidence that the Mesoamerican vigesimal calendar and year-bearer concept were already materialized in architecture by the Early Formative period.
  • Opens inter-regional interaction as a first-order explanatory variable. The geographic distribution and typological sequence of complexes shifts the field away from single-centre diffusion models toward a network-interaction account of early civilizational spread.

Core Methods

  • Multi-resolution lidar analysis: manual expert interpretation of INEGI (5 m), NCALM (0.5–1 m), G-LiHT (1 m), and CCGS lidar using hillshade and Red Relief Image Map (RRIM) visualizations; automated ML detection noted as in development but not used for primary results.
  • Point-cloud reprocessing: INEGI raw (unclassified) point clouds reclassified via TerraScan "class as ground" with loose parameters; manual cleaning of vegetation returns; kriging-based 2 m DEM generation in Surfer v.16 for key sites (San Lorenzo, Pajonal).
  • Typological classification: systematic definition of four Formative complex types (MFU, MFC, MFG, VC) plus rectangular and square complexes based on structural morphology, E-Group presence, and platform arrangement, coded with orientation and dimension attributes in ArcGIS/QGIS.
  • Detection accuracy assessment: cross-comparison of INEGI lidar feature identifications against NCALM high-resolution lidar and G-LiHT transects to estimate detection rates by feature type and vegetation cover (Supplementary Table 3).
  • Orientation measurement and astronomical analysis: azimuth measurements of complex long axes from UTM grid north, converted to true north via grid convergence; comparison with known E-Group orientation datasets from Maya lowlands; alignment testing against solar zenith-passage calendar dates.
  • Bayesian radiocarbon modelling: OxCal manual rejection model applied to AMS radiocarbon dates from excavations to constrain site occupation intervals (Supplementary Table 8, code in SI).
  • Ground-truth validation: pedestrian survey of 62 sites; targeted excavations at 5 sites to verify ceramic chronology and platform stratigraphy; integration of 17 prior systematic regional ground-survey datasets.

③ Link the paper's plaza orientations to a quantitative cosmological-encoding hypothesis

Extending this paperThe paper reports a histogram of azimuths (n = 174) concentrated around 11°–12° and connects this to the 80-day-before-zenith-passage sunrise — but the test is visual rather than statistical. An extension: apply circular statistics (Rayleigh test, Watson test, kernel density on the unit circle) to the full azimuth dataset, fit a mixture model of cosmological attractor orientations versus terrain-constrained orientations, and estimate the fraction of complexes whose alignment is plausibly intentional versus incidental. Bayesian hierarchical modelling could additionally ask whether the cosmological attractor strengthened over time — testing whether the calendar symbolism became more rigidly enforced as the interaction network matured. This converts the paper's interpretive claim into a falsifiable statistical hypothesis.
