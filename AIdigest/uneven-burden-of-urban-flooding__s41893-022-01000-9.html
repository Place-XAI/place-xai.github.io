Uneven burden of urban flooding

Paul Bates Published 2022-10-31 DOI 10.1038/s41893-022-01000-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

High-resolution flood inundation modelling now enables street- and building-level flood risk mapping for entire mega-cities. Applied to greater Los Angeles, the companion study (Sanders et al.) reveals that 425,000 people and US$56 billion of assets face dangerous flooding in a 1-in-100-year event — far exceeding official FEMA estimates — and that flood risk is deeply inequitable: the more disadvantaged half of the population bears 65% of composite flood hazard, while non-Hispanic Black, Hispanic, and Asian residents face disproportionately higher exposure than non-Hispanic white populations.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reveals the scale of official underestimation. Official US Federal flood maps cover only 33% of rivers and streams and systematically undercount who is at risk; this work provides locally accurate, high-resolution evidence of the true scope.
  • Quantifies racial and socioeconomic flood inequity. Non-Hispanic Black residents are 79% more likely to be exposed to dangerous flooding (>1 m depth) than non-Hispanic white residents in Los Angeles — a headline equity finding with direct policy implications.
  • Demonstrates that flood defences are inadequate. The study shows existing flood infrastructure in greater LA cannot contain the standard 1% annual probability event, exposing a systemic gap between design standards and actual protection.
  • Points to machine learning and remote sensing as the scaling pathway. The commentary highlights that replicating this fine-grained modelling at global scale will require automated data production and ML — framing a research frontier as much as reporting a result.
  • Links flood science directly to environmental justice. By overlaying physical flood hazard maps with sociodemographic data, the work provides a template for making flood risk a social-justice issue, not just a hydrological one.

Core Methods

  • High-resolution 2D flood inundation modelling (PRIMo): physically-based simulation at building and street scale (a few metres resolution) over the Los Angeles mega-city domain — enabled by high-performance computing to overcome the previously prohibitive computational cost.
  • Combined coastal and fluvial flood hazard: both river flooding and coastal inundation are jointly modelled and mapped, with explicit representation of flood defence structures from official and local-knowledge sources.
  • Return-period flood scenario: the 1% annual probability (1-in-100-year) event is used as the design standard, showing that even this standard cannot be met by current infrastructure.
  • Sociodemographic overlay and environmental justice analysis: census-based sociodemographic attributes (income, race/ethnicity) are intersected with the modelled flood hazard surface to determine whether particular groups are disproportionately exposed.
  • Comparison with official flood maps: PRIMo results are validated against FEMA NFIP maps and national-scale benchmarks (Wing et al. 2018, 2022) to quantify the underestimation of official data.

① Scale PRIMo-style modelling globally using ML surrogates trained on existing simulations

Extending this paperThe commentary explicitly identifies the central bottleneck: replicating LA-style fine-resolution flood simulation across other cities requires automating the painstaking model-building process — assembling terrain data, defence structures, and calibration at scale. A direct extension: train an ML surrogate (e.g., a physics-informed graph neural network) on PRIMo outputs for Los Angeles, using building footprints, elevation, and drainage topology as inputs, then transfer it to cities where detailed simulation is infeasible. This converts the one-city breakthrough into a transferable modelling infrastructure, precisely the "sophisticated algorithms" the author calls for.

③ Quantify cascading economic losses through urban supply chains, not just direct asset damage

Extending this paperThe US$56 billion exposure figure is a direct asset-damage estimate (structures in the floodplain). But this ignores cascading economic losses: when a flooded district shuts down, it disrupts supply chains, employment, and regional production far beyond the inundated area. Extending the paper's framework with a multiregional input–output (MRIO) model — as developed in the Chinese cities corpus paper — would reveal that the true economic burden of a 1-in-100-year LA event far exceeds the direct damage figure, and that indirect losses concentrate differently across socioeconomic groups.

④ Integrate flood-risk mapping into healthcare and critical-service accessibility frameworks

Extending this paperThe paper shows which communities are disproportionately exposed to floods; it does not ask what happens to their access to healthcare, emergency services, and other critical facilities during or after an event. Extending this paperPRIMo-level simulations are computationally intensive and data-hungry; most cities globally lack the inputs for such modelling. Yet the paper's equity finding — that socioeconomic disadvantage predicts flood exposure — implies that statistical relationships between observable urban features (elevation distribution, impervious surface fraction, proximity to waterways, density, income) and flood inequity can be exploited.
