How NGOs reshape urban green space justice in Beijing

Luquan Liang · Sun Sheng Han · Jennifer Day Published 2026-04-13 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00431-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

In Beijing's constrained governance environment, NGOs strategically downplay distributional inequities in urban green space (UGS) and instead push procedural justice — using supervision, networking, advocacy and legal frameworks to secure public participation. On the recognitional dimension they navigate political limits by subtly altering dominant narratives, selectively engaging governments and aligning practices with official expectations. To gain recognition and stay operational, NGOs trade distributive demands for participation and recognition; the study highlights counter-narratives, information disclosure and earlier NGO involvement in planning as the levers most likely to advance UGS justice.

Why It Matters

  • Opens the black box of who shapes green justice. Most UGS-equity work measures distributional gaps remotely; this study explains the political process through which an under-studied actor — NGOs — actually moves the needle.
  • Reframes "justice" as three dimensions, not one. By separating distributive, procedural and recognitional justice, it shows that the most cited dimension (distribution) is not where contestation happens in a constrained regime.
  • Documents a survival strategy. The finding that NGOs deliberately mute distributional critique to stay operational is a transferable insight for civil-society action under political constraint, well beyond Beijing.
  • Actionable governance levers. Counter-narratives, mandatory information disclosure and earlier NGO involvement in planning give planners and policymakers concrete entry points to widen participation.

Core Methods

  • Semi-structured in-depth interviews: 58 interviews across NGO staff, officials and other stakeholders, capturing strategy, constraints and justice framing.
  • Participant observation: embedded fieldwork with documented records and a coding protocol (Supplementary), triangulating interview accounts.
  • Social media / discourse analysis: examination of NGO narratives and advocacy communications to trace counter-narratives and recognitional strategies.
  • Tripartite environmental-justice framework: systematic analysis along distributive, procedural and recognitional justice dimensions.
  • Stakeholder / social-network mapping: qualitative mapping of NGOs' ties to governments and other actors (Fig. 2), illuminating where leverage and recognition flow.
