Synergistic action on mitigation and adaptation pilot policies to enhance low-carbon resilience of Chinese cities

Di Wang · Shiwei Chen Published 2025-08-26 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00303-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Running both low-carbon (mitigation) and climate-resilient (adaptation) pilot programs in the same city produces a synergistic gain in "low-carbon resilience" that outperforms either single pilot alone. Across 286 Chinese cities (2005–2022), the dual-pilot effect is strongest in cities facing extreme weather and high disaster risk, and is amplified by green technology innovation, human capital, and communication infrastructure. The diversity and integration of policy instruments — and the multidimensionality of policy objectives — are what make combined climate action effective.

★

Why It Matters

  • Tests the mitigation–adaptation synergy that is usually only assumed. Climate strategy is traditionally split into mitigation and adaptation silos; this is a city-level empirical demonstration that combining the two beats doing either alone, giving the long-debated "synergy" a measurable causal estimate.
  • Defines and quantifies "low-carbon resilience." The paper builds a composite that fuses ecological, economic, social, and institutional dimensions, so resilience is no longer a slogan but a comparable index across 286 cities and 18 years.
  • Shows where policy works — and where it does not. Heterogeneous effects across city types (extreme-weather/high-risk vs. MMHM vs. LLLL classes) tell governments which cities get the most leverage from dual pilots.
  • Names the enabling conditions. Green tech innovation, human capital, and communication infrastructure are identified as moderators — concrete levers a city can invest in to make climate pilots pay off.

Core Methods

  • Coordinated mitigation–adaptation framework: a conceptual model (Fig. 1) linking low-carbon and climate-resilient pilots to a multidimensional low-carbon resilience outcome.
  • Composite resilience index: construct a four-dimensional (ecological, economic, social, institutional) low-carbon resilience measure and use it to classify cities (e.g., MMHM, LLLL).
  • Double machine learning (DML): causal policy-effect estimation on the city panel using the EconML library, controlling high-dimensional confounders to isolate the dual-pilot treatment effect versus single pilots.
  • Heterogeneity analysis: compare effects across city categories — extreme-weather/high-disaster-risk cities, MMHM cities, and LLLL cities (Fig. 2).
  • Moderation / mechanism analysis: test green technology innovation, human capital development, and communication infrastructure as moderators that amplify policy effectiveness (Table 2 / Fig. 3).

① Make the dual-pilot effect spatially explicit and spillover-aware

Extending this paperThe DML design estimates an average treatment effect treating each city as independent, but Chinese pilot policies diffuse through neighboring and peer cities. Extending the paper: embed the 286-city panel in a spatial / network adjacency structure and add a spatial-DID or interference-aware estimator so the dual-pilot effect is decomposed into direct effects and cross-city spillovers, testing whether resilience gains leak to non-pilot neighbors.
