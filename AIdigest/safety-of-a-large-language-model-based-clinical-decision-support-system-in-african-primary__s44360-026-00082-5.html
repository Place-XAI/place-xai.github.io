Safety of a large language model-based clinical decision support system in African primary healthcare

Ambrose Agweyu · Paul Mwaniki · Wilkister Musau · Robert Korom · Lynda Isaaka · Conrad Wanyama · Sarah Kiptinness · Najib Adan · Mira Emmanuel-Fabula · Bilal A. Mateen Published 2026-03-10 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00082-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

An EMR-embedded GPT-4o clinical decision support system deployed across 16 primary care clinics in Nairobi, Kenya was evaluated over 1,469 encounters: hallucinations were uncommon (3.4%) and guideline alignment was near-perfect (99%), yet the system's real-world safety profile is double-edged — harmful recommendations appeared in 7.8% of encounters, and crucially, harmful outputs were adopted by clinicians at a disproportionately higher rate than beneficial ones. The findings underscore that raw output quality does not translate automatically into patient benefit: usability optimization, local guardrails, and prospective controlled trials are all needed before scaling AI-assisted care in low-resource settings.

Why It Matters

  • First real-world, multi-site EMR-embedded LLM evaluation in sub-Saharan Africa. Unlike benchmark datasets or simulated vignettes, this study captures authentic clinician–AI interactions across 16 urban clinics over 3 months (78,366 total consultations), making it the largest and most ecologically valid CDSS evaluation in an LMIC to date.
  • Reveals a dangerous asymmetry in clinician adoption. Harmful LLM recommendations were acted upon in 58% of cases where they appeared, versus only 22% for beneficial ones — a behavioral pattern that could amplify harm rather than improve care without targeted design intervention.
  • Quantifies the cost of AI-assisted care at sub-cent scale. Total API cost for 1,469 encounters was US$7.81 (~$0.005 per consultation), directly comparable to established willingness-to-pay benchmarks for malaria diagnostics, making an economic argument for scalability.
  • Demonstrates that off-the-shelf LLMs can be context-competent yet still unsafe. The system was not fine-tuned on Kenyan data, yet achieved 99% guideline alignment — but still produced context-inappropriate recommendations (e.g., drugs not on local formularies) that eroded clinician trust and indirectly suppressed uptake of correct advice.
  • Provides a governance framework applicable to LLM pharmacovigilance. By drawing the analogy between post-market drug surveillance and continuous LLM monitoring, the study charts a regulatory and institutional pathway for AI deployment in health systems.

Core Methods

  • Retrospective observational study design: secondary use of de-identified routine electronic health record data from a live deployed system; no intervention introduced; Hawthorne-effect minimized by evaluating historical logs.
  • Proportionally allocated, age-stratified random sampling: 1,500 cases drawn from eligible encounters across five age strata (0–4, 5–17, 18–39, 40–59, 60+) to preserve the marginal age distribution of the source dataset.
  • Structured expert panel evaluation: 30 locally licensed Kenyan physicians rated each record on a standardized rubric covering (1) initial documentation quality, (2) LLM diagnostic reasoning, (3) LLM clinical management appropriateness, (4) safety concerns in LLM outputs, and (5) clinician adoption and resulting safety impact.
  • Calibration exercise with reference standard: evaluators scored 30 pre-adjudicated cases; a ±1 tolerance rule within sentiment bands was applied; all 30 evaluators passed and proceeded to the main evaluation.
  • Interrater reliability assessment: Kendall's W for ordinal outcomes; Fleiss' kappa for binary/nominal outcomes; interpreted using Landis and Koch benchmarks; 10% of cases independently double-reviewed.
  • Descriptive statistics with exact binomial 95% CIs: proportions reported with CIs for all primary metrics; several rubric categories collapsed for reporting clarity; analyses conducted in R (v4.5.1).
  • Post-hoc qualitative hallucination taxonomy: evaluator comments on hallucinated content grouped into four themes: misidentified drugs/acronyms, documentation contradictions, clinical parameter misinterpretation, and unspecified rationale.

① Spatially disaggregate LLM safety signals across the 16-clinic network

Extending this paperThe paper reports aggregate safety metrics across all 16 clinics but does not geolocate where hallucinations, harmful recommendations, or adoption asymmetries cluster spatially. Clinic-level utilization already varies dramatically (29% to 81%), hinting at spatial heterogeneity in human–AI interaction. A direct extension: geocode the 16 clinic locations within the Nairobi metropolitan area and apply geographically weighted regression (GWR/MGWR) to test whether neighborhood-level factors — population density, facility catchment size, digital infrastructure quality, and clinician caseload — predict hallucination rates or adoption asymmetry. This would move the paper from a pan-city average toward a spatial safety map of LLM reliability.

Pairing GWR with an explainable ML layer (SHAP values on clinic-level predictors) would quantify which place-characteristics drive LLM underperformance — generating an LLM-reliability accessibility surface analogous to healthcare facility access maps.

② Model the adoption asymmetry as a human-AI trust calibration problem

Extending this paperThe paper's most alarming finding — harmful outputs were adopted in 58% of cases vs. 22% for beneficial ones — is documented but not mechanistically explained. This asymmetry could be attributed to confirmation bias (clinicians accept LLM suggestions that align with their existing plan), cognitive offloading (LLM provides a plausible-sounding path of least resistance), or low trust in counter-suggestions. A direct extension: use the paper's existing rich evaluation data (quality of initial documentation × LLM helpfulness rating × adoption decision) to build a Bayesian updating model of clinician trust, estimating how prior confidence and LLM output framing jointly determine adoption probability. The parallel user-experience study at Penda Health (cited as ref. 18 in the paper) provides the subjective trust scores needed to validate this model.

Added value to my researchModeling trust calibration in human-AI clinical interactions is structurally similar to modeling digital health adoption gaps in telehealth and digital divide research. My spatiotemporal methods — specifically kernel density estimation of adoption events and agent-based simulation of diffusion dynamics — could model how clinician trust evolves over repeated interactions with an LLM CDSS, informing rollout strategies that maximize beneficial adoption in resource-constrained primary care networks.

③ Introduce local fine-tuning with Kenyan clinical guidelines to close the context gap

Extending this paperThe study explicitly flags that the system was deployed off-the-shelf — no fine-tuning on Kenyan patient data, no retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) from local guidelines. Context-inappropriate outputs (e.g., prescribing unavailable drugs, flagging normal SpO2 at Nairobi's altitude of ~1,800m as abnormal) directly caused clinician distrust. The paper itself calls this out as the next necessary step. A structured extension: create a Kenya-specific RAG pipeline that prepends relevant Kenya Ministry of Health guideline chunks to each GPT-4o query, then re-run the same 1,469-encounter evaluation protocol on the augmented system, enabling a controlled before/after comparison of hallucination rates and context-appropriateness. The open Zenodo dataset and evaluation rubric are already in place — only the system needs to be updated.

Added value to my researchThis aligns directly with my geospatial foundation model fine-tuning agenda: just as fine-tuning a geographic FM on local spatial data improves downstream task performance, fine-tuning a clinical LLM on locally curated epidemiological data and formulary guidelines is the equivalent domain-adaptation step. The evaluation framework (rubric + expert panel) provides a reproducible benchmark that can validate fine-tuned clinical LLMs across different LMIC settings — analogous to FAIR benchmarks for geospatial models.

④ Quantify the spatial equity of LLM CDSS deployment across the urban hierarchy

Extending this paperPenda Health operates in a relatively well-resourced, cloud-connected urban primary care network — the paper itself cautions that outcomes will differ in settings with poorer infrastructure, lower digital literacy, or sparser referral networks. An important extension: using the paper's clinic-level utilization data (Supplementary Fig. 1) alongside publicly available data on clinic catchment populations, urban poverty indices, and internet penetration, apply a 2SFCA-style accessibility analysis to map who in Nairobi actually benefits from the LLM CDSS. Clinics with low utilization may serve more marginalized populations, meaning the current deployment pattern could be equity-reducing even though aggregate quality is high.

⑤ Design a prospective AI safety monitoring system inspired by pharmacovigilance

Extending this paperThe paper closes with an explicit call for continuous post-deployment monitoring analogous to drug pharmacovigilance and medical device post-market surveillance — but does not operationalize what such a system would look like. A direct extension: design an LLM safety signal detection framework for EMR-embedded CDSS, using the paper's evaluation rubric as the basis for a lightweight automated safety screen. Specifically, train a lightweight classifier on the expert panel's labeled encounter data (n=1,469, openly available on Zenodo) to flag encounters with high hallucination risk or context-inappropriate outputs in near real-time, enabling continuous monitoring without requiring a full expert panel at scale. This would be the first AI pharmacovigilance pipeline validated on African primary care data.

Incorporating this pipeline into a spatiotemporal monitoring dashboard (tracking safety signals over time and across clinic locations) would produce a real-world LLM governance tool for LMIC health systems, combining my data science and health geography strengths.
