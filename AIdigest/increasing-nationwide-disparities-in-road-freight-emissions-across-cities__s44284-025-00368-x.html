Increasing nationwide disparities in road freight emissions across cities

Chengcheng Yu · Quan Yuan · Anne Goodchild · Wentao Dong · Travis Fried · Haocheng Lin · Yeke Zou · Chao Yang · Yougeng Lu · Qianyao Duan · Zhengtao Qin Published 2026-01-14 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00368-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 3,107 US counties (2011–2020), road-freight emissions (PM10, PM2.5, CO2, NOx) are becoming more spatially unequal, not less. The paper isolates two engines of injustice: demand-oriented inequity — cities near freight corridors or terminals carry disproportionately high emissions relative to the freight demand they generate, with discrepancies rising 5.6–14.2% — and SES-oriented inequity — minority communities along freight networks are spatially correlated with both higher emissions and steeper declines in household income. The authors call for coordinated national, regional and city action to embed environmental justice into transport planning and compensation.

Why It Matters

  • Names a hidden externality of e-commerce. The convenience of next-day delivery and globalized supply chains lands as localized air pollution; this work makes that burden spatially explicit at national scale.
  • Separates "who causes" from "who suffers." By contrasting freight demand against freight emissions, it quantifies the gap between cities that consume freight and cities that breathe its exhaust — the essence of a demand-oriented injustice.
  • Connects pollution to economic decline. Communities near freight networks show not only higher emissions but greater drops in household income, linking environmental burden to a downward socioeconomic spiral.
  • Tracks a trend, not a snapshot. A decade-long window (2011–2020) shows disparities increasing, reframing freight equity as a worsening structural problem rather than a fixed condition.
  • Speaks directly to the SDGs. The analysis is framed against Sustainable Development Goals 3 (health), 10 (reduced inequalities) and 11 (sustainable cities), giving policymakers a measurable handle.

Core Methods

  • Emissions intensity mapping: compute county/MSA-level road-freight emissions intensities for four pollutants and overlay them on the national freight-infrastructure layout (Fig. 1) to expose corridor/terminal exposure.
  • Spatial autocorrelation: Moran's I to test clustering of emissions, and bivariate Moran's I to test cross-correlation between emissions and socioeconomic variables (minority share, income change).
  • Inequity decomposition: a multidimensional poverty index (MPI)-style evolution at national and urban (MSA) scales (Fig. 2), separating demand-oriented from SES-oriented inequity.
  • Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC): fit emissions (CO2, NOx, PM2.5, PM10) against income levels, contrasting freight-corridor vs. noncorridor cities (Fig. 3).
  • Regression analysis: OLS models (Stata) relating emissions to freight demand, network proximity and socioeconomic covariates; cartography in ArcGIS Pro.

① From county-level NEI inventories to mobility-resolved exposure

Extending this paperThe paper's emissions intensities are county/MSA aggregates from the NEI, so they smear pollution that is actually concentrated in narrow bands along corridors and around terminals. A direct extension: downscale the inventory using truck GPS trajectories and mobile-phone activity to recover who is where, when, converting static county totals into hyperlocal, time-resolved exposure surfaces that test the SES-oriented claim at the block level.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my multi-source data-fusion line: fusing GPS, mobile-phone and remote-sensing layers into a fine-grained exposure field feeds directly into spatiotemporal kernel density estimation of population-weighted exposure, the demand side of any health-accessibility model.

② Replace global OLS with explainable, spatially varying attribution

Extending this paperThe paper relates emissions to demand, proximity and SES via global OLS and bivariate Moran's I — both assume the emissions–equity relationship is stationary across the country, which the widening disparities themselves contradict. Extending it with MGWR plus an explainable ML ensemble (e.g., SHAP) would map where corridor proximity, minority share or income decline dominate, turning a single national coefficient into a geography of mechanisms.
