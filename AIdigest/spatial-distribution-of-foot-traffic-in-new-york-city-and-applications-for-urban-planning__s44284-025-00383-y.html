Spatial distribution of foot traffic in New York City and applications for urban planning

Andres Sevtsuk · Rounaq Basu · Liu Liu · Abdulaziz Alhassan · Justin Kollar Published 2026-02-06 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00383-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The authors build the first city-wide foot-traffic model for New York City — a calibrated pedestrian-flow estimate over a 315,577-segment sidewalk/crosswalk network for six peak time periods. Comparing modeled volumes to the city's official street classifications reveals that many outer-borough streets carry foot traffic comparable to central Manhattan yet remain "undercategorized" for pedestrian priority, exposing infrastructure-investment inequity. Linking volumes to crash data shows that the highest exposure-adjusted pedestrian injury risk often lies outside Manhattan, turning raw crash counts into a per-pedestrian danger metric for evidence-based, equitable street design.

Why It Matters

  • Measures the most common, least measured mode. Walking dominates urban trips but is rarely counted at scale; this delivers a continuous, city-wide pedestrian-volume surface instead of a handful of intersection counts.
  • Reframes safety as exposure-adjusted risk. Raw crash counts conflate danger with footfall — normalizing crashes by modeled pedestrian volume relocates the most dangerous intersections to the outer boroughs, changing where safety dollars should go.
  • Audits official policy with data. Overlaying modeled volumes on NYC's street classifications flags "undercategorized" corridors that warrant pedestrian-priority upgrades, making investment inequity legible.
  • Transferable planning baseline. The calibrated network, per-trip-type flows and model files are released, giving planners a reusable benchmark for infrastructure, hazard and accessibility analysis.

Core Methods

  • Origin–destination pedestrian flow model: generate trips between population/employment origins and amenity destinations (schools, parks, transit, retail), then route them over the sidewalk/crosswalk network using shortest-path / spatial-network betweenness logic to accumulate segment-level foot-traffic volumes.
  • Trip-type weighting: distinct origin–destination trip types are combined with calibrated weights (Extended Data Table 3) to reflect the mix of walking purposes.
  • Calibration to observed counts: modeled volumes are calibrated against NYC DOT pedestrian counts for six peak time periods, with summary statistics and borough/period distributions reported (Extended Data Tables 1–2).
  • Feature importance: permutation-importance analysis (Fig. 1) ranks which inputs drive predicted volumes across different time periods.
  • Policy diagnostics: (i) overlay modeled volumes on NYC street classifications to flag undercategorized corridors (Figs. 3–4); (ii) normalize crash counts by modeled pedestrian exposure to map per-pedestrian injury risk (Fig. 5).

① Replace the assumed OD/gravity trip generation with a learned mobility model

Extending this paperThe model's foot-traffic surface rests on hand-specified origin–destination trip types and weights (Extended Data Table 3) routed over the network. A direct extension: swap that fixed gravity-style generation for a closed-form, automatically-learned gravity model calibrated on the same NYC DOT counts, so trip production, attraction and distance decay are inferred rather than assumed — tightening calibration and exposing where the analytic flow assumptions break.

Added value to my researchThis is the same move I want inside R2SFCA: replace the assumed Gaussian/power distance-decay with a flow-learned kernel, yielding a demand-aware accessibility allocator validated against real pedestrian counts.

② Ground betweenness-based foot traffic in street-network structural invariants

Extending this paperThe flow model is effectively a weighted betweenness over a 315,577-segment graph. Extending it: relate the predicted volume distribution to known structural invariants of planar street networks, asking how much of NYC's foot-traffic pattern is dictated by raw topology versus land use and OD demand — separating geometry-driven flow from behavior-driven flow.

Added value to my researchFeeds my explainable-ML spatial-prediction line: structural-invariant features become interpretable priors/baselines for predicting flow and accessibility, and a clean null model against which ML gains are measured.

Extending it: cross-validate and densify the surface with large-scale passive mobility data (GPS/mobile-phone footfall), enabling continuous diurnal and seasonal foot-traffic dynamics instead of static peak snapshots — and quantifying where the analytic model and observed mobility diverge.
