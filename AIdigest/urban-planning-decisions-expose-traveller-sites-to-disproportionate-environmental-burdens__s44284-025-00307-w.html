Urban planning decisions expose Traveller sites to disproportionate environmental burdens

Nicolas Mondolfo · Antoine Leblois · Philippe Delacote · Léa Tardieu Published 2025-08-26 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00307-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

In France, designated Traveller sites ("aires d'accueil des gens du voyage") are placed where ethnic minorities bear the heaviest environmental costs. Using a linked national dataset, the authors show that cities more exposed to disamenities are more likely to host a site, and that within a hosting city the site is more exposed than any other residential area — even more than other disadvantaged households. The pattern is consistent with two non-exclusive mechanisms: cost minimization by municipalities and discriminatory preferences of local representatives and residents, demonstrating how routine urban-planning decisions manufacture systemic socio-spatial inequality in Europe.

★

Why It Matters

  • Brings environmental-justice evidence to Europe. Most environmental-racism quantification comes from the US (redlining, toxic-plant siting); this provides rare, rigorous statistical evidence for a European minority (Travellers/Gens du voyage), a chronically under-studied and under-counted group.
  • Pinpoints urban planning itself as the mechanism. Because municipalities legally decide where Travellers may stay, the work shows that discrimination is produced by the planning process — not just inherited residential sorting — making it directly actionable by policy.
  • Separates "where" from "who". It distinguishes a between-city effect (disamenity-heavy cities host sites) from a within-city effect (sites are the most-exposed parcels in town), isolating discrimination from generic poverty.
  • Names the mechanisms. By testing cost-minimization (municipal-border placement near existing nuisances) against discriminatory preferences, it gives planners and courts concrete levers and an evidentiary basis for siting reform.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Study area
France (mainland), analyzed at two units: municipalities/cities and a fine spatial grid of cells. The city-level location model uses n = 34,820 observations (Fig. 2); the cell-level proximity model uses n = 356,764 cells (Fig. 5).
Traveller sites
Locations of official Traveller reception sites ("aires d'accueil des gens du voyage"), compiled from the departmental reception schemes (DHUP / Ministère de la cohésion des territoires) and linked to environmental and socioeconomic layers. The exact total site count is reported in the paper/Supplementary rather than in the metadata available here.
Environmental layers
Exposure to environmental amenities and disamenities (e.g., proximity to polluting/hazardous facilities and nuisances) plus accessibility to public services, assembled in the deposited dataset (Tetis / Recherche Data Gouv).
Socioeconomic layers
French demographic and socioeconomic indicators (income/disadvantage, household composition) drawing on INSEE sources, used to compare Traveller sites against other residential and disadvantaged areas.
Time window

Core Methods

  • Multi-source spatial linkage: join Traveller-site locations to environmental disamenity/amenity layers, public-service accessibility, and INSEE socioeconomic data at both city and grid-cell resolution.
  • Between-city location model: regression of the probability that a city hosts a Traveller site on its disamenity exposure and socio-demographic/urban factors (Fig. 2, n = 34,820).
  • Within-city exposure comparison: contrast disamenity exposure of Traveller sites versus other residential areas — including other disadvantaged households — in hosting cities (Fig. 3).
  • Mechanism testing: analyze placement near municipal borders and near disamenities by distance band (Fig. 4) to discriminate cost-minimization from discriminatory-preference explanations.
  • Cell-level proximity model: model the likelihood that a grid cell lies near a Traveller site as a function of socio-demographic and urban factors (Fig. 5, n = 356,764).

① Move from static disamenity proximity to mobility-based exposure

Extending this paperThe paper measures exposure as residential proximity of a Traveller site to fixed disamenities. But Travellers are, by definition, mobile, and exposure is realized along daily paths and seasons. A direct extension: replace static proximity with a mobility-weighted exposure built from GPS/itinerary data and time-resolved pollution surfaces, turning a parcel attribute into a lived dose — and testing whether the discrimination gap widens once mobility is accounted for.

② Make the siting model explainable and counterfactual

Extending this paperThe location and proximity models (Figs. 2, 5) are linear/probabilistic and report average associations. Extending them with explainable machine learning (gradient boosting + SHAP, or MGWR) would expose where and for whom the disamenity-driven siting effect is strongest, and support counterfactual siting — "what would exposure be under a least-injustice placement?" — sharpening the cost-minimization-vs-preference test spatially.

③ Couple environmental burden with access to care and services

Extending this paperThe deposited dataset already includes accessibility to public services, yet the analysis foregrounds disamenities. A natural extension: jointly model burden (disamenities) and benefit (service/healthcare access) for Traveller sites versus other residents, quantifying a combined (in)justice index — Travellers may be both over-exposed to nuisances and under-served by clinics, schools, and transit.

④ Reframe siting as an efficiency–equity facility-location problem

Extending this paperThe paper diagnoses cost-minimization as a driver but stops at diagnosis. Extending it: formalize Traveller-site siting as a multi-objective facility-location optimization trading municipal cost against environmental-justice and service-access objectives, and chart the Pareto frontier to show how much exposure could be removed at modest cost — converting an indictment into a planning tool.
