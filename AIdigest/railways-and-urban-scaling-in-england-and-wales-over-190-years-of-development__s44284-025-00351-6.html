Railways and urban scaling in England and Wales over 190 years of development

Zhenhua Chen · Alexis Litvine · Leigh Shaw-Taylor Published 2025-12-05 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00351-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 190 years of England and Wales (1831–2021), the scaling of rail supply (stations, track length) and quality (accessibility) against city population — initially non-linear and divergent — gradually converged toward linear proportionality. Periods of railway expansion coincided with the rise of small- and mid-sized cities and stronger regional connectivity, whereas later contraction (the Beeching era and after) coincided with falling accessibility and renewed concentration in the largest centers. The result is read as long-term, scale-dependent co-evolution between transport networks and the urban system.

★

Why It Matters

  • A two-century natural experiment on transport and cities. Most transport–urban studies are cross-sectional or span a few decades; this assembles a 1831–2021 harmonized panel, turning the entire life cycle of a national rail system — growth, peak, contraction, partial revival — into a single scaling story.
  • It separates infrastructure supply from quality. Counting stations and track is not the same as the travel time they actually deliver; the paper tracks both and shows their scaling exponents drift differently over time.
  • It links network change to the shape of the urban hierarchy. Expansion broadens the city-size distribution (more viable mid-sized towns); contraction re-concentrates population — directly tying infrastructure policy to rank–size / Zipf structure.
  • It speaks to today's choices. Convergence toward linear proportionality is a benchmark planners can read against: under- or over-provision relative to population becomes visible and historically contextualized, informing reopening and levelling-up debates.

Core Methods

  • Urban scaling regressions: fit power-law relations Y ∝ Pᵝ linking rail supply (station counts, track length) and quality (accessibility) to city population P, estimating the exponent β for each benchmark year (1841–2021; Extended Data Table 2).
  • Convergence tracking: follow how β moves over 190 years and test for drift toward β ≈ 1 (linear proportionality between provision and population).
  • Rank–size / Pareto analysis: estimate Pareto (Zipf) exponents of the city-population distribution at benchmark years to read how expansion vs. contraction reshapes the urban hierarchy (Fig. 3).
  • Accessibility computation: build inter-city travel-time matrices on the historical rail network and derive an accessibility index whose evolution is mapped over time (Fig. 4).
  • Concentration / inequality metrics: Gini coefficients of population, track length, station count and accessibility track long-term spatial concentration (Extended Data Fig. 1).
  • Robustness: Supplementary Methods run robustness checks on the city-size distribution analysis.

① From correlational co-evolution to causal infrastructure shocks

Extending this paperThe paper documents that expansion coincides with mid-sized-city growth and contraction with re-concentration, but the design is descriptive. A direct extension treats the Beeching closures and selective reopenings as quasi-experiments: difference-in-differences or staggered-adoption estimators on the harmonized panel could isolate the causal effect of losing/gaining a station on a city's subsequent growth and scaling position — converting the paper's correlation into an identified treatment effect.

Added value to my researchThis sharpens my health-geography accessibility work: rail openings/closures become natural experiments for testing how a step-change in service-area connectivity propagates into facility access and the spatial entropy of catchments — a causal lens on 2SFCA dynamics.

② Make the 190-year scaling exponent a learned, explainable function

Extending this paperThe paper fits one scaling exponent β per benchmark year and narrates its drift toward 1. Extending it: model β (and its spatial residuals) with explainable machine learning / MGWR so the exponent becomes a function of era, region, topography and economic regime — exposing where and why a city is over- or under-provisioned relative to the national law rather than reading a single slope.

③ Turn the rail-accessibility index into a dynamic graph-learning problem

Extending this paperAccessibility here is computed year-by-year on static network snapshots.
