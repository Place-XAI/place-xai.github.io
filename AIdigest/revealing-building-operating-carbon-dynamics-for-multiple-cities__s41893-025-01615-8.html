Revealing building operating carbon dynamics for multiple cities

Winston Yap · Abraham Noah Wu · Clayton Miller · Filip Biljecki Published 2025-08-15 DOI 10.1038/s41893-025-01615-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A generalizable, open-science framework integrating multi-modal geospatial data and heterogeneous graph neural networks explains 78.4% of the variation in building operating carbon emissions across five climatically and planning-diverse cities (Melbourne, Manhattan/NYC, Seattle, Singapore, Washington DC; 595,173 buildings total). Planning history, urban form and socio-economic conditions jointly shape city-specific carbon profiles, with wealthier areas consistently exhibiting the highest per capita emissions and emissions extending well beyond dense urban cores into suburban areas — demanding place-sensitive, rather than uniform, decarbonization strategies.

Why It Matters

  • First multi-city, building-resolved carbon map using only open data. By relying exclusively on publicly available datasets (OSM, Mapillary, Mapbox, Meta population grids), the framework is transferable to any city with benchmarking mandates — breaking the proprietary-data bottleneck that has constrained prior urban-energy studies.
  • Reveals the equity paradox of decarbonization. Wealthier areas hold the highest per capita emissions, but lower-income urban areas (bottom-20th-percentile GDP) account for up to 60% of total city emissions in cities like Melbourne — meaning carbon taxes and retrofitting mandates disproportionately burden vulnerable communities unless spatially targeted.
  • Overturns the density-efficiency dogma. The study shows that the density–emissions relationship is city-specific and non-monotone; micro-climate factors (urban heat island, LCZ diversity) can reverse the expected efficiency gains in sprawled cities like Seattle and Melbourne.
  • Provides a path-dependent planning lens. Each city's historical development trajectory (low-rise sprawl vs. high-rise compact) is encoded in its carbon profile, offering transferable lessons for cities at earlier stages of urbanization.
  • Fully reproducible and extendable. Source code and datasets are released via GitHub, supporting the open-science community and enabling rapid extension to Boston, Montreal, Berlin and other cities newly mandating benchmarking disclosures.

Core Methods

  • Heterogeneous city graph construction: each city is represented as a graph G = {N, E, R, T} with four node types — buildings (B), urban plots (P), streets (S), street intersections (I) — and six edge types (BB, BS, BP, PP, PS, SI). Constructed via the Urbanity Python package; neighbour links defined by 3-nearest-neighbour Euclidean distance between building centroids.
  • Multi-modal feature extraction: building features include morphological metrics (area, shape index, complexity, height); plot, street and intersection features from OSM; street-view features from Mapillary via semantic segmentation; satellite image chips processed by ResNet-50 (1,000-dim → 128-dim fully connected layer); LCZ Shannon entropy; UHI intensity; gridded GDP; Meta population density.
  • Heterogeneous GraphSAGE model: two-layer GraphSAGE with separate convolutional modules per edge type; 2-hop neighbourhood aggregation; 128-dim embeddings per node type. Final building embedding concatenated with 128-dim aerial-image embedding (256-dim) then passed through two 1,024-dim fully connected layers for node-level regression of building operating carbon.
  • Training protocol: 70/15/15 train/val/test split; 50 epochs; Adam optimizer (lr tuned from 10⁻³ to 10⁻⁵); batch size 16–64; graph sub-batching to manage memory on large city graphs; logarithmic transformation of target variable; NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU.
  • Cross-city model: single combined model trained across all five cities achieves R² = 0.784, RMSE = 0.699 — demonstrating feasibility of a global urban-foundation model for building energy.
  • Feature ablation and baseline benchmarking: ablation study comparing machine-learning baselines (relying on building form: height, footprint area, volume) versus deep-learning models (flexible, satellite-imagery-driven); spatial data-leakage check via k-NN coordinate regression.
  • Socio-economic analysis: Pearson correlation of predicted per capita emissions vs. real GDP per capita deciles; LCZ Shannon entropy computation; spatial aggregation at 250 m (emissions maps) and 1 km (GDP vs. emissions) grids.

① Extend the framework's cross-city model toward a global urban foundation model with uncertainty quantification

Extending this paperThe paper's cross-city GraphSAGE achieves R² = 0.784 but the authors caution that the model requires >2x the training epochs of city-specific models and does not yet exceed city-specific performance. The natural extension is a pretrain-then-finetune paradigm: pre-train the heterogeneous GNN across available benchmarking cities (and newly mandated ones like Boston, Montreal, Berlin), then fine-tune with minimal labelled examples for data-scarce cities. Coupling this with conformal prediction or Monte Carlo dropout for spatial uncertainty maps would produce calibrated carbon-emission probability bands — critical for cities where benchmarking coverage is partial and model bias could misdirect retrofitting investment.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly to my interest in geospatial foundation models and multi-source data fusion. Operationally, the pretrain-finetune pipeline is identical in structure to fine-tuning geospatial foundation models I work with — the building-energy GNN becomes a testbed for transfer learning across urban spatial domains. Uncertainty maps also align with my Monte Carlo methods for spatial prediction, giving me a concrete application where calibrated uncertainty guides policy rather than just reporting accuracy.

② Couple building-carbon heterogeneity with spatially explicit health-impact pathways

Extending this paperThe paper maps per-building operating carbon and links it to GDP per capita, but stops short of tracing the downstream health burden. Fossil-fuel combustion in buildings produces local air pollutants (PM2.5, NO₂, VOCs) whose concentrations are spatially heterogeneous and disproportionately affect low-income communities. Extending this paper: overlay the 250 m building-carbon maps with airborne pollutant dispersion models and census health outcome data to quantify the co-benefits (or co-harms) of retrofit strategies by income decile. The paper's GDP-emission nexus already provides the spatial engine; adding a health-impact pathway converts a carbon-efficiency story into a public-health equity story.

Added value to my researchThis directly connects to my health-geography agenda (spatial access to care, health equity). A building-carbon-to-health pathway gives me a novel upstream determinant of urban health disparities: neighborhoods with high building emissions and low GDP face a double burden — high retrofit cost and high pollution exposure. Combined with my 2SFCA accessibility models for primary-care facilities, I can identify areas where poor air quality and poor care access co-occur, informing joint planning targets.

③ Encode the paper's Local Climate Zone entropy as a physics-informed graph prior for spatiotemporal energy forecasting

Extending this paperThe paper uses LCZ Shannon entropy as a tabular feature fed to the GNN, but the physical meaning of LCZ — radiative exchange, albedo, anthropogenic heat flux — is richer than a single scalar. Extending it: replace the scalar LCZ-entropy node feature with a physics-informed edge weight derived from the LCZ-specific thermal exchange coefficients, so the GNN's message-passing physically encodes the heat-transfer pathway between adjacent LCZ patches. This turns the graph from a topological structure to a thermodynamically grounded representation, improving performance in cities where LCZ diversity is the dominant driver of emissions variance (Melbourne, Seattle).

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my physics-informed graph learning agenda. Embedding physical priors (thermodynamic exchange laws) as learnable edge weights — rather than post-hoc features — is the canonical physics-GNN design pattern I seek to operationalize in urban contexts. The LCZ-thermal prior provides a concrete, well-defined physical constraint, making it an ideal proof-of-concept for spatially grounded, interpretable GeoAI.

④ Transform the building-scale carbon map into an equitable retrofitting prioritization tool

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates stark emissions inequity — lower-GDP areas bear a disproportionate share of total city emissions, largely from institutional and civic buildings. Yet it does not translate this finding into a prioritized retrofitting allocation. Extending it: frame the retrofitting problem as a multi-objective facility-location optimization — maximizing emissions reduction per dollar of investment while minimizing the economic burden on low-income communities (measured by the GDP-emission overlap maps in Fig. 5b). The paper's per-building carbon predictions serve as the objective function; the optimal retrofit portfolio becomes a spatially explicit policy output the paper currently lacks.

⑤ Use the paper's open multi-modal pipeline to audit global OpenStreetMap building-data completeness for carbon modelling

Extending this paperThe paper's framework depends critically on OSM building footprint completeness and attribute quality, yet OSM coverage is highly uneven globally — richer in Global North cities, sparser in the Global South. Extending the paper: run the framework's OSM-dependent feature extraction across a global sample of cities, overlay with an OSM-completeness model, and quantify how building-data gaps translate into carbon-estimation uncertainty. This would produce the first carbon-modelling-specific OSM-quality audit, identifying where open crowdsourced alternatives (Microsoft/Google building footprints) could substitute to reduce bias before the model even trains.

Added value to my researchThis connects to my data-science and reproducibility theme. Quantifying how data-quality heterogeneity propagates into model uncertainty is a core FAIR-data concern — and the paper's multi-city, open-source architecture makes it the right instrument for such an audit. For my GeoAI pipeline, a systematic audit of input-data completeness is prerequisite to deploying any spatially transferable model responsibly, whether for building energy, health-facility access or any other spatially sparse outcome.
