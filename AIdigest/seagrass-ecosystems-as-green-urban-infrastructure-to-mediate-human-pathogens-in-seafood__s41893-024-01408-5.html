Seagrass ecosystems as green urban infrastructure to mediate human pathogens in seafood

Phoebe D. Dawkins · Evan A. Fiorenza · Jeffrey L. Gaeckle · Jennifer A. Lanksbury · Jeroen A. J. M. van de Water · William E. Feeney · C. Drew Harvell · Joleah B. Lamb Published 2024-08-02 DOI 10.1038/s41893-024-01408-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Coastal seagrass meadows function as natural sanitation infrastructure: marine mussels deployed at urban locations with seagrass present showed a 65% lower relative abundance of human bacterial pathogens than locations without seagrass. More than one-third of global urban agglomerations — currently home to 1.1 billion people, projected to reach 1.3 billion by 2030 — lie within 50 km of a seagrass ecosystem, underscoring the global opportunity to integrate seagrass conservation into urban food-safety and public-health strategies.

Why It Matters

  • First empirical evidence that seagrass filtration benefits food fish. Prior work showed seagrasses reduce waterborne pathogens; this study closes the gap to the seafood supply chain, demonstrating the service is conferred to a globally important protein source — bivalves.
  • Quantifies an overlooked public-health ecosystem service at scale. The 65% pathogen reduction, combined with estimates of 24 million avoided gastrointestinal illnesses per year globally, reframes seagrass as a cost-effective health intervention alongside conventional water treatment.
  • Links biodiversity conservation to urban food security. By showing that 662 of 1,860 major urban agglomerations (>300,000 people) border seagrass habitat, the paper makes the conservation case in economic and public-health terms, not just ecological ones.
  • Bridges marine and terrestrial urban-greening policy. Extending terrestrial urban greening networks to coastal ecosystems creates new governance opportunities consistent with global targets (SDGs, CBD biodiversity framework).
  • Urgency signal from threatened ecosystems. With seagrass loss rates estimated up to 7% per year globally, the study's finding implies a rapidly vanishing natural sanitation buffer, making conservation economically urgent.

Core Methods

  • Gradient-classified field design: 20 urban locations selected using a classified gradient approach integrating population density (NASA SEDAC GPW), NOAA land cover, and watershed administrative units — 11 with seagrass, 9 without.
  • Mussel bioindicator deployment: Mytilus trossulus deployed in-situ for 90 days as passive bioindicators of local bacterial contamination, then collected for DNA extraction from gill tissues.
  • 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing: 1.7 million bacterial sequences from 60 samples characterized bacterial community composition and diversity; taxonomy assigned via the All Species Living Tree Project (LTP) database.
  • Statistical analysis: permutational multivariate analysis of variance (perMANOVA) to test community-level differences; generalized linear models to quantify pathogen relative abundance differences (estimate = −1.06, SE = 0.25, z = 4.17, P < 0.001); 30 putative pathogenic species identified and individually scored.
  • Spatial overlay modeling: 50 km buffers drawn around global seagrass ranges (IUCN); urban agglomerations intersected to count populations within seagrass proximity; UN WUP projections applied to estimate 2030 exposure.
  • Principal coordinate (PCo) analysis: ordination of full bacterial community to visualize compositional differences between seagrass-present and seagrass-absent sites.

① Map seagrass sanitation service as a spatially explicit accessibility layer for coastal urban health

Extending this paperThe paper applies a uniform 50 km buffer around all seagrass polygons, treating proximity as binary. A direct extension would replace this flat buffer with a spatially variable accessibility surface: model how pathogen reduction decays with distance from the seagrass edge, accounting for water circulation, tidal flushing, and seafood harvest locations. Integrating the paper's 65% reduction estimate with distance-decay functions would yield a continuous "seagrass sanitation catchment" — identifying coastal neighborhoods that currently receive the service and those just outside range where restoration would have the largest marginal public-health gain.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly onto my 2SFCA/R2SFCA spatial accessibility toolkit: the seagrass meadow acts as a "supply" node and the seafood-consuming coastal population acts as "demand." Replacing the fixed 50 km radius with an empirically calibrated distance-decay kernel — learned from the paper's own field data across a 200 km gradient — gives a demand-weighted seagrass-to-population accessibility score analogous to healthcare facility catchments. Such a framework could identify high-priority restoration sites using the same facility-location optimization logic I apply to EV charging and primary care access.

② Link seagrass-mediated pathogen reduction to antibiotic-resistance dynamics in coastal urban wetlands

Extending this paperThe paper focuses on relative abundance of 30 putative pathogenic bacterial species but does not interrogate antimicrobial resistance (AMR) gene carriage. Given that coastal urban runoff is a major driver of environmental AMR spread, and that the paper's own framing cites drug-resistance costs of $100 trillion globally, a compelling extension would sequence resistome profiles (shotgun metagenomics or resistance gene arrays) alongside the 16S community data. This would test whether seagrass presence suppresses not only pathogen abundance but also the reservoir of resistance genes transferred to seafood — directly addressing the AMR dimension the paper invokes but cannot currently substantiate.

Added value to my researchAn AMR-extended version of this study would generate spatially indexed microbiome + resistome data across urban gradients — precisely the multi-source data fusion challenge I work with. Applying spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and MGWR to resistome hotspots relative to seagrass coverage and population density would produce an explainable spatial model of AMR risk, connecting ecosystem services to a health geography outcome measurable at city scale.

③ Prioritize seagrass restoration sites using a multi-objective urban green infrastructure optimizer

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates the service but offers no framework for deciding where to restore or protect seagrass to maximize urban health benefit given limited conservation budgets. A natural extension is to cast restoration site selection as a multi-objective optimization problem: maximize population health benefit (estimated pathogen load reduction × population at risk within 50 km), minimize restoration cost (a function of degradation state and area), and maintain biodiversity co-benefits. The paper's 662 seagrass-adjacent urban agglomerations provide the candidate universe; integrating bivalve harvest intensity from FAO data adds a food-supply weighting layer.

Added value to my researchThis extends my facility-location and EV charging efficiency-vs-equity optimization directly to nature-based infrastructure. The "supply" is restored seagrass meadow area; the "demand" is coastal urban population with seafood consumption; the equity constraint ensures that low-income coastal communities — often disproportionately reliant on local seafood — are not deprioritized. An explainable ML model trained on the paper's and corpus data could predict the pathogen-reduction yield of candidate restoration polygons, enabling data-driven triage of conservation investment.

④ Build a GeoAI model to predict coastal pathogen load from remote sensing of seagrass cover and urban runoff

The paper's 1.7 million sequences across 20 sites provide a training signal; transfer learning to other coastal urban regions would expand scope dramatically.

⑤ Extend the paper's global population exposure model to project health outcomes under combined urbanization and seagrass loss scenarios

Extending this paperThe paper projects urban population within 50 km of seagrass by 2030 assuming seagrass extent remains at the 2019 IUCN baseline. But seagrass is declining at up to 7% per year. A richer scenario analysis would combine UN urbanization trajectories with seagrass loss projections (from remote sensing trend data or process models) to produce a compound exposure surface: for each decade, how many urban residents are within 50 km of surviving seagrass? This exposes the rapidly closing window for conservation to avert gastrointestinal disease burden, and provides quantitative inputs for economic damage assessments.

Added value to my researchThis scenario-modeling approach mirrors my SIR-based and Monte Carlo methods applied to infectious disease spatial dynamics. The output, a time-series of pathogen-exposed coastal population, would be a compelling policy-communication artifact and a natural bridge between my health-geography accessibility work and nature-based infrastructure valuation.
