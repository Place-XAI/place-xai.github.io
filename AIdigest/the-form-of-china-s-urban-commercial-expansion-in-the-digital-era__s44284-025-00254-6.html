The form of China's urban commercial expansion in the digital era

Enjia Zhang · Jingxuan Hou · Ying Long Published 2025-06-11 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00254-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

In the digital era, China's brick-and-mortar commerce is becoming less visible: shops are increasingly migrating to non-street-facing positions inside blocks and to non-ground floors of buildings. Across 287 cities (2015–2023) the share of these "hidden" establishments rose steadily and accelerated after COVID-19, even as the total shop count declined. The shift is strongest in experiential sectors (entertainment, exercise, beauty) and in higher socioeconomic-tier cities, which led the transformation earlier and more intensely — signaling that physical retail no longer needs a storefront window when discovery happens on review-and-rating platforms.

★

Why It Matters

  • Documents a structural break in retail geography. The classic premium on the ground-floor, street-facing storefront — a foundational assumption of retail location theory — is eroding because online platforms, not the street, now drive customer discovery.
  • Quantifies the "hidden city" at national scale. By measuring horizontal (within-block) and vertical (across-floor) expansion across 287 cities over nearly a decade, it turns an anecdotal trend into a measured, comparable national signal.
  • Reveals a COVID accelerant. The proportion of non-street-facing and non-ground-floor shops grew faster post-pandemic despite an overall decline in shop numbers — a compositional shift, not just contraction.
  • Has direct planning consequences. Mixed-use zoning, ground-floor retail mandates, fire-safety and signage codes, and street-vitality policies were all written for a street-facing world; the findings argue for adaptive governance of vertical and interior commerce.

Core Methods

  • Four-step analytical framework (Fig. 6): harvest establishment records → geolocate against rooftop/footprint geometry → classify each shop's horizontal (street-facing vs. interior) and vertical (ground vs. non-ground) position → aggregate trends over time, category and city tier.
  • Horizontal (non-street-facing) identification: spatial overlay of shop locations on building rooftop polygons to determine whether an establishment fronts a street or sits inside the block, with a tunable proximity threshold (sensitivity tested in Extended Data Fig. 4).
  • Vertical (non-ground-floor) identification: a conservative primary method imputing missing floor data as ground floor (all records retained), validated against a strict subset using only shops with explicit floor indicators (Extended Data Fig. 6).
  • Dataset robustness: rooftop-based identification compared against Gaode footprint data via count/proportion distributions and kernel density estimation across a 120-city subset (Extended Data Fig. 5).
  • Stratified trend analysis: proportions decomposed by establishment category (e.g., experiential vs. other sectors) and by city socioeconomic tier, comparing 2015 / 2019 / 2023 to isolate the post-COVID acceleration.

① Fuse the platform record with satellite building-function inference

Extending this paperThe paper's horizontal/vertical classification rests on overlaying Dazhong-Dianping points onto rooftop polygons — but floor-level data are often missing (hence the conservative imputation in Extended Data Fig. 6) and a rooftop says nothing about which floors are commercial. A direct extension: fuse the review-platform records with satellite-derived per-building function and built volume, so the framework infers the commercial floor-share of each structure instead of imputing it, sharpening the vertical-expansion signal and extending the analysis to shops that never appear on the platform.

Added value to my researchThis multi-source fusion (review POIs + remote-sensing building function) is exactly my data-fusion line: a building-resolved commercial layer becomes a far richer supply surface for 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility than a flat POI list.

② Validate "hidden" shops against realized human visitation

Extending this paperThe paper measures where shops are, but a platform listing does not prove a shop is alive or visited; non-street-facing experiential venues may attract very different footfall dynamics. Extending it with mobility/visitation data would test whether interior, upper-floor shops actually capture demand — distinguishing genuine reorganization of commerce from a measurement artifact of platform coverage.

Added value to my researchPairing supply position with visitation flows is the demand side of my accessibility work: it lets distance-decay and catchment functions in R2SFCA be calibrated to observed behavior rather than assumed proximity, and feeds my spatiotemporal KDE of service use.

③ Re-read the COVID acceleration as street-network resilience

Extending this paperThe paper notes a post-COVID jump in hidden-shop share but treats COVID as an exogenous shock. Extending it: model the pandemic as a stress test on the commercial street network and ask which streets / blocks shed street-facing shops and recovered as interior venues — turning the cross-sectional 2015/2019/2023 snapshots into a resilience-and-recovery trajectory at street granularity.

④ Recompute walkable-city accessibility with vertical & interior shops

Extending this paperIf commerce is migrating off the street and up the building, the standard "15-minute city" accounting — which implicitly counts street-facing, ground-floor amenities — systematically mis-measures real proximity. Extending this paper: feed its horizontal/vertical classification into a walkability/accessibility model so that vertical access cost (finding and reaching an upper-floor venue) is priced into the proximity score.

Extending it: model hidden-shop share as a function of city/block covariates with explainable machine learning (or MGWR for spatial non-stationarity), turning the tier ranking into attributed, spatially varying causes.
