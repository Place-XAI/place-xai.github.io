Reinforcement learning in artificial and biological systems

Emre O. Neftci · Bruno B. Averbeck Published 2019-03-04 DOI 10.1038/s42256-019-0025-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Biological and artificial reinforcement learning (RL) systems have co-evolved through mutual inspiration but remain fundamentally mismatched: biological agents excel at multi-timescale, on-line learning in dynamic environments while artificial agents excel at single-task mastery in static environments requiring massive training data. Bridging the two — by importing biological principles such as multiple parallel learning systems, hierarchical decomposition, and spike-based local learning into artificial agents, and applying artificial RL formalisms such as temporal-difference learning and hierarchical RL to understand the brain — is the most productive frontier for both neuroscience and machine intelligence.

Why It Matters

  • A unified conceptual map across two fields. This review is a comprehensive synthesis charting how RL ideas flow bidirectionally between neuroscience and AI — from the Rescorla–Wagner rule and Pavlovian conditioning to deep Q-networks and temporal-difference dopamine coding.
  • Names the core gap that limits both fields. Biological agents are data-efficient but handle only simple tasks; artificial agents handle complex tasks but are data-hungry and inflexible. Articulating this gap as a research programme gives the field a concrete agenda rather than accepting it as a fixed limitation.
  • Connects hardware to biology. The review frames neuromorphic engineering (Intel Loihi, IBM TrueNorth) not as a curiosity but as the natural platform for biologically-plausible, energy-efficient RL — bridging computational neuroscience to real-world deployment on mobile and embedded platforms.
  • Identifies tractable interaction points. Experience replay (hippocampus), mixture-of-experts (amygdala/striatum), hierarchical RL (prefrontal gradient), and local learning rules (DCLL) are each shown to have concrete neural analogues, providing a roadmap for cross-disciplinary transfer rather than vague metaphor.

Core Methods

  • Theoretical synthesis and comparative analysis: the authors organize RL into a common agent–environment framework (Fig. 1) applied symmetrically to biological and artificial systems, enabling principled comparison of learning rules, timescales, and architectures.
  • Model-free vs. model-based RL taxonomy: uses the Rescorla–Wagner / TD / Bellman hierarchy to classify biological findings — mapping amygdala to fast model-free and prefrontal inference to model-based — and compares data efficiency across the hierarchy.
  • Multiple-timescale learning analysis: characterizes how biological systems (amygdala fast / striatum slow, spike-timing-dependent plasticity windows of 1–100 ms, LSTM clock rates) implement multiple learning rates — a principle also critical for continual artificial learning.
  • Catastrophic interference framing: identifies correlated, non-i.i.d. experience as the core challenge for on-line RL, and reviews complementary learning systems (hippocampal replay, synaptic intelligence, memory-aware synapses) as biological and artificial solutions.
  • Hierarchical RL review: surveys options framework, feudal networks, option-critic, and DCLL auxiliary targets, linking each to prefrontal gradient evidence in primates — framing HRL as the key bridge to complex sequential behaviour.
  • Neuromorphic engineering assessment: evaluates spike-based hardware as the physically-plausible substrate for bio-inspired RL, addressing device noise, weight quantization (<8-bit precision), and energy-efficiency constraints.

① Operationalize multi-timescale RL for adaptive spatial accessibility estimation

Extending this paperThe paper identifies multi-timescale learning as the brain's key advantage — fast amygdala and slow striatal systems in parallel — but stops short of translating this into a concrete algorithm for artificial agents operating in spatially structured environments. A direct extension: implement a dual-rate Q-network (fast head tracking short-term demand shifts, slow head encoding structural accessibility patterns) trained on dynamic urban mobility data, testing whether the biological design principle yields measurable data-efficiency gains over standard deep RL on real route-planning tasks that change at diurnal and seasonal timescales.

Added value to my researchMy GeoAI work on spatiotemporal accessibility suffers from the same static-environment assumption the paper critiques. Casting R2SFCA catchment delineation as a dual-rate RL problem — where slow weights encode baseline spatial structure and fast weights track ephemeral demand spikes (e.g., pandemic surges at primary care clinics) — would yield a principled, biologically-motivated adaptive accessibility estimator for public health policy without requiring model retraining at every timestep.

② Ground dopamine reward-prediction error in a spatial value function for urban environments

Extending this paperThe paper's core biological model encodes value in frontal-striatal synapses updated via dopamine RPEs. Extending this paper: map the RPE signal onto a spatially explicit value surface — where states are geographic locations and actions are travel-mode choices — and test whether temporal-difference RL trained on GPS mobility traces can recover a reward landscape that predicts revealed preferences for destinations (parks, clinics, transit stops) without explicit labeling. This would operationalize the paper's TD-RL framework on real-world spatial trajectory data.

Added value to my researchRevealed-preference destination modelling is central to my health-geography work. A TD-RPE-derived spatial value surface trained on mobile-phone trajectory data would replace ad-hoc gravity decay functions in my accessibility models with a learned, behaviorally-calibrated reward landscape — directly improving the R2SFCA distance-decay specification without requiring costly travel surveys or assumed functional forms.

③ Import experience-replay and complementary learning systems to non-stationary geospatial AI

Extending this paperThe paper reviews two biological solutions to catastrophic forgetting — hippocampal experience replay and synapse-selective learning rate stiffening — arising precisely because RL data are correlated and non-i.i.d. Urban geospatial datasets are exactly this: gentrification, infrastructure investment, and disease outbreaks introduce sequential, correlated shifts. Extending the paper: implement and compare biological-memory-inspired rehearsal strategies (prioritized replay of historically anomalous neighborhoods vs. elastic-weight consolidation of stable spatial features) in a continual GeoAI model trained on multi-year urban panel data.

Added value to my researchMy multi-source data fusion work (mobile phone + POI + census) faces catastrophic forgetting when fine-tuning geospatial foundation models on new cities. The paper's framing directly motivates a complementary learning system for GeoAI transfer: a slow "hippocampal" component retaining cross-city priors and a fast "striatal" head adapting to city-specific patterns — reducing the labeled data required for domain adaptation of spatial prediction models and strengthening reproducibility.

④ Apply hierarchical RL sub-goal decomposition to multi-period facility siting

Extending this paperThe paper argues that hierarchical RL (options, feudal networks) is the missing bridge to complex, multi-step behaviour — the kind biological agents solve routinely. HRL gains efficiency by decomposing a long-horizon task into sub-goals that simplify credit assignment. A direct extension: reformulate multi-period infrastructure siting (e.g., EV charging rollout across city districts over years) as a hierarchical MDP where the high-level manager sets coverage and equity targets per district and the low-level worker selects individual site locations — exploiting the paper's insight that meaningful sub-goals eliminate credit assignment across hundreds of planning timesteps.

Added value to my researchMy EV charging efficiency-vs-equity optimization currently treats siting as a one-shot combinatorial problem. Recasting it as hierarchical RL with a fairness-aware manager — where the high-level policy enforces equity constraints on district-level coverage while the low-level policy maximizes throughput — would enable dynamic re-optimization as demand patterns shift, directly addressing the temporal inflexibility and scalability limits of current approaches.

⑤ Translate the distributional striatal coding insight into uncertainty-aware spatial health prediction

Extending this paperThe paper's dopamine/striatum model codes scalar expected rewards; subsequent neuroscience has extended this to distributional RL coding (opponent D1/D2 pathways representing full return distributions, not just means). Extending this paper: implement distributional RL (C51 or QR-DQN) on spatial prediction tasks where the output is a full predictive distribution over outcomes — matching the brain's known representation of outcome uncertainty — and evaluate whether this improves calibration and equity fairness in risk prediction compared to point-estimate models.

Added value to my researchUncertainty quantification is a recognized gap in my explainable ML for spatial health risk prediction (telehealth access disparities, mental health facility demand). A distributional RL head grafted onto a spatial GNN would produce calibrated uncertainty intervals over health outcome predictions — enabling policy makers to see not just the expected risk but the full range of plausible futures, improving trustworthiness and supporting equity-informed resource allocation in GeoAI-driven health geography models.
