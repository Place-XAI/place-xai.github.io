Surveillance camera prevalence and racial diversity in ten US cities

Nima Dahir · Hao Sheng · Keniel Yao · Sharad Goel · Jackelyn Hwang Published 2025-07-02 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00274-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using computer vision plus human verification on Google Street View images across ten densely populated US cities, the study finds that — contrary to theories predicting heaviest surveillance in predominantly Black neighborhoods — outdoor surveillance cameras are most prevalent where racial diversity is highest. Over time, rising diversity driven by white in-movers is associated with more cameras, and the pattern persists after adjusting for crime rates. The authors interpret this as surveillance being deployed to monitor and control minority groups in diverse and diversifying neighborhoods, potentially reinforcing spatial inequality and eroding social trust.

★

Why It Matters

  • Overturns a long-standing assumption. Social-control theory long predicted the densest surveillance in predominantly Black neighborhoods; the data instead point to racially diverse and diversifying areas — reframing how scholars think about the geography of monitoring.
  • Connects surveillance to neighborhood change. Linking camera growth to white in-movement ties physical surveillance infrastructure to gentrification and demographic turnover, not just to static composition.
  • Survives the obvious confounder. The diversity–camera association holds net of crime rates, undercutting a purely safety-driven explanation and foregrounding social-control dynamics.
  • Demonstrates a scalable measurement pipeline. Treating street imagery as a sensor for the built environment shows how computer vision can quantify hard-to-survey features of urban inequality at city scale.

Core Methods

  • Computer-vision camera detection: an object-detection model identifies surveillance cameras in Google Street View images, followed by human verification to control false positives, yielding a per-block-group camera identification rate.
  • Bivariate description (Fig. 1): relate the camera rate to share Black, share white, and neighborhood diversity across all cities in 2015.
  • Multivariable regression (Fig. 2): a sequence of models estimating the camera identification rate while progressively adding controls — crucially net of crime rates and other covariates.
  • Over-time / change analysis (Fig. 3): model the expected change in camera rate as a function of changes in diversity and racial composition, isolating the role of white in-movement in diversifying neighborhoods.
  • Interpretive framing: contrast results against social-control theory to argue for surveillance as a tool of intra-neighborhood monitoring of minority groups.

① From static composition to experienced, mobility-weighted exposure to surveillance

Extending this paperThe paper measures cameras against residential block-group composition, but surveillance is experienced where people move, not only where they live. Extending it: overlay the camera identification surface with GPS-based mobility flows to compute who is actually exposed to cameras across their daily activity space — turning a residential map into an experienced-surveillance measure, the surveillance analogue of experienced segregation.

② Make the camera–diversity model explainable and spatially non-stationary

Extending this paperThe paper's regressions estimate a largely global diversity effect on cameras. The mechanism is likely to vary block-by-block (downtown vs. residential, commercial frontage, recent turnover). Extending it: fit MGWR or an explainable ML model (gradient boosting + SHAP) to map where diversity, white in-movement, and crime each drive camera prevalence, exposing local heterogeneity behind the headline coefficient.
