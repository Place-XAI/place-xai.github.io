Urbanization exacerbated the rainfall and flooding caused by hurricane Harvey in Houston

Wei Zhang · Gabriele Villarini · Gabriel A. Vecchi · James A. Smith Published 2018-11-14 DOI 10.1038/s41586-018-0676-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Urbanization did not merely amplify flooding from hurricane Harvey — it also intensified the storm total rainfall itself. Using WRF numerical weather simulations with and without urban land use, plus nonstationary statistical models fitted to streamflow records across five Houston-area watersheds, the authors find that urbanization increased the probability of extreme flood events by roughly 21 times during 25–30 August 2017. This dual amplification — of both precipitation and peak discharge — underscores the need to incorporate urban land-use dynamics explicitly into global climate models and future flood-risk assessments for coastal megacities.

★

Why It Matters

  • Separates urbanization from climate change as a driver. The dominant narrative after Harvey focused on anthropogenic warming; this study isolates the urbanization signal and quantifies it independently — a crucial distinction for local adaptation policy.
  • Dual amplification finding. Showing that urbanization increases both rainfall totals and flood discharge (not just runoff given rainfall) is a mechanistic advance: impervious cover alters boundary layer dynamics and thus storm-feeding moisture fluxes.
  • 21× increase in flood probability. A concrete, statistically defensible multiplier transforms the analysis from qualitative attribution to risk quantification relevant to insurance, infrastructure design, and emergency management.
  • General lesson for coastal megacities. Houston is the exemplar for a global phenomenon — rapidly urbanizing low-lying cities exposed to tropical cyclones — so findings transfer to Jakarta, Lagos, Mumbai, and similar settings.

Core Methods

  • WRF ensemble simulation: two parallel ensembles — Urban BEM (Building Energy Model active) and NoUrban (urban pixels replaced by cropland) — initialized at 6-hour intervals (23–24 Aug 2017) to generate 7 members each. Ensemble spread quantifies uncertainty; differences between ensembles isolate the urbanization signal.
  • Urban canopy parameterization: the Building Energy Model (BEM) within WRF explicitly represents urban morphology effects on surface energy balance, boundary-layer heat flux, roughness length, Bowen ratio, and boundary-layer height — diagnosing the physical mechanism linking urban cover to rainfall enhancement.
  • Nonstationary flood-frequency analysis: annual maximum peak discharge records from 5 Houston-area USGS gauges are fitted with a nonstationary GEV/GAMLSS model in which the location parameter is a function of impervious surface fraction over time, allowing the flood-probability distribution to evolve with urbanization.
  • Counterfactual design: pairing the Urban BEM simulations with the NoUrban counterfactual, and comparing pre-urban vs. post-urban statistical distributions, enables a clean attribution of rainfall and flood-frequency changes to urbanization alone.
  • Statistical significance testing: differences in accumulated precipitation between ensembles tested with Student's t-test at the P = 0.05 significance level; worm plots used to evaluate goodness of fit for the statistical models.
