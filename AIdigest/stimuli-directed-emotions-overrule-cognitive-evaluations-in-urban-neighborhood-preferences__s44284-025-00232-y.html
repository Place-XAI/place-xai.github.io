Stimuli-directed emotions overrule cognitive evaluations in urban neighborhood preferences

Marcelo Galleguillos-Torres · Adrienne Grêt-Regamey Published 2025-04-30 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00232-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using immersive 360° virtual-reality experiments with 269 participants in Switzerland and the Netherlands, the study shows that stimuli-directed emotions (self-reported valence/arousal plus electrodermal physiological signals) override the cognitive evaluation of neighborhood characteristics in shaping residential preference. Peri-urban neighborhoods were the least preferred (12.8%) versus urban (42.1%) and rural (45.1%), and — counter to expectations — the cognitive rating of neighborhood traits did not directly drive preference; instead, emotional valence and personal experience with a place type did. Planning should therefore align housing options with people's emotional bonds, not just functional attributes.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reframes what drives residential choice. Decades of preference research emphasized cognitive/functional attributes (aesthetics, walkability, accessibility, affordability); this work shows emotion supersedes cognition in the preference step — a fundamental reordering of the causal story.
  • Explains the peri-urban paradox. People keep migrating to peri-urban areas that scored highest on measured characteristics yet were least preferred (12.8%), suggesting choice is constrained by economics/practicality while preference is emotional — a wedge planners can act on.
  • Brings physiology into urban science. By pairing the self-assessment manikin (SAM) with electrodermal activity (EDA), it captures both conscious and non-conscious emotional responses to neighborhoods, a methodology urban studies has largely lacked.
  • Actionable for participation and health. Points to mental mapping, psychogeography and "digital emotional urban tagging" as tools to fold quantified emotions into citizen participation and urban-health promotion.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Participants
n = 269 recruited in person at shopping-mall parking areas and busy squares in two case-study regions; a few additional people started but did not finish. Sample rated "fair to good" by the authors. Slight overrepresentation of males; sample slightly older and more educated than the regional averages but judged broadly representative.
Case studies
Two Western-European small/medium city regions: Olten–Aarau (Switzerland) and Utrecht (Netherlands). Participants only viewed scenes from their own country.
Stimuli
12 immersive 360° stimuli total — 3 neighborhood types (urban / rural / peri-urban) × 2 sets × 2 countries; each participant saw 3 (one of each type, pseudo-randomized). 360° images at 18 MP (6,080 × 3,040), recorded with an Insta360 ONE X plus ambisonic Sennheiser AMBEO audio, captured in early autumn (September).
Measures
Conscious emotion via SAM (valence + arousal); non-conscious arousal via EDA (phasic + tonic, sampled every 10 s over 2-min exposures); a questionnaire with 15 polar adjectives for neighborhood character plus sociodemographics; a forced 3-way preference choice.
Hardware / software
HTC Vive Pro goggles + controller in a mobile VR lab (adapted Renault Trafic van); scenes built in Unity v2018.3.3f1; images edited in Affinity Photo v1.10; survey in SoSci Survey; analysis in R (lavaan, semPlot, nnet).

Core Methods

  • Emotion-imbued choice model: adapts Lerner et al.'s framework into an emotion-imbued neighborhood preference model with six testable hypotheses (H1–H6) linking participant characteristics, cognitive evaluation, stimuli-directed emotions and preference.
  • Immersive 360° VR protocol: 4-min relaxation/adaptation period, neutral tree-canopy "reset" image (IAPS) before each scene, then 2-min exposures to urban/rural/peri-urban neighborhoods with SAM ratings; ~17 min total, seated, pseudo-randomized order.
  • Dual emotion measurement: conscious valence/arousal via SAM; non-conscious arousal via EDA, decomposed into phasic and tonic components with slope features estimated by regression over the exposure.
  • Structural equation modeling (SEM): three separate models (one per neighborhood type) built in R lavaan; a 15-adjective measurement model reduced to 9 polar adjectives loading one latent "neighborhood characteristics" factor (loading cutoff 0.6); fit judged by CFI > 0.9, SRMR/RMSEA ≤ 0.08.
  • Multinomial logistic regression: R nnet::multinom() with predicted probabilities to compare preference shares across the three neighborhood types and the two countries; chi-squared tests against the 33.3/33.3/33.3% null.
  • Settlement morphometrics: building shape index, area and elongation plus homogeneity and pixel-count (built/green/sky) metrics in a 500-m buffer to objectively differentiate the three neighborhood types.

Expanded Ideas — this paper's extensibility & added value to my research

Each idea stays anchored to this paper: first how the paper itself could be extended (drawing on our corpus), then the added value for my research lines (migis.net — GeoAI, health geography & accessibility, explainable & physics-informed spatial learning, multi-source data fusion).

① Scale 12 hand-built stimuli to street-view-driven emotion at city scale

Extending this paperThe paper's strongest limitation is breadth: just 12 immersive 360° stimuli across 3 archetypal neighborhood types cannot span real urban form. A direct extension: pair the same SAM+EDA emotional ground truth with large-scale street-view imagery to train a model that predicts stimuli-directed emotional valence for any location, turning a 269-person lab experiment into a city-wide emotional accessibility surface.

Added value to my researchThis is multi-source data fusion in my wheelhouse — combining street-view, POIs and remote sensing to build an emotion-weighted layer that I can feed into healthy-city and mental-health accessibility analyses, where "how a place feels" complements "how reachable it is."

② Replace SEM with explainable ML to expose emotion→preference nonlinearity

Extending this paperThe paper uses linear SEM + multinomial logit, which assumes additive, mostly linear pathways and reduces 15 adjectives to a single latent factor. Re-running the same data through explainable gradient-boosting / SHAP would test whether the emotion-overrules-cognition finding survives interaction effects and thresholds, and reveal which physiological features (phasic vs tonic EDA slopes) matter most — strengthening or qualifying the causal claim.

Added value to my researchDirectly feeds my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction line: an emotional-response model with SHAP attributions becomes a transparent input for service-siting and accessibility scoring, where I already use ML ensembles and want interpretable feature importance.

Cross-ref (corpus): A psychophysical measurement on subjective well-being and air pollution — 10.1038/s41467-019-13459-w

③ Add a spatial-accessibility covariate the experiment deliberately suppressed

Extending this paperThe authors intentionally framed preference around aesthetics, suppressing functionality, economics and social factors — yet they note peri-urban areas suffer car dependence and poor access. Extending the model with an explicit 2SFCA/accessibility covariate (to schools, clinics, green space) would let the SEM separate emotional valence from realized accessibility, testing whether emotion still dominates once access is controlled.

Added value to my researchThis couples emotion with my R2SFCA accessibility work: I can ask whether low-access peri-urban neighborhoods are emotionally penalized because of poor service catchments, linking affective response to the spatial-accessibility metrics I compute for primary care and mental health.

Cross-ref (corpus): A universal framework for inclusive 15-minute cities — 10.1038/s44284-024-00119-4

④ Connect neighborhood physiology to longer-run brain/behavior and health outcomes

Extending this paperThe paper captures momentary physiological responses (2-min EDA) but stops at stated preference. A longitudinal extension would link the same VR-elicited emotional signatures to downstream mental-health and behavioral outcomes, testing whether the neighborhoods that feel worse in VR predict real stress, reduced civic engagement or worse well-being over time.

Added value to my researchThis bridges affect to my health-geography agenda: an emotion-derived environmental exposure becomes a candidate predictor in spatiotemporal mental-health models, complementing telehealth/digital-divide and care-accessibility work with a psychophysiological exposure dimension.

⑤ Turn emotional preference into an equity-aware planning objective

Extending this paperThe paper argues planning should "align housing options with individuals' emotions," but offers no allocation mechanism. Extending it: embed the emotion-overrules-cognition result into a nature-inclusive / well-being optimization that distributes emotionally restorative features (greenery, diversity, sense of place) across neighborhoods while monitoring fairness, so emotional uplift does not become green-gentrification.

Added value to my researchThis generalizes my efficiency-vs-equity facility-optimization framing (as in EV charging) to an emotion/well-being objective, and supports agent-based modeling of how emotion-driven preference reshapes job-housing and migration patterns.
