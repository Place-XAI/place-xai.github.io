Arousal as a universal embedding for spatiotemporal brain dynamics

Ryan V. Raut · Zachary P. Rosenthal · Xiaodan Wang · Hanyang Miao · Zhanqi Zhang · Jin-Moo Lee · Marcus E. Raichle · Adam Q. Bauer · Steven L. Brunton · Bingni W. Brunton · J. Nathan Kutz Published 2025-09-24 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09544-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A single scalar measurement of arousal — pupil diameter — suffices to reconstruct the continuous, high-dimensional spatiotemporal dynamics of cortex-wide neural calcium, metabolism, and haemodynamics in awake mice. This is achieved through a time delay embedding that recovers a low-dimensional, nonlinear arousal manifold, from which diverse observables are decoded via time-invariant mappings. Extended to electrophysiological recordings in the Allen Brain Observatory, the same arousal manifold serves as a unified generative model that integrates brain states across modalities, individuals, and datasets — challenging the conventional view that large-scale spontaneous brain fluctuations reflect regionally specific neural communication.

Why It Matters

  • Reframes arousal as a low-dimensional dynamical system. Rather than a scalar "wakefulness" dial, arousal is a multidimensional latent process whose trajectory on a manifold accounts for the spatiotemporally structured covariation of neural, metabolic, and vascular signals brain-wide.
  • Demonstrates cross-modal unification from a single observable. A pupil diameter time series — cheap to record — reconstructs 60–85% of variance in widefield calcium, flavoprotein autofluorescence, and haemoglobin across n = 7 mice, plus electrophysiology in n = 16 Allen Brain Observatory recordings.
  • Challenges the dominant paradigm in functional connectivity. Spontaneous spatiotemporal brain fluctuations conventionally attributed to region-to-region communication are shown to be largely expressions of a single organism-wide dynamical process — reshaping how resting-state fMRI and EEG signals should be interpreted.
  • Provides a data-driven, generative reference frame. The arousal manifold aligns and aggregates observables across datasets without requiring simultaneous co-recording — a practical tool for multi-dataset neuroscience integration.
  • Open data and code. Widefield and pupillometry data deposited on Zenodo; all analysis code on GitHub — fully reproducible and extensible by the community.

Core Methods

  • Time delay embedding (Takens' theorem): pupil diameter is embedded in a high-dimensional Hankel matrix (d = 100 delays, Δt = 3 steps → 15-s window at 20 Hz) and projected onto the first r = 10 discrete Legendre polynomials to obtain a compact, universal set of delay coordinates.
  • Variational Autoencoder (VAE): a probabilistic encoder ψ maps Legendre-projected delay vectors to a 4-dimensional latent arousal state z; a decoder ϕ maps z back to widefield image space. A β-VAE objective (KL-annealing) enforces a smooth, continuous latent manifold. Separate decoders are trained for calcium, FAF, and haemoglobin from a frozen shared encoder.
  • Leave-one-out cross-validation: models trained on 6 mice, tested on the 7th (iterated); for Allen data, population decoders are trained group-level, then individual decoders are fine-tuned per mouse. This prevents overfitting in low-n regimes.
  • Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD): applied to original and reconstructed widefield data to extract spatiotemporal propagation modes; phase maps from the leading complex-valued eigenvector quantify travelling waves, compared via circular spatial correlation.
  • Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM) + Hidden Markov Models (HMM): frame-by-frame clustering of variance-normalized widefield images; HMM captures state-transition dynamics; both used to assess whether reconstructed data preserves spatial and temporal structure.
  • Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics (SINDy): applied to the 2D latent coordinates to discover a compact analytic vector field governing arousal dynamics — yielding a simple nonlinear oscillator with three free parameters.
  • Principal Component Analysis (PCA) + randomized SVD: used throughout for dimensionality reduction of widefield images; group-level spatial modes learned from training mice and applied to held-out data.

① Scale the arousal manifold framework to a foundation model of brain state

Extending this paperThe paper's VAE encoder-decoder is trained on n = 7 mice from a single lab, with cross-validation holding out one mouse at a time. A direct extension: pre-train a large, shared encoder on thousands of widefield and electrophysiology sessions (spanning species, brain areas, recording conditions) so that the arousal manifold becomes a foundation model of brain state — one that can be fine-tuned to any new recording with a handful of examples, and whose latent space transfers across experimental paradigms. The paper's SINDy-derived oscillator dynamics could provide a physics-based regularizer analogous to a physics-informed loss term.

Added value to my researchThis maps onto my interest in fine-tuning geospatial foundation models: just as the arousal encoder learns a universal embedding from a scalar observable, a spatiotemporal GeoAI foundation model could learn a universal embedding of human mobility or urban dynamics from a single cheap signal (e.g., mobile phone call-detail records), then decode population health or accessibility patterns across cities — the same encoder-decoder architecture with domain-specific decoders per outcome.

② Validate the attractor-manifold hypothesis with causal perturbation experiments

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that a low-dimensional arousal manifold reconstructs brain-wide dynamics but does not causally intervene in the manifold to prove it generates those dynamics. A key extension: apply optogenetic or chemogenetic perturbations to neuromodulatory nuclei (e.g., locus coeruleus) to shift the latent arousal state z to a specified point on the manifold, then test whether the observed widefield topography matches the model's prediction for that z. This would validate the manifold as a true dynamical attractor encoding an affective/arousal state — moving from correlation to causation.

Added value to my researchThe causal perturbation logic parallels "what-if" scenario simulation in urban analytics: just as optogenetic stimulation tests whether a predicted manifold state is causally real, policy counterfactuals in my facility-accessibility work test whether a model's predicted accessibility surface correctly shifts when a new clinic is placed. Both require the model to be a true generative causal graph, not just a predictive correlator — motivating adoption of causal graph structures over pure predictive ML in my spatial modeling pipeline.

③ Discover the conservation laws and invariants of the arousal dynamical system

Extending this paperThe paper fits a SINDy oscillator to the 2D latent coordinates, obtaining three free parameters. A deeper extension: apply manifold learning + optimal-transport methods to the full arousal state trajectories to discover implicit conservation laws — quantities preserved along arousal cycles. For example, does a "total arousal energy" conserve across wake-rest transitions? Identifying conserved quantities would sharpen the dynamical systems interpretation, predict how the manifold deforms under pathological states (sleep disorders, anesthesia, psychiatric illness), and provide interpretable biomarkers beyond variance explained.

Added value to my researchConservation-law discovery translates directly to my physics-informed graph learning agenda: geospatial systems (e.g., population flow networks, epidemic spreading) also obey implicit conservation constraints (population conservation, mass balance). Embedding these as soft constraints in a graph neural network — discovered data-drivenly from trajectory data rather than assumed — would make my spatiotemporal prediction models more physically interpretable and generalizable across cities or regions with different demographic compositions.

④ Extend the delay-embedding framework to multi-individual and social brain contexts

Extending this paperThe paper reconstructs a single individual's brain state from their own pupil signal. A natural extension: apply the delay-embedding framework to two simultaneously recorded individuals (dyadic or social neuroscience settings), asking whether a shared arousal manifold captures inter-brain synchrony observed during social interaction. The paper's "shared latent space" architecture (Fig. 4) already provides the scaffolding — one would add cross-individual coupling terms to f(z) and train a joint encoder across dyads.

⑤ Apply arousal-manifold logic to urban "physiological" signals for city-scale brain-state analogues

Extending this paperThe paper's central claim — that a single cheap scalar (pupil) encodes a low-dimensional manifold from which high-dimensional brain physiology can be reconstructed — invites testing in other complex, spatiotemporally coupled systems. One extension: replace "pupil diameter" with a city-level aggregate mobility scalar (e.g., daily transit ridership or Google mobility index) and "widefield calcium" with multi-layer urban dynamics (crime counts, 911-call rates, retail sales, air quality). If a city's "arousal" — its overall activity level — similarly embeds a latent manifold, the delay-embedding VAE could reconstruct fine-grained spatiotemporal urban physiology from a single aggregate signal.
