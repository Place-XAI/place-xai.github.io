Tools and methods for monitoring the health of the urban greenery

Akshit Gupta · Simone Mora · Yakir Preisler · Fàbio Duarte · Venkatesha Prasad · Carlo Ratti Published 2024-03-04 DOI 10.1038/s41893-024-01295-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Cities urgently need scalable, technology-assisted tools to monitor urban greenery health, but no single method resolves the fundamental space–time resolution trade-off: embedded sensors yield high temporal granularity for individual trees while satellite and airborne methods cover large areas at lower temporal frequency. Hyper-local greenery health data — collected via combinations of physical sensors, thermal/hyperspectral imaging, LiDAR, and citizen science — can directly support achieving at least six Sustainable Development Goal use cases, from selective irrigation and early disease detection to urban heat island mitigation and community engagement.

Why It Matters

  • Bridges a $2 trillion maintenance gap. Global costs for tree inspection, maintenance, and damage settlement exceed US$2 trillion, yet most cities survey their greenery only every 3–5 years or have never done so; scalable monitoring technology is the missing lever.
  • Exposes the resolution dilemma clearly. The review is the first systematic mapping of the space–time resolution trade-offs across all major sensing classes — manual, embedded sensors, and imaging-based — giving planners an evidence-based method-selection guide.
  • Links tree health directly to SDG delivery. Six concrete use cases (selective watering, disease detection, continuous monitoring, cost–benefit quantification, UHI mitigation, community engagement) connect monitoring data to SDGs 3, 4, 8, 10, 11, 13, 15, and 17.
  • Opens a frontier for citizen science and open-source tools. Low-cost, open-source platforms (e.g., DIY dendrometers, modified RGB cameras for multispectral imaging) make greenery health monitoring accessible to under-resourced cities and communities.
  • Identifies the gaps that can spur future research. Most reviewed techniques have been evaluated on only a few dozen trees in controlled settings; the review pinpoints the need for large-scale, species-transferable, and automated validation pipelines.

Core Methods

  • Systematic literature review: survey of technology-assisted methods for urban greenery health monitoring, organized into three sensing clusters — manual techniques, physical/chemical/electrical sensors, and imaging-based sensors.
  • Space–time resolution framework: each method class is evaluated on two axes — achievable spatial granularity and temporal resolution — and mapped onto SDG use cases (Fig. 1). Manual methods offer fine spatial detail but low automation; embedded sensors offer high temporal frequency but limited spatial coverage; remote sensing covers large areas but at lower temporal frequency.
  • Manual inspection techniques: visual inspection by arborists, non-invasive acoustic/electronic tools for fungal decay, electrical resistivity measurements, increment borers, and electromagnetic/stress-wave tomography — ordered from least to most invasive and costly.
  • Physical, chemical & electrical sensors: accelerometers (insect vibration detection), electrical impedance spectroscopy (water stress and disease), digital and analogue dendrometers (stem growth), IoT multi-sensor platforms with neural-network-based health classification.
  • Imaging-based sensors: infrared thermography (IRT) for cavity/decay detection and water stress; hyperspectral and multispectral imaging (HMI) for NDVI and vegetation index calculation; LiDAR for geometric parameters (crown diameter, LAI, leaf count); street-view image analysis (Google Street View, Baidu panoramas) for tree location, species, and shade extent.
  • Multi-sensor fusion: combining LiDAR with hyperspectral cameras for species identification and stressor mapping; fusing thermal with RGB imagery for automated leaf segmentation.
  • Machine learning integration: cited use of neural networks for sensor calibration, geometric property extraction, health classification from IoT data, and automated disease identification from multi-spectral inputs.
  • Citizen-science deployment: low-cost and open-source tools (DIY dendrometer bands, modified RGB cameras for near-IR capture) to enable community participation and complement institutional surveys.

② Coupling remote sensing of green-roof vegetation health to street-level urban greenery monitoring

Extending this paperThis review focuses on ground-level greenery (primarily street trees) and explicitly excludes green roofs and vertical green walls from most of its sensing discussion. A direct extension: couple the review's ground-level sensor framework with the multispectral and thermal approaches used for green roof vegetation health — using satellite-derived NDVI and thermal anomaly time series as a complementary city-scale signal to validate or calibrate the costlier ground-based embedded sensors the paper reviews. The corpus paper on remote sensing for healthy vegetation on green roofs provides exactly the technical bridge needed, employing satellite-based temporal NDVI trends to detect vegetation decline on rooftops across multiple cities.

③ Quantifying cooling benefit heterogeneity using the review's monitoring toolkit to validate urban tree planting strategies

Extending this paperThe review identifies UHI mitigation (UC5) as a key SDG 13 use case and notes that cooling effect varies with species, canopy density, and health status — but it stops short of quantifying how much the choice of monitoring method affects the accuracy of cooling attribution. An extension of this paper: deploy the reviewed sensor suite (IRT for surface temperature, LiDAR for crown geometry, HMI for NDVI as a proxy for transpiration rate) at matched street segments across a gradient of tree health states, then validate the cooling attributions against ground-truth temperature loggers. The corpus paper on urban trees reducing land surface temperatures in European cities provides the empirical benchmark and the climate-zone stratification needed to test transferability of species-specific cooling models — a gap the review itself flags.

④ IoT sensor networks for urban trees as a data backbone for real-time public-health co-benefits monitoring

Extending this paperThe review discusses IoT tree health platforms for disease and water stress detection (UC1, UC2) but treats human health outcomes as downstream policy benefits rather than as co-monitored variables. A direct extension: instrument IoT tree sensor nodes with ambient air quality sensors (particulate matter, ozone, NO₂) co-located with tree health sensors, enabling the review's UC3 continuous monitoring framework to simultaneously track both tree physiological status and the air-filtration ecosystem service delivered to nearby residents. This would operationalize the review's SDG 3 (good health) claim with direct evidence rather than inference. The corpus paper on green infrastructure and air pollution injustice shows that greenery's air quality benefits are unevenly distributed — IoT co-monitoring would make that inequality spatially explicit at the block level.

⑤ Citizen-science tree health data as a spatially-biased open dataset — correcting for sampling inequity with GeoAI

Extending this paperThe review advocates citizen science (UC6) as a scalable, low-cost complement to institutional monitoring, citing NYC TreesCount! and i-Tree Eco as examples. However, it does not address a fundamental limitation of citizen-science approaches: spatial sampling bias — volunteers cluster in higher-income, accessible neighborhoods, systematically under-sampling trees in low-income or high-crime areas where monitoring is arguably most needed. An extension of the paper: apply spatial bias correction (e.g., Maxent-style preferential sampling models or spatially stratified active learning) to citizen-collected tree health records, using the paper's low-cost sensor outputs as ground-truth labels in under-sampled areas. The corpus paper on improved human greenspace exposure equality demonstrates that greenspace data gaps track socio-economic gradients — the same mechanism applies to tree health monitoring deserts.

Added value to my researchSampling bias correction in geospatial citizen-science data is directly analogous to my multi-source data fusion work (mobile phone records, social media, remote sensing) where self-selection biases require explicit statistical correction before spatial models are valid. Developing a transferable bias-correction pipeline for tree health citizen-science data would produce a template applicable to any participatory urban environmental monitoring program — and the corrected data would directly feed my health geography accessibility analyses with equity-aware greenery exposure surfaces.
