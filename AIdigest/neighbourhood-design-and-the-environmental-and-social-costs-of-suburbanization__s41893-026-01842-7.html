Neighbourhood design and the environmental and social costs of suburbanization

Arianna Salazar-Miranda Published 2026-06-17 DOI 10.1038/s41893-026-01842-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The garden city design (GCD) paradigm — winding streets, cul-de-sacs and hierarchical road networks — independently and substantially raises greenhouse gas emissions, social isolation and sedentary behaviour across more than 60,000 US neighbourhoods. Neighbourhood design alone accounts for 25–41% of the sustainability costs routinely attributed to suburban location, demonstrating that how suburbs are designed matters independently of where they are built. Redesigning suburban street layouts is therefore an actionable, spatially-targeted lever for sustainability policy that does not require relocating population centres.

Why It Matters

  • Disentangles design from location for the first time at scale. The longstanding assumption that suburban costs stem from remoteness is empirically tested across the entire continental US; a large, design-specific effect survives three distinct identification strategies.
  • Quantifies design's share of the sustainability penalty. GCD accounts for 25–41% of the total suburbanization effect on GHG emissions, social isolation and sedentary behaviour — a magnitude large enough to shift the policy calculus from "where to build" to "how to build."
  • Opens a new retrofitting policy agenda. Because design is modifiable after construction (street-network retrofits, missing-middle infill, connectivity improvements), the finding implies that existing suburban stock can be made more sustainable without population relocation.
  • Links built-environment geometry directly to health and climate outcomes. The paper joins the causal thread from road topology to three distinct societal harms — carbon emissions, loneliness and physical inactivity — under one unified empirical framework.
  • Exploits historical waves of GCD adoption as a novel quasi-experiment. The instrumental-variable strategy gives the causal estimates a credibility well beyond observational correlation.

Core Methods

  • Composite GCD measure construction: derives a continuous, neighbourhood-level index of garden city design from OSM street-layout geometry — encoding winding streets, cul-de-sac frequency and road-network hierarchy into a single composite score across >60,000 CBGs.
  • Ordinary Least Squares (OLS): baseline multivariate regression of GHG emissions, social isolation and sedentary behaviour on GCD, controlling for location, demographics and geographic covariates.
  • Propensity Score Matching (PSM): matches neighbourhoods on observable confounders to balance GCD and non-GCD units — providing a non-parametric robustness check on the OLS estimates.
  • Instrumental Variable (IV) strategy: exploits historical waves of GCD adoption (instrumented with IRS county-level migration flows 1999–2000) to identify the causal effect of design, purging endogenous correlation between design choice and unobserved location preferences.
  • Effect decomposition: separates the "design" channel from the "remoteness" channel to attribute 25–41% of the standard suburbanization sustainability cost specifically to GCD.
  • Software: R and Stata; code and processed data publicly available on GitHub.

Extending this paperThe paper's GCD measure is a composite index — winding streets, cul-de-sac density and road hierarchy are bundled together. The OLS/PSM/IV pipeline estimates the composite effect, but not which sub-feature matters most for GHG versus social isolation versus sedentarism. ④ Combine GCD with zoning-reform data to simulate joint design-regulation interventions

Extending this paperThe paper treats zoning as a covariate (Landgrid parcel data), not an intervention. Yet zoning and street-network design co-evolved: cul-de-sac networks emerged alongside single-family residential zoning. An extension would pair the paper's GCD estimates with AI-based zoning-reform scenarios — encoding proposed upzoning maps as spatial inputs — and project the joint effect on GHG and social isolation under different reform packages. This would answer: does upzoning alone reduce the GCD penalty, or must street connectivity be independently retrofitted?

⑤ Map the superblock retrofit as an inverse of GCD and project its sustainability co-benefits

Extending this paperThe paper's GCD measure captures the negative end of a spectrum: high values encode the disconnected, car-centric patterns of garden city design. The superblock (Barcelona model) is almost the diametric opposite — high intersection density, pedestrian primacy, traffic exclusion from interior streets. An extension would compute GCD scores for superblock-eligible zones in US cities, apply the paper's IV-estimated effect sizes, and project the sustainability gains achievable if those zones were retrofitted — turning the paper's causal coefficients into a practical infrastructure-investment ranking tool.
