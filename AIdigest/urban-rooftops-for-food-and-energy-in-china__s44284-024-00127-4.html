Urban rooftops for food and energy in China

Rui Yang · Chao Xu · Haoran Zhang · Zhen Wang · Prajal Pradhan · Xihong Lian · Limin Jiao · Xuemei Bai · Shenghui Cui · Yuanchao Hu · Yong-Guan Zhu Published 2024-09-16 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00127-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 13 million buildings in 124 Chinese cities, rooftop agriculture (RA) and rooftop photovoltaics (RPV) both offer large but competing benefits: RA delivers superior economic returns while RPV leads in GHG reductions. A single-winner allocation loses 71–100% of the runner-up's benefits, but an optimal split — 61% of flat rooftop to RA, all remaining (including pitched roofs) to RPV — retains more than half of each potential, meeting ~15% of urban vegetable demand and ~5% of electricity demand on average, though with substantial resource costs including up to 15% of urban residential water use and 13 kt of silver in aggregate.

Why It Matters

  • First building-scale national assessment of the RA–RPV trade-off in China. Prior work studied single cities or small rooftop samples; this study spans 124 cities and 13 million individual buildings, setting a new empirical benchmark for the food–energy nexus.
  • Quantifies the cost of winner-take-all strategies. Dedicating all rooftops to either RA or RPV alone retains only 0–29% of the alternative's benefits — a stark illustration that the binary choice is economically and environmentally irrational.
  • Delivers a universal allocation rule with local nuance. The 61/39 flat-roof split emerges from multi-objective optimization across climate zones and building typologies, yet the authors show city-level variance (0.5–99% vegetable coverage, 0.5–27% electricity coverage) demanding locally adapted implementation.
  • Surfaces hidden resource constraints. RA's substantial water demand (up to 15% of residential use) and materials requirements (e.g., 13 kt silver for PV panels in aggregate) that are rarely foregrounded in rooftop decarbonization discourse are quantified here.
  • Directly actionable for urban planners and policymakers. The building-level results — together with open code at GitHub — enable city governments to map their own rooftop potential and set evidence-based targets.

Core Methods

  • Building-scale rooftop delineation: each of 13 million buildings is assigned a usable productive rooftop area, classified as flat or pitched, which determines eligibility for RA vs. RPV.
  • RA productivity modeling: vegetable yield estimated by combining regional open-air GAEZ benchmarks with rooftop growing-condition corrections (temperature, solar exposure), then scaled to the available flat-roof area per building.
  • RPV energy yield modeling: hourly MERRA-2 irradiance and ERA5-Land temperature inputs feed a standard PV performance model to estimate annual electricity generation per building; pitched roofs contribute to RPV only.
  • Benefit quantification (three dimensions): for each city and each allocation scenario, three benefit metrics are computed — (1) economic value (market price of vegetables vs. electricity), (2) environmental GHG reductions, and (3) provisioning fraction of city needs met.
  • Multi-objective Pareto optimization (pymoo): the RA–RPV area-split problem is formulated as a bi-objective maximization over RA and RPV benefits simultaneously; the Pareto frontier is mapped across flat-rooftop allocation ratios (0–100% to RA), with a "balanced" optimum identified at 61% RA / 39% RPV on flat roofs.
  • Resource cost accounting: water demand for RA irrigation and silver/material demand for RPV modules are computed as auxiliary outputs to contextualize hidden constraints.
  • Spatial aggregation: building-level results aggregated to city level to produce the cross-city distributions (0.5–99th percentile ranges) reported in the abstract.

① Extend the paper's 61/39 rule into a city-adaptive allocation model driven by GeoAI

Extending this paperThe paper derives a single global optimum (61% flat roof to RA) across all 124 cities, then shows that per-city outcomes vary wildly (0.5–99% vegetable coverage). The optimization is static and runs from scratch per city with no learning across the corpus. A direct extension is to train a spatiotemporal GeoAI model that maps a city's observable features — building density, climate zone, grid emission intensity, dietary patterns — to its local optimal allocation ratio, so the model generalizes to any Chinese (or other) city without re-running the full Pareto optimization. Corpus support: the deployable RPV study across Chinese cities already demonstrates that "deployable" potential departs substantially from technical potential due to local regulations and consumption constraints — the same logic applies to RA/RPV co-optimization.

② Add urban heat island feedbacks to the paper's benefit accounting via coupled RPV–temperature modeling

Extending this paperThe paper treats RPV and RA benefits in isolation from their thermal consequences, yet rooftop photovoltaics have documented — and spatially heterogeneous — warming and cooling effects on urban air temperature depending on latitude, albedo, and shading. A corpus paper (Rooftop photovoltaic solar panels warm up and cool down cities) shows that the sign of the temperature effect flips with climate context. Extending the present paper: fold the city-specific RPV warming/cooling effect into the benefit function so that the Pareto frontier accounts for urban heat as a fourth objective. In hotter southern Chinese cities, RPV's warming side-effect erodes the comfort and health co-benefits of the food–energy calculation; in northern cities it may add a winter heating bonus for RA greenhouses.

Added value to my researchThis extends my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation toolkit toward urban heat exposure mapping: combining building-footprint RPV coverage with temperature anomaly fields generates a high-resolution heat-exposure surface that my 2SFCA accessibility framework can then use to weight health-adjusted access to cooling centers or green space — coupling the food–energy paper's spatial outputs to my health-geography research line.

④ Replace the paper's assumed economic valuation with a social-equity-weighted benefit function

Extending this paperThe paper's economic benefit metric uses market prices for vegetables and electricity uniformly across cities, ignoring that a unit of vegetable production is worth far more to a food-insecure low-income neighborhood than to an affluent one. An equity-weighted extension would replace the monetary benefit function with a social welfare function that up-weights RA productivity in neighborhoods where vegetable prices consume a higher share of household income (or where food deserts exist), and up-weights RPV in areas with high energy poverty. The corpus paper on comparing carbon footprints of urban and conventional agriculture already shows that environmental performance of urban agriculture is highly context-dependent, suggesting that social context should enter the objective function alongside carbon.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly onto my efficiency-vs-equity optimization agenda — the same Pareto framework I apply to EV-charging station siting can be adapted here: substitute "charging demand equity" for "food-access equity" and "grid carbon reduction" for "vegetable self-sufficiency." The technical transfer is clean and adds a new application domain (urban food systems) to my optimization toolkit, while the equity weighting methodology advances my healthy-cities research line.

⑤ Integrate the paper's rooftop allocation model with district-scale hydrogen storage for temporal load balancing

Extending this paperThe paper treats RPV output as direct grid injection and RA as year-round steady production, ignoring the temporal mismatch between solar generation peaks and urban electricity/food demand cycles. A corpus paper (Decarbonizing urban residential communities with green hydrogen systems) demonstrates that rooftop PV paired with hydrogen-battery hybrid storage can smooth this mismatch and dramatically improve the economic case for community-level RPV. Extending the present paper: add a third allocation category — rooftop area allocated to solar-hydrogen charging stations — and re-run the multi-objective optimization as a three-way Pareto problem (RA vs. RPV-direct vs. RPV-hydrogen). This would reveal whether the 61/39 RA/RPV split changes when temporal storage is available and whether certain city types (high solar variability, high evening demand) benefit disproportionately from the hydrogen buffer.

Added value to my researchThis connects to my EV charging-station efficiency-vs-equity optimization in an interesting way: hydrogen-charged EVs drawing from community rooftop RPV represent a distributed, equity-sensitive charging model where the spatial co-location of charging infrastructure and food production could be jointly optimized. My agent-based modeling and network community-detection methods are well-suited to simulate the community-level demand flows that determine the ROI of this three-way allocation — creating a bridge between my urban-energy and healthy-cities research lines.
