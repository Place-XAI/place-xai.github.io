Event-level prediction of urban crime reveals a signature of enforcement bias in US cities

Victor Rotaru · Yi Huang · Timmy Li · James Evans · Ishanu Chattopadhyay Published 2022-06-30 DOI 10.1038/s41562-022-01372-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A stochastic Granger network algorithm predicts individual crime events one week in advance at ~1,000 ft resolution with a mean AUC of ~90% in Chicago — outperforming state-of-the-art deep learning with only historical event data. Crucially, the same model enables surveillance of the state: perturbation experiments across eight major US cities reveal a consistent enforcement bias in which policing resources are drained from socio-economically disadvantaged neighbourhoods to wealthier ones when crime increases, a pattern replicated over more than a decade of Chicago data and confirmed in seven additional cities.

  • Inverts the surveillance paradigm. Prior work used crime prediction to extend state power; this paper flips the lens — prediction quality high enough to detect systemic bias in police response, enabling citizen-side audit of enforcement equity.
  • State-of-the-art accuracy with minimal inputs. ~90% mean AUC at individual event level, one week ahead, using only past crime reports — no demographics, weather, or street imagery that risk encoding existing biases — while substantially surpassing comparable deep learning baselines.
  • Cross-city generality. The enforcement-bias signature (resources reallocated from poor to wealthy neighbourhoods under increased crime) is documented consistently across Chicago, Atlanta, Austin, Detroit, Los Angeles, Philadelphia, San Francisco and Portland — ruling out city-specific confounds.
  • Policy-actionable. The tool can quantify how far any candidate policing policy is from equitable, need-based resource allocation — providing a measurable target for reform rather than a descriptive critique.
  • Methodological bridge between computational social science and criminology. Shows that Granger networks on point-process data can recover macro-sociological theories (suburban bias, SES-driven resource concentration) from raw event logs.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Cities covered
8 major US cities: Chicago (primary, 2014–2017), Philadelphia, San Francisco, Austin, Los Angeles, Detroit, Atlanta, and Portland. Portland data also served as the NIJ 2017 forecasting-challenge benchmark.
Crime incident logs
Publicly available event-level crime records from each city's open-data portal (see table below). Records include spatio-temporal geolocation, event category, and — for Chicago only — the number of arrests per event.
Spatial resolution
Chicago: 2,205 spatial tiles of ~0.00276° × 0.0035° (≈1,000 ft / ~2 city blocks). Other cities rescaled to comparable tile sizes that maximise empirical entropy of the resulting time series.
Temporal resolution
1-day time windows for most cities (2-day for Atlanta). Chicago predictions made ~1 week in advance; model trained on 3 prior years, tested on 1 following year per cycle.
Crime categories
Violent crimes (homicide, assault, battery) and property crimes (burglary, theft, motor vehicle theft). Drug crimes excluded due to potential reporting bias. Portland additionally includes street crimes and all calls for service (for NIJ challenge comparability).
Socioeconomic data
Chicago community-area hardship index and census-tract variables (crowded housing, poverty rate, unemployment, per-capita income) from the City of Chicago data portal. Poverty estimates for other cities from census.gov (2018 US Census).
Computational scale
Up to ~2.67 billion candidate XPFSA models inferred per city run (Chicago 2014–2016 window); ~61.7 million retained (γ ≥ 0.01); approximately 200,000 core-hours on 28-core Intel Broadwell processors.

Core Methods

  • Spatial and temporal discretization: cities are tessellated into small tiles (~1,000 ft across in Chicago); event logs are binarized into daily presence/absence streams per tile per crime category.
  • Crossed Probabilistic Finite State Automata (XPFSA): for each source–target tile pair and time delay Δ (0–60 days), a finite-state transducer is learned that maps source history to target event probability — yielding up to 2.67 billion candidate models per city.
  • Granger network construction: a directed Granger network is assembled by retaining source–target links where the source predicts the target better than the target predicts itself. This preserves long-range, asymmetric, multi-scale dependencies that local diffusion models miss.
  • Gradient boosting ensemble: per-target, a gradient boosting regressor linearly combines the weak transducer outputs (weighted by learned coefficients ω) to minimize zero-one loss — the analogue of a learned linear activation combination over non-linear transducer features.
  • Evaluation: AUC-ROC (primary), Prediction Accuracy Index (PAI), and Prediction Efficiency Index (PEI). A prediction is a hit if an event occurs within ±1 day in the target tile; ARIMA baselines confirm the non-trivial gain from multi-stream learning.
  • Perturbation analysis: synthetic perturbations of ±1–10% in crime rates are injected into the inferred model, and the resulting change in predicted arrest rate is regressed against neighbourhood SES variables (hardship index, poverty rate) — the bias signature.
  • Seasonal natural experiment: winter-to-summer seasonality in Chicago is used as an empirical (model-free) validation of the biased enforcement response, confirming the perturbation result without relying on the inferred models.

① Extend the bias audit from enforcement to healthcare and public-service response

Extending this paperThe paper's core innovation — using a high-fidelity predictive model to perturb event streams and measure the differential response by neighbourhood SES — is entirely general. The Granger network framework does not intrinsically depend on crime data; it requires only timestamped, geolocated event logs. A direct extension: apply the same pipeline to emergency medical service (EMS) dispatches, 311 service requests, or hospital admissions to test whether a similar SES-biased resource-reallocation signature appears in public-health or municipal-service delivery. The corpus paper on surveillance cameras already shows that monitoring infrastructure is unevenly distributed by race; the perturbation audit could reveal whether public-health response is similarly stratified.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly onto my healthcare-accessibility agenda: I study spatial equity of primary care and mental-health access via 2SFCA/R2SFCA. The perturbation-audit logic — inject a synthetic demand shock and measure differential system response — translates to testing whether healthcare systems respond more slowly or with lower throughput to demand increases in low-SES areas, complementing static accessibility metrics with a dynamic stress-response dimension. The event-log methodology also opens mobile-phone or EHR records as data sources for demand shocks.

② Couple the Granger network's inferred spatial dependencies to an explainable ML layer

Extending this paperThe paper infers a directed Granger network of ~61.7 million XPFSA links per city, yet the analysis collapses this rich structure into aggregate perturbation statistics. A natural extension: apply SHAP or attention-based attribution to identify which source tiles and time delays dominate prediction for each target, producing interpretable "influence maps" that reveal whether predictive dependencies cluster along transit corridors, racial boundaries, or economic zones. This would transform the Granger network from a black-box predictor into an explainable spatial dependence atlas — and test mechanistic hypotheses (e.g., crime diffuses along subway lines, not Euclidean distance).

Adapting SHAP attribution to the XPFSA ensemble would create an "XAI criminology" pipeline that parallels my use of SHAP/MGWR for understanding non-stationary spatial regression in health-geography models. The inferred influence-range maps also dovetail with my spatiotemporal KDE work — both characterize the effective neighbourhood from which events aggregate.

③ Incorporate mobility flow data to separate crime-report bias from underlying crime

Extending this paperThe authors explicitly acknowledge that crime reports — not underlying crime — drive their models, and that differential reporting by community trust in police is a confound they cannot fully resolve. A concrete extension: augment the Granger network's event streams with fine-grained mobility data (foot-traffic, GPS trajectories) to estimate population-at-risk denominators per tile per hour. Normalizing event rates by dynamic exposure would disentangle sparse reporting in low-trust communities from genuinely lower crime rates, sharpening the bias estimate. The corpus paper on foot traffic in New York City provides exactly this kind of fine-grained spatial mobility layer.

Added value to my researchMulti-source data fusion (mobile phone, GPS, social media) is a defined theme in my research portfolio. Exposure-adjusted crime rates feed directly into my spatiotemporal KDE framework, where the kernel should weight by population-at-risk rather than uniform area — a correction I currently apply in health-geography contexts (disease incidence per capita) that would transfer to event-level crime modelling.

④ Leverage the automatic neighbourhood decomposition for spatially adaptive community detection

Extending this paperExtended Data Fig. 6 shows that a sparse set of "skeleton tiles" — identified from the Granger network's bi-clustering — generates a Voronoi decomposition that outlines functional crime neighbourhoods, adaptively sized to local event dynamics. This is a data-driven neighbourhood-boundary method that does not rely on pre-defined census tracts or police beats. Extending it: benchmark this decomposition against standard administrative geographies for spatial autocorrelation and predictive accuracy, and test whether the functional boundaries align with socioeconomic or racial segregation lines — bridging the paper's predictive framework to the broader literature on US residential segregation.

⑤ Use the enforcement-bias finding as a causal prior in a physics-informed graph learning model of urban inequality dynamics

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that enforcement resources flow away from disadvantaged neighbourhoods under crime stress — a directed, SES-conditioned flow rule. This is precisely the type of mechanistic constraint that can be encoded as a soft physics prior in a graph neural network trained on multi-city event logs. Extending the paper: train a GNN on Granger network topologies across the eight cities, enforcing the inferred "resource-drain" flow rule as a physics constraint, and evaluate whether the resulting model generalises the bias detection to unseen cities without re-running the full XPFSA inference (which costs ~200,000 core-hours). The resulting model would be a transferable urban-inequality dynamics emulator.

Added value to my researchPhysics-informed graph learning is a named research direction of mine. The enforcement-bias "flow rule" (policing resources drain from low-SES to high-SES areas under stress) is exactly the type of mechanism-based constraint I seek to bake into GNN priors, replacing purely data-driven fits with models grounded in social theory. This also connects to my interest in SIR-type spreading processes: the crime-and-enforcement co-evolution can be modelled as a coupled contagion on the Granger graph — with the SES-mediated resource allocation acting as a spatially heterogeneous recovery rate.
