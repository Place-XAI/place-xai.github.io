Foundation model of neural activity predicts response to new stimulus types

Wang, Eric Y. · Fahey, Paul G. · Ding, Zhuokun · Papadopoulos, Stelios · Ponder, Kayla · Weis, Marissa A. · Chang, Andersen · Muhammad, Taliah · Patel, Saumil · Ding, Zhiwei · Tran, Dat · Fu, Jiakun · Schneider-Mizell, Casey M. · Reid, R. Published 2025-04-09 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-08829-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A foundation model trained on calcium-imaging data from ~135,000 mouse visual-cortex neurons across 14 mice achieves state-of-the-art prediction of dynamic neural responses to natural videos, then generalizes out-of-distribution — to Gabor patches, noise patterns, dot kinematograms, and static images — without any in-distribution training on those stimuli. Beyond response prediction, the model's compact readout embeddings ("functional barcodes") accurately predict anatomical cell types, dendritic morphology, and synaptic connectivity within the MICrONS connectomics dataset, demonstrating that a single foundation model can bridge functional and structural neuroscience at scale.

★

Why It Matters

  • First brain foundation model with genuine out-of-distribution generalization. Previous data-driven neural models were trained and tested within the same stimulus domain; this model, trained only on natural videos, successfully predicts responses to wholly different parametric stimulus classes, validating the foundation-model paradigm for neuroscience.
  • Data efficiency breakthrough for new subjects. A new mouse needs fewer than 30 minutes of natural video to reach the accuracy that a from-scratch model requires more than 60 minutes — directly reducing the experimental burden of in vivo neuroscience.
  • Unlocks in silico experimentation after tissue destruction. Once trained, the model performs unlimited virtual experiments on any stimulus, solving a key bottleneck in projects (like MICrONS) where histological preparation ends in vivo recording.
  • Bridges function and structure without anatomical supervision. Readout feature vectors predict morphologically defined excitatory cell types (11 classes, balanced accuracy 32% vs. 9% chance) and visual areas (68% vs. 25% chance), demonstrating emergent structure-function alignment learned purely from functional data.
  • Open science infrastructure. Model weights, training pipeline, and recording pipeline are all publicly released (cajal/fnn), lowering barriers for the broader computational neuroscience community.

Core Methods

  • Modular ANN architecture: four modules — perspective (ray tracing + eye tracking to model the mouse's retinal view), modulation (LSTM encoding locomotion and pupil dilation as behavioral state), core (3D-convolutional DenseNet + convolutional LSTM or CvT-LSTM recurrent architecture), and readout (bilinear-interpolation + linear combination mapping core outputs to individual neuron responses).
  • Foundation core training: a single shared core is trained on combined data from 8 mice ("foundation cohort"), capturing latent representations of vision shared across animals, cortical layers, and areas.
  • Transfer learning: for new mice, only the perspective, modulation, and readout modules are fit; the foundation core is frozen. This reduces sample complexity from >60 min to <30 min of natural video to achieve high predictive accuracy.
  • Performance metric: normalized correlation coefficient (CCnorm) = CCabs / CCmax, correcting for in vivo response variability (Schoppe et al. upper bound).
  • Parametric tuning estimation: orientation/direction tuning via directional pink noise + von Mises mixture model fitting; spatial tuning via flashing Gaussian dots + spike-triggered average (STA) + 2D Gaussian fitting.
  • Functional barcode extraction: 512-dimensional readout feature weights per neuron extracted from the trained model's readout module, then used as inputs to logistic regression classifiers for visual area and morphological cell-type prediction.
  • Training objective: Poisson negative log-likelihood loss (outperforms MSE by 9.6%); stochastic gradient descent with Nesterov momentum; cosine decay learning rate with warm restart over 200 epochs.
  • Lesion studies: systematic ablation of individual modules to quantify their contribution to predictive accuracy.

① Apply the foundation-core + transfer-learning paradigm to geospatial foundation models

Extending this paperThe paper's central insight is that a shared "foundation core" trained on pooled multi-subject data generalizes better with less per-subject data than individually trained models. The paper itself calls for extending this paradigm to additional modalities and domains. A direct extension would test whether the same principle — freeze a shared spatiotemporal encoder, fine-tune only lightweight task heads — scales from mouse visual cortex to other biological systems with heterogeneous recording conditions (e.g., human EEG/fMRI across sites). The compact 512-dim readout embedding is also unexploited: it could serve as a universal neural-circuit fingerprint to transfer across species or brain regions using the connectome-constrained approach explored in companion MICrONS studies.

Added value to my researchThe paradigm maps directly onto my agenda of fine-tuning geospatial foundation models. Just as the paper freezes a visual-cortex core and fits lightweight readouts for new mice, I can freeze a pretrained geospatial encoder (e.g., a remote-sensing or trajectory FM) and fine-tune only a task-specific spatiotemporal readout head for new cities or new health outcomes — dramatically reducing the labeled data needed per city. The "functional barcode" concept (a compact embedding that predicts structural properties) parallels extracting learned place embeddings to predict unobserved health or equity indicators from mobility traces.

② Use functional barcodes as a blueprint for spatially-indexed explainability in GeoAI

Extending this paperThe paper's readout module assigns each neuron a 512-dimensional "functional barcode" that encodes its input-output tuning and — without any anatomical supervision — recovers morphological cell type and visual area membership. This is a form of unsupervised structure discovery through a learned linear probe. Extending the paper: apply nonlinear probing (gradient-boosted trees, SHAP) rather than logistic regression to the barcodes, and examine whether the top barcode dimensions correspond to interpretable tuning properties (orientation, spatial frequency, temporal dynamics), converting the barcode from a predictive tool into an explanatory one. Cross-referencing with connectome-constrained models (Drosophila visual system) would test whether barcode dimensions align with known circuit motifs.

Added value to my researchThis maps to my explainable ML work: spatially referenced feature embeddings learned from mobility or social media data could function as "place barcodes" — compact vectors whose dimensions SHAP-explains as contributions of specific spatial, demographic, or temporal features. The neuroscience validation pipeline (in vivo vs. in silico tuning comparisons) is a model for how I can validate learned spatial representations against held-out ground-truth outcomes (e.g., comparing model-predicted accessibility to measured travel times).

③ Replace the fixed kernel density / Gaussian tuning assumptions with learned spatiotemporal decoders

Extending this paperFor orientation and spatial tuning, the paper fits parametric models (von Mises mixture, 2D Gaussian) to neuronal responses — classical parametric assumptions that may fail for complex, non-isotropic, or multi-modal tuning curves. A direct extension is to replace these fixed parametric families with the foundation model's own nonlinear decoder: present the same parametric stimuli to the in silico neurons, run the full model, and extract tuning curves without any functional-form assumption, then compare the resulting tuning curve diversity with classical estimates. This leverages the model's generative capacity (in silico experiments are unlimited) to systematically probe the failure modes of Gaussian/von Mises approximations at scale.

Added value to my researchThe methodological parallel is direct: in my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation (KDE) work, the bandwidth and kernel shape are assumed rather than data-driven. The paper's in silico tuning approach suggests a validation protocol I can adopt — generate synthetic "stimulus" conditions (e.g., synthetic population distributions) and compare parametric KDE outputs against a data-driven, model-inferred density surface, using held-out ground-truth to choose the better estimator. This would sharpen the methodological contribution of my KDE papers.

④ Extend the behavioral modulation module to model context-dependent spatial behavior in urban systems

Extending this paperThe paper's modulation module encodes the mouse's internal state (locomotion speed, pupil dilation) as a dynamic, context-dependent scalar that multiplicatively gates the visual representation. This is an architectural solution to the fact that neural responses are not stationary — the same stimulus elicits different responses depending on arousal and attention. Extending the paper: generalize the modulation module to encode richer behavioral context (social interaction, task engagement) and test whether modulation-aware models reduce the residual variance currently attributed to "noise." Scaling this across species would test whether the same attentional gating architecture is universal.

Added value to my researchThe modulation architecture inspires a "context gate" for my spatiotemporal models of urban mobility and health-seeking behavior. Just as locomotion modulates visual processing, time-of-day, weather, and socioeconomic status modulate how people access facilities. Adding a lightweight LSTM context-modulation module — encoding time, weather, and socioeconomic features — to a spatial accessibility model would let the catchment area and demand weight adapt dynamically, turning a static 2SFCA into a context-aware dynamic accessibility estimator.
