Lessons from infant learning for unsupervised machine learning

Lorijn Zaadnoordijk · Tarek R. Besold · Rhodri Cusack Published 2022-06-22 DOI 10.1038/s42256-022-00488-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Unsupervised machine learning has stalled because it has largely mimicked supervised ML's conventions: unimodal data, fixed architectures, and stationary training sets. Drawing on developmental cognitive science, this Perspective identifies three transferable lessons from infant learning: (1) information processing guided by innate architectural biases and constraints; (2) richer representations built from diverse, multimodal inputs; and (3) learning inputs shaped by a natural developmental curriculum and curiosity-driven active exploration. Adopting these principles could unlock qualitatively new performance levels in unsupervised learning that incremental data scaling alone cannot achieve.

Why It Matters

  • Diagnoses the ceiling of current unsupervised ML. The paper argues that "the data will fix it" is insufficient; the community inherits supervised ML's data limitations while lacking its supervision signal — a fundamental structural problem.
  • Grounds inspiration in the most relevant biological learner. Prior neuro-inspired ML focuses on adult cognition. Infants are the true counterpart: they build flexible representations from unlabeled data with far fewer examples than any state-of-the-art DNN requires.
  • Proposes concrete, actionable architectural directions. From fibred "networks of networks" that encode complex connectivity to curriculum schedules mimicking developmental non-stationarity, the recommendations go beyond vague biologically inspired rhetoric.
  • Identifies the catastrophic interference problem as unsolved and urgent. The analysis ties plasticity loss directly to the mismatch between static ANN architectures and the infant brain's temporally evolving structure, motivating new solutions.
  • Bridges two research communities. By translating specific developmental-science findings (the Goldilocks effect, subplate transience, joint-attention scaffolding) into ML design principles, the paper creates a sustained bilateral exchange rather than a one-off analogy.

① Operationalize the paper's "natural curriculum" principle in geospatial foundation-model fine-tuning

Extending this paperThe paper's Lesson 3 proposes that ANNs should train on inputs ordered by an increasing complexity schedule analogous to infants' developmental progression from blurry-low-acuity to sharp-high-resolution vision. The paper remains conceptual; a direct extension is to empirically validate whether spatially coarse-to-fine curriculum ordering (training a geospatial model first at low spatial resolution, then at progressively finer scales) improves representation quality and data efficiency, directly testing the core hypothesis on real-world spatial data. This would also formalize "what counts as complexity" for spatial inputs — the paper leaves this open.

Added value to my researchThis directly informs my work on fine-tuning geospatial foundation models. A resolution-escalating curriculum during fine-tuning — coarse regional patterns first, then neighborhood-level details — could improve sample efficiency when adapting a pre-trained model to sparse ground-truth labels (e.g., healthcare facility access surveys), exactly the labeled-data bottleneck the paper identifies. It gives a principled, infant-inspired protocol for multi-scale fine-tuning.

② Build a multimodal spatiotemporal self-supervised learner inspired by the paper's cross-modal binding principle

Extending this paperLesson 2 argues that infants' cross-modal temporal synchrony (linking sight, sound, touch) produces richer representations than any single-modality training. Current multimodal ML has barely moved beyond image–text pairs. A concrete extension of the paper: construct a self-supervised pre-training objective that binds spatially registered but modality-diverse inputs — satellite imagery, street-level imagery, mobility traces, and POI sequences — via a temporal-synchrony contrastive objective inspired by the infant's multimodal binding, then evaluate whether representations transfer better than single-modality baselines.

Added value to my researchMy data science agenda fuses remote sensing, GPS trajectories, social media, and POI records — precisely the multi-source, unlabeled streams the paper identifies as the untapped potential of unsupervised learning. A cross-modal contrastive pre-training step over these heterogeneous urban data streams would give me a foundation-model backbone tailored to GeoAI tasks (health geography, urban accessibility), without relying on ImageNet pre-training that encodes non-spatial visual biases.

③ Translate the paper's inductive-bias lesson into spatially-structured priors for explainable GeoAI

Extending this paperLesson 1 argues that not all inductive biases are equal and that the specific structure of the bias matters — a point the paper makes for neural architecture but does not develop for learned spatial representations. An extension: test whether explicitly encoding spatial autocorrelation as an architectural prior (e.g., geographically weighted attention heads or spatial-kernel constraints in the first network layers, analogous to the cortical microcircuitry biases the paper describes) produces more sample-efficient and interpretable representations than unstructured architectures trained on the same spatial data.

Added value to my researchThis speaks directly to my MGWR/GWR and explainable ML agenda. If spatial non-stationarity is encoded as an inductive bias — not estimated post hoc — then the model's attention maps carry geographic meaning by construction, making explanations (which spatial features drive prediction where) directly interpretable without Shapley post-processing. This bridges the paper's architectural-bias lesson with my explainability work on health-geography predictions.

④ Formalize curiosity-driven active learning for sparse spatial label acquisition

Extending this paperLesson 3 describes the Goldilocks effect: infants preferentially attend to stimuli at intermediate complexity — not too familiar, not too novel — maximizing learning progress per unit of exposure. The paper reviews computational counterparts (prediction-error curiosity, multi-armed bandit syllabi) but does not propose a specific geographic or spatial instantiation. A direct extension: formalize a spatial active-learning policy where a model queries the most informative unlabeled locations (intermediate uncertainty × spatial novelty) rather than sampling at random, implementing the infant's curiosity mechanism for geospatial data collection.

Added value to my researchEfficient ground-truth collection is the binding constraint in spatial accessibility research — surveying healthcare facility quality across hundreds of cities is expensive. A curiosity-inspired spatial active-learner that prioritizes locations where the model's uncertainty is high and the local spatial context is underrepresented would let me build training sets for R2SFCA calibration or telehealth digital-divide mapping with far fewer surveys, directly reducing data-collection cost while improving geographic coverage.

⑤ Address the paper's unsolved catastrophic-interference problem through spatiotemporal continual learning

Extending this paperThe paper flags catastrophic interference — new knowledge overwriting previous representations — as an unsolved obstacle for developmental-inspired ML, and suggests that the infant cortex's temporally evolving architecture (especially the transient subplate structure) holds hints for biological solutions. A concrete extension: implement a spatiotemporal dual-memory architecture (fast elastic weights for current context, slow plastic weights for long-term geography) where the "context" is defined by spatial region and time period, mimicking how the subplate mediates local cortical organization before disappearing — testing whether geographically segmented replay buffers reduce catastrophic forgetting when a model is sequentially trained across cities.

Added value to my researchUrban dynamics are non-stationary: mobility patterns, health access, and POI distributions shift over years and across cities. A spatiotemporal continual-learning framework would let my GeoAI models update on new city data (e.g., post-pandemic mobility shifts or new healthcare facility openings) without forgetting what was learned on prior cities — a capability essential for building transferable, updatable urban health-geography tools. The paper's catastrophic-interference discussion gives a biological mandate for this engineering investment.
