Role of behaviour change in controlling the 2022 Paris mpox outbreak

Davide Maniscalco · Olivier Robineau · Pierre-Yves Boëlle · Mattia Mazzoli · Anne-Sophie Barret · Emilie Chazelle · Alexandra Mailles · Harold Noël · Arnaud Tarantola · Annie Velter · Laura Zanetti · Vittoria Colizza Published 2026-01-21 DOI 10.1038/s44360-025-00022-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Spontaneous, widespread behavioural change — not postexposure prophylaxis vaccination or immunity in highly active MSM — was the primary driver of the rapid 2022 mpox decline in the Paris region. The model estimates that ~49% of MSM reduced sexual contacts, preventing an estimated 68% of potential cases that summer. A 2023 survey independently confirmed that 46% of MSM in the region reduced partners during that period, closely validating the model. Effective risk communication and community engagement, not clinical interventions alone, are therefore decisive in controlling sexually-transmitted outbreaks.

★

Why It Matters

  • Quantifies what actually stopped the outbreak. The 2022 mpox surge fell abruptly — this study is one of the first to disentangle the three candidate drivers (vaccination, high-risk immunity, behavioural change) using a calibrated network model and independent survey validation.
  • Challenges the vaccine-centric narrative. Post-exposure prophylaxis vaccination and partial immunity in high-frequency MSM were each insufficient to explain the decline; spontaneous community behaviour change was the decisive factor.
  • Generalizes beyond mpox. The result underscores that community-level risk communication can generate rapid, population-wide protective behaviour change even without targeted interventions — a lesson for any STI or outbreak managed in at-risk communities.
  • Cross-validates model with survey data. The close agreement between mechanistic model output (~49% MSM) and a nationally representative survey (ERAS 2023, 46% MSM) provides rare independent validation, raising confidence in model-based outbreak inference.
  • Informs outbreak preparedness policy. Findings stress that investing in effective risk communication and community engagement infrastructure before outbreaks is as important as vaccine stockpiling.

Core Methods

  • Network transmission model: stochastic SIR-type model on a scale-free/heavy-tailed sexual contact network parameterised from PREVAGAY survey data. Network nodes = MSM individuals; edges weighted by sexual contact frequency. Age structure incorporated.
  • Three-hypothesis testing: model tested three candidate drivers of epidemic decline — (1) post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) vaccination coverage, (2) natural immunity accumulating in highly active MSM, and (3) uniform behavioural change across all MSM. Each hypothesis implemented as a separate model variant.
  • Two behavioural change variants: (a) uniform reduction in sexual contacts for all MSM and (b) preferential change among high-risk MSM — fitted separately to assess which sub-population mechanism best matches observed decline.
  • Simulation-based Bayesian inference: Approximate Bayesian Computation (ABC, Gouriéroux–Monfort method) used to infer free parameters from case time series. Each scenario run with n = 250 stochastic realisations; results reported as means with 95% prediction intervals.
  • Averted-case counterfactual: best-fit behavioural-change model compared against a no-change counterfactual to estimate the fraction of cases averted by behavioural adaptation (68%, 95% PI 15–99%).
  • Survey cross-validation: ERAS 2023 survey self-reported partner reduction (46%) compared to model-inferred behavioural uptake (~49%) as an independent out-of-model validation step.
  • Sensitivity analyses: varied detection probability (p_d = 20% vs 80%), asymptomatic transmissibility, and whether vaccinated MSM could also change behaviour (7 extended data figures, 7 supplementary tables).

① Embed spatial heterogeneity into the network model using geolocated venue data

Extending this paperThe current model treats the Paris MSM population as spatially homogeneous — contacts are assigned stochastically without geography. A direct extension: layer the existing sexual contact network onto a geolocated venue graph (gay bars, saunas, PrEP clinics) so that transmission clusters reflect real spatial proximity. This would test whether the inferred behavioural change was geographically uniform or concentrated in specific urban sub-areas, and allow simulation of spatially targeted communication campaigns — something the current model cannot distinguish.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly to my spatiotemporal kernel density and 2SFCA work: modelling access to PrEP clinics and sexual health services as part of the contact suppression mechanism links healthcare spatial accessibility (my 2SFCA/R2SFCA framework) to epidemic dynamics. I could test whether areas with poor sexual-health-service access showed slower behavioural uptake — making accessibility a predictor in outbreak models.

② Replace the static contact network with mobility-driven dynamic contact matrices

Extending this paperThe paper's network is calibrated from the 2017–2019 PREVAGAY survey and held fixed across the simulation. A key limitation acknowledged in the study is that the network cannot capture time-varying contact patterns. Extending it: substitute the static degree distribution with mobility-driven synthetic contact matrices derived from mobile-phone data, updating weekly. This would let the model automatically detect when contacts dropped (signalling behavioural change onset) rather than inferring a step-function behavioural switch at a fitted date.

Added value to my researchI work with mobile-phone trajectory data for spatiotemporal analysis. Ingesting mobility data into epidemic contact matrices creates a bridge between my GPS/mobile-data pipeline and public-health SIR models — a reproducible workflow I could apply to future outbreak monitoring using near-real-time mobility feeds.

Cross-ref (corpus): Mobility-driven synthetic contact matrices as a scalable solution for real-time pandemic response modeling — 10.1038/s41467-026-68557-3

③ Apply the model's behavioural-change inference to mpox clade I — extending to a different outbreak context

Extending this paperThe Paris model is calibrated on the 2022 clade IIb outbreak, which spread predominantly via sexual contact among MSM. The more recent clade Ib outbreak in eastern Democratic Republic of the Congo involves a broader community transmission mode including household and heterosexual contacts. Extending the behavioural-change framework to clade I would require re-parameterising the contact network with both community and sexual contact layers, and testing whether community-level risk communication is similarly effective in a different epidemiological context.

Added value to my researchMy Monte Carlo and agent-based modelling expertise applies here: running cross-scenario sensitivity analyses across outbreak types, network structures, and community contact heterogeneity to produce a generalised understanding of which community characteristics amplify or dampen the effect of risk communication on outbreak trajectories.

④ Integrate vaccination and behavioural change as jointly optimised interventions

Extending this paperThe paper tests vaccination and behavioural change as alternative hypotheses explaining the decline, not as complementary levers to be co-optimised. An extension: use the calibrated model as a policy simulator and solve an optimal resource allocation problem — allocating a fixed vaccine supply and communication budget across sub-populations to jointly maximise the reduction in cases. This turns the paper's explanatory model into a decision-support tool for future outbreak preparedness.

⑤ Build an explainable ML layer to predict behavioural change uptake from community-level predictors

Extending this paperThe paper's key output — ~49% of MSM changed behaviour — is a single aggregate estimate for the Paris region. It cannot predict which sub-populations or which cities are more likely to exhibit high versus low behavioural uptake in a future outbreak. Extending it: train an explainable gradient-boosted or XGBoost model across multiple European city outbreaks (Italy, UK, Netherlands, Belgium — all referenced in the paper) to predict the proportion adopting behaviour change from community-level features (pre-existing STI surveillance reach, PrEP uptake rates, community organisation density, prior risk communication campaigns). SHAP attribution would reveal which structural community factors most drive protective behaviour change.
