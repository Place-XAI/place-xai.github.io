The universal visitation law of human mobility

Markus Schläpfer · Lei Dong · Kevin O'Keeffe · Paolo Santi · Michael Szell · Hadrien Salat · Samuel Anklesaria · Mohammad Vazifeh · Carlo Ratti · Geoffrey B. West Published 2021-05-26 DOI 10.1038/s41586-021-03480-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The number of visitors to any urban location decreases as the inverse square of the product of visiting frequency and travel distance — a universal scaling law ρ ∝ (rf)⁻² that holds robustly across cities on multiple continents. Spatial clusters of visitor flows follow Zipf's law in their area distribution, and an individual-level Exploration and Preferential Return (EPR) model provides a mechanistic derivation of the law — corroborating central place theory and enabling predictions of recurrent urban flows.

Why It Matters

  • Unifies the spatial and temporal dimensions of mobility. Classical models (gravity law, radiation model) capture only spatial distance; this law adds visit frequency as a co-equal axis, producing a two-dimensional spectral view of population flows.
  • Empirically universal across diverse contexts. The (rf)⁻² exponent is validated in Greater Boston (USA), Singapore, Dakar (Senegal), Abidjan (Ivory Coast), and Lisbon (Portugal) — spanning different continents, income levels, and urban morphologies.
  • Provides a mechanistic explanation. The EPR individual model generates the correct scaling at the population level, bridging microscopic behavior and macroscopic flow structure.
  • Corroborates and formalizes long-standing geographic theory. The law gives quantitative support to central place theory (Christaller) and Weber's optimality theory, upgrading them from qualitative sketches to predictive physics.
  • Applications in urban planning, traffic engineering, and epidemic modeling. Recurrent flow predictions can optimize transport network design and inform spatially targeted interventions during disease outbreaks.

Core Methods

  • Spectral flow decomposition: define the visitor influx ρ(r, f) as the density of visitors to a grid cell arriving from distance r with visitation frequency f, then empirically measure this two-dimensional distribution across all locations and cities.
  • Scaling law estimation: fit the power-law exponent η in ρ ∝ (rf)⁻η via ordinary least-squares regression on log-log transformed data; validate that η ≈ 2 universally across locations and cities.
  • Zipf's law analysis of spatial clusters: identify cohesive visitor-flow clusters for each visitation-frequency band and test whether their area distribution follows a Zipf (power-law rank–size) relationship.
  • EPR (Exploration and Preferential Return) model: simulate individual trajectories using an agent that either explores a new location with probability ρnew ∝ S⁻γ (S = distinct locations visited) or returns to a known location with probability proportional to its attractiveness; aggregate over many agents to predict the population-level spectral flow.
  • Fermat–Torricelli–Weber (FTW) efficiency analysis: measure the fraction by which moving each grid cell to its optimal FTW point would reduce the total effective travel distance — benchmarking how close the actual urban layout is to the theoretical optimum.
  • Central place theory (CPT) validation: compute the average travel distance at fixed frequency ⟨r⟩f and verify the predicted inverse relationship ⟨r⟩f ∝ 1/f across all five cities.

③ Decode how the EPR attractiveness field encodes health and service accessibility

Extending this paperThe paper's EPR model assigns each location a latent attractiveness μi that the simulation currently treats as homogeneous and spatially uniform — in contrast to the empirically heterogeneous field. Extending the paper: replace the homogeneous prior with a covariate-conditioned attractiveness surface incorporating land use, POI types (hospitals, parks, transit), and socioeconomic status. This would transform the EPR from a statistical reproducer into a behavioral model of where people go and why, with direct relevance to public health interventions.
