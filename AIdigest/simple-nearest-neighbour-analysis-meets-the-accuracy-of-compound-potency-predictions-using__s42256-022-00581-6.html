Simple nearest-neighbour analysis meets the accuracy of compound potency predictions using complex machine learning models

Tiago Janela · Jürgen Bajorath Published 2022-12-14 DOI 10.1038/s42256-022-00581-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across ten pharmaceutical activity classes (totalling 13,444 compounds from ChEMBL), a simple k-nearest-neighbour (kNN) analysis consistently matched or exceeded the prediction accuracy of support vector regression, random forests, deep neural networks, and graph convolutional networks. More strikingly, completely random predictions reproduce experimental potency values to within one order of magnitude — an artefact of the narrow potency-value distributions inherent in standard benchmark data sets. These findings reveal a fundamental flaw in conventional benchmarking: the gap between the best ML model and a random predictor is only ~0.5 MAE (log units), making it nearly impossible to credibly demonstrate model superiority. The authors call for simple kNN baselines and alternative benchmark criteria that target structurally unique and highly potent compounds.

Why It Matters

  • Challenges a decade of reported ML "progress" in drug discovery. The standard benchmarking practice has systematically overestimated the value of increasingly complex models; kNN is an adequate baseline that is almost never reported.
  • Identifies a data-distribution artefact as the root cause. Potency distributions in medicinal-chemistry libraries cluster in the micromolar-to-high-nanomolar range, ensuring that even random predictions stay within one order of magnitude — a structural problem, not a model-quality signal.
  • Provides concrete, actionable benchmark reforms. Using hold-out sets of structurally unique or most potent compounds, and reporting kNN alongside any new model, are feasible changes that would make benchmark scores meaningful again.
  • Generalizes beyond drug discovery. The logic — that baseline simplicity is underestimated and data distributions distort apparent accuracy — applies to any supervised regression task on structured chemical or property data.
  • Open, reproducible science. All data and code are freely available (GitHub + Zenodo), enabling community replication and extension.

Core Methods

  • k-Nearest Neighbour (kNN) regression: non-parametric baseline; potency of a test compound assigned as the average pIC50 of its 1, 3, or 5 most similar training compounds (Tanimoto similarity on ECFP4). Best k selected per class.
  • Support Vector Regression (SVR): Tanimoto kernel; cost parameter C optimized over 7 values (0.001–1,000) via 10-fold cross-validation; implemented in scikit-learn.
  • Random Forest Regression (RFR): ensemble of decision trees; grid search over number of trees (25/100/200) and leaf/split parameters; scikit-learn.
  • Deep Neural Network (DNN): feedforward network with 2–3 hidden layers (100–500 neurons); Adam optimizer; tanh/ReLU activations; 200 epochs with early stopping; TensorFlow/Keras.
  • Graph Convolutional Network (GCN): two convolutional layers; channel width up to 1,024; learned molecular graph representation (neural fingerprint); DeepChem; batch normalization, early stopping.
  • Median Regressor (MR): constant baseline assigning training-set median potency to every test compound — establishes the floor of "pseudoaccuracy."
  • Randomized model controls: y-randomization (potency values randomly shuffled across compounds before training) applied to kNN and SVR to establish the true random prediction floor.
  • Evaluation: Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) across 10 independent trials per model per class; statistical significance via Wilcoxon test (α = 0.05).
  • Analogue-series clustering: compound–core relationship algorithm for structurally unique hold-out sets; MaxMin dissimilarity algorithm (Tanimoto on ECFP4) for diverse training sets.

① Apply the paper's "kNN-as-ceiling" test to geospatial property prediction models

Extending this paperThe paper's central finding — that kNN matches complex ML in potency prediction — was demonstrated exclusively in chemical space. A direct extension would test whether this pattern generalizes to other high-dimensional property prediction tasks, particularly those where the training distribution is similarly narrow. Graph-based molecular property prediction (GCN) is topologically analogous to graph-based spatial prediction; the same structural argument (similar nodes have similar labels, dominating benchmark accuracy) applies. Running the paper's exact experimental protocol on a different domain's benchmark datasets — e.g., protein–ligand binding affinities — would determine whether the pseudoaccuracy artefact is universal or domain-specific.

Added value to my researchThis reasoning transfers directly to geospatial regression benchmarks: in spatiotemporal prediction (air quality, disease rates, housing prices), nearby observations tend to have similar values, so spatial kNN (using geographic neighbours) likely performs on par with complex GNNs. Before claiming any GeoAI model outperforms baselines, I should include a geographic-kNN control — the same discipline this paper advocates for chemistry.

② Replace the potency-distribution benchmark flaw with activity-cliff-aware evaluation

Extending this paperThe paper identifies that narrow potency distributions are the root cause of pseudoaccuracy, and proposes hold-out sets of structurally unique and most potent compounds as a partial fix. A natural extension is a fully activity-cliff-stratified benchmark: partition compounds into cliff-forming pairs (large potency difference, high structural similarity) and non-cliff pairs, and report MAE separately for each stratum. This would isolate whether any complex model — DNN, GCN, or learned molecular representations — provides genuine advantage specifically on the difficult, high-discontinuity examples the paper acknowledges are underrepresented.

Added value to my researchThe "activity cliff" concept maps to spatial outlier detection in GeoAI: locations where spatially similar contexts yield dramatically different outcomes (e.g., two adjacent neighborhoods with very different health outcomes). My explainable ML workflow should explicitly stratify evaluation into spatially smooth and spatially discontinuous strata — reporting SHAP-based feature attribution only for the hard (discontinuous) cases where the model actually needs to generalize.

Cross-ref (corpus): Molecular deep learning at the edge of chemical space — 10.1038/s42256-026-01216-w

③ Probe whether learned molecular representations break the kNN ceiling

Extending this paperThe paper tests ECFP4-based kNN (hand-crafted fingerprints) against DNN and GCN with learned representations, finding no advantage for the latter. However, it predates self-supervised molecular contrastive pre-training, which encodes chemical space geometry from unlabelled data. An extension: replace ECFP4 with a contrastively pre-trained embedding and rerun the paper's exact kNN benchmark. If contrastive embeddings dramatically close the kNN ceiling (i.e., kNN in a better space yields much lower MAE), the paper's conclusion shifts: the problem is not model architecture complexity, but representation quality. This is a precise, testable hypothesis anchored directly to the paper's protocol.

Added value to my researchThis tests a hypothesis relevant to geospatial foundation model fine-tuning: if I replace hand-crafted spatial features (POI counts, road density) with embeddings from a pre-trained geospatial model, does kNN in that representation space already achieve strong accuracy before any task-specific model is trained? If yes, my fine-tuning effort should focus on the embedding, not the prediction head.

④ Link the paper's y-randomization results to out-of-distribution generalization

Extending this paperThe paper shows that y-randomized models achieve ~0.8–1.0 MAE — nearly indistinguishable from best models. A mechanistic extension: examine what the model has actually learned (or failed to learn) by coupling y-randomization analysis with chemical-space coverage metrics. Specifically, models trained on randomized labels but evaluated on nearby (in-distribution) test compounds benefit from the same proximity effect as kNN. Extending to out-of-distribution (OOD) test sets — compounds from different chemical series, scaffolds, or potency bins than the training set — would reveal whether complex models carry any genuine structural learning advantage when proximity to training data cannot rescue the prediction.

Added value to my researchSpatial analogue: in GeoAI, spatial autocorrelation inflates benchmark accuracy just as potency autocorrelation does in chemistry. My spatiotemporal kernel density and MGWR models trained on nearby training points perform well not because of model capacity but because of spatial proximity. Adopting a spatial cross-validation protocol (buffered LOO or blocked k-fold by geographic region) is the direct GeoAI equivalent of the paper's OOD hold-out — and would be more honest.

⑤ Use the paper's benchmark reform proposals to design a principled ML evaluation framework for accessibility prediction

Extending this paperThe paper recommends two concrete benchmark reforms: (1) always include kNN as a reference model, and (2) design hold-out sets that oversample the hardest prediction targets (structurally unique and highest-potency compounds). Applied back to molecular property prediction, this implies a benchmark meta-standard — a checklist of required baselines and data splits that any paper claiming superior ML performance must satisfy. Building such a meta-standard (analogous to the CASP competition in protein structure) and applying it to generative molecular design would directly operationalize the paper's call to action.

Added value to my researchMy work on healthcare accessibility prediction (2SFCA, spatial ML for service-area delineation) suffers the same problem: most studies benchmark on spatially clustered test sets where spatial kNN trivially succeeds. Adopting the paper's discipline — reporting spatial kNN accuracy first, then demonstrating incremental ML gain on geographically isolated test areas — would make my accessibility model claims defensible and publishable in high-impact venues that demand rigorous baseline comparisons.
