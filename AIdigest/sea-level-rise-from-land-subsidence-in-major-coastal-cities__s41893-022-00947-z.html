Sea-level rise from land subsidence in major coastal cities

Cheryl Tay · Eric O. Lindsey · Shi Tong Chin · Jamie W. McCaughey · David Bekaert · Michele Nguyen · Hook Hua · Gerald Manipon · Mohammed Karim · Benjamin P. Horton · Tanghua Li · Emma M. Hill Published 2022-09-12 DOI 10.1038/s41893-022-00947-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Applying standardized Interferometric SAR (InSAR) processing to Sentinel-1 data (2014–2020) across the 48 largest coastal cities worldwide (representing 20% of the global urban population), this study reveals that local land subsidence — not just climate-driven mean sea-level rise — drives relative sea-level rise (RSLR) at rates up to −16.2 mm/yr, far exceeding IPCC AR6 estimates of vertical land motion. Cities with the fastest subsidence are concentrated in Asian megacities (Tianjin, Ho Chi Minh City, Jakarta, Chittagong, Yangon, Ahmedabad), where anthropogenic groundwater extraction compounds sediment compaction. The standardized method enables, for the first time, self-consistent, city-to-city comparison of relative vulnerability to land subsidence and its contribution to RSLR globally.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reveals a systematically underestimated hazard. IPCC AR6 VLM estimates (−5.2 to 4.9 mm/yr) miss the full spread: locally measured RLLS spans −16.2 to 1.1 mm/yr, showing that many cities face far greater RSLR than globally recognized.
  • First globally consistent InSAR benchmark for 48 major cities. Prior studies were city-by-city, using incompatible methods and time spans; this work harmonizes observations to enable comparative vulnerability ranking.
  • Exposes within-city spatial heterogeneity as critical for policy. Subsidence is not uniform — localized clusters in specific districts (e.g., Penjaringan, Jakarta) demand targeted intervention, not city-averaged estimates.
  • Empowers local mitigation independent of global climate action. Because most coastal subsidence stems from groundwater over-extraction, communities can reduce their own RSLR risk — a locally actionable lever distinct from global emissions reductions.
  • Fills a key gap in inundation and adaptation modelling. The RLLS velocities are compatible with DEM-based bathtub inundation models (e.g., CoastalDEM + SRTM), enabling near-term (decadal) flood extent updates for planning.

Core Methods

  • Sentinel-1 InSAR time-series (SBAS): Sentinel-1 SAR image pairs processed via ARIA-SG → ISCE → ARIA-Tools → MintPy. Small BAseline Subset (SBAS) weighted least-squares inversion yields per-pixel linear velocity and standard deviation (goodness-of-fit metric for non-linearity detection).
  • Tropospheric correction: ERA-5 Global Atmospheric Model delay correction applied via PyAPS (Python-based Atmospheric Phase Screen package) to reduce unwanted tropospheric noise in C-band signals.
  • Phase unwrapping error correction: bridging + phase closure methods applied in MintPy to correct phase jumps before velocity estimation.
  • Spatial interpolation of InSAR gaps: a modified kriging approach (variance quadtree subsampling + spherical variogram fit via maximum likelihood estimation) fills decorrelated areas (vegetation, disturbed land) assuming spatial autocorrelation of land motion; interpolation limited to pixels with ≥40 neighbors within ~1 km radius.
  • Line-of-sight to vertical projection: LOS velocity divided by cos(incidence angle θ, 29°–41° range) to recover vertical RLLS component, assuming minimal horizontal land motion gradients.
  • Standardized reference-point selection: zero-velocity reference chosen from candidate pixels over stable man-made structures (coherence > 0.85) using Google Earth visual identification, avoiding GNSS dependency for consistency across all 48 cities.
  • Bathtub inundation modelling (illustrative): RLLS velocities projected 10 years (to 2030) combined with CoastalDEM and regional VLM to estimate inundation extent increments for Ho Chi Minh City (+20 km²) and Rio de Janeiro (+2 km²).

A direct extension: overlay the 90 m RLLS maps with gridded population (WorldPop/GHS-POP) and socioeconomic deprivation layers to compute population-weighted RSLR exposure scores per neighborhood. This would quantify whether subsidence preferentially burdens disadvantaged communities — a question the paper raises but cannot answer from velocity maps alone. The InSAR footprint already covers Jakarta, Ho Chi Minh City, Chittagong, Yangon, and Ahmedabad, where rapid informal urbanization on flood-prone deltas suggests strong equity gradients.

② Extend the standardized InSAR pipeline to inland US metropolises and infrastructure risk

Extending this paperThe paper restricts scope to 48 coastal cities selected by distance-to-coast and low elevation (≤5 m MSL). However, the same standardized ARIA-SG/MintPy pipeline is immediately applicable to inland metropolises where subsidence threatens infrastructure (roads, bridges, pipelines) independently of sea-level. ③ Replace the linear RLLS trend assumption with a learned non-linear temporal model

Extending this paperThe paper derives a single best-fit linear velocity per pixel over 2014–2020, with standard deviation as a proxy for non-linearity. However, groundwater extraction, seasonal recharge, and anthropogenic loading are inherently non-linear and episodic. Extending the paper: apply a physics-informed temporal decomposition (e.g., separating seasonal, secular, and transient components with a Bayesian or GP model) to the MintPy displacement time series, yielding acceleration/deceleration maps rather than a single trend. This would directly address the paper's acknowledged limitation that "velocities may be accelerated, decelerated or even ceased in the long-term."

Added value to my researchThe non-linear temporal decomposition of InSAR signals is methodologically analogous to my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation for disease or mobility events: both require separating a slowly evolving spatial field from short-term fluctuations. A physics-informed GP applied to InSAR time series would port directly into my spatiotemporal analysis toolkit, and the resulting acceleration maps would feed forward into Monte Carlo-based RSLR projection with uncertainty quantification — a methodology I already use in epidemiological modeling.

④ Couple RLLS maps to flood-risk inundation models for compound hazard assessment

Extending this paperThe paper briefly demonstrates bathtub inundation for Ho Chi Minh City and Rio de Janeiro (10-year projection, +20 km² and +2 km² respectively) but treats RLLS as uniform subsidence added to mean sea-level rise. A full extension: couple the spatially heterogeneous RLLS velocity field with a hydrodynamic flood model (rather than the static bathtub), incorporating storm surge return periods, drainage networks, and time-varying groundwater pumping scenarios. This would transform the RLLS maps from a hazard characterization product into a decision-support tool for specific adaptation investments (levees, managed aquifer recharge, coastal green infrastructure).

⑤ Build a GeoAI model that predicts RLLS from urban morphology and governance features

Extending this paperThe paper produces RLLS observations but does not model why subsidence rates differ so dramatically across cities (e.g., why Tianjin subsides at 95th percentile >−20 mm/yr while Tokyo is near zero). A major extension: treat the 48-city RLLS velocity distributions as target variables and regress them against features including urban density, groundwater extraction volumes, building load intensity, sediment geology type, delta age, and regulatory quality. An explainable ML ensemble (e.g., MGWR + SHAP-attributed gradient boosting) would quantify which drivers dominate across the Asia-Pacific vs. other regions, directly testing the groundwater-extraction hypothesis at scale.
