Citizen science illuminates the nature of city lights

Christopher C. M. Kyba · Nachtlicht-BüHNE / Nachtlichter citizen-science collaboration (author list not provided in source metadata) Published 2025-06-16 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00239-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Citizen scientists using the Nachtlichter app counted and classified 234,044 light sources across ~22 km² (33 communities, mainly Germany), creating the first large-scale ground-truth inventory of city lights. The data translate satellite radiance into an intuitive unit — lights per km² (≈219 lights km⁻² per nW cm⁻² sr⁻¹ at midnight in Germany), revealing that in German city centers advertising and aesthetic lighting outnumber streetlights, and that roughly 78 ± 3 million light sources still burn across Germany at midnight — a large, currently mis-targeted mitigation opportunity.

★

Why It Matters

  • Closes the satellite "what-is-it" gap. Space sensors (VIIRS DNB) see radiance but cannot tell a streetlight from a billboard; this work supplies the missing ground-truth classification across 18 light categories, letting researchers convert radiance to installed lights per km² in human-relatable terms.
  • Refutes the streetlight-centric policy frame. Public inventories and lighting policy focus on streetlights, yet here advertising/aesthetic lights and private/commercial windows dominate — streetlights are outnumbered by a factor of 5–7 in dense urban fabric. Policy aimed only at streetlights misses most of the emission.
  • Quantifies the mitigation prize. An estimated 78 ± 3 million lights are on at midnight nationwide (vs. only ~9–9.5 million public streetlights), and only ~half of streetlights are fully shielded — concrete levers for energy, climate, and biodiversity policy.
  • Proves a scalable participatory method. 258 participants, 3,868 transects, 4,409 surveys, ~600 km walked over 500+ hours — demonstrating citizen science as a credible, low-cost complement to remote sensing for urban environmental monitoring.

Core Methods

  • Co-designed citizen-science protocol: participants walk pre-defined transects covering a reprojected DNB pixel, counting and classifying all visible lights via the app after mandatory online training (inter-observer SD ~10–20%).
  • Radiance-to-lights translation: Spearman correlation of light density vs. DNB radiance (r = 0.67 overall); a single contiguous-area estimator yields the lights-per-km² conversion factor, validated against public streetlight databases (agreement <7% in Berlin/Cologne/Dresden).
  • Time-of-night correction: a logistic switch-off model p(t) extrapolates counts to 19:00 and 00:00 for five time-varying light types; fit by a robust least-squares-like cost and validated with Monte Carlo simulation.
  • Uncertainty quantification: person-to-person variability (Monte Carlo, 2.1%), logistic-fit uncertainty (bootstrap, 3.2%), and DNB sum uncertainty combined in quadrature (~4.1% for Germany).
  • Land-cover stratification: each transect assigned a CORINE 2018 class at its midpoint; lighting mix, shielding, color and brightness analyzed by class.
  • Per-light weighting attempt (negative result): a linear cost-minimization sought physical weights wi reproducing DNB/SDGSat/aerial radiance from ground counts; all returned unphysical weights — honestly reported as a failure.

① Replace the failed linear weighting with an explainable, physics-informed radiance model

Extending this paperThe paper's attempt to assign per-light radiance weights (wi) failed — linear cost-minimization returned unphysical values (e.g. small signs heavier than large signs) because it ignores street width, building height and shielding geometry. A direct extension: build a physics-informed / explainable model that fuses ground counts with urban morphology and shielding so that an additive, non-negative radiance-conservation constraint is enforced, then use SHAP-style attribution to recover physically interpretable per-light contributions.
