Research gaps in knowledge of the impact of urban growth on biodiversity

Robert I. McDonald · Andressa V. Mansur · Fernando Ascensão · M'lisa Colbert · Katie Crossman · Thomas Elmqvist · Andrew Gonzalez · Burak Güneralp · Dagmar Haase · Maike Hamann · Oliver Hillel · Kangning Huang · Belinda Kahnt · David Maddox · Andrea Pacheco · Henrique M. Core Conclusion

A systematic review of 922 studies finds that urban growth will convert 290,000 km² of natural habitat to urban land uses between 2000 and 2030 — yet the science is geographically biased toward high-income countries, leaving the regions forecast to suffer the greatest biodiversity loss the least studied. Indirect impacts (food consumption, water use, pollution teleconnections) likely dwarf direct habitat loss in total area affected, but only 34% of studies have quantified them.

★

Why It Matters

  • Quantifies the global direct footprint. Urban expansion will consume 290,000 km² of natural habitat by 2030 — 2.9% of the entire mangrove biome and large fractions of tropical moist and temperate broadleaf forests, translating into measurable extinction pressure on 13% of terrestrial vertebrate ecoregional endemics.
  • Exposes a critical geographic blind spot. 72% of published urban-biodiversity studies come from high-income countries, yet the largest forecast losses occur in lower- and middle-income nations (China, Nigeria, Brazil, India). Policymakers in the most affected regions lack the evidence base they need.
  • Indirect impacts are massively underestimated. The food-consumption footprint of urban populations spans roughly 26.5 million km² of agricultural land — 36× the total urban area in 2015 — yet only a third of studies have tried to measure such indirect effects.
  • Defines 30 priority conservation ecoregions. By combining urban growth forecasts with vertebrate endemism data, the review identifies specific landscapes where future urban growth will cause the most irreversible damage, giving conservation funding a spatial target.
  • Sets the research agenda for the next decade. The systematic gap analysis creates a roadmap for science funders, international networks (ICLEI, Future Earth), and national governments in the Global South to close both geographic and thematic knowledge gaps before the forecast losses materialize.

Newbold et al. (2015, Nature) — 11,525 sampled sites (including 613 urban sites) with local species richness and abundance under three urban land-use intensities (minimal / light / intense). Used to quantify mean species richness and abundance declines.

Core Methods

  • Systematic literature review: searched published urban biodiversity studies from 2010–2018 (n = 922); classified each by study type (direct vs. indirect), geography, country income group (World Bank classification), and taxonomic focus to map knowledge gaps.
  • Spatial overlay analysis: intersected Seto et al. urban growth forecasts (2000–2030) with the ESA CCI Land Cover dataset to estimate area of natural habitat directly converted to urban land cover, broken down by biome type (11 biomes) and country income category.
  • Geographic bias quantification: chi-squared tests against a null hypothesis of studies distributed proportional to population in each income group (χ² = 1,521; d.f. = 3; P < 0.001 for direct impacts), confirming statistically significant over-representation of high-income countries.
  • Counterfactual scenario construction: estimated the hypothetical land area urban populations would have occupied at the global mean rural density (~58 people/km²) to benchmark the land-saving effect of urban concentration; yielded a 26× multiplier relative to 2030 projected urban area.
  • Priority ecoregion mapping: overlaid forecast habitat loss (2000–2030) with vertebrate endemism data to identify 30 priority conservation ecoregions containing 78% of all endemics threatened by urban growth.
  • Indirect impact scoping: calculated the implied agricultural land footprint of urban food consumption using FAO land statistics and UNPD urbanization rates (26.5 million km²); expressed as a multiple (36×) of total urban area to illustrate scale of indirect vs. direct impact.

① Replace static land-cover overlays with a GeoAI-based habitat-conversion probability surface

Extending this paperThe paper estimates direct habitat loss by overlaying Seto et al. urban growth forecasts with ESA CCI Land Cover in a deterministic spatial union — producing a single point estimate with no uncertainty quantification and no learning from historical conversion dynamics. A direct extension: train a spatially explicit ML model on observed CCI land-cover transitions (1992–2015) conditioned on topography, proximity to existing urban cores, road networks, and per-capita income to produce conversion probability surfaces at sub-national resolution. This would decompose the 290,000 km² headline figure into high-, medium-, and low-confidence zones and identify which landscapes are at imminent risk before the Seto et al. scenarios materialize — closing the key methodological gap the paper acknowledges in its supplementary methods.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my explainable ML for spatial prediction toolkit: SHAP-attributed gradient boosting or GWR-calibrated ensembles can identify which drivers (density gradient, road accessibility, land price, terrain) explain conversion risk locally, producing both a prediction surface and a policy-legible attribution. The habitat-conversion probability layer also functions as a spatial covariate for environmental-justice models linking green-space loss trajectories to population health outcomes — a natural bridge between urban biodiversity science and my healthy-cities research.

② Refine the priority-ecoregion ranking with hillside urban expansion data

Extending this paperThe paper's 30 priority ecoregions are derived from planimetric habitat loss and vertebrate endemism, but ignore terrain: hillside urban expansion follows different spatial dynamics (fragmentation along elevation contours, different endemic species assemblages) and is tracked by distinct data products. A recent corpus paper documents that hillside cities expand significantly faster than flat-terrain cities and threaten partially disjoint endemic species sets. Extending this paper: stratify the 30 priority ecoregions by terrain class (flat / sloped / mountain) and re-derive threat rankings using hillside-specific expansion rates, yielding a more actionable spatial conservation layer that separates lowland from upland conversion pressure.

Added value to my researchTerrain stratification directly enriches my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation methodology: incorporating a digital elevation model as a bandwidth modifier produces density surfaces that respect topographic barriers rather than treating flat and hilly cities as isotropic. This has immediate application in urban agglomeration and regional-collaboration analyses, where terrain discontinuities define functional boundaries more accurately than administrative units — improving both the spatial accuracy and interpretability of KDE-based urban delineation.

Cross-ref (corpus): Extensive terrestrial biodiversity threats from global hillside urban expansion — 10.1038/s44284-025-00316-9

③ Close the indirect-impact gap with urban telecoupling networks derived from commodity trade flows

Extending this paperThe paper flags indirect impacts (food consumption, long-distance teleconnections) as the most important unquantified research gap — only 34% of 922 studies address them — but stops short of mapping the supply chains that couple cities to distant landscapes. An extension: combine FAO commodity trade matrices with UNPD urban population forecasts and network community-detection algorithms to build a directed city-to-agricultural-landscape telecoupling graph. Each city node would carry an indirect biodiversity pressure score derived from the biome coverage of its major food-sourcing regions, disaggregating the paper's "36× multiplier" claim into city-specific, crop-specific, and biome-specific pressure estimates that can be mapped and ranked.

Added value to my researchTelecoupling graphs are a natural extension of my network community detection and job-housing network work into the food-system domain. The same community-detection algorithms I apply to identify urban functional regions can partition the global food-trade network into supply clusters; MGWR/GWR can then quantify how local city characteristics (income, dietary transition stage, population density) modulate the per-capita indirect biodiversity footprint — enabling spatial heterogeneity analysis of indirect pressures that the paper currently cannot provide.

④ Transform the geographic-bias diagnosis into an equity-aware research-allocation optimization

Extending this paperThe chi-squared finding that 72% of studies come from high-income countries while the greatest forecast losses are in lower-income nations is one of the paper's strongest contributions — but the paper does not quantify how much evidence is missing per ecoregion or per taxonomic group, nor does it prescribe how to close those gaps. A direct extension: for each of the 30 priority ecoregions, compute a "research deficit index" (forecast habitat loss × endemism threat ÷ existing study density within 500 km), then solve a fixed-budget allocation problem that maximizes total deficit reduction across ecoregions — converting the paper's diagnosis into a prescriptive funding roadmap for bodies like Future Earth or ICLEI.

Added value to my researchThis is the biodiversity analogue of my EV-charging efficiency-vs-equity optimization and 2SFCA spatial accessibility work: the research-deficit index is formally equivalent to an unmet-need surface, and the funding-allocation problem mirrors my healthcare facility-location models. The methodological transfer is direct — a two-step floating catchment area model parameterized on research output rather than clinical supply can identify the geographic zones where a given funding increment buys the greatest reduction in scientific blind spots, with results immediately actionable by international science funders.

⑤ Couple green-space accessibility metrics to within-city biodiversity outcomes via a GeoAI model

Extending this paperThe paper treats biodiversity loss as an outcome of urban land conversion at the city fringe, but does not connect the spatial configuration of urban green infrastructure — parks, street trees, squares, gardens — to its capacity to buffer biodiversity loss within already urbanized zones. A corpus paper on public-square design demonstrates measurable species richness responses to specific design features (vegetation volume, connectivity). Extending this paper: integrate its biome-scale threat framework with fine-grained urban green-space datasets and a GeoAI model that predicts within-city species richness from green-space area, shape, connectivity, and design — extending the review's mostly peri-urban focus into the dense intra-urban mosaic where future billions will live.

Added value to my researchThis creates a direct bridge between urban biodiversity science and my healthy cities and 15-minute neighborhood agenda: if green-space accessibility (measurable via my R2SFCA methods) predicts local biodiversity outcomes, then the same accessibility-optimization tools I use for healthcare facilities and EV charging can be applied to co-maximize human health and local biodiversity — a dual-objective facility-location problem with strong policy resonance for SDG 11 (Sustainable Cities) and SDG 15 (Life on Land).
