Real-world clinical and economic evaluation of remote programming for deep brain stimulation in Parkinson's disease

Lu Yang · Sun Yida · Yu Huiling · Ma Bozhi · Hu Chunhua · Wang Weiming · Jiang Changqing · Cai Xiaodong · Cui Zhiqiang · Chen Liang · Chen Wei · Chen Ling · Chen Zhijuan · Geng Xin · Guo Yi · Huang Wei · Li Weiguo · Li Dianyou · Ling Zhipei · Liang Zhanhua · Liang Siquang · Meng Fangang · Ma Yu · Niu Chaoshi · Qi Faying · Ren Haibo · Sun Peng · Tao Yingqun · Wu Xi · Wang Wei · Wang Xuelian · Xu Xin · Xu Shujun · Yang Anchao · Yang Zhiquan · Yang Chunhui · Yi Zhiqiang · Zhang Wenbin · Zhang Jie · Zhang Liangwen · Zhu Junming · Zeng Ran · Zhang Jianguo · Guan Dabo · Li Luming Published 2026-06-12 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00131-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 20,383 Parkinson's disease patients and 42,163 remote programming (RP) sessions in China (2012–2024), RP for deep brain stimulation (DBS) is clinically equivalent to conventional onsite programming while reducing the healthcare access inequality index by 30%. Economic modelling projects annual benefits of ¥1.09 billion (reduced domestic medical tourism) and ¥8.15 billion (labour savings), with cumulative gains of ¥115–270 billion by 2050 — disproportionately favouring the poorest and most remote patients, whose cost savings are two to ten times greater than those of urban peers.

Why It Matters

  • Largest real-world DBS telemedicine dataset. With 20,383 patients and 42,163 RP sessions across 439 hospitals (2012–2024), this is the most comprehensive study of interventional telemedicine at national scale, moving far beyond small feasibility trials.
  • Clinically equivalent yet dramatically more equitable. Patient-reported satisfaction and symptom improvement are statistically comparable between RP and onsite programming, yet RP reduced the demographically adjusted Parkinson Care Inequity Index (DPII) 30% faster — and halved it relative to onsite care by 2022.
  • Quantifies the equity-economy double dividend. Vulnerable groups (low-income, remote, advanced-disease) capture 2–10× higher cost savings per session, revealing a rare alignment between equity and economic efficiency that strengthens the policy case for reimbursement.
  • Long-horizon scenario modelling under population ageing. Integrated Bass diffusion, age-stratified epidemiological, and Multi-Regional Input–Output (MRIO) models project RP's cumulative economic impact through 2050–2070 under three SSP fertility/migration scenarios, translating clinical evidence into fiscal planning.
  • Blueprint for interventional telemedicine globally. The framework is generalizable to any chronic neurological condition requiring device-mediated follow-up — providing a rigorous template applicable across low- and middle-income countries where specialist concentration is a binding constraint.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Primary registry
NERCN DBS Registry (National Engineering Research Center of Neuromodulation, Tsinghua University) — 20,383 PD patients who underwent DBS implantation at 439 hospitals across mainland China, December 2012 – February 2024. Of these, 8,225 used RP and contributed 42,163 RP encounters (April 2015 – February 2024). A partial pre-2019 snapshot is publicly available via Zenodo.

3,048 patients from 216 medical centres who completed structured telephone interviews at 3 days and 6 months post-implantation (invited January 2021 – July 2022), covering satisfaction (6-point Likert), Patient Global Impression of Improvement (PGI-I), and a 22-item motor/non-motor symptom instrument.

Core Methods

  • Integrated clinical–population–economics framework: a three-module pipeline combining (1) cohort clinical analysis, (2) geospatial equity measurement, and (3) economic projection, applied jointly to the same patient registry.
  • Demographically Adjusted Parkinson Care Inequity Index (DPII): a novel Gini-type equity index that weights regional RP access by age-standardized PD burden (population × age-stratified prevalence), correcting for structural demographic differences across regions — applied longitudinally from 2015 to 2024.
  • Propensity-score matching (PSM): 1:1 nearest-neighbour matching on sex, age, disease course, implanting hospital, and geographic distance to reduce confounding in RP-vs-onsite comparisons; balance verified by absolute SMDs <0.10, yielding 745 matched pairs.
  • Bass diffusion model: fitted to the temporal adoption curve of DBS/RP (2012–2024) to project technology diffusion under low/moderate/high scenarios through 2060, parameterized by innovation rate p and imitation rate q.
  • Multi-Regional Input–Output (MRIO) modelling: China's 2017 provincial MRIO table used with an Adaptive Regional Input–Output (ARIO) framework to trace indirect (labour and demand-side) economic benefits of RP across sectors and provinces under future ageing scenarios.
  • Origin–destination travel-cost modelling: Amap geocoding converts patient addresses and hospital locations to coordinates; domestic medical tourism (DMT) costs computed as transport (high-speed rail at ¥0.46/person-km) + accommodation, disaggregated by province.
  • Statistical analysis: Wilcoxon rank-sum and signed-rank tests for non-normal outcomes; Kruskal–Wallis with Bonferroni-adjusted pairwise tests; Shapiro–Wilk normality checks; Pearson correlation for severity–savings associations. R version 4.3.1.

① Build a DPII-inspired spatial equity index for telehealth access using 2SFCA

Extending this paperThe paper's DPII elegantly corrects the Gini coefficient for age-stratified disease burden, but it is computed at the provincial/city level using aggregate RP session counts. A direct extension: replace the coarse regional aggregation with a fine-grained, network-based accessibility score — specifically an R2SFCA service-area delineation that captures the spatial friction between individual patients and RP-capable hospitals. This would reveal whether DPII's apparent equity gains reflect genuine reachability or are artifacts of aggregation, and would identify sub-city areas still beyond effective RP reach.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my 2SFCA/R2SFCA toolkit to a new domain: DBS follow-up care. The disease-burden weighting (Pop × age-stratified prevalence) maps exactly onto the demand-side of R2SFCA, while RP-capable hospitals become the supply nodes. Constructing this for Chinese prefecture cities would yield a publication-ready spatial accessibility analysis of interventional telemedicine, tightly coupled to the GBD disease prevalence data the paper already uses.

② Map the digital divide that underlies RP adoption with mobile phone data

Extending this paperThe paper notes that over 50% of non-adopters cited digital illiteracy or lack of caregiver support, and that rural/elderly populations face connectivity barriers — but this barrier is modelled only qualitatively. A concrete extension: link the RP session registry to a mobile phone data layer (e.g., 4G/5G coverage maps, smartphone ownership rates by age cohort and prefecture) to quantify how much of the residual DPII gap is explained by infrastructure deprivation versus clinical or attitudinal factors. This would close the circuit from clinical equity to digital infrastructure policy.

Fusing the NERCN RP session geography with prefecture-level connectivity statistics (or commercial signal-strength rasters) gives a concrete spatial digital-divide analysis that connects to my telehealth/digital-divide research theme. The resulting "RP accessibility under connectivity constraints" map would be publishable as a standalone health geography study.

③ Integrate explainable ML to predict which patients will benefit most from RP

Extending this paperThe paper uses propensity-score matching and Pearson correlation to characterize RP adoption and cost-savings heterogeneity, but the analysis is stratified by pre-defined regions rather than individual-level feature importance. An extension: train an explainable gradient-boosted model (XGBoost with SHAP) on the 3,048-patient follow-up cohort to predict which patients will achieve the highest satisfaction/cost-saving gains from RP, using variables the registry already captures (age, disease stage, geographic distance, household income proxy, digital literacy, care frequency). This would operationalize the paper's equity findings into a targeting algorithm for clinical practice.

Added value to my researchThis is a straightforward application of my explainable ML for spatial prediction agenda: the "spatial feature" here is geographic distance and regional medical resource scarcity. SHAP decomposition across spatial strata would reveal whether the high benefits in remote populations are driven by distance itself, income, or disease severity — separating geographic from socioeconomic mechanisms, a core concern in health geography.

④ Extend the MRIO economic model to a full global health-system spillover analysis

Extending this paperThe paper's MRIO analysis is restricted to China's provincial trade structure (2017 benchmark table), and the ARIO labour-shock component assumes a Leontief production function with no factor substitution. Two direct extensions of the paper's own economic module: (1) replace the static 2017 MRIO with a dynamic time-series of tables (the CEADs dataset includes annual tables) to capture structural change; and (2) apply the same framework cross-nationally — at least for OECD countries with comparable DBS registries — to test whether the China-derived ¥1.09 billion / ¥8.15 billion ratio generalizes across healthcare financing systems.

⑤ Use spatiotemporal diffusion modelling to project RP adoption in low-resource settings

Extending this paperThe paper fits a Bass diffusion model to China's national RP adoption curve and projects city-level demand under three SSP pathways, but the diffusion parameters (p, q) are estimated from a single national trajectory. Extending it: decompose adoption into spatial diffusion waves — using a spatiotemporal kernel density + network community detection approach — to identify regional "seed cities" that accelerate diffusion into their hinterlands. This would make the diffusion model spatially explicit, enabling targeted infrastructure investment (e.g., which tier-3 cities to prioritize for RP centre establishment) rather than aggregate projections.

Added value to my researchSpatiotemporal kernel density estimation and network community detection are core methods in my toolkit. Applying them to the RP session geography (the paper's Fig. 2 maps show clear diffusion waves from Beijing/Shanghai/Guangzhou to periphery) would produce a spatial diffusion study of interventional telemedicine that bridges health geography and GeoAI — a novel intersection that could be framed as a general method for monitoring digital health technology diffusion using administrative health records.
