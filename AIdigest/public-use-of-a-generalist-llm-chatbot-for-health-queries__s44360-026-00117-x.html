Public use of a generalist LLM chatbot for health queries

Beatriz Costa-Gomes · Pavel Tolmachev · Eloise Taysom · Viknesh Sounderajah · Hannah Richardson · Philipp Schoenegger · Xiaoxuan Liu · Matthew M. Nour · Seth Spielman · Samuel F. Way · Yash Shah · Michael Bhaskar · Harsha Nori · Christopher Kelly · Peter Hames · Bay Gross · Mustafa Suleyman · Dominic King Published 2026-04-16 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00117-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

An analysis of over 500,000 de-identified Microsoft Copilot health conversations from January 2026 reveals that nearly one in five conversations involves personal symptom assessment, and personal health queries spike sharply in the evening and nighttime when traditional healthcare is least accessible. Usage diverges strikingly by device — mobile concentrates on personal health and emotional well-being, while desktop is dominated by professional research and medical paperwork — and one in seven personal health queries concerns a third party (child, parent, or partner), reframing conversational AI as a caregiving tool alongside a self-care one.

★

Why It Matters

  • First large-scale taxonomy of health AI intent. With N = 617,827 conversations and a validated 12-category intent taxonomy, this is one of the first studies to characterize what people actually ask a general-purpose LLM chatbot about their health at population scale.
  • Reveals unmet after-hours healthcare demand. Personal symptom queries and emotional well-being conversations increase markedly at night, quantifying the temporal gap where traditional healthcare is unavailable and AI fills the void.
  • Reframes the user population. One in seven personal health queries is asked on behalf of a dependent (child, elderly parent, partner), showing that health AI is already a caregiving tool with implications for design and safety beyond the individual user.
  • Device as a contextual signal for intent. Mobile vs. desktop divergence is strong and statistically significant, providing actionable evidence for platform-specific AI health experience design.
  • Quantifies AI's role in healthcare navigation. A substantial share of queries concern finding providers, understanding insurance and completing paperwork — revealing friction in existing healthcare systems and new roles for AI beyond clinical information.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Primary dataset
Microsoft Copilot de-identified conversation logs, randomly sampled from January 2026. Health conversations extracted as those classified as "Health and Fitness" in an upstream 30-topic classifier. Final intent-analysis sample: N = 617,827 conversations.
Geographic scope
Global; approximately 22% from the USA and the remainder from across the world. Approximately 45% in English. Timestamp normalization applied using local timezones.
Privacy pipeline
Two-stage: automated PII scrubbing (names, addresses, SSN, financial details) followed by LLM-generated privacy-preserving English summaries. All subsequent analysis operated on summaries only — "eyes-off" model with no human access to raw conversation content.
Intent taxonomy
12-category hierarchical taxonomy developed a priori by 4 clinician scientists (mean 13.5 years post-graduation experience). Validated on n = 131 human-reviewed conversations at 84% exact-match agreement with the LLM classifier.
Topic clustering
LLM-driven TnT-LLM clustering applied to a random subsample of n = 10,000 conversations preserving the intent distribution. Batches of ~250 conversation summaries per intent category. No model fine-tuning; in-context prompting only.
Caregiver subsample
n = 2,165 conversations from three personal health intents (Symptom Questions, Condition Information, Emotional Well-being) annotated for "who the query is about" (self / dependent / other / unknown).
Availability
Data cannot be shared publicly due to privacy constraints and Microsoft data-governance policies. Analysis code is internal and proprietary. No open data or code release.

Core Methods

  • Privacy-preserving pipeline: two-stage de-identification — automated PII scrubbing followed by LLM-generated conversation summaries. All downstream analysis on summaries, never raw text.
  • Hierarchical intent taxonomy (step 1): a priori 12-category taxonomy developed iteratively by clinician scientists, applied via LLM-based classifier; validated at 84% exact-match agreement against human annotation of 131 conversations.
  • LLM-driven topic clustering (step 2): TnT-LLM method — batches of ~250 conversation summaries per intent category grouped by the LLM into named thematic clusters; consolidation step merges overlapping clusters across batches. In-context prompting only, no fine-tuning.
  • Confidence intervals: Wilson score 95% CIs computed for all percentage estimates to quantify uncertainty.
  • Platform and temporal inference: Pearson's chi-squared test of independence for mobile-vs-desktop intent distributions (χ²(8, N=612,330) = 73,981.6, p<0.001) and hourly intent shifts (χ²(23, N=613,026) = 903.3–1,445.8, p<0.001).
  • Timezone normalization: all timestamps normalized to local timezone for diurnal analysis. Missing-data exclusion rate below 1% for all variables.

① Spatialize the intent taxonomy: where do after-hours health AI queries originate?

Extending this paperThis paper characterizes when personal health queries spike (evenings and nights) and on which device, but has no spatial dimension — it cannot tell whether the after-hours, mobile-centric health queries come disproportionately from areas with poor primary-care access (rural, underserved urban neighborhoods) or from all areas equally. A direct extension would link de-identified conversation-level local timezone and coarse region metadata to healthcare access indicators (e.g., physician-to-population ratios, FQHC coverage) to test whether AI chatbot health use is compensatory for access deficits or complementary everywhere. A community-codesigned chatbot RCT in primary-care-constrained settings already demonstrates this demand signal exists at the point of care.

Added value to my researchThis is precisely my spatial accessibility agenda: using the paper's diurnal intent patterns as a demand proxy for after-hours primary-care deficit, I could layer them onto 2SFCA-derived spatial accessibility surfaces to identify areas where AI chatbot use is filling access gaps — a novel "digital access gap" indicator for health geography and telehealth equity research.

② Trust and health literacy as moderators of intent: linking chatbot use patterns to user beliefs

Extending this paperThe paper reveals what users ask but cannot explain why some turn to AI for personal symptom assessment versus general information. A natural extension is to link intent patterns to user-level trust in digital health and health information quality perceptions — the same variables measured in the global trust survey corpus paper. If low-trust users preferentially ask general questions while high-trust users ask personal symptom questions, that has direct implications for responsible AI design: the users most willing to act on AI health advice may be exactly those who should be most carefully redirected to professionals.

Added value to my researchFor my digital divide and telehealth research line, this creates a joined analytical framework: spatial accessibility (2SFCA surface) × digital health literacy (trust survey) × AI usage intent (this paper's taxonomy) → a three-layer model of who uses health AI how, and where the equity risks are greatest. This is directly applicable to my work on telehealth/digital divides.

Cross-ref (corpus): A global survey on trust, digital health literacy and health information quality — 10.1038/s44360-026-00102-4

③ From intent classification to outcome prediction: does AI response quality differ by intent category?

Extending this paperThe paper's acknowledged limitation is that it observes queries but not outcomes — it cannot determine whether users received good advice or were appropriately redirected to professional care. An extension using the paper's 12-category taxonomy as a stratification variable for a response-quality evaluation study would directly address this: classify responses within each intent category using clinical expert review or automated safety metrics, and test whether quality degrades in the high-stakes personal intents (symptom questions, emotional well-being) where the paper shows concentration. Improving ML quality in health applications requires exactly this stratified evaluation framework.

Added value to my researchFor my explainable machine learning work, this creates an opportunity to apply SHAP-based attribution to a health response quality classifier: which features of the conversation (intent category, time of day, device type, query length) most strongly predict response quality or safety failures? The paper's taxonomy becomes a structured input to an explainable health AI auditing framework.

④ Fairness and bias auditing across the 12-intent taxonomy

Extending this paperThe paper notes that ~45% of conversations are in English, yet the dataset is global. An important extension is a fairness analysis: do LLM responses within personal health intents (symptom assessment, emotional well-being) differ in quality, completeness or safety across languages, regions or demographic proxies? The paper's taxonomy and platform-level disaggregation provide the structural scaffolding needed for a systematic bias audit — testing whether the LLM classifier itself introduces language bias (systematically miscategorizing non-English conversations into "Other Health/Fitness") and whether response quality degrades for non-English speakers in high-stakes intents.

⑤ Healthcare navigation queries as an indicator of healthcare system friction

Extending this paperThe paper observes that a meaningful fraction of health AI queries concern navigating healthcare systems (finding providers, understanding insurance, completing paperwork) — which it interprets as a signal of "unmet need for streamlining administrative aspects of accessing healthcare." A direct extension is to use the prevalence of these navigation queries as a spatially disaggregated indicator of healthcare system complexity and access barriers: where navigation queries are highest (after controlling for overall AI usage), the healthcare system is likely most complex or fragmented. This bridges the paper's behavioral signal to the well-documented relationship between healthcare access disparities and the reliability of health data.

Added value to my researchFor my health geography and healthcare facility access research, this proposes a new proxy variable: AI-chatbot navigation query density as a digital indicator of spatial healthcare access barriers, analogous to how I use 2SFCA supply-demand ratios. Combined with the access-to-care and EHR reliability corpus paper, this creates a triangulated view: areas with poor spatial access → more EHR gaps → more AI navigation queries — a reinforcing cycle that my spatiotemporal GeoAI framework could model and intervene on.
