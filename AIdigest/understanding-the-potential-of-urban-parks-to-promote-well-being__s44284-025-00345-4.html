Understanding the potential of urban parks to promote well-being

Linus W. Dietz · Sanja Šćepanović · Ke Zhou · André Felipe Zanella · Daniele Quercia Published 2025-11-21 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00345-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Parks are more than green polygons: by mapping the elements and spaces inside them to six health-promoting activities, the study scored 23,477 parks across 35 cities using OpenStreetMap. The results expose clear, actionable patterns — North American parks favor physical activity while European parks favor nature appreciation; inner-city parks consistently out-equip peripheral ones (challenging the "greener suburbs" assumption); cultural and social features are the scarcest and most unequally distributed; and city-level inequity ranges widely, from balanced Tokyo and Paris to sharply unequal Copenhagen and Rio de Janeiro.

★

Why It Matters

  • Moves park assessment from quantity to quality at scale. Most prior work treated parks as size/distance polygons; this is a fine-grained, globally scalable audit of what a park actually offers — benches, courts, ponds, meadows — linked to concrete health uses.
  • Turns crowdsourced map data into a health metric. A reusable lexicon of 1,441 OSM tags mapped to activities means any city with reasonable OSM coverage can be scored without expensive field surveys (which existing tools like SOPARC require).
  • Reframes the equity narrative. It shows inequity is not only about access to greenery but about the distribution of health-promoting amenities — and that the periphery, not the center, is most under-served.
  • Directly usable by planners. Expert interviews confirmed the scores serve as a quantitative baseline for master planning and a gap-finder for districts; the project page and replication package make this operational.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Study scope
23,477 parks across 35 cities on 5 continents (Table 1). Cities selected by three criteria: population ≥ 650,000 (exception for Christchurch), country internet access ≥ 80%, and ≥ 1/8 of park area carrying health-related OSM tags. China largely excluded due to restricted non-governmental mapping.
Primary map data
OpenStreetMap snapshot from fall 2023 — park elements (points: benches, trees, statues) and park spaces (areas: meadows, lakes, forests). Over 30,000 unique key–value pairs encountered; full lexicon spans 1,441 OSM tags.
Activity taxonomy
Six activity categories (physical, mindfulness, nature appreciation, environmental, social, cultural) distilled by a 3-expert panel from 91 unique scholarly papers. Mindfulness was dropped — no OSM tags mapped to it.
Validation data 1
Flickr geotagged photos (posted 2004–2015): 10,788,686 images within park boundaries; semantic matching yielded 2,171 Flickr-to-OSM matches (1,432 health-related). Mean OSM–Flickr correlation = 0.39 (σ = 0.07). Flickr data cannot be shared per its terms.
Validation data 2
Wikipedia pages of top-scoring parks, used to qualitatively confirm activity rankings (e.g., Ueno Park for cultural, Bois de Boulogne for physical).
Annotation model
GPT-4 (temperature 0.9) as the tag-to-activity classifier (best F1 = 0.77 vs. 100 expert-labeled tags); sentence embeddings via all-mpnet-base-v2 (S-BERT), cosine threshold 0.7.
Availability
Replication package (park health-score tables, Python & R code) at social-dynamics.net/healthy-parks; raw OSM obtainable from third-party servers such as Geofabrik.

Core Methods

  • Activity lexicon construction: a literature-grounded, expert-defined taxonomy of six health-promoting activities, with each OSM tag classified into a category (or "none") by an LLM benchmarked against expert labels.
  • Density-normalized scoring: per-city log–log linear regressions of element count / space area against park area (eq. 1) define an "average park line"; each park's score is its residual, capturing whether it over- or under-provides relative to similar-sized local parks.
  • Score combination: element and space residuals are z-score normalized and averaged (eq. 2) into one combined score per activity; a value near 0 = city average, ±1 = one SD from it.
  • Multi-tag proportional assignment: facilities matching several activities (e.g., apple tree → nature + environmental) are split proportionally to avoid underestimating secondary uses.
  • Inequality quantification: a min–max-normalized Gini-style disparity index (eq. 3, 0 = equality, 1 = max inequality) per city and activity, plus a distance-to-center quartile analysis.
  • Cross-source validation: Flickr photo tags semantically matched to OSM tags (S-BERT embeddings) and compared via Pearson correlation; Wikipedia and expert interviews provide qualitative checks.

Expanded Ideas — this paper's extensibility & added value to my research

Each idea stays anchored to this paper: first how the paper itself could be extended (drawing on our corpus), then the added value for my research lines (migis.net — GeoAI, 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility, explainable & physics-informed graph learning, multi-source data fusion).

① Couple supply-side park scores with demand-side accessibility (2SFCA)

Extending this paperThe paper scores what each park contains but stops at the park boundary — it never asks who can reach it. A direct extension: feed the per-activity park scores in as the "supply" term of a 2SFCA/R2SFCA model, weighted by surrounding population density and walking-network travel time, so a city's map of "physical-activity capacity" becomes a map of realized physical-activity accessibility. This converts the paper's static catalogue into a population-weighted equity diagnostic the authors explicitly call for.

Added value to my researchThis gives my R2SFCA pipeline a rich, activity-typed supply signal instead of a single bed/POI count — letting me compute separate accessibility surfaces for mental-restoration, social, and physical health, exactly the kind of disaggregated service-area delineation my health-geography work targets.

Cross-ref (corpus): Unequal access to social, environmental and health amenities in US urban parks — 10.1038/s44284-024-00153-2

② Replace the GPT-4 zero-shot tagger with a fine-tuned geospatial foundation model

Extending this paperThe lexicon hinges on GPT-4 mapping OSM tags to activities at F1 = 0.77 — leaving roughly a quarter mislabeled, and it ignores spatial context (a "bench" next to a court vs. a pond). Extending the paper: fine-tune a geospatial/multimodal foundation model that ingests the tag plus its neighborhood graph and satellite/street-view imagery, raising classification accuracy and unlocking the mindfulness category the paper had to drop entirely.

Added value to my researchThis is a concrete testbed for my foundation-model fine-tuning agenda: a reusable "OSM-tag → function" classifier conditioned on local geographic context, transferable to my POI-based accessibility and facility-typing work far beyond parks.

Cross-ref (corpus): A spatio-temporal analysis investigating completeness and inequalities of global urban building data in OpenStreetMap — 10.1038/s41467-023-39698-6

③ Correct the OSM completeness bias the paper acknowledges but does not model

Extending this paperThe paper's 1/8-tagged-area inclusion rule and its exclusion of China reveal that scores are entangled with OSM completeness, which itself correlates with wealth and "tech-savvy" contributors. Extending it: build an explicit completeness model (per-city, per-quartile mapping intensity) and treat observed scores as completeness-discounted estimates — separating real provision gaps from data gaps, especially in under-mapped peripheries.

Added value to my researchCompleteness-aware weighting maps straight onto my digital-divide and data-fusion work: a generalizable correction layer for any volunteered-geographic-information input, letting me fuse OSM with remote sensing and mobile-phone data while quantifying the uncertainty each source injects.

Cross-ref (corpus): Improved human greenspace exposure equality during 21st century urbanization — 10.1038/s41467-023-41620-z

④ Validate park-activity scores against actual behavior and health outcomes

Extending this paperValidation rests on Flickr photos and Wikipedia — both record what is photogenic, not what is used (and Flickr ends in 2015). The authors flag this. Extending it: link the activity scores to objective behavior (wearable step/activity counts, mobile-phone dwell time) and, where available, prescription/medical data, to test whether a high "physical" score actually yields more measured physical activity.

Added value to my researchPairing infrastructure scores with GPS-trajectory and mobile-phone dwell data is precisely my space–human coupling theme; it turns the paper's supply lexicon into a calibrated dose–response input for public-health policy modeling.

Cross-ref (corpus): Wearable data link urban green space to physical activity — 10.1038/s44360-025-00011-y

⑤ Make the scores prescriptive: optimize equitable upgrades with explainable ML

Extending this paperThe disparity index diagnoses inequity but offers no allocation plan, and the paper warns that upgrades can trigger gentrification. Extending it: wrap the scores in a multi-objective facility-placement optimizer (which elements/spaces to add to which peripheral parks) balancing equity gain against gentrification risk, with explainable ML attributing which features drive each park's score change.

Added value to my researchThis reuses the efficiency-vs-equity machinery from my EV-charging optimization, now on park amenities, and the explainable-ML attribution layer is directly my spatial-prediction interpretability line — a transferable "diagnose → explain → allocate" GeoAI loop.
