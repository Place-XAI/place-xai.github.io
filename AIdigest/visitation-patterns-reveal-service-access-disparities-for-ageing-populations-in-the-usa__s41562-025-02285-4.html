Visitation patterns reveal service access disparities for ageing populations in the USA

Yilong Han · Peng Liao · Weiyu Li · Qi R. Wang Published 2025-08-15 DOI 10.1038/s41562-025-02285-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using large-scale SafeGraph mobility data across all US Census Block Groups, this study shows that high-ageing communities (where >50% of residents are 65+) generally exhibit higher revealed accessibility to healthcare, grocery, and housing services than low-ageing communities — but this advantage is unevenly distributed, regionally variable, and projected to erode by 2060 as demographics shift. Critically, up to 70% of high-ageing CBGs in some states lack any access to ageing-specific services within 5 miles, exposing deep intra-group service deserts that aggregate statistics conceal.

Why It Matters

  • Shifts the debate from supply to revealed demand. Prior accessibility research counts facilities; this study measures whether residents actually visit them — revealing that 50.4% of high-ageing CBGs nationally have zero revealed access to elder-specific services despite formal availability.
  • National scale with CBG-level resolution. Covering 2,306 high-ageing and 164,604 low-ageing CBGs across the entire USA, the analysis exposes regional heterogeneity that state-level statistics obscure, with Florida, Arizona, and California showing divergent patterns.
  • Forward-looking projection to 2060. The fairness index is projected under US Census Bureau population forecasts, showing that the current accessibility advantage enjoyed by some high-ageing communities will diminish — and in some states reverse — as older populations grow.
  • Introduces a transferable fairness metric. The ΔF̄ index is scalable to any geographic unit (county, state, nation) and any service type, providing a quantitative complement to qualitative age-friendly city frameworks like the WHO's age-friendly cities model.
  • Identifies actionable intervention targets. Six states (AZ, CA, FL, NJ, NY, TX) account for over 70% of high-ageing CBGs with unmet service needs, giving policymakers a concrete spatial priority list.

Core Methods

  • CBG classification: Census Block Groups classified as high-ageing (≥50% aged 65+) or low-ageing (<20% aged 65+), yielding a binary comparative design across the full USA.
  • Revealed accessibility metric A: proportion of a CBG's total visits to a given service type (HCS/GFS/HRE) that fall within a 5-mile radius of the CBG centroid. Captures actual usage rather than mere proximity; tested at 7.5- and 10-mile thresholds for robustness.
  • Fairness index ΔF̄: normalized distribution distance between the revealed accessibility distributions of high-ageing and low-ageing CBGs within any geographic unit. Based on stochastic dominance principles; values range from −1 (fully youth-friendly) to +1 (fully age-friendly).
  • OLS regression with state fixed effects: country-wide and state-level OLS models regressing revealed accessibility on high-ageing status, socio-demographic controls (income, education, minority share, low-wage workers, visit/population ratio), and built-environment controls (land use, walkability index, urban area indicator).
  • Statistical testing: Welch's t-tests for group comparisons; Mann-Whitney U tests for non-normal distributions; Shapiro-Wilk normality tests; Benjamini-Hochberg correction for multiple comparisons; Cohen's d effect sizes reported.
  • Demographic projection: CBG-level ageing proportions projected to 2030 and 2060 by applying national Census Bureau growth rates, then fairness index recomputed under these shifted demographics, holding service locations fixed.
  • Ageing-specific services: four NAICS-coded service categories specifically relevant to older adults (nursing/residential care, elder services, home health care, personal care) analyzed separately; CBGs with A=0 quantified to reveal service deserts.

① Replace the fixed-radius revealed accessibility with a mobility-flow-informed distance-decay 2SFCA

Extending this paperThe paper's revealed accessibility metric A uses a binary 5-mile buffer to classify visits as "local" or not — a simple but blunt instrument. A direct extension: replace the binary threshold with a continuous, CBG-pair-specific distance-decay function learned from the same SafeGraph flows. This reframes the paper's revealed accessibility as a demand-weighted, floating catchment measure, making it more sensitive to the spatial friction that limits older adults' mobility. The mobility constraints documented in Fig. 1 (high-ageing CBGs travel ~16% shorter distances) provide the empirical basis for a tighter, demographically stratified decay curve.

For my spatial accessibility of primary care and mental health research, this yields a demand-side correction term that is demographic-specific, not just geographic.

② Apply the fairness index ΔF̄ to telehealth and digital service access for older adults

Extending this paperThe paper's fairness index is computed for physical services (HCS, GFS, HRE) that require bodily travel. A natural extension is to compute ΔF̄ for digitally-delivered services — telehealth consultations, online grocery delivery, and digital housing platforms — and compare whether the accessibility advantage of high-ageing CBGs persists, shrinks, or inverts when the spatial friction is removed. The paper's own finding that mobility-constrained older adults spend 87.7% of their time at home (vs. 80.4% for low-ageing CBGs) raises the question: does digital substitution reduce the gap, or does a digital divide create a new one?

I can use this paper's CBG-level ageing composition as an explanatory variable in my models of telehealth adoption rates, testing whether high-ageing CBGs with high physical A also show high digital service uptake (complementary access) or vice versa (substitutive access). The ΔF̄ framework generalizes naturally to a digital-vs-physical access fairness comparison.

③ Extend the projection analysis with spatially explicit migration and urban growth scenarios

Extending this paperThe paper's 2030/2060 projections apply a single national growth rate uniformly across all CBGs — a deliberate simplification the authors acknowledge. A richer extension would couple the paper's ΔF̄ projection model with spatial migration flows and urban growth models, allowing high-ageing CBGs to gain or lose residents through selective ageing-in-place, retirement migration (relevant given Florida's already-extreme ageing), and urban densification. The paper already identifies migration as an unexplored confound (Supplementary Section 11); making it explicit would substantially sharpen the policy relevance of Fig. 7.

④ Build an explainable ML model to predict which CBGs will develop accessibility deserts

Extending this paperThe paper's regression (Table 1) uses OLS with fixed effects and identifies built-environment and socio-demographic correlates of accessibility, but offers no predictive model for which CBGs are at risk of becoming A=0 deserts. An extension: train a gradient boosting or GWR-based model on the paper's full feature set (walkability, land use, income, minority share, ageing proportion) to predict the probability of A=0 for ageing-specific services. SHAP-based explainability would reveal which features most differentiate "serviced" from "desert" CBGs — directly actionable for planners.

⑤ Incorporate brain-health and cognitive outcomes to close the loop from access to health consequence

Extending this paperThe paper quantifies revealed accessibility but does not link it to health outcomes — a limitation the authors note. A powerful extension: use the paper's CBG-level A and ΔF̄ metrics as independent variables in health outcome models, connecting service accessibility deficits to measurable cognitive or neurological consequences in older adults. The paper's finding that mobility-constrained ageing populations spend more time at home suggests isolation; quantifying whether low A predicts accelerated cognitive decline or hippocampal volume reduction would directly elevate the paper from a planning to a public health contribution.
