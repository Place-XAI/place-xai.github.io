Progress and gaps in climate change adaptation in coastal cities across the globe

Mia Wannewitz · Idowu Ajibade · Katharine J. Mach · Alexandre Magnan · Jan Petzold · Diana Reckien · Nicola Ulibarri · … · Matthias Garschagen Published 2024-08-26 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00106-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A systematic global stocktake of the peer-reviewed literature on 199 coastal cities finds that adaptation is slow, narrow in scope and non-transformative. Measures are designed mostly around past and current hazards, exposure and vulnerability rather than future trends. There is a sharp income gradient: high-income city governments lead with institutional/infrastructural responses, while in lower-middle-income cities the adaptation burden falls on households via behavioral change. Knowledge is highly uneven — the Global South, ecosystem-based adaptation, and future-oriented risk assessment are systematically under-documented.

Why It Matters

  • First global, systematic stocktake for coastal urban adaptation. It turns scattered case studies into a comparable, evidence-mapped picture of whether cities are on track — a direct knowledge base for the Paris Agreement's Global Stocktake.
  • Exposes a structural equity gap. The poorer the country, the more adaptation is left to individuals/households; the richer, the more it is carried by city governments — quantifying who bears the adaptation burden.
  • Flags a dangerous "rear-view-mirror" bias. Adaptation keyed to historical hazards and current exposure — without quantified future scenarios — risks systematic under-protection as climate and urbanization accelerate.
  • Maps the blind spots. Small/mid-sized cities in Africa, Asia and Latin America, and ecosystem-based adaptation, are barely represented in the English-language literature — a research-funding priority list.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Evidence sample
683 publications entered screening; 183 included after applying inclusion/exclusion criteria. These cover 284 cases across 199 coastal cities/settlements (plus 4 unspecified urban areas) worldwide. Each case was coded with a 30-question questionnaire.
Literature search
GAMI database (Global Adaptation Mapping Initiative, >1,600 climate-adaptation articles; 361 "cities and settlements by the sea" entries) extended via a Boolean search of Web of Science (Core Collection) and Scopus, peer-reviewed English-language only, 2013–2020 (spanning IPCC AR5–AR6 cut-offs).
Population / exposure
Low Elevation Coastal Zone (LECZ) Urban-Rural Population and Land Area Estimates, v3 (CIESIN/NASA, 2021), using Gridded Population of the World v4 (GPWv4), Rev. 11 and the CoastalDEM90 elevation product to derive LECZ (0–10 m) population shares used to benchmark sample representativeness.
Economic strata
World Bank Country and Lending Groups (income classification) and GNI per capita used as explanatory variables for actor/response patterns.
Base map

Coding & stats stack
Coding via SoSci Survey v3.5.01; analysis in IBM SPSS Statistics 23. Reliability: ~10% (72 publications) double-coded; ~80% inter-coder convergence; only medium/high-confidence transformation judgements retained.

Core Methods

  • Systematic evidence map (ROSES protocol): transparent four-tiered search, screening and de-duplication producing a reproducible flow diagram (Extended Data Fig. 1).
  • Structured content coding (GAMI): one questionnaire per city (30 questions) capturing hazard type, exposure/vulnerability, actor type, response type, and the three transformation dimensions — depth, speed, scope.
  • Transformation assessment: coders rated depth/speed/scope as low/medium/high with confidence levels; only medium/high-confidence judgements entered the final analysis.
  • Stratified comparison: patterns assessed across World Bank income groups, world regions, and city-size classes (<250k, 250k–1M, >1M, megacities).
  • Correlation analysis: Spearman's ρ linking GNI per capita and city size to actor/response patterns; χ² tests with the ϕ coefficient for associations between actor and response categories.
  • Representativeness benchmarking: sample shares compared against LECZ population shares per region/income group to quantify over- and under-representation.

① Close the Global-South gap with multi-source data fusion beyond English peer review

Extending this paperThe authors' own central limitation is that the sample is peer-reviewed, English-language only, systematically under-counting small/mid-sized cities in Africa, Asia and Latin America. A direct extension: fuse the coded literature with grey literature, NGO/government reports, news and social-media signals via multilingual NLP, then re-estimate the income/region representativeness benchmarks. This would test whether the "slow, narrow, non-transformative" verdict is real or partly an artifact of the evidence base.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my multi-source data-fusion + FAIR/open-science agenda: building a reproducible pipeline that fuses mobile-phone, social-media and remote-sensing signals with documentary evidence to de-bias a spatial sample — the same correction I need for healthcare-access studies where formal records under-represent underserved populations.

③ From "past-and-current" framing to forward-looking, scenario-based exposure surfaces

Extending this paperThe paper's most alarming finding is that adaptation is keyed to historical hazards and current exposure, rarely to quantified future scenarios. Extending it: couple each coded city with downscaled SSP/RCP population-and-exposure projections on the LECZ grid to compute, per city, the gap between the future-risk it should be planning for and the time-horizon its reported adaptation actually addresses — an operational "future-readiness index".

④ Model the income–actor adaptation gradient as a spatially non-stationary process

Extending this paperThe paper reports global Spearman/ϕ correlations (e.g. richer cities → city-government action; poorer → household burden) as single coefficients. Extending it: re-estimate these relationships with MGWR / geographically weighted models and explainable ML to reveal where the income–actor gradient is steepest, where it breaks, and which contextual features (informality, governance capacity, hazard mix) drive the residual burden on households.

⑤ Add a resilience/efficiency–equity optimization layer for ecosystem-based adaptation siting

Extending this paperThe paper identifies ecosystem-based adaptation as the least-reported, most-neglected response type and warns of maladaptive lock-in from hard infrastructure. Extending it: build a facility/measure-siting optimization that places green-infrastructure and protective measures to jointly maximize risk reduction (efficiency), distribution across vulnerable groups (equity) and robustness to facility failure (resilience) — giving planners the mixed-strategy portfolio the paper calls for.
