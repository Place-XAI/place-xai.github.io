Environmental burden and health inequity in China's road-based express delivery

Baojie Li · Hong Liao · Ke Li · Jintai Lin · Cheng Gong · Huan Liu · Yan Li · Lei Chen · Yang Yang · Xipeng Jin · Yongqi Zhao · Teng Wang · Jianbing Jin · Ruijun Dang · Daniel J. Jacob Published 2025-08-15 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00300-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

China's road-based express delivery emitted 23.9 Mt CO₂-equivalent and 166.4 kt atmospheric-pollutant-equivalent in 2021, and the burden is profoundly unequal across space. Transit regions connecting major urban agglomerations handled only 12.7% of parcels yet bore 37.3% of emissions, with 75.2% of their pollution-related premature deaths caused by other regions' deliveries. Delivery pollution caused ~5,100 premature deaths in 2021, but synergistic mitigation could avert over 256,000 cumulative premature deaths by 2050 — exposing the cross-regional externalities hidden behind urban e-commerce convenience.

Why It Matters

  • Names a fast-growing, previously invisible emission source. E-commerce parcel volumes have exploded, yet the cross-regional environmental and health footprint of the transport leg had not been quantified. This is a spatially explicit, nationwide accounting of that footprint.
  • Reframes delivery as a spatial-justice problem. The work shows that where parcels pass through, not where they are bought or consumed, determines who breathes the pollution — transit corridors absorb harm generated by distant consumers.
  • Quantifies the consumption–exposure mismatch. A region can handle a small share of parcels yet inherit a large share of deaths, with three-quarters of its mortality "imported" from others' logistics — a concrete metric of environmental inequity.
  • Turns the analysis into actionable policy futures. Scenario projections to 2050 show that synergistic decarbonization + clean-air strategies could prevent >256,000 cumulative premature deaths, giving planners a quantified prize for sustainable logistics.
  • Ships an open gridded inventory. The gridded road-based express-delivery emission inventory is released on Zenodo, becoming reusable infrastructure for transport, health, and climate research.

Core Methods

  • Spatially explicit emission inventory: integrate large-scale shipping records with geospatial network modeling to build a gridded road-based express-delivery emission inventory (GHG + air pollutants) for China, 2021.
  • Route & distance modeling: identify delivery routes and compute gridded transportation distances over the OpenStreetMap network using ArcGIS Network Analyst / Spatial Analyst, then allocate emissions spatially.
  • Atmospheric chemical-transport modeling: simulate the resulting air quality with GEOS-Chem v13.4.0 and WRF-Chem v4.6.0, driven by ERA5/MERRA-2 and ABaCAS-EI background emissions.
  • Health-burden attribution: combine simulated pollution fields with LandScan population to estimate premature mortality, then trace cross-provincial attribution (deaths flow to the regions whose deliveries caused them).
  • Inequality metrics: quantify the mismatch between parcel handling share and emission/death share, exposing transit-region inequity.
  • Scenario projection: project emissions and health impacts to 2050 under multiple mitigation scenarios; Monte Carlo analysis characterizes uncertainty.

① Push the inventory from inter-city transport to the last mile

Extending this paperThe paper resolves the road-based, inter-regional transport leg, but the parcel's final hop — vans, e-bikes, and depots inside dense neighborhoods — is exactly where exposure meets the most people. Extending it: graft a last-mile delivery emission module onto the existing gridded inventory, so the cross-regional ledger closes at the doorstep and the transit-region inequity can be compared against intra-city exposure.

Added value to my researchLast-mile delivery is a POI-and-network problem I can fuse with my multi-source data (POIs, GPS trajectories, mobile-phone records) to model courier service areas — effectively a supply-side R2SFCA of logistics load at neighborhood scale.

② Make cross-regional mortality attribution an explainable transfer matrix

Extending this paperThe paper's striking result — 75.2% of transit-region deaths are "imported" — is delivered as aggregate shares and provincial arrows. Extending it: formalize the emitter→victim mapping as a full origin–destination mortality-transfer matrix and attribute it with explainable ML, so each death-flow edge carries the meteorological, network, and demographic features that drive it.

Added value to my researchThis is directly my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction line: an attribution matrix with SHAP-style feature contributions becomes a reusable template for tracing telehealth or care-access spillovers across administrative borders.

Extending this paperThe paper measures where pollution and deaths land, but not who by income or vulnerability. Extending it: intersect the gridded exposure surface with socio-economic and poverty layers so the inequity claim becomes distributional — does the imported delivery burden fall disproportionately on poorer transit-corridor populations?
