Comparing the carbon footprints of urban and conventional agriculture

Jason K. Hawes · Benjamin P. Goldstein · Joshua P. Newell · Erica Dorr · Silvio Caputo · Runrid Fox-Kämper · Baptiste Grard · Rositsa T. Ilieva · Agnès Fargue-Lelièvre · Lidia Poniży · Victoria Schoen · Kathrin Specht · Nevin Cohen Published 2024-01-22 DOI 10.1038/s44284-023-00023-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 73 urban-agriculture (UA) sites in Europe and the United States, the carbon footprint of food from UA is on average six times higher than from conventional agriculture — ~420 gCO₂e versus ~70 gCO₂e per serving — driven mainly by the emissions embodied in infrastructure (raised beds, sheds, paths). Yet the story is not uniform: certain crops (e.g., tomatoes) and roughly 25% of individually managed gardens already beat conventional farming. UA can close the gap by growing crops that are normally greenhouse-grown or air-freighted, keeping sites in use for many years, and embracing circularity (using waste as inputs).

Why It Matters

  • Settles a contested sustainability claim with evidence. Urban agriculture is widely promoted as a climate solution, but prior studies were small and contradictory. This is the first large-scale, multi-site, multi-country comparison, so it replaces anecdote with a defensible average.
  • Reframes the problem from "grow local" to "build smart." The dominant emission source is infrastructure, not transport — meaning the leverage for low-carbon UA lies in materials, design, and site longevity rather than simply shortening food miles.
  • Identifies who already wins. Tomatoes and a quarter of individual gardens outperform conventional farms, giving practitioners concrete crop- and management-level levers instead of a blanket verdict.
  • Demonstrates citizen science at policy scale. Data were gathered by gardeners and farmers themselves across many sites, showing a replicable model for environmental monitoring of distributed urban systems.

Core Methods

  • Distributed citizen-science data collection: practitioners at 73 sites logged inputs, yields, and infrastructure over the study period, building a harmonized cross-site dataset.
  • Comparative life-cycle assessment (LCA): cradle-to-gate carbon footprint per serving for UA products versus conventional benchmarks, using ecoinvent impact factors.
  • Functional-unit normalization: heterogeneous garden outputs converted to a common per-serving basis using USDA FPED/FNDDS/FICRCD, enabling apples-to-apples comparison.
  • Infrastructure attribution: emissions decomposed by source (infrastructure, supplies, irrigation), revealing infrastructure as the dominant driver and motivating amortization over site lifetime.
  • Heterogeneity & scenario analysis: identification of outperforming crops/sites and of mitigation levers — high-value crop selection, multi-year site longevity, and circularity (waste-as-input).

① Scale 73 sites to a city-wide spatial footprint surface

Extending this paperThe study's carbon footprints are site-level point estimates from 73 locations — it cannot yet say what a whole city's UA footprint is, or how it varies across neighborhoods. A direct extension: fuse the citizen-science LCA values with remote sensing, rooftop inventories, and POI/land-use layers to interpolate a continuous, city-scale UA carbon surface, turning a sample into a planning map. The corpus's rooftop food–energy allocation work shows exactly the spatial-allocation machinery this needs.

② Replace the static LCA with an explainable, learnable footprint predictor

Extending this paperThe paper computes footprints with a fixed LCA pipeline (ecoinvent factors + manual functional-unit conversion), which is data-hungry per site and hard to extrapolate to new gardens. Extending it: train an explainable ML surrogate mapping site attributes (materials, age, crop mix, irrigation) → gCO₂e/serving, calibrated on the 73 sites, so any new garden can be scored instantly and the model surfaces which design choices dominate.

③ Close the loop: model circularity as a network optimization

Extending this paperThe paper names circularity (waste as inputs) as a key mitigation lever but treats it qualitatively. Extending it: formalize urban nutrient/waste reuse as a spatial flow-optimization problem — match compost/greywater/nutrient sources to UA sites to minimize embodied carbon — quantifying how much of the 6× gap circularity can actually erase across a city.

Added value to my researchThis maps onto my facility-location / supply-demand optimization toolkit (the same family as EV-charging efficiency-vs-equity and 2SFCA service-area matching): here the "demand" is UA sites and the "supply" is urban waste streams.

④ Add an accessibility/equity axis: who actually reaches low-carbon food?

Extending this paperThe paper measures the carbon of UA produce but not its spatial accessibility — a low-footprint garden is irrelevant to residents who cannot reach it. Extending it: overlay the footprint results with a 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility model of UA sites versus population, revealing whether low-carbon urban food is equitably distributed or concentrated in advantaged neighborhoods.

Extending this paperThe data live in Supplementary PDFs and rely on a proprietary (ecoinvent) impact database, limiting reuse and replication. Extending it: publish the 73-site records as a versioned, machine-readable FAIR dataset with open emission-factor mappings, so the comparison can be re-run, audited, and grown by other cities — converting a one-off study into a living benchmark.
