Future urban research

Monica Contestabile (interviewing Prof. Anu Ramaswami) Published 2018-03-12 DOI 10.1038/s41893-018-0037-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The next generation of urban sustainability science must move beyond single cities and single sectors to study sustainable urban systems — recognizing the transboundary, multiscale, and supra-aggregate nature of cities. Achieving this requires new data, methods, and conceptual frameworks, as well as co-produced knowledge with practitioners at neighbourhood, regional, and cross-city governance levels, explicitly situated within the UN 2030 Sustainable Development Agenda.

Core Methods

  • Committee deliberation and synthesis: an NSF-appointed subcommittee of urban scholars and practitioners from diverse disciplines met multiple times in 2017 to co-develop the research agenda.
  • Conceptual framework building: the committee proposed moving from single-sector/single-city studies to a multiscale systems framework that explicitly encompasses transboundary impacts, urban typologies, and supra-aggregation of cities.
  • Knowledge co-production design: the agenda advocates for practitioner–researcher partnerships at neighbourhood, city, regional, and cross-city levels as a core methodological stance.
  • Portfolio design: the report proposes a mixed-mode portfolio distinguishing long-horizon large-team projects from compact focused investigations — a meta-methodological recommendation for NSF program officers and funders worldwide.
  • Scope framing: three integrative lenses are proposed — (1) multiscale sustainability linkages, (2) urban typologies, and (3) supra-aggregation of cities at national/world-regional scale.

④ Scale the "multiscale sustainability linkages" with urban-traffic co-evolution models

Extending this paperThe report proposes studying multiscale sustainability linkages — yet provides no dynamic model connecting urban form changes to infrastructure and mobility outcomes over time. Extending this with bidirectional causality analysis between urban system evolution and traffic dynamics across multiple cities would operationalize the multiscale linkage concept: when a city's form changes (densification, sprawl), how do traffic patterns respond, and vice versa? This gives Ramaswami's programmatic call a concrete, testable, cross-city empirical architecture.

Added value to my researchBidirectional urban–traffic causality directly informs my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation of mobility patterns: understanding that urban structure and traffic co-evolve (rather than one being exogenous) refines how I model catchment areas in 2SFCA — particularly for healthcare facilities whose accessibility is jointly determined by neighbourhood density and route congestion dynamics.
