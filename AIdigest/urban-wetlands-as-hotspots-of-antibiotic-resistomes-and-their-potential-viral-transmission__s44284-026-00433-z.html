Urban wetlands as hotspots of antibiotic resistomes and their potential viral transmission

Da Lin · Ying Liu · Xiaohui Liu · Shuai Du · Kenneth Mei Yee Leung · Yong-Guan Zhu · Fengchang Wu · Dong Zhu Published 2026-04-17 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00433-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 17 urban wetlands in China, antibiotic resistance genes (ARGs) are present at average abundances roughly nine times higher than in natural lakes and comparable to raw urban sewage — making these "sponge" landscapes unexpected resistome hotspots. The wetlands harbor human bacterial pathogens and signatures of horizontal gene transfer, and bacteriophages carrying ARGs may help spread resistance through bacterial communities. Higher economic development is associated with lower ARG abundance, implying socioeconomic conditions (likely sewer-system quality) shape the geography of resistance and that stormwater should be collected and treated before reaching wetlands.

Why It Matters

  • Reframes a "green infrastructure" as a public-health liability. Urban wetlands are promoted as flood sponges, recreation and habitat; this work shows the same systems concentrate antibiotic resistance to sewage-like levels — a tension every sponge-city program must now weigh.
  • Names a transmission mechanism, not just a pattern. By co-detecting human pathogens, mobile-genetic-element signatures, and ARG-carrying phages, the study points to viral / horizontal transfer as a route by which resistance could move through wetland microbiomes and onward to wildlife and people.
  • Links resistance to socioeconomics. The negative association between economic development and ARG abundance turns a microbiology result into an urban-planning and equity finding — poorer drainage infrastructure may translate into higher environmental resistance loads.
  • Yields a concrete intervention. The actionable message is specific: collect and treat stormwater / surface runoff before it enters wetlands, rather than treating wetlands as self-cleaning.

Core Methods

  • Shotgun metagenomics: sequence wetland samples and quantify the resistome (ARG abundance, diversity, resistance mechanisms) using ARGs-OAP v3.0.
  • Cross-environment benchmarking: compare wetland ARGs and risk indices against natural lakes and raw/effluent sewage; non-normal data tested with two-sided Kruskal–Wallis H and Mann–Whitney U (FDR/Benjamini–Hochberg corrected).
  • Pathogen & HGT screening: detect human bacterial pathogens (HBPs) and mobile-genetic-element indicators of horizontal gene transfer (Fig. 2).
  • Virome & phage–host networks: recover MAGs and viral contigs (MEGAHIT, VirSorter2/VIBRANT, CheckV), build phage–host interaction networks, and quantify ARGs carried by phages and their hosts via linear-regression relationships (Fig. 3, Extended Data Figs. 3–8).
  • Socioeconomic-driver modeling: relate ARG abundance to economic-development predictors to identify factors shaping the geographical distribution of resistance (Fig. 4).

① Turn the 17-wetland snapshot into a national resistome surveillance grid

Extending this paperThe study samples 17 wetlands once and infers a national hotspot pattern. A direct extension is a spatiotemporal wastewater/wetland surveillance network — repeating metagenomic sampling on a schedule and fusing it with city sewer maps, so the "~9× vs. lakes" finding becomes a monitored, time-resolved indicator rather than a single comparison. India's metropolitan wastewater-surveillance design offers a transferable template for sampling cadence and ARG/MGE reporting.

② Make the socioeconomic driver explainable and spatially non-stationary

Extending this paperThe paper reports that higher economic development ↔ lower ARG abundance and attributes it to sewer quality, but the relationship is treated globally. Extending it: model ARG abundance with explainable ML + MGWR over candidate drivers (GDP, drainage coverage, population density, land use), letting the development–ARG coefficient vary by place and quantifying how much of the gradient sewer infrastructure actually explains.
