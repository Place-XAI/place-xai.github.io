Modeling context-dependent RNA splicing by deep learning

Chengxuan Chen · Leng Han Published 2026-05-19 DOI 10.1038/s43588-026-00987-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The HELIX framework predicts full-length transcript isoform usage across diverse biological contexts by combining sequence features with RNA-binding protein (RBP) expression. At the splice-site level, sequence and RBP signals are integrated to infer context-dependent splice-site usage; a bidirectional LSTM then aggregates these site-level signals into transcript-level isoform abundance — enabling context-sensitive, not just sequence-only, splicing prediction.

★

Why It Matters

  • Moves splicing models beyond pure sequence. Most existing tools (e.g., SpliceAI, Pangolin) predict splice-site strength from DNA sequence alone. HELIX incorporates RBP expression as a context variable, making predictions tissue- and condition-specific.
  • Full isoform, not just site, prediction. By operating at the transcript level with a bidirectional LSTM, the framework predicts relative isoform abundance — a step closer to what RNA-seq actually measures — rather than treating each splice site in isolation.
  • Bridges genomic variation and functional impact. Context-dependent splicing is central to disease: mutations that disrupt RBP binding alter isoform ratios. HELIX provides a principled framework for assessing such variant effects across cell types.
  • Scalable computational strategy. The two-stage hierarchy (site-level signal → transcript-level LSTM) is modular and extensible, allowing new context variables (e.g., epigenomic marks, RNA structure) to be plugged in without redesigning the whole model.

Core Methods

  • Two-stage hierarchical architecture: (1) a splice-site-level model integrates sequence features and RBP expression to produce context-dependent splice-site signals; (2) a bidirectional LSTM aggregates these site-level signals across the transcript to predict relative isoform abundance.
  • Context injection via RBP expression: instead of predicting a fixed "spliceability" score per site, HELIX conditions predictions on the expression vector of RNA-binding proteins — capturing regulatory factors that shift splice-site selection across tissues and perturbation states.
  • Transcript-level isoform modeling: the LSTM reads splice-site signals in both directions along the pre-mRNA, enabling long-range dependencies (e.g., exon-skipping interactions) to influence final isoform ratio predictions.
  • Comparison baseline: positioned against sequence-only deep learning splicing models (SpliceAI, Pangolin) that do not model biological context, establishing the incremental value of RBP-conditioned context.
  • Method positioning: the HELIX design is modular — additional context types (epigenomic tracks, RNA secondary structure, m6A modification) could replace or augment the RBP expression vector without altering the LSTM backbone.

① Replace the RBP expression vector with a foundation-model embedding of the regulatory context

Extending this paperHELIX represents biological context as an expression vector of individual RBPs. But RBP measurements are sparse and noisy across tissues. A direct extension: substitute the RBP expression vector with a dense embedding from a transcription/chromatin foundation model (e.g., the Borzoi-style model of Linder et al.) pre-trained on thousands of genomic tracks. The frozen embedding would encode a richer, lower-noise context signal — improving HELIX's generalization to rare cell types where RBP panels are unmeasured.

Added value to my researchThe same substitution principle applies in my work: replacing hand-crafted feature vectors (e.g., POI counts or census indicators) with embeddings from geospatial foundation models (e.g., SatCLIP, GeoCLIP) as context vectors in spatiotemporal prediction tasks — a direct methodological parallel that reinforces my fine-tuning-of-foundation-models agenda.

② Add explainability: attribute isoform shifts to specific RBPs and sequence motifs

Extending this paperHELIX predicts context-dependent isoform usage but does not decompose which RBPs or sequence motifs drive the context-shift. Extending it: apply gradient-based attribution or SHAP at the splice-site-level sub-model to rank the contribution of each RBP to the change in splice-site signal between contexts (e.g., healthy vs. tumor). This would convert HELIX from a black-box predictor into an interpretable regulatory map, directly exposing the RBP–splice-site interaction grammar.

③ Encode genomic regulatory grammar as a structured prior in HELIX's LSTM

Extending this paperHELIX's bidirectional LSTM treats the sequence of splice-site signals as an unconstrained sequence. But regulatory syntax has known structure: certain exon–intron architectures are physically constrained (e.g., first-exon definition rules, U1/U2 snRNP pairing logic). Extending HELIX: inject known splicing grammar rules as soft constraints into the LSTM's training objective — a physics-informed approach that regularizes the model toward biologically feasible isoform architectures, improving both accuracy and generalization to novel gene structures.

⑤ Generalize HELIX to design synthetic exon sequences with controlled isoform ratios

Extending this paperHELIX is currently a discriminative model: given a sequence and RBP context, predict isoform usage. A generative extension: invert the model to design exon sequences that achieve a target isoform ratio under a specified RBP context — a therapeutic design tool for splice-switching antisense oligonucleotides or exon-editing strategies. The bidirectional LSTM's internal representations could seed a conditional generative model trained on the same sequence–RBP–isoform triplets.
