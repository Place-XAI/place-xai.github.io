Deep learning incorporating biologically inspired neural dynamics and in-memory computing

Stanisław Woźniak · Angeliki Pantazi · Thomas Bohnstingl · Evangelos Eleftheriou Published 2020-06-15 DOI 10.1038/s42256-020-0187-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The authors introduce the Spiking Neural Unit (SNU) — a recurrent ANN building block that faithfully re-expresses the leaky integrate-and-fire neuron's biologically plausible dynamics inside standard deep-learning frameworks. SNUs match or surpass LSTM and GRU performance on three benchmark tasks (handwritten digit recognition, language modelling, polyphonic music prediction) while requiring fewer parameters per neuron and remaining accurate down to 4-bit weight precision. Crucially, the SNU concept is experimentally validated on a phase-change memory (PCM) prototype chip using 52,800 devices, demonstrating that hardware-in-the-loop training compensates for PCM non-idealities and yields an energy-efficient in-memory inference engine — bridging the long-standing divide between deep learning and neuromorphic hardware.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

MNIST
Handwritten digit recognition dataset (LeCun et al., 1998). Standard split: 60,000 training and 10,000 test images, each 28×28 pixels greyscale. Rate-coded as binary input spikes via Poisson process. Optionally augmented with elastic distortions as a regularizer.
Penn Treebank
Word-level language corpus preprocessed by Mikolov, comprising Wall Street Journal sentences restricted to the most frequent 10,000 tokens. Split into ~930,000 training, ~74,000 validation, and ~82,000 test tokens. Tokens fed sequentially, one per time step.
J. S. Bach Chorales
Polyphonic music dataset (Boulanger-Lewandowski et al., 2012). Over seven hours of music across 382 pieces split into 229 training / 76 validation / 77 test pieces. Each time step is an 88-dimensional binary piano-note vector (A0–C8).
PCM hardware
Prototype PCM chip in 90-nm CMOS technology (Ge₂Sb₂Te₅ alloy). Arrays totalling ~3 million devices; experiment used 52,800 PCM devices from the 1 M cell sub-array to represent 26,400 synaptic weights in 2-PCM differential configuration. Characterized on 10,000 devices.
Software
TensorFlow framework (Abadi et al., 2015); Brian2 SNN simulator used for reference LIF validation; Python–MATLAB interface for FPGA-board communication.
Availability
All benchmark datasets are publicly available. Sample TensorFlow source code provided in Supplementary Information. PCM chip is an IBM Research prototype and not publicly available, but the training methodology is described in full detail.

Core Methods

  • Spiking Neural Unit (SNU) formulation: a biologically motivated LIF neuron is recast as a 2-neuron ANN subunit — N₁ accumulates membrane potential via a self-looping connection; N₂ emits spikes via a step function (SNN mode) or analog values via sigmoid (soft sSNU mode). The structure reduces to a simple recurrent ANN trainable with standard deep-learning tools.
  • Backpropagation through time (BPTT) with pseudo-derivative: the non-differentiable step function at N₂ is handled by substituting the derivative of tanh during the backward pass, enabling gradient-based end-to-end training of deep SNU stacks.
  • Deep architectures: fully connected SNUs stacked to 7 layers (MNIST), convolutional SNU-CNN replicating an existing ANN architecture, two-layer recurrent sSNU networks (Penn Treebank, music). Directly reuses ANN architecture insights without SNN-specific re-derivation.
  • Rate-coding for static inputs: greyscale pixel values encoded as binary spike trains via Poisson process — a standard SNN convention bridged here into the ANN training loop.
  • Low-precision simulation: fixed-point precision swept from 32-bit down to 2-bit to characterize hardware requirements; reveals SNU robustness to 4-bit weights (vs. 16-bit for LSTM).
  • Hardware-in-the-loop training: the PCM chip is embedded in the BPTT training loop — forward pass reads conductances from hardware, backward pass computes gradients in TensorFlow, programming pulses update device conductances. 2-PCM differential configuration provides ~4-bit precision per synapse.
  • Drift compensation: constant drift calibration factor applied during reads; 2-PCM rebalancing with reset pulses avoids conductance saturation, compensating for PCM's inherent physical non-idealities.

① Apply SNU's temporal dynamics to spatiotemporal urban-sensor sequences

Extending this paperThe SNU paper evaluates temporal sequence modelling on music and language but does not explore spatiotemporal irregularly-sampled sensor streams — precisely the domain of urban sensing (GPS traces, mobile-phone activity counts, hourly air-quality readings). A natural extension: replace the fixed-timestep BPTT used here with an event-driven SNU that ingests asynchronous sensor events, exploiting the LIF membrane's native charge-accumulation to integrate irregular inter-event intervals without zero-padding, and validate on a city-scale anomaly-detection or traffic-flow prediction task.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and mobile-phone trajectory analyses require models that handle irregular temporal sampling without losing the ordering signal. An event-driven SNU layer would provide a biologically plausible, hardware-deployable alternative to LSTM/GRU as the temporal backbone in my GeoAI pipelines — and would run on low-power edge chips co-located with sensors, enabling privacy-preserving on-device spatiotemporal prediction for public-health surveillance.

② Replace fixed-precision quantization with learned mixed-precision SNU graphs

Extending this paperThe paper sweeps weight precision uniformly from 32-bit down to 2-bit to establish hardware requirements, finding that SNU tolerates 4-bit while LSTM does not. A direct extension: instead of uniform quantization, learn a layer-wise or neuron-wise precision assignment policy jointly with the network weights, treating bit-width as a discrete parameter optimized under an energy/area budget constraint. The SNU's simple parameterization (only W, b, and l(τ) per layer) makes it far cheaper to explore mixed-precision schedules than LSTM.

Added value to my researchExplainable ML for spatial prediction routinely involves ensembles (Random Forest, XGBoost, GNN) whose inference cost is dominated by memory bandwidth — not arithmetic. A precision-aware SNU-style gating where neurons selectively activate (spike) only for high-information inputs offers a sparse, energy-efficient inference primitive for my geospatial models running on mobile or embedded edge nodes in field health surveys.

③ Embed SNU dynamics as a physics-informed temporal prior in graph neural networks

Extending this paperThe paper positions SNUs as ANN units with built-in physical dynamics (membrane potential decay, threshold, reset), but confines them to fully connected or convolutional architectures. Extending it: encode SNU dynamics as a message-passing rule on a spatial graph — each node is an SNU whose state corresponds to local signal intensity, edges carry synaptic weights between spatially adjacent nodes, and the LIF time-constant l(τ) is learned per edge type. This creates a physics-informed spatiotemporal GNN grounded in the paper's validated dynamics.

Added value to my researchMy physics-informed graph learning agenda — encoding epidemic diffusion (SIR) or accessibility flow priors into GNNs — aligns directly with this extension. SNU nodes would encode propagation timescales as biologically interpretable constants rather than black-box weights, making the learned dynamics both physically constrained and explainable — crucial for convincing urban-health policy stakeholders.

④ Leverage SNU's analog-reset gate as a continual-learning mechanism for drifting distributions

Extending this paperThe soft SNU's sigmoid output controls a proportional reset of the membrane state — a biologically motivated analog of selective forgetting. The paper demonstrates this in a fixed training regime but does not explore it for non-stationary or continually arriving data. Extending it: use the sSNU's proportional-reset gate as an explicit gating mechanism for continual learning, where the magnitude of the reset can be conditioned on a detected distribution shift, providing catastrophic-forgetting resistance without replay buffers.

Added value to my researchHealth geography and urban-analytics datasets exhibit distributional drift (COVID period shifts in mobility, seasonal flu patterns, evolving telehealth adoption). A continual SNU layer that selectively resets stale spatial representations while retaining stable long-term structure would let my GeoAI models update from streaming data without full retraining — directly supporting real-time spatial accessibility monitoring for primary care and mental health service areas.

⑤ Transfer the hardware-in-the-loop training methodology to training physical photonic GeoAI networks

Extending this paperThe paper's most experimentally distinctive contribution is embedding a PCM chip as a differentiable node inside TensorFlow's BPTT loop, compensating for device non-idealities through gradient updates rather than post-hoc calibration. This hardware-in-the-loop training methodology is general: it applies to any analog hardware whose forward computation is readable and whose weights are adjustable. A high-impact extension would be applying the same loop to emerging photonic AI accelerators, where computation occurs at the speed of light but device non-linearity is even more severe than in PCM.

Added value to my researchFine-tuning geospatial foundation models (my research direction) requires enormous compute at inference time for large-area spatial queries. If the hardware-in-the-loop paradigm can be extended to photonic inference engines, it opens a path to ultrafast, low-power GeoAI foundation-model inference for real-time urban analytics — relevant to my interest in deploying accessible, energy-efficient spatial AI at city scale.
