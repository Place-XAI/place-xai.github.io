Synaptic and neural behaviours in a standard silicon transistor

Sebastian Pazos · Kaichen Zhu · Marco A. Villena · Osamah Alharbi · Wenwen Zheng · Yaqing Shen · Yue Yuan · Yue Ping · Mario Lanza Published 2025-03-26 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-08742-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A single standard bulk-silicon CMOS transistor — already in mass production — can simultaneously mimic both neural (leaky-integrate-and-fire) and synaptic (short- and long-term plasticity) behaviours when biased unconventionally in the punch-through impact-ionization regime. Connecting just one additional CMOS transistor in series creates a neuro-synaptic RAM (NS-RAM) cell that is continuously tunable between neuron and synapse modes, achieves 100% yield and ultra-low device-to-device variability, and requires no exotic materials or process changes — enabling immediate integration into current CMOS fabrication flows for energy-efficient artificial neural networks.

★

Why It Matters

  • Collapses the transistor count for neuromorphic hardware by an order of magnitude. Current state-of-the-art chips need at least 18 CMOS transistors per neuron and 6 per synapse; this work replaces both with 1–2 standard transistors without modifying the process.
  • Eliminates exotic-materials risk. Memristors, ferroelectric FETs, and 2D-material devices all face integration hurdles. The NS-RAM cell runs on commercially available 180-nm and 500-nm bulk-silicon MOSFETs — already the most mature manufacturing platform on Earth.
  • Bridges short-term and long-term plasticity in one device. The same floating-bulk transistor displays short-term facilitation/depression (ms timescale) and long-term potentiation/depression (retention >10,000 s, >100,000 cycles), covering most ANN learning paradigms.
  • Enables SPICE-level circuit simulation today. The physics are well-described by standard compact MOSFET and bipolar SPICE models, so system designers can simulate and optimize full ANN circuits without new device models — dramatically shortening the path to a chip prototype.
  • Opens a concrete route to compute-in-memory at lower latency than DRAM. Long-term synaptic retention through read pulses (up to 85 °C) suggests the NS-RAM cell could replace embedded Flash or DRAM weight-storage arrays in inference accelerators.

Core Methods

  • Floating-bulk biasing in punch-through regime: the bulk terminal of a standard n-channel MOSFET is left ungrounded (or controlled via a second MOSFET), allowing impact ionization to generate excess carriers that create abrupt hysteretic I–V transitions — the physical mechanism for both neural firing and synaptic weight change.
  • 2-transistor NS-RAM cell: a bulk-bias-control MOSFET is connected in series to set the effective bulk spreading resistance (RB), enabling continuous, reproducible tuning of the neuro-synaptic operating point across 30 dies and removing die-to-die variability from the punch-through response.
  • Quasi-stationary I–V characterization: drain current vs. drain voltage (ID–VD) sweeps at constant gate voltages with grounded vs. floating bulk, establishing the hysteresis window and resistance states (RHRS/RLRS).
  • Pulsed time-domain cycling: Keysight B1530A WGFMU used to apply microsecond-resolution voltage pulses (potentiation, depression, read) in custom MATLAB+C++ loops for endurance tests up to 15 million pulses; read resistance extracted after each pulse.
  • TCAD + SPICE co-simulation: Synopsys Sentaurus 2D TCAD calibrated against grounded-bulk substrate currents; mixed-mode environment couples TCAD device to SPICE bulk biasing network; results validate impact-ionization, avalanche, and charge-trapping mechanisms.
  • Short-term vs. long-term plasticity regimes: short-term relies on excess carrier dynamics (charge redistribution, ms timescale); long-term relies on hot-electron/hot-hole injection into the gate dielectric (threshold-voltage shift, retention >10,000 s). Regime is selected by pulse amplitude and biasing configuration, not by a different device.
  • Statistical linearity analysis: cumulative density functions and box plots (25th/75th/5th/95th percentiles) extracted over tens of thousands of potentiation/depression cycles to quantify Neff (effective number of synaptic levels = 14) and nonlinearity exponential factors.

① Use the NS-RAM cell as the compute substrate for reservoir computing on spatiotemporal data streams

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates short-term synaptic plasticity with exponential decay times of 1–100 ms and 14 effective resistance levels — precisely the fading-memory property that makes a physical substrate ideal for reservoir computing. The paper does not build or simulate a reservoir network, yet the NS-RAM cell is a natural candidate: connecting an array of floating-bulk transistors with randomized weights would create a fixed, high-dimensional temporal projection layer that feeds a trainable linear readout. Extending the paper means characterizing inter-device coupling dynamics, measuring cross-talk in a small NS-RAM array, and benchmarking reservoir accuracy on standard time-series tasks (e.g., NARMA-10) compared with memristor-based reservoirs.

Added value to my researchSpatiotemporal kernel density estimation and spatiotemporal analysis underpin much of my GeoAI pipeline — these methods require high-dimensional feature extraction from irregularly sampled urban sensor streams (GPS trajectories, mobile phone records, social media). A silicon CMOS reservoir layer that is already compatible with edge inference chips could serve as a hardware front-end for spatiotemporal feature extraction, compressing raw mobility streams into low-dimensional reservoir states before a lightweight spatial ML model (e.g., MGWR or a GNN) makes the downstream prediction. This directly connects the NS-RAM physics paper to my reproducible, multi-source data-fusion agenda.

② Replace floating-point weight matrices in spiking neural network accelerators with NS-RAM arrays and quantify the energy-vs-accuracy trade-off

Extending this paperThe paper achieves 14 effective synaptic levels and >100,000 long-term cycling endurance, which positions the NS-RAM cell as a candidate weight-storage element for binarized or low-precision ANNs. The paper stops at the device level; the natural next step is a circuit-level simulation (the SPICE models are already published on Zenodo) of a small crossbar array where NS-RAM cells implement the weight matrix of a spiking network layer. Extending the paper means: (a) mapping the 14 resistance levels to discrete weight quantization bins, (b) running the SPICE crossbar simulation for a representative inference task, and (c) benchmarking firing energy (currently 415 pJ µm−1) against Intel Loihi 2 and IBM NorthPole on equivalent tasks to quantify the actual area and energy savings of reducing the transistor count from 6+ to 2 per synapse.

Added value to my researchMy work on fine-tuning geospatial foundation models and explainable ML for spatial prediction routinely hits energy and memory bottlenecks when deploying models on mobile or edge devices. A concrete energy-vs-accuracy benchmark from NS-RAM crossbar simulations would let me evaluate whether hardware-aware quantization of geospatial foundation models (e.g., reducing from float32 to 4-bit weights) is compatible with the NS-RAM device characteristics — bridging hardware efficiency to the GeoAI model deployment question.

③ Exploit the NS-RAM cell's tunable punch-through regime to implement online, local learning rules without backpropagation

Extending this paperThe paper shows that both the potentiation rate and the relaxation time constant are continuously adjustable via VG and VG2, which are exact analogs of the learning rate and the time constant in spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP) rules. The paper does not connect this tunability to a learning rule, but the NS-RAM cell's two control voltages could implement a hardware STDP controller: VG sets the potentiation sensitivity (analogous to learning rate), VG2 sets the eligibility trace decay (analogous to the temporal credit-assignment window). Extending the paper means designing a minimal CMOS circuit that derives VG and VG2 from the relative timing of pre- and post-synaptic spikes, then measuring whether the resulting weight update matches a target STDP kernel — enabling fully on-chip, backpropagation-free learning.

Added value to my researchMy physics-informed graph learning agenda seeks to embed domain conservation laws into GNN training. An STDP-like local learning rule on an NS-RAM array is essentially a hardware implementation of Hebbian locality — the spatial proximity principle that underlies spatial autocorrelation in geographic data. Exploring this connection could yield a novel theoretical bridge: Tobler's First Law of Geography (nearby places are more related) maps directly onto the temporal locality encoded by the STDP eligibility trace, suggesting that a GNN with STDP-like weight updates would naturally learn spatially smooth representations without explicit regularization.

④ Benchmark the NS-RAM cell against biomimetic 2D-material transistors for audiomorphic and tonotopic neural mapping

Extending this paperThe paper explicitly discusses tonotopic mapping of audio signals (cochlea-inspired frequency selectivity) as an application, and demonstrates firing-time control from 20 Hz to 200 kHz — covering the full auditory range. This is precisely the task targeted by 2D-material audiomorphic transistors in the literature, but those devices require exotic materials and non-CMOS processes. Extending the paper: fabricate a small array of NS-RAM cells with different VG2 biases (each set to a different characteristic frequency), feed a recorded audio waveform as the drain voltage, and measure whether the array produces a tonotopic activity map comparable to that of MoS2-based audiomorphic transistors — establishing whether silicon CMOS can match 2D-material neuromorphic hardware on this benchmark without leaving the foundry.

Added value to my researchTonotopic mapping is a specialized case of spatiotemporal frequency decomposition, which is structurally identical to the decomposition of spatial-frequency components in remote sensing imagery (e.g., wavelet-based feature extraction from satellite data). Understanding how a hardware neural filter bank performs frequency-selective attention on temporal signals would inform how I design spatially frequency-aware attention modules in geospatial foundation models — particularly for tasks that fuse multi-resolution satellite bands with heterogeneous temporal coverage.

⑤ Integrate the NS-RAM cell into a fully analogue reinforcement learning loop for edge decision-making in urban sensor networks

Extending this paperThe paper shows that the NS-RAM cell's resistance state can be incrementally updated (potentiated/depressed) by local voltage pulses without a global controller — the exact property required by analogue reinforcement learning (RL) hardware, where the weight update must happen in-situ and in real time. Extending the paper: connect a small NS-RAM array to an analogue critic circuit (e.g., a comparator that computes reward signal from environmental feedback) and train it on a simple RL task (e.g., balancing). The published SPICE models make this directly simulatable before tape-out, and the long-term retention (>10,000 s) means the policy survives across episodes without refresh — something DRAM-based RL accelerators cannot guarantee.

Added value to my researchMy EV charging-station efficiency-vs-equity optimization currently runs as an offline spatial optimization. A key limitation is that EV charging demand is dynamic and spatially non-stationary — a fixed optimization solution becomes suboptimal as patterns shift. An analogue RL chip built on NS-RAM cells could implement a lightweight, always-on spatial policy network deployed at the charging station controller level, continuously updating pricing and routing signals in response to real-time occupancy feedback — a direct hardware realization of the adaptive facility-location problem that runs at the edge without cloud connectivity.
