A foundation model for continuous glucose monitoring data

Guy Lutsker · Gal Sapir · Smadar Shilo · Jordi Merino · Anastasia Godneva · Jerry R. Greenfield · Dorit Samocha-Bonet · Raja Dhir · Francisco Gude · Shie Mannor · Eli Meirom · Eric P. Xing · Gal Chechik · Hagai Rossman · Eran Segal Published 2026-01-14 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09925-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

GluFormer, a generative transformer foundation model trained with self-supervised learning on 10+ million glucose measurements from 10,812 adults, learns transferable glycaemic representations that generalize across 19 external cohorts, 5 countries, and 8 CGM devices. These representations outperform HbA1c for stratifying risk of glycaemic progression and cardiovascular mortality (66% of incident diabetes cases and 69% of cardiovascular deaths fell in the top risk quartile vs. 7% and 0% in the bottom). A multimodal extension integrating dietary data further generates plausible postprandial glucose trajectories, offering a generalizable framework for precision metabolic health.

★

Why It Matters

  • Turns continuous glucose streams into a clinical risk signal. CGM captures minute-by-minute physiology that HbA1c cannot; this work is the first to encode that richness into a generalizable foundation model representation that consistently improves prognostic performance across diverse populations.
  • Dramatic long-term risk stratification. In a cohort followed for a median of 11 years, GluFormer's risk score placed 66% of incident diabetes cases and 69% of cardiovascular deaths into the highest-risk quartile — outperforming the clinical gold standard HbA1c.
  • Cross-device, cross-cohort generalization. The model transfers to 8 different CGM devices and spans prediabetes, type 1, type 2, gestational diabetes, and obesity cohorts across 5 countries, demonstrating broad domain generalization without task-specific retraining.
  • Multimodal dietary integration. By conditioning on dietary logs, the model generates individualized postprandial glucose responses — enabling personalized nutrition interventions at scale and without invasive testing beyond CGM.
  • Open science infrastructure. The model code is publicly available (github.com/Guylu/GluFormer), and training data from the Human Phenotype Project is accessible to bona fide researchers, supporting reproducibility and extension.

Core Methods

  • Self-supervised pre-training: GluFormer is a generative transformer trained autoregressively on CGM time-series sequences; the model predicts the next glucose token, encoding temporal glucose dynamics without any task-specific labels.
  • Autoregressive representation extraction: after pre-training, the internal representations (embeddings) serve as compact encoders of glycaemic patterns, extracted and used as features for downstream prediction tasks.
  • Transfer learning across cohorts: pre-trained representations are transferred to 19 external cohorts spanning diverse CGM devices and pathophysiological states, with minimal or no fine-tuning — testing generalizability under distributional shift.
  • Benchmark comparisons: representations are evaluated against fasting blood glucose, HbA1c, composite CGM-derived metrics (e.g., time-in-range, glucose variability indices), and the Glucose Management Indicator (GMI) across forecasting and stratification tasks.
  • Risk stratification: quartile-based stratification of GluFormer-derived risk scores assessed against long-term incident diabetes and cardiovascular mortality, with comparisons to HbA1c using decision-curve analysis (net benefit framework).
  • Multimodal extension (GluFormer-Diet): dietary intake data is integrated into the generative model via cross-attention or conditioning, enabling the model to generate individualized postprandial glucose trajectory simulations given a meal context.
  • Clinical trial transfer: baseline CGM representations are used as input features for outcome prediction in clinical trial cohorts (including diabetes prevention and dietary intervention trials), demonstrating utility beyond observational studies.

① Spatially stratify GluFormer risk scores to reveal metabolic health geography

Extending this paperGluFormer produces participant-level risk scores but the paper treats all individuals as spatially anonymous — cohort heterogeneity is characterized by device type, country, and clinical phenotype but never by where people live. A direct extension would geocode the HPP and AEGIS participants and examine whether GluFormer's risk stratification performance varies systematically with neighbourhood walkability, food environment, and healthcare accessibility — testing whether spatial context explains residual variance that CGM patterns alone cannot.

Added value to my researchThis connects GluFormer's temporal biomarker directly to my health geography and spatial accessibility agenda: GluFormer risk scores become a precision outcome variable for 2SFCA/R2SFCA models, letting me measure how primary-care and endocrinology access gradients predict metabolic risk over and above individuals' own glucose profiles. It also grounds the digital-health signal in the built environment, bridging wearable sensing to place-based public health policy.

② Build a multi-task foundation model jointly encoding glucose, activity, and dietary modalities

Extending this paperGluFormer's multimodal extension (Fig. 5) integrates dietary logs but treats diet as a conditioning signal rather than a co-equal modality trained jointly from the start. The paper's own architecture could be extended by adopting a multi-task pre-training objective that simultaneously reconstructs CGM sequences, predicts meal macronutrient compositions, and forecasts step counts from wearable accelerometers — learning a shared metabolic state representation across all sensor streams. This would leverage the full richness of HPP's multi-omic phenotyping beyond glucose alone.

Added value to my researchMulti-source data fusion is central to my methods toolkit (mobile phone, social media, remote sensing). A metabolic multi-task foundation model is the precision-health analogue: it demonstrates that spatiotemporal fusion principles transfer from urban sensing to clinical wearables, reinforcing a unified GeoAI data-fusion methodology applicable to both built-environment and body-level temporal streams.

③ Integrate explainable ML to decode which glucose features drive GluFormer's risk predictions

Extending this paperGluFormer's representations are dense embeddings that outperform HbA1c but remain opaque — clinicians cannot readily interrogate which patterns in the CGM trace are responsible for a high-risk classification. Extending the paper with attention rollout, SHAP-on-embeddings, or gradient-based feature attribution would decompose risk scores into interpretable CGM features (nocturnal hypoglycaemia spikes, postprandial variance, time-in-range segments), directly bridging foundation model performance to clinical explainability standards required for regulatory approval.

Applying SHAP/attention attribution to a time-series foundation model is the temporal analogue of my GeoShapley work: it converts black-box representations into human-readable decision rules — a methodological template I can port back to spatial foundation models for urban accessibility. This cross-domain explainability transfer is a publishable methodological contribution in its own right.

④ Apply GluFormer's transfer protocol as a blueprint for fine-tuning geospatial foundation models

Extending this paperGluFormer demonstrates a specific and reproducible transfer pipeline: pre-train autoregressively on a large unlabeled corpus → freeze or lightly fine-tune for small downstream cohorts → evaluate zero-shot generalization across distributional shifts (device, country, pathology). This protocol is general and the paper's 19-cohort transfer stress-test provides a methodological template. An extension would formalize this as a domain-shift benchmarking framework — quantifying how representation quality degrades as training-to-test distribution distance grows — applicable to any temporal physiological sensor.

Added value to my researchMy agenda on fine-tuning geospatial foundation models faces the exact same challenge: a model pre-trained on global satellite imagery must transfer to local urban health contexts with limited labeled data. GluFormer's explicit cross-cohort transfer evaluation design provides a concrete benchmarking recipe I can adopt: replace CGM devices with geographic domains (cities or regions), replace pathological states with urban development stages, and measure how GeoFM representations degrade across spatial distribution shifts.

⑤ Use GluFormer's generative simulation to create synthetic data for underrepresented populations

Extending this paperGluFormer's generative (autoregressive) architecture can produce plausible synthetic CGM traces given a patient context — a capability demonstrated for dietary simulation in Fig. 5. A natural extension: use the model as a conditional synthetic data generator to augment rare subgroups (gestational diabetes, paediatric type 1, elderly populations) that are chronically under-represented in real cohorts. Generated synthetic CGM sequences could be validated using distributional metrics (MMD, FID adapted for time series) and downstream task parity, supporting fairer model evaluation and regulatory submissions.

Added value to my researchSynthetic data augmentation for underrepresented groups resonates directly with my equity-focused research on digital divides and telehealth accessibility. If certain populations are under-monitored with wearables (rural, elderly, low-income), generative models can fill the sensor gap — a methodological precedent applicable to synthetic mobility traces for digital-divide research. It also feeds my work on FAIR and open science: synthetic data sharing sidesteps privacy barriers while preserving statistical utility.
