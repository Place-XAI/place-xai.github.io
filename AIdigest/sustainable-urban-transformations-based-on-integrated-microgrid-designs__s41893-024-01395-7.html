Sustainable urban transformations based on integrated microgrid designs

Sadeeb S. Ottenburger · Rob Cox · Badrul H. Chowdhury · Dmytro Trybushnyi · Ehmedi Al Omar · Sujay A. Kaloti · Ulrich Ufer · Witold-R. Poganietz · Weijia Liu · Evgenia Deines · Tim O. Müller · Stella Möhrle · Wolfgang Raskob Published 2024-07-16 DOI 10.1038/s41893-024-01395-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Urban microgrid planning that considers only economic feasibility risks creating "energy gerrymandering" — systematic exclusion of socially vulnerable groups from equitable energy access, analogous to partisan gerrymandering in electoral systems. Integrating the Social Vulnerability Index, critical-infrastructure criticality, and renewable energy siting potential into a multi-objective evolutionary algorithm yields microgrid boundary solutions for New Hanover County, NC that simultaneously maximize urban resilience, well-being, and equity while remaining cost-efficient. The study demonstrates that a six-microgrid districting outperforms purely economic solutions on all equity and resilience metrics, advancing SDG 7 (clean energy), SDG 10 (reduced inequalities), and multiple SDG 11 targets in tandem.

Core Methods

  • Criticality scoring: stakeholder-surveyed criticality values (0–1 scale) assigned to all critical infrastructure types in New Hanover County via focus groups; household criticality derived from SVI Themes 1 and 4 using equation (1).
  • Spatial indicator design: eight complementary metrics operationalize the multi-objective optimization — infrastructure criticality density (R1), RHS distribution balance (R2), cost factors (F1–F3), photovoltaic potential equity (SPV), neighbourhood energy storage potential equity (SST), and Social Vulnerability Index household distribution fairness (FD).
  • Pareto multi-objective optimization via evolutionary algorithm: a custom evolutionary algorithm maximizes a weighted-sum objective over up to 1042 feasible partitions of 64 medium-voltage circuit building blocks, subject to geographic contiguity and economic feasibility constraints.
  • Monte Carlo well-being loss simulation: 100,000 scenario runs per districting solution combine stochastic hurricane-scale outage rates (based on Florence) with random cyber-attack microgrid failures; two well-being loss metrics (socioeconomic type 1 and housing/mobility type 2) are accumulated per circuit boundary.
  • LiDAR-based rooftop PV potential estimation: 3D DEM from NCFMP LiDAR points reconstructs roof Aspect grids; solar efficiency coefficients applied per azimuth class to yield integral rooftop solar potential per SVI region and per medium-voltage circuit.
  • Participatory focus groups: New Hanover County Emergency Management and community leaders from high-SVI neighbourhoods co-identified Community Lifeline Facility locations and assessed preparedness gaps post-Hurricane Florence — qualitative inputs informing quantitative criticality weights.

① Quantify energy gerrymandering risk as a spatial accessibility metric

Extending this paperThe paper introduces "energy gerrymandering" conceptually and uses a fairness metric (FD) embedded in its optimizer, but never reports a standalone spatial index of gerrymandering risk that planners could compute independently of the full optimization pipeline. A direct extension: define an energy gerrymandering index — analogous to compactness scores in electoral redistricting — combining SVI spatial autocorrelation, microgrid boundary perimeter ratios, and minority-group containment rates. Applying this index across multiple US counties (using the publicly available CDC SVI) would test whether the phenomenon is universal and calibrate how much integration actually matters. The corpus paper on energy resilience disparities in NYC provides a complementary lens: it shows that distributed energy resources can either dampen or amplify existing disparities depending on their siting, motivating the need for an ex-ante gerrymandering risk score before any microgrid is built.

Mapping energy gerrymandering risk using SVI data alongside my healthcare-access deprivation surfaces would reveal whether the same neighbourhoods suffer compound infrastructure disadvantage — a basis for multi-sector equity policy dashboards integrating energy, health, and transit accessibility in a single GeoAI framework.

② Replace the evolutionary optimizer with a spatiotemporal GNN trained across cities

Extending this paperThe paper's evolutionary algorithm solves the districting problem from scratch for each city, with complexity that becomes intractable for large cities (the authors note this explicitly for mega-cities with more medium-voltage circuits). A natural extension: reframe microgrid districting as a graph partitioning learning problem — encode the urban grid as a graph where nodes are medium-voltage circuits with SVI, criticality, and PV-potential node features, and edges encode connectivity costs — then train a graph neural network to approximate the Pareto front across many cities. A GNN trained on multiple US counties (all of which have public SVI data and NREL load profiles) would transfer to unseen cities without re-optimization. The paper on interconnectedness and network resilience in multimodal urban infrastructure demonstrates the power of graph-based resilience modeling for large urban networks, providing a methodological blueprint.

Added value to my researchThis extends my physics-informed graph learning agenda directly: the SVI-weighted criticality distribution and the well-being loss equations can serve as physics constraints baked into the GNN loss, preventing it from producing solutions that violate equity invariants. The resulting model would generalize my existing explainable ML methods for spatial prediction into a transferable facility-districting operator — applicable beyond microgrids to health catchment delineation, school district redrawing, and transit zone planning.

③ Extend well-being loss assessment to dynamic mobility-weighted accessibility during blackouts

Extending this paperThe paper's well-being loss formulas (equations 5–6) assume that households are served by the nearest functioning RHS infrastructure — a static, Euclidean proximity assumption. In practice, after a hurricane, road closures and mobility constraints (captured in SVI Theme 4) mean that actual access to relief, health, and security infrastructure is far worse than proximity suggests. Extending the model: integrate post-disaster network routing (degraded road topology from flood/wind damage) and demand surge at surviving facilities into the well-being loss functions, replacing the distance term d_i in equation (3) with a dynamic travel-time derived from mobility data. This would make the paper's well-being assessment operationally realistic rather than idealized. The corpus paper on measuring inequality in community resilience using large-scale mobility data offers exactly the kind of mobility telemetry (GPS-based post-disaster travel behavior) needed to calibrate this extension.

Added value to my researchThis converges directly with my 2SFCA family: the supply-to-demand ratio at each functioning facility during a power outage is a disaster-mode accessibility problem. My R2SFCA framework with variable catchment radii (reflecting road network degradation) applied to post-hurricane scenarios would yield spatially explicit "resilience-adjusted accessibility" surfaces — linking energy infrastructure resilience to healthcare access deprivation in the same spatial unit, ideal for multi-hazard public health planning.

④ Scale the LiDAR-based rooftop PV assessment to street-level using geospatial foundation models

Extending this paperThe paper's rooftop PV potential estimation relies on a 2014 NCFMP LiDAR survey — a one-time acquisition with acknowledged temporal misalignment to 2021 building permits. This limits both temporal currency and transferability to cities without LiDAR coverage. Extending it: fine-tune a geospatial foundation model (e.g., a vision transformer pre-trained on satellite imagery) to predict rooftop PV potential directly from recent high-resolution optical imagery, using the LiDAR-derived ground-truth as training labels. This would make the paper's spatial equity metric for PV potential (equation 15, SPV) computable for any city globally with access to satellite imagery — dramatically expanding transferability. The equity-based SVI metric would then directly drive site selection for community solar in low-income areas, operationalizing SDG 7.1 at scale. Adding neighbourhood energy-storage potential (SST) as a second output head would give planners a complete spatial siting map from a single model pass.

Using LiDAR-derived PV potential as supervised labels for satellite-based inference is a concrete application: the resulting model would feed into urban analytics workflows I develop for assessing distributed renewable energy equity — pairing seamlessly with my spatial KDE and MGWR methods to characterize rooftop solar adoption gaps across socioeconomic gradients.
