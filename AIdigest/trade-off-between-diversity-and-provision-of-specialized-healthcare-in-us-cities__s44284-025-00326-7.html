Trade-off between diversity and provision of specialized healthcare in US cities

Tian Gan · Tanisha Dighe · Maurizio Porfiri Published 2025-09-17 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00326-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 898 US cities and 75 medical specialties, big cities offer a more diverse menu of specialists — but, surprisingly, the per-capita and per-area provision of most specialties scales sublinearly with city size. In other words, the larger the city, the fewer specialist providers each resident has. The authors attribute this diversity-vs-provision trade-off to economic clustering of the healthcare sector and to higher patient loading per provider in large cities, and call for city-specific rather than one-size-fits-all access policies.

★

Why It Matters

  • Overturns the "urban = better access" shortcut. The well-worn urban-rural narrative is built almost entirely on primary care; this is a multidimensional look at specialist access, and it finds the cities advantage is real for variety but reverses for volume per person.
  • Names a hidden inequality. The sublinear scaling of provision means residents of large cities can be quietly under-served per capita even while the city looks well-equipped — a form of inequity invisible to aggregate counts.
  • Mechanistic, not just descriptive. By linking the pattern to economic clustering (agglomeration of providers) and patient loading, it offers a causal story planners and health systems can act on.
  • Actionable policy framing. The diversity-vs-provision tension argues against uniform national targets and for city-specific workforce and siting strategies tied to a city's size class.

Core Methods

  • Urban scaling analysis: fit log–log power laws of provider counts against city population and area for each of the 75 specialties, classifying each as superlinear, linear or sublinear via its scaling exponent (Fig. 1, Fig. 4).
  • Per-capita / per-area provision mapping: compute and map provider density across the USA to expose the sublinear provision pattern in large cities (Fig. 2).
  • Diversity measurement: quantify the breadth/diversity of specialties available within each city to show the diversity advantage of large cities (Fig. 3).
  • Economic-geography lens: interpret results through clustering/agglomeration of healthcare firms and patient loading per provider as the proposed mechanism for the trade-off.
  • Network science: treat the specialty composition as a structured system to relate a specialty's breadth to its scaling exponent.

① From provider counts to demand-aware access (replace proximity with 2SFCA)

Extending this paperThe paper measures provision as providers per capita / per area — a supply-side count that ignores who can actually reach which specialist and how providers compete for the same catchment. A direct extension: recompute its 898-city, 75-specialty signal with a specialty-resolved 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility surface so the "sublinear provision" claim is restated in terms of realized reachable supply, not raw density — testing whether the trade-off survives when travel and competition are modeled.

Extending it: decompose each city's provision into within-city accessibility distributions (e.g., by neighborhood density and SES) and test whether the cross-city scaling law is generated by within-city inequality — directly applying the "scaling-from-within" thesis to specialist access.

Extending this paperThe paper attributes the trade-off partly to high patient loading but uses no actual patient-flow data. Extending it: couple the provider scaling with an intercity healthcare mobility network (where patients travel for specialty care) so loading becomes measured rather than assumed — turning the proposed mechanism into a tested, flow-based one and revealing which large cities absorb others' specialist demand.
