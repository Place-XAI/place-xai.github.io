Extensive terrestrial biodiversity threats from global hillside urban expansion

Kaifang Shi · Yizhen Wu · Xiufeng Sun · Yuanzheng Cui · Zuoqi Chen · Lisheng Song · Fengman Fang · Weidong Cao · Jinji Ma · Chang Huang · Lei Gao · Bailang Yu · Brett A. Bryan Published 2025-09-09 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00316-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Between 2000 and 2020 the world saw 11.65 Mha of hillside (sloping-terrain) urban expansion, with 35% falling inside biodiversity hotspots. Compared with flat-land growth, hillside expansion disproportionately encroaches on natural habitat and intensifies habitat fragmentation, directly affecting ~70% of globally threatened terrestrial vertebrate species. Under future Shared Socioeconomic Pathway scenarios, continued hillside sprawl would hinder biodiversity-conservation targets out to 2050 — calling for terrain-aware, conservation-prioritizing land-use and urban-planning strategies in ecologically sensitive sloping areas.

Why It Matters

  • Names a blind spot in urban-growth science. Most global urban-expansion studies treat the urban footprint as flat; this is the first systematic worldwide quantification of hillside (sloped) urbanization and its distinct ecological cost.
  • Connects terrain to biodiversity loss. It shows that where on the landscape cities climb — onto slopes — matters as much as how much they grow, because hillsides carry richer, more intact habitat and fragment more severely when built on.
  • Speaks directly to two global agendas. Results are framed against the UN Sustainable Development Goals and the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework, quantifying how many of 186 countries and 35 hotspots can still meet conservation targets by 2030/2050.

Core Methods

  • Terrain-stratified change mapping: overlay global landform units with multiple urban/impervious products to separate hillside vs. plain urban expansion within 3 km × 3 km grid cells, 2000–2020.
  • Multi-product cross-validation: reconcile four independent global urban datasets (GlobeLand30, GAIA, GISA 2.0, GISD30) and validate against high-resolution imagery (Google Earth) at distributed validation sites to bound mapping uncertainty.
  • Habitat-loss & fragmentation metrics: quantify natural-habitat encroachment and compute landscape metrics (e.g., mean patch size) to compare fragmentation from hillside vs. flat expansion.
  • Species-impact assessment: intersect IUCN range polygons with habitat change to estimate effects on threatened terrestrial vertebrates at national and hotspot scales.
  • Scenario projection: apply SSP-based future urban-expansion datasets to assess attainability of biodiversity-conservation targets by 2030 and 2050 across 186 countries and 35 hotspots.

① Reconcile the four conflicting urban products with a learned data-fusion layer

Extending this paperThe paper rests on cross-comparing four independent global urban/impervious products (GlobeLand30, GAIA, GISA 2.0, GISD30) that are known to disagree substantially across scales and terrain. Rather than reconciling them by overlay rules, the hillside-expansion product could be re-estimated with a probabilistic, terrain-aware data-fusion model that learns each sensor's slope-dependent error and outputs a calibrated uncertainty layer — sharpening the 11.65 Mha headline and the "where slopes were built" map.

Added value to my researchThis is directly my multi-source data fusion line: the same calibrated-uncertainty fusion (remote sensing + impervious products + terrain) is exactly what I need to harden land-cover inputs feeding 2SFCA service-area delineation and exposure metrics in urban-health studies.

② Turn the static species-overlap into an explainable predictor of slope-driven threat

Extending this paperThe paper intersects IUCN ranges with habitat change to report ~70% of threatened species affected, but treats the species-threat link as a geometric overlay. Extending it: train an explainable machine-learning model that predicts per-species threat from terrain, fragmentation metrics (mean patch size), elevation and hotspot membership, then use SHAP-style attribution to reveal which slope and landscape features drive vertebrate vulnerability — converting a descriptive overlay into a mechanistic, transferable risk model.

③ Replace SSP overlays with a learned, terrain-constrained urban-growth simulator

Extending this paperFuture threats (Fig. 5) come from pre-computed SSP urban-expansion layers that do not explicitly model the slope-preference dynamics this paper itself documents. Extending it: build a physics-/terrain-informed urban-growth simulator (graph- or cellular-automata-based) whose growth probabilities are constrained by slope, habitat value and connectivity, so 2030/2050 projections internalize the very hillside bias the paper measures — and can be re-run under conservation-zoning interventions.

④ Reframe conservation targets as an efficiency–equity facility/zoning optimization

Extending this paperThe paper diagnoses which of 186 countries and 35 hotspots can meet 2030/2050 targets but stops short of prescribing where to protect. Extending it: cast hillside conservation as a spatial optimization that trades off development pressure vs. biodiversity benefit vs. equitable burden across countries — solving for the minimal set of slope cells to protect to keep the most species above threshold, exactly the structure of a facility-location problem.

⑤ Couple hillside habitat loss to human health and the wildland–urban interface

Extending this paperThe paper frames hillside expansion purely as a biodiversity threat, yet sloping fringes are also the wildland–urban interface where habitat fragmentation reshapes human exposure (zoonoses, disaster risk, green-space access). Extending it: link the hillside-expansion product to human-health and exposure layers, testing whether fragmented hillside fringes co-locate with health vulnerability — a space–human-system coupling the paper sets up but does not pursue.
