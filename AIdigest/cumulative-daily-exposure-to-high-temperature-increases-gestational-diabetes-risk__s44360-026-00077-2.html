Cumulative daily exposure to high temperature increases gestational diabetes risk

Laura Granés · Anna Palomar-Cros · Albert Espiell · Elena Roel · Elisa Gallo · Xavier Basagaña · Joan Ballester · Andrea Pistillo · Talita Duarte-Salles Published 2026-03-05 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00077-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Cumulative exposure to 30 days of high ambient temperature is associated with a twofold increased risk of gestational diabetes (GD) in the Barcelona metropolitan area (2011–2022, n = 8,796 cases). The risk is disproportionately concentrated in the most socioeconomically deprived neighbourhoods, underscoring that climate-health burdens compound existing social inequalities. Given projected Mediterranean warming, equity-centred public health and climate-resilience policies are urgently needed to protect pregnant populations.

★

Why It Matters

  • First cumulative-exposure analysis for heat and GD. Prior studies examined single-day or trimester-averaged temperature; this is among the first to apply distributed lag nonlinear models (DLNM) over a 30-day window, showing that it is the accumulation of heat days — not a single extreme event — that drives GD risk.
  • Quantifies a twofold risk amplification. The absolute increase in relative risk (RR ≈ 2×) at the highest cumulative temperatures is clinically significant, providing a concrete magnitude for risk communication and antenatal guidelines.
  • Reveals socioeconomic fault lines. Stratification by census-tract deprivation index shows that the heat–GD association is steeper in the most deprived areas, directly linking environmental exposure to health inequity in a high-income European setting.
  • Policy-actionable in a warming Mediterranean context. Barcelona and comparable Mediterranean cities are already experiencing rising summer temperatures; findings give decision-makers a quantitative basis for prioritizing cooling interventions and prenatal monitoring in deprived urban areas.
  • Methodological template for other pregnancy outcomes. The DLNM + time-series + deprivation-stratification workflow can be replicated for other heat-sensitive outcomes (preterm birth, pre-eclampsia, low birth weight) using the same SIDIAP primary-care infrastructure.

Core Methods

  • Study design: time-series study — daily counts of new GD diagnoses modelled as a function of daily mean temperature, with the temporal correlation structure as the unit of analysis rather than individual cohort follow-up.
  • Exposure model — DLNM: Distributed Lag Nonlinear Models simultaneously capture (a) the nonlinear dose–response between temperature and GD risk at each lag, and (b) the lag structure over the 30 days preceding diagnosis — yielding a bivariate temperature–lag response surface.
  • Lag window: 30-day exposure period (primary analysis); sensitivity analyses at 20, 40, and 60 days confirm robustness.
  • Cumulative relative risk (RR): the summary measure integrates the lag-specific effects across all 30 days, expressing the total risk amplification from sustained high-temperature exposure.
  • Confounder control: adjustment for seasonality, long-term temporal trends, and national holiday days (included as factor variables — see Extended Data Table 3). Day-of-week effects and other calendar confounders addressed within the time-series framework.
  • Stratification: models re-run by (1) census-tract deprivation quartile, (2) maternal age (<35 / ≥35 years), (3) pre-pregnancy BMI (normal vs. overweight/obese), (4) nationality (Europe, Africa, America, Asia) to unpack effect heterogeneity.
  • Sensitivity analyses: GD diagnosis date shifted 1 and 2 weeks earlier to probe reverse causation; exposure windows varied (20, 40, 60 days).
  • Reference temperature: the minimum-morbidity temperature (22.3 °C) used as the referent for lag-specific and cumulative RR reporting.

① Spatially explicit heat-exposure mapping: from census-tract index to fine-grained UHI surfaces

Extending this paperThe current study stratifies risk by a census-tract deprivation index but uses a single area-wide temperature time series — it cannot distinguish whether deprived residents experience objectively hotter microclimates (UHI) or are more biologically susceptible given the same temperature. A direct extension would couple the DLNM with high-resolution urban heat island (UHI) rasters (e.g., satellite land-surface temperature products or local sensor networks) to assign resident-level heat exposure, making the exposure–deprivation interaction spatially explicit rather than ecologically inferred.

② Mobility-adjusted exposure: accounting for where pregnant women actually spend time

Extending this paperThe paper assigns temperature exposure by residential location, but pregnant women move between home, workplaces, and healthcare facilities throughout their day — especially relevant for the deprivation analysis, since lower-income individuals may commute through hotter micro-environments or lack air-conditioned spaces. Extending the paper by integrating anonymized GPS or mobile-phone mobility traces would yield activity-weighted daily temperature exposure rather than a residential proxy, potentially sharpening the deprivation effect and revealing activity-specific risk windows (e.g., outdoor commute hours).

④ Neighbourhood-level equity audit: green-space cooling as a modifiable mediator

Extending this paperThe paper documents that deprived areas suffer greater heat–GD risk but does not model why. Urban greenness and tree cover are well-established temperature moderators that are also socially unequally distributed. Extending the deprivation-stratified DLNM with NDVI or urban-green-space exposure as an effect modifier would test whether differential tree cover mediates the deprivation–heat interaction — providing a specific, spatially actionable lever (targeted greening of deprived census tracts) alongside the broader equity narrative.
