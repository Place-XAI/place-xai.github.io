The interplay of cloud cover and 3D urban structures reduces human access to sunlight

Shengbiao Wu · Bin Chen · Jiafu An · Chen Lin · Peng Gong Published 2024-09-02 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00120-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Sunlight in cities is eroded by two stacked filters that urban studies usually treat separately: cloud cover and 3D building geometry. Applying a unified framework (natural day length × cloud cover × sky-view-factor shading) to 1,353 US cities for 2020, the authors find a total loss of 2,896 h (≈121 days) of available sunlight per year — of which 2,448 h (≈102 days) comes from clouds and 448 h (≈19 days) from 3D urban structures. Because both cloudiness and vertical urbanization are trending upward, the deficit is projected to worsen, making sunlight access a first-class urban-planning and public-health concern.

Why It Matters

  • Names a resource that planning ignores. Daylight is treated as free and infinite; this paper makes it a measurable, budgetable quantity (hours/days lost per year per city) that planners and public-health agencies can actually track.
  • Disentangles two confounded drivers. By separating the atmospheric (cloud) and geometric (building) contributions, it shows where the leverage is — clouds dominate the loss, but the 19-day urban-structure penalty is the part cities can directly design away.
  • Links physical form to human health. Sunlight deficiency is tied to vitamin D, circadian regulation, mood/mental health, and myopia; the framework converts opaque urban morphology into a health-relevant exposure metric.
  • Reveals a worsening trajectory. Three independent cloud datasets show rising cloud cover, and vertical urbanization is accelerating — so the sunlight deficit is a compounding, not static, problem.
  • Reusable, satellite-driven pipeline. Built entirely on open Earth-observation products (lidar DSM, MODIS/GOES/ISCCP cloud masks, day-length astronomy), the method is transferable to any city worldwide.

Core Methods

  • Three-filter sunlight model: available sunlight = natural day length (astronomy) × cloud-free fraction (satellite cloud masks) × open-sky fraction (3D building shading), evaluated per location and aggregated to city level.
  • Sky view factor (SVF) from lidar: compute per-pixel SVF from the 3DEP DSM (via RVT_py) to translate 3D urban geometry into a quantitative shading/obstruction term.
  • Astronomical day-length computation: sunrise–sunset duration derived with the NOAA solar-position algorithm to set the theoretical sunlight ceiling at each latitude/date.
  • Multi-sensor cloud cross-validation: reconcile GOES-16, MODIS (Terra+Aqua), and ISCCP cloud products to estimate cloud-cover loss robustly and to test the cloud-cover trend.
  • Attribution & association analysis: decompose total loss into cloud vs. structure components, and relate the built-up sunshine-reduction fraction to mean building height across the 1,353 cities (Fig. 3).

① Turn the supply-side sunlight map into a population-weighted exposure surface

Extending this paperThe paper computes sunlight loss over built-up area, but never asks how many people actually stand where the sunlight is lost. A direct extension: overlay gridded population (WorldPop/LandScan) and human-activity proxies (mobile-phone dwell time, time-use) onto the per-pixel sunlight surface to convert hours-lost-per-place into person-hours of sunlight deficit, and break it down by neighborhood income to expose an environmental-justice gradient the current city-mean masks.

② Replace the static SVF term with a learned 3D-morphology operator

Extending this paperThe paper's structural penalty rests on a geometric SVF computed once from a DSM. Extending it: train a graph/CNN operator that maps the 3D building field (height, footprint, spacing, street canyon orientation) to the sunshine-reduction fraction, so the framework can run on coarser global 3D building products and predict sunlight loss for cities lacking dense lidar — generalizing the US-only result worldwide.

Added value to my researchThis sits squarely in my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction agenda: an SVF surrogate with SHAP/attention attribution would tell planners which morphological knobs (height vs. spacing vs. orientation) buy back the most daylight — a directly actionable XAI output.

③ Couple sunlight loss with the city's own cloud-feedback loop

Extending this paperThe paper treats cloud cover as an exogenous filter, yet cites evidence that urban morphology itself enhances local clouds. Extending it: close the loop by modeling how 3D form and surface heating modulate convective cloud formation, so the framework captures the feedback between vertical urbanization and the cloud term — turning two independent filters into one coupled system.

Added value to my researchThis is a natural target for physics-informed graph learning: encode the morphology→cloud relationship as a soft physical constraint in a spatiotemporal GNN, the same coupling-of-space-human-systems framing I use for urban health prediction.
