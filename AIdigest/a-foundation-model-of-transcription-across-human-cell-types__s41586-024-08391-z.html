A foundation model of transcription across human cell types

Fu, Xi · Mo, Shentong · Buendia, Alejandro · Laurent, Anouchka P. · Shao, Anqi · Alvarez-Torres, Maria del Mar · Yu, Tianji · Tan, Jimin · Su, Jiayu · Sagatelian, Romella · Ferrando, Adolfo A. · Ciccia, Alberto · Lan, Yanyan · Owens, David M. · Palomero, Teresa · Xing, Eric P. · Rabadan, Raul Published 2025-01-08 DOI 10.1038/s41586-024-08391-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

GET (General Expression Transformer) is an interpretable foundation model pre-trained on chromatin accessibility data across 213 human fetal and adult cell types that achieves experimental-level accuracy (Pearson r = 0.94) in predicting gene expression in previously unseen cell types, relying solely on motif binding scores and chromatin accessibility without any paired expression measurement. Beyond expression prediction, GET uncovers universal and cell-type-specific transcription factor interaction networks via causal discovery and AlphaFold structural analysis, and in B cells identifies a lymphocyte-specific PAX5–NR2C2 interaction that mechanistically explains a leukaemia-predisposing germline mutation.

★

Why It Matters

  • First generalizable transcription foundation model. Prior state-of-the-art models (Enformer, Basenji2, Expecto) are fine-tuned on training cell types and cannot extrapolate; GET achieves Pearson r = 0.94 on leave-out cell types — on par with experimental replicates.
  • Cross-platform and cross-assay generalizability. GET transfers to new sequencing platforms (10x multiome lymph nodes, CAGE, OmniATAC, tumour cells from GBM patients) via LoRA fine-tuning in minutes, reducing 99% of parameters.
  • Interpretable causal discovery of TF networks. Using the gene-by-motif Jacobian matrix and LiNGAM causal discovery, GET constructs a structural catalogue of 1,718 TF pairs with interaction directionality — a first at this scale.
  • Biomedical disease insight. GET identified a novel PAX5–NR2C2 interaction in B cells and showed that the familial leukaemia germline variant G183S increases this interaction, providing a mechanistic explanation of a previously elusive mutation in the PAX5 intrinsically disordered region.
  • Open science. Pretrained models, data and inference results are publicly hosted on HuggingFace, AWS S3, Zenodo, and GitHub (github.com/GET-Foundation), ensuring full reproducibility.

Core Methods

  • Two-stage transformer architecture: (1) Self-supervised masked pretraining — randomly masking 50% of accessible regions, predicting motif scores of masked regions; (2) Supervised fine-tuning of a linear expression head with Poisson negative log-likelihood loss. Input is a 200 × 283 matrix (200 peaks × 282 motif clusters + optional accessibility).
  • Regulatory element embedding + attention: RegionEmb linear projection → 12 transformer encoder layers with multi-head attention (MHA) over regulatory elements, learning cis- and trans-interactions between TF binding sites within a ~2–4 Mbp genomic window.
  • Parameter-efficient transfer learning (LoRA): low-rank adaptation of region embedding and encoder layers reduces fine-tuning parameters by 99%, enabling rapid adaptation to new assays (CAGE, OmniATAC, scATAC) in <30 minutes on a single GPU.
  • Model interpretation via Jacobian: gradient of expression output with respect to motif inputs yields region importance scores and gene-by-motif contribution matrices — enabling identification of cis-regulatory elements and upstream regulators cell-type-specifically.
  • Causal discovery (LiNGAM): applied to the gene-by-motif matrix to infer directed motif–motif interaction networks; benchmarked against STRING physical interactions, achieving 25.2% precision at top-1% pairs (vs. 5.6% random background).
  • AlphaFold2/3 structural analysis: pLDDT-based protein domain segmentation, multimer structure prediction across TF segment pairs, and AlphaFold3 validation of PAX5–NR2C2 interaction; molecular dynamics simulation (100 ns, GROMACS, a99SBdisp force field) to confirm IDR folding upon binding.
  • Zero-shot lentiMPRA: in silico insertion of 226,243 sequences across 600 genome positions each, predicting regulatory activity as mean predicted expression × predicted accessibility; outperforms Enformer (Pearson r = 0.55 vs. 0.44) despite using no lentiMPRA training data.

① Adapt GET's masked pretraining paradigm to geospatial foundation models

Extending this paperGET's core innovation is its two-stage design: self-supervised masked pretraining on chromatin accessibility across 213 cell types, then supervised fine-tuning on expression with the same architecture. The paper's own Discussion explicitly calls for incorporating additional data layers (3D chromatin architecture, TF footprints, perturbation states) via the same LoRA fine-tuning approach. A direct extension is to apply this masked pretraining philosophy to a broader regulatory atlas — including disease cell states, perturbed transcriptomes, and spatial transcriptomics — using GET as a backbone and adding new output heads, exactly as the paper proposes for future work.

Added value to my researchThe masked-pretraining + LoRA fine-tuning paradigm is directly transferable to geospatial foundation models: instead of regulatory elements, mask out grid cells or spatial patches of remote sensing imagery or urban sensor data; pretrain on a broad multi-city corpus; then fine-tune on a sparse labeled target city. This is the recipe for parameter-efficient adaptation of a geospatial FM for public health or accessibility mapping in data-sparse regions — exactly the "fine-tuning geospatial foundation models" direction in my research agenda.

② Extend the Jacobian-based interpretability to spatial attribution maps

Extending this paperGET uses the Jacobian of expression output with respect to motif inputs to produce region importance scores — a form of gradient-based attribution that the paper validates against base-editing and CRISPRi benchmarks. An extension of this paper would apply integrated gradients or DeepLIFT (which the authors explicitly flag as future improvements) to produce more robust, baseline-corrected attribution, and further couple the region importance score to three-dimensional chromatin contact maps to distinguish proximal vs. distal regulatory logic systematically rather than using the heuristic DistanceContactMap module the paper currently relies on.

Added value to my researchGET's Jacobian attribution is structurally identical to the spatially-indexed gradient maps in explainable ML for spatial prediction — my MGWR/GWR and ML ensemble workflows already produce feature attribution that varies across space. The lesson from GET is to treat the gradient as a first-class scientific output, benchmarked against ground-truth perturbation experiments. For geospatial ML, this motivates coupling SHAP/integrated-gradient spatial heatmaps with quasi-experimental designs (e.g., policy changes, natural experiments) as validation analogues to CRISPRi.

③ Replace scalar motif contribution with a causal graph operator for network-level GeoAI

Extending this paperGET's most novel methodological contribution is applying LiNGAM causal discovery to the gene-by-motif matrix, converting correlational co-regulation signals into directed TF–TF interaction networks with quantified effect sizes. The current implementation is cell-type-agnostic: one global matrix is used. An extension would perform cell-type-specific causal discovery (leveraging GET's per-cell-type motif matrices) and then learn a graph neural network to propagate causal signals across the TF interaction network — turning GET from a node-level predictor into an edge-informed network model capable of simulating perturbation cascades across the full TF interactome.

Added value to my researchThis graph-operator framing is directly applicable to my physics-informed graph learning agenda: urban systems also have directed causal graphs (job → commute → pollution → health), and applying LiNGAM-style causal discovery to spatiotemporal co-variation matrices (mobile phone, POI, census) could reveal the urban regulatory grammar analogously to GET's TF interaction catalogue. The directed causal graph would then anchor a physics-informed GNN where edge weights are causally estimated rather than learned from correlation alone.

④ Transfer GET's cross-domain generalizability design to cross-city health prediction

Extending this paperGET is explicitly designed for leave-out cell-type generalization: it is pretrained on a diverse cell-type corpus so that fine-tuning on any single new cell type (or tumour sample) requires only 100 epochs or a few minutes of LoRA training. The paper demonstrates this for K562, GBM tumour cells, lymph nodes, and CAGE/OmniATAC assays. An extension would formally characterize what makes a target cell type "near" vs. "far" from training distribution — and provide a cell-type similarity metric that predicts how much fine-tuning data is needed — converting the empirical leave-out benchmark into a principled sample-efficiency curve.

Added value to my researchThe same leave-out design maps directly onto cross-city generalization in GeoAI: pre-train a spatial health model on a large multi-city corpus (analogous to GET's 213 cell types), then fine-tune on a new city with minimal labeled data. GET's result — that one-shot fine-tuning on a single GBM patient achieves Pearson r > 0.9 — is a strong proof-of-concept for how few-shot spatial adaptation could work for primary-care accessibility or telehealth prediction in underserved cities where labeled outcomes are sparse.

⑤ Build a multi-task GET variant using multi-source data fusion analogous to mobile-phone + remote-sensing fusion

Extending this paperGET currently uses two modalities — chromatin accessibility and DNA sequence (encoded as motif scores) — with optional quantitative vs. binary ATAC settings. The Discussion explicitly calls for incorporating TF footprints, 3D chromatin architecture, regulator expression profiles, and single-cell embeddings as additional input modalities. A concrete extension would design a multi-task GET with separate input encoders for each modality (scATAC, scRNA, HiChIP, ChIP-seq) fused via cross-modal attention, trained jointly across all 213 cell types, and evaluated on multi-modal benchmarks — turning GET into a true multi-omic foundation model consistent with the broader trend examined in the cited literature.

Added value to my researchGET's modality-fusion challenge is structurally identical to my multi-source data fusion work with mobile-phone records, social media, remote sensing, and POIs: each source has different spatial/temporal resolution and missingness patterns, requiring principled fusion rather than naive concatenation. GET's solution — separate pretraining on the most abundant modality (ATAC) then fine-tuning with paired multi-modal data — is a practical blueprint for how to fuse sparse GPS trajectories with dense satellite imagery for spatiotemporal health modeling, especially in settings where multi-source ground truth is rare.
