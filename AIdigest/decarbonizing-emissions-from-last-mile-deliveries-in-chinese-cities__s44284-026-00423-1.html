Decarbonizing emissions from last-mile deliveries in Chinese cities

Zhiqing Hong · Zelong Li · Shuxin Zhong · Wenjun Lyu · Haotian Wang · Shenhao Wang · Shuai Wang · Yunhuai Liu · Dabo Guan · Tian He · Guang Wang · Desheng Zhang Published 2026-04-28 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00423-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using 14 billion orders and location traces from 1.9 million couriers across 365 Chinese cities, the study delivers the first nationwide, fine-grained estimate of last-mile delivery greenhouse-gas (GHG) emissions. It finds that emissions decouple from order growth — an 83.5% rise in orders (2023→2024) produced only a 31.3% rise in emissions — while smaller cities emit up to four times more per order because lower urban density depresses delivery efficiency. Mitigation simulations show last-mile emissions could be cut by up to 84.2%, revealing concrete pathways to sustainable urban logistics.

★

Why It Matters

  • Fills a measurement vacuum at national scale. Mitigation has been blocked by the absence of nationwide, fine-grained last-mile emission accounting; this is the first estimate built bottom-up from individual courier trajectories rather than top-down inventory averages.
  • Reveals a decoupling that reframes the e-commerce debate. Order volume and emissions do not scale together — order growth of 83.5% yielded only 31.3% more emissions, showing efficiency gains can absorb much of demand growth.
  • Exposes a density-driven equity gradient. Per-order emissions are up to 4× higher in smaller, lower-density cities, linking the geometry of urban form directly to logistics carbon and to inter-city fairness.
  • Quantifies an actionable ceiling. Mitigation scenarios bound the achievable reduction at up to 84.2%, giving planners and platforms a concrete target rather than a vague aspiration.

Core Methods

  • Trajectory-based emission estimation: reconstruct last-mile delivery routes from 1.9M courier location traces and 14B orders, then convert distance/mode/energy into GHG emissions using IPCC-aligned factors — a bottom-up, fine-grained alternative to top-down inventories.
  • Multi-scale aggregation: emissions resolved at the city, courier and order level (Fig. 4) and mapped nationwide across 365 cities (Fig. 2).
  • Decoupling / growth analysis: track the temporal divergence between order volume growth and emission growth (2023→2024) to quantify efficiency-driven decoupling (Fig. 3).
  • Density–efficiency attribution: relate per-order emissions to urban density across city sizes, identifying lower density as the primary driver of higher per-order emissions in smaller cities (up to 4×).
  • Mitigation simulation: simulate operational/structural interventions to bound the achievable emission reduction at up to 84.2%, with robustness analyses reported in the Supplementary Information.

① Turn the density–efficiency finding into an explainable spatial model

Extending this paperThe paper reports that smaller, lower-density cities emit up to 4× more per order, attributing this to delivery efficiency shaped by urban density — but the relationship is presented descriptively. A direct extension: fit an explainable ML / MGWR model of per-order emissions on density, road structure, station spacing and city tier, exposing where and why the density-efficiency penalty is strongest and whether it is locally non-stationary across the 365 cities.

② Recast mitigation as an efficiency–equity facility-location optimization

Extending this paperThe paper's mitigation simulation bounds reductions at up to 84.2% but treats the 106,238-station network as given. Extending it: jointly optimize station siting, courier zone assignment and consolidation as a facility-location problem, explicitly trading mean emission reduction against the per-order inter-city equity gap the paper uncovers — so smaller cities are not left subsidizing the carbon of denser ones.

Added value to my researchThis is the same efficiency-vs-equity optimization I run for EV charging-station placement, transplanted to delivery stations: a multi-objective siting model where the equity term penalizes the low-density per-order penalty, directly reusing my Monte-Carlo / network-allocation toolkit.

③ Couple last-mile delivery flows with a learned mobility/flow layer

Extending this paperEmissions here are reconstructed from realized courier traces; the paper does not predict how flows — and thus emissions — would reorganize under new demand or new station layouts. Extending it with a learned delivery-flow generator (deep gravity / graph model) would let the framework forecast emission maps for counterfactual demand and urban-form scenarios rather than only reporting the observed network.

④ Move from annual estimates to near-real-time emission monitoring

Extending this paperThe decoupling result (83.5% vs 31.3%) is computed over a 2023→2024 window; the estimate is essentially retrospective. Extending it: build a streaming, near-real-time last-mile emission monitor from continuously arriving courier traces, so platforms and regulators can observe the decoupling trajectory as it forms and intervene mid-cycle rather than after the fact.

⑤ Link delivery-carbon efficiency to the 15-minute / compact-city paradigm

Extending this paperThe paper shows density raises delivery efficiency and lowers per-order emissions, but stops at logistics.
