Addressing food security in African cities

Jane Battersby · Vanessa Watson Published 2018-04-18 DOI 10.1038/s41893-018-0051-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Urban food insecurity in sub-Saharan Africa is not simply a rural spillover: over 50% of urban populations in least-developed countries are food insecure. Informal food traders remain the primary and most accessible source for poor households — not supermarkets — yet current planning visions systematically displace informal markets in favor of formal retail. Effective policy must recognize and plan for the entire food system, including the complex symbiosis between formal and informal retail, to protect urban food security and livelihoods.

Why It Matters

  • Reframes food insecurity as an urban challenge. Drawing on FAO's 146-country survey (2014–2015), the paper documents that urban food insecurity rates (50% in least-developed countries) exceed rural rates (43%) — overturning the assumption that hunger is primarily a rural problem.
  • Centers informal traders as critical infrastructure. Survey data from 840 households in Kisumu, Kenya reveal that informal retailers — not supermarkets — are the dominant, most accessible, and most affordable food source for poor urban households, yet they remain outside formal planning frameworks.
  • Identifies a planning-market mismatch. Urban master plans in Africa actively encourage supermarket-led mall development while removing informal traders, thereby eroding the very retail networks that sustain food-insecure populations.
  • Connects food to multiple urban crises. The paper traces food insecurity to poverty, unemployment, non-communicable disease burden, and greenhouse gas emissions — making food-system planning a lever for broad urban development outcomes.
  • Offers a policy-action roadmap. The paper calls for revised urban planning regulations, dedicated public food markets, and cross-sectoral food policy that supports — rather than marginalizes — informal food distribution networks.

Core Methods

  • Household survey analysis: frequency analysis of food-source use across 840 sampled households in Kisumu, Kenya — identifying the ten most-used food sources and the proportion of food purchased from supermarkets vs. informal and other outlets.
  • Comparative case synthesis: structured review of existing empirical literature across several sub-Saharan African cities (Harare, Lusaka, Nairobi, Kenyan small towns) to triangulate patterns of informal retail use, supermarket penetration, and household food strategies.
  • Policy analysis: review of urban planning frameworks and municipal governance documents to trace how formal plans accommodate or exclude informal food retail.
  • Systems framing: the paper positions informal and formal food retail as parts of a single, interconnected food system — arguing against binary policy prescriptions that treat them as competing, separate entities.

① Map the spatial footprint of informal vs. formal food access across African cities using geospatial AI

Extending this paperThe paper's core empirical claim — that informal traders are the primary food source for poor households — rests on a single-city household survey (Kisumu, 840 households). A major extension would be to scale this finding spatially and comparatively across sub-Saharan African cities by fusing satellite-derived settlement maps, OpenStreetMap point-of-interest data, and gridded poverty indices to estimate informal food retail density as a spatial surface. Coupling this with the paper's qualitative framework would test whether Kisumu is representative or exceptional, and identify cities most at risk from supermarket-led displacement — an empirical gap the paper explicitly acknowledges but cannot fill.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct extension of my GeoAI and multi-source data fusion agenda: combining remote sensing slum maps, POI density from OSM, and household survey anchor points to build a spatial accessibility surface for informal food retail — essentially a food-sector 2SFCA. My spatiotemporal kernel density and R2SFCA methods could replace the static radius assumed in supermarket-accessibility studies, using mobility-informed catchment areas suited to the walking-dependent patterns of low-income African households.

② Quantify the food-access deficit caused by informal retail displacement using facility-location optimization

Extending this paperThe paper argues that removing informal traders harms food security, but provides no quantitative estimate of the access deficit created. A direct extension: model informal markets as a facility layer in a food-access graph, simulate their removal under different urban formalization scenarios (as observed in Harare and Lusaka), and measure the resulting increase in travel time or cost for food-insecure households. The paper's insight that supermarkets cannot replicate informal traders' spatial proximity, small-unit packaging, and credit arrangements means displacement effects are not simply a matter of re-routing — they need to be captured as multi-attribute accessibility deficits.

Added value to my researchThis translates directly into my facility-location and 2SFCA research: treating informal food markets as supply nodes with distance-decay functions calibrated to the walking and transit patterns of poor households — and then running counterfactual removal simulations using my Monte Carlo accessibility framework. The efficiency-vs-equity trade-off I study in EV-charging contexts applies here: formal retail maximizes throughput but fails equity; informal retail is equitable but fragile to policy shocks.

④ Model the informal–formal food supply symbiosis as a network and stress-test it under shocks

Extending this paperThe paper highlights a striking symbiosis: informal traders in Lusaka use a Shoprite supermarket as a wholesaler, creating a supply dependency. This hints at a supply-chain network structure — formal retailers as wholesale hubs, informal traders as last-mile distributors — that the paper describes anecdotally but never models. Extending the paper: construct a bipartite supply network (formal wholesale nodes → informal retail nodes → household demand nodes) and simulate how shocks propagate — trade disruptions, store closures, urban displacement events — to identify which network topologies preserve food access for the urban poor under stress.

Added value to my researchThis connects to my network community detection and physics-informed graph learning agenda: the informal-formal food network has a community structure (clusters of informal traders served by specific wholesale nodes) that can be learned from transaction data or OSM proximity graphs. My SIR-like diffusion models could simulate how a supermarket closure or informal market eviction propagates as a food-access "contagion" through the network — producing resilience maps that planners can act on.
