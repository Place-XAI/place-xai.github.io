Public transport accessibility in villages in and around major Chinese cities

Zihua Chen · Xiaowei Li · Bingzhi Liu · Shaohua Wang · Xiao Li · Jiaxin Li · Xiaohui Yang · Zhenbo Wang Published 2025-07-11 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00277-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Headline gains in public-transport (PT) access hide deep rural shortfalls. Against the 2023 SDG report's claim of over 80% convenient PT access in major Chinese cities, the authors find that only two of ten cities' villages meet the benchmark, with the lowest at just 34%. New bus stations are often placed where they do not track population shifts, and although average fares to city centres fell, the share of villagers stuck with high-fare-only routes rose in several cities. A narrow "10-minute" walking metric masks these added time and cost burdens, so SDG 11 needs a population-responsive planning approach to stay inclusive for peri-urban villagers.

Why It Matters

  • Audits an official SDG claim against ground truth. It directly contradicts the 2023 SDG report's "over 80%" PT-access figure, showing villages near even the biggest Chinese cities can sit at 34% — a cautionary tale about how aggregate indicators conceal rural exclusion.
  • Reframes accessibility as more than walking distance. By tracking commute time, fare burden and demographic change together (2017–2023), it shows the popular "10-minute walk to a stop" metric is necessary but far from sufficient.
  • Exposes a planning–population mismatch. New bus stations frequently fail to follow where villagers actually move to, so investment and need drift apart — a measurable governance failure.
  • Centres the peri-urban village. The forgotten zone between "city" and "countryside" is where SDG 11's inclusivity promise is most fragile; this is one of the few studies to quantify it at scale across China.

Core Methods

  • Multi-source data fusion: merge OSM and scraped AutoNavi transit data, WorldPop population grids and government village records into a consistent 2017–2023 panel for ten cities.
  • Walking-transit accessibility: compute the population share/number within a 10-minute walking-transit zone of a city-bound stop, evaluated against SDG 11 convenient-access benchmarks.
  • Commute-time decomposition: break city-centre travel into walking, riding, transfer and waiting (stopping) components to reveal burdens hidden by a single proximity metric.
  • Fare-burden analysis: track minimum single-trip fares per village stop and the share of villagers served only by high-fare routes (starting fare > 5 CNY), summarised with box-and-whisker statistics.
  • Station-growth vs. population-change matching: standardise bus-station and population change across 20 concentric rings per village (Z-score), measure spatial uniformity with a density-weighted coefficient of variation, and test ring-level alignment with two-sided Pearson correlation (df = 18).

① Replace the binary "10-minute" metric with a demand-weighted 2SFCA accessibility surface

Extending this paperThe paper itself argues the single "10-minute" walking-transit threshold is too narrow, yet its main metric stays a proximity cutoff. A direct extension: rebuild village accessibility as a two-step floating catchment (2SFCA/R2SFCA) surface where supply is route capacity and frequency, demand is WorldPop population, and the friction term is the paper's own decomposed commute time (walk + ride + transfer + wait) plus fare. This converts a yes/no benchmark into a continuous, competition-aware score that the paper's own data already support.

Added value to my researchThis is a clean transit-mode instantiation of my R2SFCA service-area work: it shows how to swap the usual road-network friction for a multi-component public-transport impedance, directly transferable to my primary-care and mental-health access models in peri-urban regions.

② Explain the station–population mismatch with interpretable spatial ML

Extending this paperThe paper documents that new bus stations often fail to follow population shifts and reports ring-level Pearson correlations, but it does not model why the mismatch occurs. Extending it: fit an explainable ML / MGWR model of station-growth-minus-population-change against local covariates (density, road class, land use, fare regime, distance-to-centre), using SHAP and local coefficients to map where and which factors drive misallocation across the ten cities.

③ Add multimodal resilience to the single-mode bus picture

Extending this paperThe paper measures bus-only accessibility at two snapshots and treats it as static. A natural extension: embed villages in a multimodal network (bus + metro feeder + rail) and stress-test it, asking how village accessibility degrades when a route or interchange fails — turning the paper's static 2017/2023 gaps into a resilience surface that flags which villages are one-route-away from isolation.

④ Reframe villages as a peri-urban transition gradient, not a city/rural binary

Extending this paperThe paper splits space into "city" vs. "village," but its concentric-ring design hints at a continuous peri-urban gradient. Extending it: replace the binary with a turbulence/transition index and test whether accessibility shortfalls and fare burdens concentrate in specific transition bands, linking PT inequity to the broader dynamics of rural–urban transformation around megacities.

Extending this paperThe paper diagnoses misplaced stations and rising high-fare-only coverage but stops short of prescribing better placements.
