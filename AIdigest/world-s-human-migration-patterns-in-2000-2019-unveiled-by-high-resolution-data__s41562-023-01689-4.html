World's human migration patterns in 2000–2019 unveiled by high-resolution data

Venla Niva · Alexander Horton · Vili Virkki · Matias Heino · Maria Kosonen · Marko Kallio · Pekka Kinnunen · Guy J. Abel · Raya Muttarak · Maija Taka · Olli Varis · Matti Kummu Published 2023-09-07 DOI 10.1038/s41562-023-01689-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using a novel annual gridded net migration dataset at ~10 km resolution covering 216 countries from 2000 to 2019, this study shows that globally around 50% of the world's urban population lived in areas where migration accelerated urban growth, while a third of the global population lived in provinces where rural areas experienced positive net migration. Critically, socioeconomic factors (human development index) are more strongly associated with migration patterns than climatic factors (aridity), and the direction and impact of migration vary substantially depending on the spatial scale of analysis — from commune to province to nation.

Why It Matters

  • First global annual subnational dataset of net migration at fine resolution. Previous global datasets were coarse (national level) or decadal in resolution; this is the first to provide annual 5 arcmin (~10 km) gridded estimates for 2000–2019, covering 216 countries and enabling both cross-country and within-country analyses.
  • Disentangles the urban–rural migration mosaic at multiple scales. The study reveals that the classic "urban pull–rural push" story holds globally but breaks down at finer scales — 37% of global population lives in communes where both urban and rural areas were net receivers, challenging simplistic narratives.
  • Quantifies the relative roles of socioeconomic vs. climatic drivers. Positive net migration co-occurs far more strongly with high HDI than with aridity — a finding with direct relevance to debates over climate-induced migration.
  • Openly available dataset and interactive explorer. The gridded GeoTIFF datasets and polygon layers are deposited at Zenodo (zenodo.7997134) and visualized via an online net-migration explorer, making the data immediately reusable.
  • Essential for subnational planning. By capturing where in-migration strains or supports local infrastructure, the data support urban service planning, rural development policy, and international migration governance.

Core Methods

  • Subnational census harmonization: collect, gap-fill, interpolate, and extrapolate annual birth and death rate data for 163–124 countries from STATcompiler, Eurostat, OECD, and national census sources; harmonize all observations to a common rate (births or deaths per 1,000 people); bias-correct subnational aggregates against UN national statistics.
  • Downscaling via multiple linear regression + ATPK: fit income-stratified regression models (4 groups) of birth/death rates against subnational HDI, scaled population density, share of reproductive women (for births) and ratio of average age to life expectancy (for deaths); distribute residuals at admin-unit scale to 5 arcmin grid using area-to-point kriging (ATPK); final population-weighted adjustment ensures subnational totals agree with census data (model R² = 0.74 for births, 0.60 for deaths).
  • Net migration calculation: net migration = reported population change (from WorldPop annual counts) minus natural population change (downscaled births minus deaths), applied per grid cell per year.
  • Custom urban extent mapping: annual binary urban/rural classification per country by propagating urban population share (UN WUP) through descending population-density cells, producing a consistent 2000–2019 time series.
  • Socioclimatic binning: divide global inhabited population into 100 bins (10 HDI quantiles × 10 aridity quantiles, each ~1% of global population) to assess migration co-occurrence with socioeconomic and climatic conditions.
  • Multi-scale zonal statistics: aggregate gridded net migration to communal (admin-2), provincial (admin-1), and national (admin-0) levels using the terra R package; compute cumulative sums and linear trend slopes (2000–2019) per unit.
  • Impact classification: categorize each administrative unit into seven impact classes by comparing signs and magnitudes of total population change, cumulative net migration, and natural population change.
  • Validation: compare gridded outputs against independent subnational data (OECD, Eurostat, US counties, South Korea provinces, UN national totals) for WorldPop population, downscaled births/deaths, and net migration.

① Couple the gridded migration dataset with urban public-service demand to map healthcare access stress

Extending this paperThe paper quantifies where migration accelerates urban population growth but stops short of asking what this means for service provision. A direct extension: combine this paper's annual gridded net migration time series with facility inventories and population layers to compute the dynamic demand-to-supply ratio for healthcare, schools, and water services at the 5 arcmin grid level. Regions like West Africa and South Asia where migration accelerated urban growth under low HDI are precisely where infrastructural capacity falls furthest below need — the paper's own socioclimatic bins (Fig. 6) identify these hotspots but do not quantify the service gap.

Added value to my researchThis directly extends my 2SFCA/R2SFCA healthcare accessibility framework: instead of a static population denominator, supply-and-demand ratios become annually updated using the Zenodo migration layers as the demand-change driver. The result is a dynamic accessibility score that reveals how migration-induced population shifts erode or improve spatial equity of primary-care access over two decades — a new application of my service-area delineation methods at global scale.

② Build an explainable ML model of migration from socioclimatic and socioeconomic predictors, validated against this paper's gridded data

Extending this paperThe paper's socioclimatic-bin analysis (Fig. 6) is essentially a two-dimensional binned regression of migration against HDI and aridity. A natural extension is to replace this binning with a spatially explicit, explainable ML (e.g., SHAP-augmented gradient boosted trees or MGWR) that incorporates additional local predictors — conflict, infrastructure density, land-use change, natural disasters — and produces spatially varying importance maps. The paper's Zenodo dataset provides the ideal ground-truth target for such a model, which in turn would make the HDI-vs-aridity finding locally nuanced rather than bin-averaged.

Added value to my researchThis is a natural fit for my explainable GeoAI agenda: using SHAP spatial decomposition or MGWR on a global grid, I can map where HDI dominates migration decisions versus where aridity or conflict takes over — a spatially non-stationary drivers map. MGWR's bandwidth selection would reveal the spatial scale at which socioeconomic versus climatic drivers operate, directly informing policy targeting at the right administrative level.

③ Translate communal-level migration trends into urban scaling laws linking migration rate to city size and density

Extending this paperThe paper reveals that migration patterns depend strongly on spatial scale (Fig. 2) but does not ask whether migration rates themselves follow urban scaling laws (e.g., does net in-migration per capita scale super-linearly with city population?). An extension: extract communal net migration rates for cities in the dataset and fit power-law scaling models, testing whether migration intensity and city rank evolve according to the same Zipfian dynamics as economic output. The paper's three-level administrative data (adm0/1/2) and annual time steps make this a tractable but unexplored question.

Added value to my researchThis connects to my interest in urban agglomeration and regional collaboration: if migration scales predictably with city size, the scaling exponent becomes a summary statistic for the strength of urban pull — actionable for regional planning models. Combined with my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation methods, I can estimate the spatial extent of city influence zones from migration flow data rather than arbitrary administrative buffers.

④ Use the paper's rural out-migration hotspots as a digital-divide risk layer for telehealth accessibility

Extending this paperThe paper identifies rural areas experiencing persistent out-migration and population decline (e.g., parts of Central America, northeastern Brazil, Central Africa, Southeast Asia — see Fig. 6c). These are also areas where the remaining population skews older and less mobile. An extension: cross-link the paper's rural net-migration trend grids with broadband/mobile connectivity infrastructure data to identify where out-migration both drives and is driven by digital-service deserts — a feedback loop the paper's framework could quantify if connected to digital infrastructure datasets.

Added value to my researchThis is central to my telehealth and digital-divide research: the paper's annual communal-level migration maps can serve as a leading indicator for where digital healthcare demand is growing (urban in-migration zones) or where remote populations are becoming increasingly isolated (rural out-migration zones).
