Ecological and demographic drivers of Oropouche virus transmission

Xinyi Hua · Laura W. Alexander · Ingra M. Claro · Yucai Shao · Ronaldo de Jesus · Shirlene T. S. de Lima · Paulo C. Ventura · Rodrigo B. Kato · Marco Ajelli · André B. B. Wilke · Marc A. Suchard · Nuno R. Faria · José Luiz Proenca-Modena · Laura B. Dickson · Ana I. Bento · William M. de Souza Published 2026-03-24 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00065-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Oropouche fever is a strongly rural disease: cumulative incidence in rural Brazilian municipalities was 11.3 times higher than in urban municipalities between 2014 and 2025, inverting the pattern seen for dengue, chikungunya, and Zika (urban-to-rural ratios 2.5–2.8 vs. OROV's 0.6). After fluctuating regionally in the North, OROV underwent a major geographic expansion in 2024 with transmission peaks (Rt) of 3.2–5.5. Municipality-level risk maps across all 5,570 Brazilian municipalities reveal that risk is shaped by interactions among demographic, climatic, and environmental conditions, providing a foundation for targeted surveillance and control of this neglected arbovirus.

Why It Matters

  • Elevates a neglected pathogen with demonstrated pandemic potential. OROV has circulated since the 1950s yet remained understudied; the 2024 expansion into non-endemic Brazilian states and reports of fatal cases and vertical transmission (microcephaly) signal it could follow a Zika-like trajectory.
  • Overturns the urban-centric narrative of arboviral risk. OROV's rural dominance — 11.3× higher incidence than urban — distinguishes it sharply from all other major arboviruses and demands a different surveillance and vector-control strategy.
  • First nationwide, municipality-level spatiotemporal analysis. Covering 30,086 confirmed cases across all 26 states and the Federal District (2014–2025), this is the most comprehensive epidemiological record of OROV ever assembled.
  • Integrates phylodynamics with ecological risk mapping. Pairing Bayesian birth-death phylodynamics (590 genomic segments, BEAST 1.10) with machine-learning risk maps bridges molecular epidemiology and spatial ecology.
  • Directly actionable for public health. Monthly risk maps and the identified climatic/demographic predictors give health authorities practical, spatially explicit targets for vector surveillance and outbreak preparedness.

Core Methods

  • Spatiotemporal descriptive epidemiology: municipality-level mapping of confirmed OROV cases by year, epidemiological week, and urban/rural classification across 11 years; computation of cumulative and period-specific incidence rates; comparison with parallel arboviral time-series (dengue, chikungunya, Zika).
  • Effective reproduction number estimation (Rt): time-varying Rt estimated using the EpiEstim framework (Cori et al., 2013) on weekly case data at national and regional levels to characterize transmission intensity peaks (Rt 3.2–5.5 in the 2024 expansion).
  • Bayesian phylodynamics: birth-death skyline and episodic birth-death-sampling models implemented in BEAST 1.10 on 590 concatenated genomic segments, using Hamiltonian Monte Carlo sampling (scalable gradients); estimates the effective reproductive number Re through time to cross-validate surveillance-derived dynamics.
  • Machine-learning risk mapping (Random Forest): municipality-level risk of OROV outbreak following case introduction modeled as a function of demographic, climatic, and environmental covariates; trained using Random Forest (Breiman, 2001) and Generalized Linear Models; applied to monthly climate normals to produce 12 seasonal risk maps.
  • Digital-signal validation: Google Trends data correlated with laboratory-confirmed case counts at the state level (Spearman's rank correlation) to assess infodemic signals as proxy surveillance.
  • Demographic stratification: age-sex pyramids of cases in rural vs. urban municipalities computed separately for pre-expansion (2014–2022) and expansion (2023–2025) periods.

① Replace static Random Forest risk maps with mobility-aware spatiotemporal contagion models

Extending this paperThe paper's Risk Maps (Fig. 5) are generated from static monthly climate normals plus demographic covariates — they capture potential risk but do not model how OROV cases actually propagate between municipalities via human movement. A direct extension would incorporate inter-municipality mobility flows (e.g., from mobile-phone records or commuting matrices) into a metapopulation SIR framework, testing whether the 2024 geographic expansion was spatially autocorrelated along transport corridors or followed the Random Forest climate-risk surface. The paper already identifies Culicoides midge habitat as the primary ecological driver; mobility could explain the sequence of municipal invasion rather than just the susceptibility.

③ Decode the rural dominance with a landscape-ecology accessibility lens

Extending this paperThe 11.3× rural-vs-urban incidence differential is the paper's most striking finding, but its mechanistic explanation remains incomplete: is rural dominance driven by midge habitat abundance (forest cover, standing water, deforestation edges), poor healthcare access (delayed diagnosis inflating apparent incidence), or under-testing in urban areas? Extending the paper: stratify municipalities by healthcare facility density and testing positivity rate (Fig. S5 already shows state-level testing rates), and disentangle true transmission intensity from ascertainment bias using a spatial Bayesian case-finding model. Linking MapBiomas land-cover change layers with midge presence/absence data could further separate habitat from access effects.

④ Build an explainable machine-learning pipeline to rank OROV risk drivers across spatial scales

Extending this paperThe paper applies Random Forest and GLM to produce risk maps but does not systematically quantify which climate or demographic features most drive risk, nor does it address potential spatial non-stationarity in those relationships (a municipality in the Amazon may respond to temperature very differently from one in the Cerrado). Extending it: apply Shapley Additive Explanations (SHAP) and Geographically Weighted Regression (GWR/MGWR) to decompose the Random Forest predictions, revealing whether the key predictors are spatially invariant (a universal ecological law) or zone-specific — with direct implications for targeting control measures.
