Scaling and network evolution of technology transfer in US cities

Qixiang Li · Debin Du · Yingjie Yu Published 2025-03-07 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00209-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using 166 years (1858–2023) of US patent records, the authors reconstruct technology-transfer networks and show that transfers scale superlinearly with city population, with exponents ordered as intracity transfer > intercity transfer-in > intercity transfer-out. The network's long-run evolution combines a nationwide space-filling process, a hierarchical (core–periphery) structure among cities, and growing functional polycentricity within cities — and these three dynamics rise in tandem with the scaling exponents, tying urban scaling laws directly to network evolution and to local vs. nonlocal knowledge interactions.

★

Why It Matters

  • Closes a gap in urban scaling theory. Superlinear scaling is well documented for patents and incomes, but technology transfer — the bridge from knowledge to economic value — had no scaling law. This is the first systematic quantification.
  • Unifies scaling with network evolution. Rather than treating the scaling exponent as a static constant, the paper shows the exponent grows together with structural changes in the transfer network, offering a dynamical, mechanistic account of why cities scale.
  • Distinguishes local vs. nonlocal knowledge. The ranking intracity > intercity-in > intercity-out empirically validates that within-city (local) knowledge recombination scales most strongly — a long-debated mechanism in innovation geography.
  • 166-year longitudinal lens. Spanning the entire history of US patenting lets the authors observe a frontier economy fill, hierarchize, and polycentrify — a rare deep-time view of urban system evolution.

Core Methods

  • Technology-transfer network construction: link patents to cities via inventor/assignee locations and assignment records to build directed intracity, intercity transfer-in, and intercity transfer-out networks, reconstructed for each period over 166 years.
  • Urban scaling analysis: fit power-law (log–log) regressions of transfer volume against city population to estimate scaling exponents β and test for superlinearity (β > 1) across transfer types and over time.
  • Space-filling characterization: track the nationwide geographic spread of the transfer network — a frontier "space-filling" process as cities and links proliferate.
  • Hierarchy / core–periphery detection: identify hierarchical structure among cities (hubs vs. periphery) and analyze top hubs (Fig. 4).
  • Polycentricity measurement: quantify functional polycentricity within cities and its temporal variation (Fig. 5), then relate it to the growth of scaling exponents.
  • Coupling analysis & regression: link the three structural dynamics to scaling-exponent growth (Supplementary regression analysis), supporting local vs. nonlocal knowledge-interaction mechanisms.

① Decompose the superlinear exponent with a within-city inequality lens

Extending this paperThe paper reports superlinear exponents (intracity > intercity-in > intercity-out) but treats each as a single aggregate slope. A direct extension: ask whether the superlinearity is generated by a few dominant inventor clusters or is broadly distributed, by decomposing each exponent into within-city inequality components across the 166-year window. That would test if rising scaling reflects genuine system-wide knowledge recombination or concentration in a handful of hubs the paper already flags in Fig. 4.

Added value to my researchThe same Gini/entropy decomposition of a scaling slope plugs straight into my spatial-entropy and intercity-role work: I can quantify how much of a region's healthcare or telemedicine "advantage" is an artifact of one hub versus a balanced network.

Cross-ref (corpus): Urban scaling laws arise from within-city inequalities — 10.1038/s41562-022-01509-1

② Connect the space-filling process to a spatial-scaling-rules generator

Extending this paperThe paper documents a nationwide "space-filling" process descriptively. It could be extended into a generative spatial model: ask whether the observed transfer-network growth is reproduced by simple spatial scaling rules (density-dependent link formation), which would turn the paper's narrative of space-filling into a falsifiable mechanism and predict where the next transfer links should appear.

Added value to my researchA generative density-driven link model is reusable for my agent-based and Monte-Carlo work on how service/job-housing networks fill space — and gives a null model against which to test whether observed accessibility networks are "more than" simple geometry.

Cross-ref (corpus): Simple spatial scaling rules behind complex cities — 10.1038/s41467-017-01882-w

③ Reframe core–periphery hubs as differentiated functional roles

Extending this paperThe paper identifies a core–periphery hierarchy and asymmetric transfer-in vs. transfer-out exponents, which implies cities play distinct directional roles (net knowledge importers vs. exporters vs. brokers). Extending it: formally classify every city by its in/out/intra signature over time and map role transitions — a far richer taxonomy than a single hierarchy axis.

Added value to my researchThis role-classification template mirrors exactly my interest in unequal, differentiated city roles in intercity systems (healthcare, telemedicine): the same in/out brokerage metrics let me label cities as care exporters, importers, or hubs and study how those roles co-evolve.

④ Forecast network evolution with a graph-learning surrogate

Extending this paperThe paper's 166-year reconstruction is purely retrospective. Because it already encodes the system as evolving directed graphs, it is a natural training set for a temporal graph neural network that learns the space-filling + hierarchization dynamics and forecasts future transfer links — and, via attention, surfaces which structural features drive the rising exponent.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my physics-informed / explainable graph-learning agenda: I can embed the paper's empirical scaling law as a soft constraint inside a GNN so the surrogate respects superlinearity while remaining interpretable — a transferable engine for predicting intercity service networks.

⑤ Test whether technology-transfer hierarchy predicts economic complexity and outcomes

Extending this paperThe paper motivates technology transfer as the "bridge to economic progress" but stops at network structure. Extending it: couple each city's transfer signature (intracity intensity, in/out balance, polycentricity) to downstream economic-complexity and growth outcomes, testing whether the scaling/structure features the paper measures actually cause or predict the income superlinearity they invoke.

Added value to my researchThis supply-of-knowledge → economic-outcome linkage is the same explanatory move I use in explainable ML for spatial prediction: I can treat transfer-network features as predictors and use SHAP/MGWR to attribute where knowledge structure, not just size, drives outcomes — directly relevant to healthy-cities and regional-collaboration policy.
