The potential of implementing superblocks for multifunctional street use in cities

Sven Eggimann Published 2022-03-03 DOI 10.1038/s41893-022-00855-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A data-driven geospatial network analysis across 18 cities worldwide shows that superblock and miniblock potential varies enormously with urban layout — from a few percent to over 40% of the street network in some cities. A grid-like street pattern is not sufficient for high implementation potential; urban density also matters critically. The newly introduced Network Disruption Indicator (NDI) enables planners to prioritize the least traffic-disruptive sites for neighbourhood transformation.

Why It Matters

  • First systematic cross-city quantification of superblock feasibility. The Barcelona superblock concept had never been rigorously benchmarked across diverse urban morphologies; this fills that gap with automated geospatial analysis for 18 cities.
  • Introduces the miniblock as a scalable gateway strategy. By conceptualizing a 2×2 block variant, the paper vastly broadens the cities and neighbourhoods that can begin street transformation — without requiring Barcelona-like grid geometry.
  • Disruption-aware prioritization tool for planners. The NDI translates a network-flow algorithm (Edmonds–Karp) into a practical site-ranking score, letting cities identify the highest-value, lowest-disruption transformation targets.
  • Delivers open, reusable outputs. All classified streets, NDI values, and simulated superblock/miniblock configurations are published as open shapefiles and GeoJSON on Zenodo — immediately reusable by any city.
  • Links urban form to liveability and climate co-benefits. Quantifying where street space can be reclaimed connects infrastructure planning directly to urban heat mitigation, air/noise pollution reduction, and biodiversity corridors.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Spatial coverage
18 case-study cities worldwide, each analysed over a 25 km² (5 × 5 km) extent centred on the city centre. Cities span a wide range of urban morphologies from grid-dominated (Mexico City, Tokyo) to irregular layouts (Zürich, London). 12 of the 18 cities are C40 cities initiative members.
Street network
OpenStreetMap (via Overpass API) — full street graph for each city including street hierarchy tags, lane counts, and footprint data. Downloaded and processed using Python packages NetworkX and Shapely. Network nodes within 15 m are clustered; tunnels >300 m and isolated subgraphs are removed.
Population density
CIESIN / Meta High Resolution Settlement Layer — population estimates based on satellite imagery and census data at approximately 30 m resolution. Used to classify streets by local population density for superblock suitability.
Building footprints
OpenStreetMap building footprints — used to compute building coverage ratio within a 100 m radius of each network node as a proxy for urban imperviousness and structural density.

Core Methods

  • Graph-based street network analysis: the OSM street network is represented as a directed graph; nodes are classified into higher-level (degree ≥ 3, intersections) and lower-level (street segments). NetworkX and Shapely are used for all graph and geometric operations.
  • Geometric scenario parameterization (Gf): the Barcelona superblock geometry (400 × 400 m, three-block width) serves as the baseline (G0); a deviation factor f scales acceptable exterior/interior street lengths. G1 (f = 1, ±100% of Barcelona dimensions) is used for the main analysis. Sensitivity is assessed across multiple f values.
  • Superblock and miniblock detection algorithm: network cycles with four nodes of degree ≥ 3 are identified as superblock candidates; single interior-intersection nodes are miniblock candidates. A shortest-path algorithm connects all neighbours of candidate nodes to delineate exterior streets. Geometric boundary conditions from Table 1 are then checked.
  • Density filtering: streets in areas below a population density threshold or below a building coverage threshold (Table 1) are excluded from superblock/miniblock candidacy to focus on dense, compact urban areas.
  • Network Disruption Indicator (NDI): the Edmonds–Karp maximum-flow algorithm is run for four cardinal directions using artificial supernodes. Local flows (on a 2.5 km raster) and global flows (for the full 5 × 5 km extent) are equally averaged into per-edge NDI scores, enabling three-tier disruption classification (low ≤5%, medium 5–15%, high >15%).

① Enrich the NDI with real-time mobility flows rather than capacity proxies

Extending this paperThe paper's NDI uses lane counts as a capacity proxy and the Edmonds–Karp algorithm as a structural flow surrogate — both are static and topology-derived. A direct extension: replace the proxy-based capacity assignment with empirical traffic counts or GPS-derived flow estimates from mobility datasets, giving the NDI calibrated, time-of-day-sensitive disruption estimates. This would also allow the paper to answer "which sites are disruptive during peak hours vs. off-peak" — a key gap the authors acknowledge when calling for "detailed traffic simulations."

Added value to my researchThis connects directly to my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and mobile-phone / GPS trajectory analysis toolkit: superblock-site NDI scores could be recast as dynamic accessibility surfaces, updating in near real-time and feeding into R2SFCA catchment delineations that incorporate road congestion. The combination would let me measure how superblock implementation changes effective healthcare or facility access during commute peaks.

② Extend the single-city optimization to a cross-city transferable GeoAI detector

Extending this paperThe paper's detection algorithm is rule-based and re-run from scratch for each city. A machine-learning extension would treat the 18-city outputs as a labelled training set — pairing local street-network graph features (node degree distribution, block dimensions, density, building coverage) with the binary superblock/miniblock label — and train a graph neural network classifier that generalizes to unseen cities, including data-poor cities with incomplete OSM coverage. This would scale the paper's methodology from 18 to hundreds of cities and expose what morphological signatures most predict high superblock potential.

Added value to my researchThis is precisely my physics-informed graph learning agenda applied to urban infrastructure: encode the paper's geometric boundary conditions as hard constraints in a GNN layer so the learned detector cannot violate the superblock topology — a physics-informed graph model that transfers across urban morphologies with minimal fine-tuning, reusable as a GeoAI foundation module for any urban network classification task.

③ Add a health-outcome layer: from street reclamation to measurable population health gain

Extending this paperThe paper identifies where superblocks could go and how disruptive they would be, but does not quantify the population health benefit. An integrated extension would couple the paper's classified street outputs with spatially explicit health-exposure models — mapping how each superblock or miniblock site would reduce local air pollutant and noise exposure for surrounding residents, weighted by population density. The paper itself cites a Barcelona estimate of 200-day life-expectancy gain from 503 superblocks; the extension would replicate and generalize that calculation to all 18 cities using the paper's open Zenodo dataset.
