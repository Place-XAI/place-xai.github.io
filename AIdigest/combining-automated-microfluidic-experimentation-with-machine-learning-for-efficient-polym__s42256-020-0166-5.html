Combining automated microfluidic experimentation with machine learning for efficient polymerization design

Benjamin A. Rizkin · Albert S. Shkolnik · Neil J. Ferraro · Ryan L. Hartman Published 2020-04-06 DOI 10.1038/s42256-020-0166-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A semi-autonomous microfluidic platform integrating in situ infrared thermography with Bayesian regularization backpropagation neural networks can map the reaction space of a zirconocene polymerization catalyst from just 37 experiments — reducing chemical waste by two orders of magnitude and compressing catalytic discovery from weeks to hours. The approach demonstrates that high-throughput microfluidics and ML fitting can jointly yield high-fidelity kinetic datasets on complex reactions, providing a template for machine-assisted chemical discovery.

★

Why It Matters

  • Drastic reduction in chemical waste and time. Conventional batch-based catalyst discovery consumes litres of solvent per trial and takes weeks; the microreactor system generates less than 30 g of total waste across all experiments and produces actionable data in hours.
  • Proof of concept for machine-assisted chemistry. By coupling a custom-fabricated microreactor with Latin hypercube design of experiments and ANN fitting, the paper shows a complete pipeline — experiment control, in situ data collection, kinetic parameter extraction, and reaction-space visualization — with commercial relevance to polymer manufacturing.
  • Bayesian regularization provides robust extrapolation. The chosen Bayesian regularization backpropagation algorithm, combined with normalization and two hidden layers of nine neurons, achieves a mean squared error of 6.0 × 10⁻⁵ on training data and generalizes reliably, enabling the ANN to compute catalytic productivity across 1,000 additional concentration–temperature combinations beyond the measured trials.
  • Environmental and economic implications for polymer manufacturing. The US polymer industry accounts for ~6% of national energy use and contributes ~37% of global greenhouse gas emissions indirectly. Faster, waste-minimizing catalyst discovery directly impacts this footprint.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Experimental system
Custom-fabricated continuous-flow microreactor manufactured by photopolymerization stereolithography, bonded to an infrared-transparent fluoropolymer film, housed in a vacuum enclosure. Reagent delivery by electromagnetic diaphragm pumps; thermal control by Peltier elements.
Analytical method
In situ infrared thermography at up to 24 frames per second using an ICI infrared camera with proprietary non-uniformity correction and autocalibration libraries. Raw pixel-level temperature distributions converted to catalytic exotherms via an energy balance for laminar reacting flow.
Reagents / catalyst
Homogeneous metallocene catalyst (SBI)ZrMe₂ (I) with B(C₆F₅)₃ (II) activator and 1-hexene (III) monomer in toluene solvent. All reagents handled under inert glovebox conditions due to moisture/oxygen sensitivity.
Experimental design
Latin hypercube sampling across monomer, activator, and catalyst concentrations: 29 isothermal training trials + 8 anisothermal trials at temperatures of 6, 8, 11, 16, 42, 46, 61, and 79 °C (37 total). Cross-validation on trials 9, 10, 13, and 34.
Chemical waste
Total waste generated across all experiments: < 30 g — a 2–3 order of magnitude reduction vs. batch-based methods. Experimental footprint: a 9 sq ft fume hood.

Datasets and analysis code publicly available at Zenodo (doi:10.5281/zenodo.3706730). Experimental control code available on request (contains third-party code).
Control software

Core Methods

  • Microreactor design and transport analysis: dimensionless numbers (Damköhler Da = 0.3–101, beta number O(10⁻³)–O(10), Reynolds number O(10⁻³)–O(1)) guide the reactor geometry to ensure laminar flow, perceptible exotherm for thermography, and scalable kinetic data.
  • Finite element analysis (FEA) characterization: computational verification of infrared radiative flux contrast (~30 W m⁻² with a 20 K gradient), ensuring the thermal signal is detectable before hardware build.
  • Latin hypercube design of experiments (DoE): near-random sampling across the multidimensional concentration space (catalyst, activator, monomer) with deliberate decoupling of isothermal and anisothermal trial batches to avoid confounded statistical interpretations.
  • In situ infrared thermography data pipeline: raw camera frames at up to 24 fps → ICI non-uniformity correction → energy balance for laminar reacting flow → catalytic productivity (g polymer / (mol Zr × mol 1-hexene × h)) and kinetic rate constants (k_i, k_p) per trial.
  • Bayesian regularization backpropagation ANN: feedforward network (input layer → 2 hidden layers, 9 neurons each → output layer); Bayesian regularization combines Levenberg–Marquardt optimization with regularization to enhance generalization; trained with normalization; hyperparameters selected by MATLAB automated routines + manual hidden-layer/neuron tuning.
  • Reaction-space topology visualization: trained ANN evaluated across 1,000 random concentration–temperature combinations to generate a high-resolution decision matrix for optimal catalyst operating conditions (identified optimum: ~54 mM catalyst, ~56 mM activator, 1.04 M monomer, 40–78 °C).

① Replace the Latin hypercube static sampling with an adaptive active-learning query strategy

Extending this paperThe paper's experimental design uses a Latin hypercube to pre-select the 29 training concentrations before any experiment is run. This is efficient but non-adaptive — it cannot redirect sampling toward high-uncertainty or high-value regions discovered mid-campaign. A direct extension of the paper: replace the fixed DoE with a Bayesian active learning loop that, after each batch of microreactor trials, queries the ANN's predictive uncertainty to select the next most informative concentration–temperature point. The paper's own Bayesian regularization framework already estimates effective weight distributions, making this extension architecturally natural. This would reduce the 37 trials further — possibly to 15–20 — while improving reaction-space resolution near the productivity maximum.

Added value to my researchThe same adaptive query logic maps directly onto my spatiotemporal sampling problems: e.g., selecting the next survey location for collecting ground-truth healthcare accessibility data, or choosing which spatial units to label in a semi-supervised geospatial ML workflow. An uncertainty-guided spatial sampler — analogous to the microreactor's Bayesian active learning loop — would reduce field-survey costs for my 2SFCA/R2SFCA model calibration while concentrating label budget on areas where the accessibility model is most uncertain.

② Upgrade the feedforward ANN to a closed-loop, self-driving experimentation agent

Extending this paperThe paper describes the system as "semi-autonomous": reagent preparation remains manual, and the ANN is trained post-hoc on completed experiments rather than guiding them in real time. The logical next step — which the authors acknowledge — is a closed-loop transfer agent that autonomously proposes experiments, dispatches them to the microreactor controller, ingests the thermographic results, retrains the ANN online, and repeats until a convergence criterion is met. This would transform the platform from a high-throughput screening tool into a self-driving catalyst discovery engine, eliminating the manual reagent-preparation bottleneck and enabling round-the-clock operation. The paper's MATLAB/LabVIEW architecture already provides the communication backbone for such a loop.

This would dramatically accelerate calibration of my spatiotemporal agent-based models against real GPS or mobile-phone trajectory data — an expensive Monte Carlo problem today.

③ Embed physical kinetic priors as hard constraints in the neural network architecture

Extending this paperThe paper uses the ANN purely as a black-box nonlinear fitter — the network has no built-in knowledge of the reaction mechanism shown in Fig. 1 (Michaelis-Menten-like initiation, propagation, and chain-transfer kinetics). A physics-informed extension would encode the known rate-law structure as architectural inductive biases or soft penalty terms: e.g., requiring the ANN output to satisfy the energy balance used for thermographic data extraction, or constraining the partial derivatives of productivity with respect to concentration to match the signs predicted by the mechanistic model. This would improve extrapolation beyond the 37-trial training domain and make the model interpretable — answering why productivity peaks at a specific catalyst loading, not just that it does.

The lesson here — that physical constraints tame overfitting in small-data regimes and improve extrapolation — directly motivates my spatially constrained graph neural network designs for healthcare accessibility prediction in data-sparse regions.

④ Extend the technoeconomic topology to a multi-objective Pareto surface (yield, cost, emissions)

Extending this paperThe paper's ANN produces a single scalar output — catalytic productivity — and identifies a single "optimal" operating point by inspection of the reaction-space topology (Fig. 6). But the authors acknowledge that optimal catalyst conditions also depend on competing economic and environmental objectives: the cost of catalyst vs. activator, the energy consumed by the microreactor vs. the mass of polymer produced, and the GHG footprint per kilogram of output. Extending the paper: train a multi-output ANN predicting productivity, estimated reagent cost, and thermal energy input simultaneously, then compute the Pareto frontier across these objectives. The experimental data from the existing 37 trials plus FEA simulation of reactor energy balance already provide the inputs needed to seed this extension.

⑤ Transfer the trained ANN across catalyst families using molecular graph representations

Extending this paperThe paper trains a separate ANN for one specific catalyst ((SBI)ZrMe₂ + B(C₆F₅)₃). Generalizing to a new metallocene requires collecting another 37-trial dataset from scratch — the methodology does not reuse kinetic knowledge from related catalysts. A transfer-learning extension: represent each catalyst–activator pair as a molecular graph (atoms as nodes, bonds as edges) and train a graph-conditioned ANN that maps (molecular graph, concentration, temperature) → catalytic productivity. Pre-trained on a library of existing metallocene datasets from the literature, the model could require only 5–10 microreactor experiments to fine-tune for a new catalyst — reducing the campaign cost by ~75% while inheriting learned kinetic structure from related systems.

Added value to my researchTransfer learning from a source-rich domain to a data-sparse target is a central challenge in my fine-tuning of geospatial foundation models for regional healthcare accessibility. The microreactor paper's context — where each new catalyst is a "new city" with sparse local data and shared global kinetic priors — is structurally identical to fine-tuning a pre-trained urban GeoAI model on a new city with limited local ground truth. The lesson: a molecular graph encoder (there) or spatial context encoder (here) provides the shared representation that enables efficient transfer.
