An AI-based symptom checker integrated into MomConnect maternal healthcare platform: a prospective, observational mixed-methods study

Marcel Schmude · Stephen Gilbert · Caroline Moor Larsson · Noxolo Gqada · Kris Lemon · Lauren Kotze · Reza Omar · Ebrahim January · Doreen Schlagenhauf · Hila Azadzoy · Tauseef Mehrali · Leo Wolansky · Jane Sebidi · Vedika Kundi · François Bergey · Asmaa Foda · Fabienne Cotte Published 2026-06-16 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00125-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Integrating a Bayesian-network Diagnostic Decision Support System (DDSS) into South Africa's government WhatsApp maternal health platform MomConnect was associated with clinically safe advice in 98.4% of 184 physician-reviewed cases and meaningfully improved health-seeking behaviour: 44.6% of users changed care-seeking intentions after use, safe behaviours rose by 18.1 percentage points, and the proportion who ultimately sought care climbed from 17.0% to 43.0% at one-week follow-up — even among the 86.3% of participants facing multiple barriers to care. Half of those who received treatment had not initially planned to seek it.

★

Why It Matters

  • First large-scale, safety-rated evaluation of a patient-facing DDSS on a government LMIC platform. A meta-analysis of 87 maternity clinical decision support studies found the evidence base to be almost entirely provider-facing; this is among the first prospective studies of a symptom-assessment tool given directly to patients in a low- and middle-income country.
  • Reaches the hardest-to-reach. By embedding in WhatsApp — pre-installed, low-data, familiar — the study enrolled 968 women with high unemployment (64%), very low incomes (50% below ~US$97/month), and significant spatial/financial barriers; 86.3% faced three or more structural barriers to care.
  • Demonstrates behaviour change, not just intention change. Unlike most mHealth trials that report only usage or intention shifts, this study tracks actual care-seeking at 1-week follow-up, showing 53.3% of treatment recipients had not initially planned to seek care — suggesting the DDSS surfaces genuinely unrecognized need.
  • Quantifies the gap between decision support and access. Lower-income participants were significantly less likely to act safely (AOR 2.68 for higher income, P = 0.013) despite safe intentions, revealing financial constraints as the binding bottleneck — an important policy signal beyond the tool itself.
  • Policy-embedded and scalable. The study is conducted within SA's National Department of Health MomConnect platform, which registers pregnancies nationally and serves over 250,000 queries per year — making adoption at scale a natural next step rather than a separate challenge.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Study population
968 pregnant women and mothers enrolled May–June 2023 via MomConnect/WhatsApp, across South Africa (9 provinces). Participants aged 18–47 years (mean 26.9 years). 68.4% pregnant at assessment; 65.1% first-time pregnancies.
Geographic scope
All 9 South African provinces; 36.8% from Gauteng. Mapping used administrative boundaries from the Humanitarian Data Exchange (OCHA, data.humdata.org). Location data available for 94.7% (917/968); participant locations geomasked via random spatial displacement for privacy.
Socioeconomic index
International Wealth Index (IWI) (Smits & Steendijk, 2015; Global Data Lab) — asset-based measure of household economic status calculated via principal component analysis from survey responses.
Health system data
District Health Barometer 2022/23 (Health Systems Trust, hst.org.za) — district-level indicators for all 52 South African districts: antenatal first-visit coverage, MMR in facilities, early neonatal mortality, physician density per 100,000 uninsured, hospital bed availability per 10,000 population.
Reference data
South Africa provincial MMR from World Bank World Development Indicators 2024 (data.worldbank.org). National unemployment rate from Statista.
DDSS platform
Ada Health Diagnostic Decision Support System — Bayesian network with peer-reviewed medical knowledge base; integrated within MomConnect/WhatsApp. The system uses information-theoretical question selection to optimize diagnostic information gain.
Follow-up
1-week phone/WhatsApp follow-up survey; stratified phone subsample (n = 99): 50% random + 25% serious symptoms + 25% care-intention changers.
Physician panel
3 senior physicians (gynaecology/family medicine) reviewed a random subset of 184 cases in duplicate (third resolves disagreements).

Core Methods

  • Prospective, observational mixed-methods design: quantitative pre-/post-DDSS intention and behaviour measurement plus thematic analysis of phone interview transcripts, with embedded physician panel validation.
  • Bayesian network DDSS: Ada Health's symptom-assessment engine derives disease probabilities via approximate inference, selecting follow-up questions by information-theoretical gain; outputs an eight-level urgency advice mapped to four levels for analysis.
  • Composite barrier score: 0–9 point index combining five district-level health system indicators (from the District Health Barometer) and four individual-level socioeconomic variables, dichotomized against national averages; participants classified as minimal (0–2), moderate (3–5), or severe (6–9) barriers.
  • Primary endpoint — safety: one-sample exact binomial test (Clopper–Pearson 95% CI) testing whether physician-rated unsafe advice rate is significantly below a 10% threshold (n = 184, power = 0.8, α = 0.05).
  • Behaviour change analysis: Stuart–Maxwell test for paired categorical shifts; chi-squared tests for alignment between intention and behaviour; Fisher's exact tests for barrier subgroup comparisons with relative risk and Newcombe–Wilson CIs.
  • Multivariable logistic regression: three models — upward change in urgency category vs. rest, downward change vs. rest, and safe behaviour — with predictors including baseline intent, DDSS urgency level, income, pregnancy status, employment, age, distance and cost to care; complete-case analysis with sensitivity checks.
  • IWI calculation: principal component analysis on asset ownership items from the phone survey, consistent with Global Data Lab methodology.
  • Geographic mapping: choropleth visualization of participant distribution overlaid with provincial MMR, IWI, and barrier scores using OCHA administrative boundaries.

Expanded Ideas — this paper's extensibility & added value to my research

① Map the spatial inequality in DDSS impact using a barrier-informed accessibility model

Extending this paper The paper's composite barrier score (0–9, combining district health system indicators and individual socioeconomics) captures heterogeneity but is treated as a single covariate in regression — it is never spatially modelled. A direct extension: treat each participant's barrier score and post-DDSS behaviour change as geospatial observations and fit a geographically weighted regression (GWR) to detect where DDSS impact is highest and lowest across South Africa's 52 districts. This would move the paper's finding — "income predicts unsafe behaviour, not intent" — from a global odds ratio to a spatially varying effect, pinpointing which provincial health systems the DDSS compensates for and which it cannot overcome alone.

Added value to my research This is a canonical application of my MGWR/GWR toolkit to healthcare digital-divide mapping. The IWI and District Health Barometer provide ready-made covariates for a spatially non-stationary model of mHealth effectiveness. The resulting bandwidth maps translate directly into my telehealth/digital-divide accessibility agenda — identifying where AI decision support is a genuine substitute for proximity to primary care and where structural inequities dominate.

② Replace the fixed follow-up survey with a spatiotemporal kernel density model of care-seeking timing

Extending this paper The 1-week follow-up is the study's most important limitation — it cannot distinguish a rapid DDSS-triggered care visit (hours) from a slow, marginally-motivated one (days), nor can it capture relapses or additional symptoms that emerge later. Extending the paper: instrument a longitudinal cohort that logs care contact via electronic health records or clinic check-ins and apply spatiotemporal kernel density estimation to the resulting event streams, characterizing the temporal decay of DDSS influence on care-seeking as a function of urgency level and geographic distance to facility. This would replace the binary "sought care by Day 7" with a continuous, time-resolved dose-response curve.

Added value to my research Spatiotemporal KDE on healthcare contact events is a direct extension of my spatiotemporal analysis methods. The decay function estimated from this data would provide an empirical, LMIC-calibrated version of the distance-decay parameters I currently assume in 2SFCA models — grounding catchment delineation in observed behavior rather than administrative radii.

Cross-ref (corpus): Access to care affects electronic health record reliability and AI-driven disease prediction — 10.1038/s44360-026-00054-9

③ Build a trust-stratified DDSS routing model using LLM-based natural language understanding

Extending this paper The paper finds that 98.1% of users found the DDSS useful, but qualitative feedback reveals that trust in government clinics/hospitals (37%) and trustworthiness (17.4%) are what participants want most from health information. Yet the Bayesian DDSS does not adapt its communication style to trust level. A direct extension: integrate an LLM layer that reads the DDSS output and re-frames urgency recommendations with culturally appropriate motivational framing calibrated to the user's reported financial barrier level — analogous to the corpus study that co-designed an LLM chatbot for primary care with community input (randomized controlled trial).

Added value to my research For my GeoAI research, this raises the question of how to fine-tune geospatial foundation models on local health-seeking behavior corpora. The MomConnect dataset (968 participants, multilevel barrier data, qualitative transcripts) is exactly the kind of culturally grounded, spatially tagged interaction log that could anchor a domain-specific fine-tuning of an LLM health agent for Sub-Saharan Africa — connecting my fine-tuning agenda to health equity.

Cross-ref (corpus): A community-codesigned LLM-powered chatbot for primary care: a randomized controlled trial — 10.1038/s44360-025-00021-w

④ Use explainable ML to decode why income dominates DDSS non-compliance, not geography

Extending this paper The paper's logistic regression finds that household income (AOR 2.68, P = 0.013) — not distance to facility, not pregnancy trimester, not employment — predicts safe behaviour after DDSS use. This is a striking finding that the paper cannot fully explain. An extension: apply SHAP-based explainable ML to a richer feature matrix (including interaction terms between income × distance, income × urgency level, and income × barrier score) to decompose the pathway — does income moderate financial cost of travel, cognitive burden of acting on advice, or social network support? The District Health Barometer's district-level indicators can be cross-matched to individual data to add supply-side features.

Added value to my research This is a direct application of my explainable machine learning for spatial prediction agenda to a health behaviour outcome. SHAP spatial interaction plots (SHAP values × geographic coordinate) would reveal whether the income effect is spatially uniform or concentrated in specific provinces — linking explainability to my GWR/MGWR spatial non-stationarity toolkit and generating policy-relevant heterogeneity maps.

Cross-ref (corpus): A global survey on trust, digital health literacy and health information quality — 10.1038/s44360-026-00102-4

⑤ Extend the safety-rating framework into an LLM clinical decision support benchmark for African primary care

Extending this paper The physician-panel safety framework (three blinded physicians, duplicated review, 8-level to 4-level urgency mapping) is a validated, replicable evaluation protocol for patient-facing diagnostic AI. The paper applies it to Ada Health's Bayesian DDSS; it can be directly extended to evaluate newer large language model-based clinical decision support systems in the same African primary care setting — the corpus contains a concurrent study doing exactly this in African primary care, offering a natural head-to-head comparison. Extending it: apply an identical protocol across DDSS architectures (Bayesian network vs. LLM), symptom categories, and language/literacy strata, producing a multi-arm comparative safety benchmark.

Added value to my research For my open science / FAIR data agenda, the physician-panel protocol and the MomConnect barrier composite score represent reusable evaluation infrastructure: codifying them as an open benchmark would enable rigorous comparison of AI health tools in LMIC settings. This connects to my multi-source data fusion work — fusing electronic health records, survey data, and spatially referenced barrier scores into a FAIR dataset that other researchers can use to train and validate health AI for Sub-Saharan Africa.
