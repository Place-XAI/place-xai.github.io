Urban scaling of firearm violence, ownership and accessibility in the United States

Rayan Succar · Maurizio Porfiri Published 2024-02-29 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00034-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 929 US cities (populations 10,000–20,000,000), firearm violence scales superlinearly with city size — larger cities suffer disproportionately more firearm homicides and armed robberies — while firearm ownership and accessibility scale sublinearly, meaning guns are actually less prevalent per capita in bigger cities. A novel information-theoretic framework reveals that both violence and firearm accessibility exert significant influence on ownership, consistent with a self-protection motivation modeled via a Cobb–Douglas production function.

Why It Matters

  • First city-scale, high-resolution account of the US firearm ecosystem. Prior work focused on national or state averages; this study collects and analyzes data at the level of 929 individual cities, exposing within-country heterogeneity invisible at coarser scales.
  • Counterintuitive finding that challenges conventional narratives. Despite popular associations between large cities and gun prevalence, ownership and dealer density are actually lower per capita in larger cities — while violence is higher — demanding a rethinking of policy levers.
  • Quantifies the self-protection feedback loop. The Cobb–Douglas model suggests that elevated violence in large cities drives higher ownership demand, which itself elevates violence — a feedback cycle with direct implications for intervention design.
  • Provides a transferable methodology. The information-theoretic "Scaling Association and Mediation Indices" (SAMIs) framework can be applied to other urban phenomena where separating direct and mediated influences from scaling residuals is needed.

Core Methods

  • Urban scaling analysis: fit power-law scaling relationships Y ∝ Nβ between city population N and each firearm indicator Y (violence, ownership, accessibility). Ordinary least-squares regression on log-log scale with White's heteroskedasticity-consistent standard errors (implemented in Statsmodels).
  • Scale-adjusted metric (SAM) computation: subtract the scaling-law prediction from each city's observed value to obtain residuals that remove population-size confounding, enabling city-to-city comparison on equal footing.
  • Scaling Association and Mediation Indices (SAMIs): a novel information-theoretic framework applied to SAM residuals — uses mutual information and partial mutual information (k-nearest-neighbor estimators) to quantify direct and mediated associations among violence, ownership, and accessibility, while controlling for confounders.
  • Cobb–Douglas production function: models firearm ownership as a function of violence and accessibility, fitting the classic two-factor production form to the scaling residuals and providing an interpretable economic-style mechanism.
  • Causal inference: draws on Granger causality and convergent cross-mapping concepts to interpret directionality; the SAMI framework is designed to be association-based (not causal) and is carefully distinguished from causal claims.

① Extend the SAMI framework to fine-grained spatiotemporal panels

Extending this paperThe SAMI analysis is conducted on a single cross-sectional snapshot of 929 cities. A direct extension would apply the same information-theoretic framework to time-stacked panels — tracking how the mutual-information associations among violence, ownership, and accessibility evolve year by year. This would test whether the self-protection feedback strengthens or weakens over time (for example, in response to policy shocks such as background-check expansions or red-flag laws), moving the framework from a static portrait to a dynamic systems account of the firearm ecosystem. The African-cities violence study offers a useful comparative anchor for isolating context-specific versus universal scaling dynamics.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal kernel density and agent-based modeling toolkit maps naturally onto this extension: encoding the scaling residuals as spatiotemporal fields enables me to apply KDE-based change-point detection to identify when and where the violence-ownership feedback intensifies, and to link those intensity shifts to observable urban structural variables (density, commute, housing) via MGWR — connecting firearm dynamics to my broader urban-health-geography agenda.

② Disaggregate city-level scaling to the intra-city neighborhood level

Extending this paperThe paper's scaling laws operate at the city level (population 10 k–20 M), but within a single city the variance in firearm violence across neighborhoods can be as large as the between-city variance. Extending the paper's own argument — that urban scaling arises partly from within-city inequalities — to a nested, multi-scale design (census tracts nested within cities) would reveal whether the superlinear scaling exponent for violence is primarily a within-city or between-city phenomenon, and whether the Cobb–Douglas ownership model holds at the sub-city level. The urban-scaling-from-within-city-inequalities corpus paper provides the theoretical grounding for this decomposition.

Added value to my researchThis nested design mirrors my multi-scale accessibility work: just as R2SFCA delineates service areas at sub-city granularity, a nested firearm-scaling analysis would require sub-city violence and ownership surfaces — a natural task for my spatiotemporal KDE and geographically weighted regression (MGWR/GWR) methods, producing locally varying scaling exponents that reveal which neighborhood factors amplify the city-level superlinear trend.

③ Couple the Cobb–Douglas ownership model with a spatial accessibility measure

Extending this paperThe paper uses the raw count of licensed dealers (FFLs) per city as its accessibility proxy, which treats all dealers as equally reachable regardless of geographic distribution within the city. An extension would replace this blunt metric with a network-based spatial accessibility score — specifically, a gravity-model or 2SFCA measure accounting for travel time to each FFL location and the population's demand distribution. This would sharpen the Cobb–Douglas inputs and test whether the ownership–accessibility association strengthens once access is properly spatial rather than aggregate-count-based.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my 2SFCA/R2SFCA methodology to a new domain: computing firearm-dealer accessibility surfaces at the city level, using the same catchment-delineation and distance-decay framework I apply to primary-care and mental-health access. The firearm context adds a novel dimension — service-area competition is minimal for dealers — that would let me isolate the pure geographic-access component from demand competition effects, sharpening the R2SFCA decomposition.

④ Use explainable ML to unpack what drives deviations from the scaling law

Extending this paperThe paper's SAMs are residuals from the power-law scaling fit — cities above or below expectation. The paper does not systematically explain which city-level features predict high positive or negative deviations. Extending it with an explainable machine-learning model (gradient boosting + SHAP) trained on the SAMs as outcomes, with socioeconomic, demographic, geographic, and policy covariates as features, would identify the city characteristics most strongly associated with "more violence than expected for its size" or "more ownership than expected" — directly actionable for policy targeting.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction agenda. Using SHAP on a spatial ensemble model of violence SAMs would produce local feature-importance maps that reveal spatial non-stationarity in the violence-drivers — a natural input for MGWR, where the bandwidth can be informed by the SHAP-identified scale of each covariate's influence. The result is a bridge between urban scaling theory and geographically varying regression models.

⑤ Benchmark SAMI against graph-based causal discovery on the same data

Extending this paperThe paper carefully distinguishes its SAMI framework from causal inference, yet its central scientific claim — that violence and accessibility drive ownership via self-protection motivation — is causal in spirit. An important extension would run a formal causal discovery algorithm (e.g., PC algorithm, NOTEARS, or a structural causal model with instrumental variables) on the same 929-city dataset as a robustness check and calibration of the SAMI associations against a principled causal benchmark. This would either validate the SAMI directional inferences or reveal confounders the mutual-information approach missed.

Added value to my researchMy physics-informed graph-learning work is well-positioned to implement structural causal models on urban networks: treating cities as nodes and embedding socioeconomic flows as graph edges, a GNN with causal structure constraints (hard-coded from the paper's Cobb–Douglas prior) could generalize the causal discovery to new cities and time periods — turning SAMI associations into transferable, predictive causal models for urban health policy.
