Large-scale screening of blinding eye diseases from routine blood tests

Shengjie Li · Jun Ren · Jianing Wu · Yingzhu Li · Xuanxuan Wang · Mengyu Zhang · Henggui Hu · Yunxiao Song · Shunxiang Gao · Wenjun Cao · Meiyan Li · Xingtao Zhou Published 2026-04-06 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00101-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A machine learning-based multidisease eye screening (MES) framework trained on routine blood tests can identify individuals at elevated risk of seven major blinding conditions with high accuracy (AUC 0.926–0.956 for binary detection; macro-AUC 0.889–0.900 for subtype classification). Validated across more than 600,000 participants in internal, external, hospital-based, and community-based cohorts, the MES test performs robustly across age and comorbidity subgroups — making it a scalable, non-invasive triage tool that can prioritize ophthalmic referral without specialist equipment, particularly for underserved populations where ophthalmology access is limited.

Why It Matters

  • Turns ubiquitous routine labs into an ophthalmology screening tool. Blood tests ordered for virtually any clinical encounter become the raw material for identifying patients at risk of glaucoma, cataracts, diabetic retinopathy, AMD, retinal detachment, retinal vein occlusion, and retinitis pigmentosa — no fundus camera needed.
  • Addresses a critical access bottleneck. Current blinding-disease screening requires ophthalmic specialists and imaging infrastructure that is scarce in primary care and community settings globally. The MES framework provides a first-line filter that can be deployed wherever routine blood work is available.
  • Massive, multi-context validation. The model was tested across six cohorts totaling more than 600,000 participants — including a community-based cross-sectional cohort of 498,095 — achieving positive and negative predictive values above 0.90 in real-world clinical and community settings.
  • Seven-disease multiclass output, not just binary detection. Differentiating among seven blinding subtypes in one model enables appropriate triage routing rather than a single "yes/no" flag, reducing downstream specialist burden.
  • Open, reproducible code. Analysis code is publicly available on GitHub and archived on Zenodo, lowering barriers for independent replication and adaptation to other disease panels.

Core Methods

  • Dual-stage MES architecture: a binary classifier first distinguishes eye-disease patients from controls; a multiclass classifier then differentiates among seven blinding subtypes. This cascade design balances sensitivity (binary triage) with differential specificity (subtype routing).
  • Feature selection via SHAP: all included blood parameters are fed to an initial model, and SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values quantify each feature's contribution; a reduced feature set is selected for the final deployable model (Fig. 3), improving interpretability and reducing collection burden.
  • Clinlabomics approach: the paper applies the "clinlabomics" framework — systematic mining of clinical laboratory data — to ophthalmology, building on prior authors' work linking blood indices to individual eye diseases (glaucoma, cataract, retinal detachment, AMD, RVO).
  • Multi-stage validation pipeline: internal validation (n = 33,622) → three independent external cohorts (n = 34,087) → prospective hospital cohort (n = 43,556) → community-based population cohort (n = 498,095). Each stage tests a different dimension of generalizability.
  • Performance metrics: AUC, positive predictive value (PPV), negative predictive value (NPV) as primary metrics; macroaveraged AUC for the multiclass output. Subgroup analyses by age and comorbidity confirm robustness.
  • Transparent reporting: TRIPOD-compliant reporting for multivariable prediction models; missing-value imputation with population median (ref. 63), a well-validated low-complexity strategy.

① Add spatial geography: map where the MES framework would and would not close the screening gap

Extending this paperThe MES framework's core premise is that it can reach underserved settings — yet the paper never spatializes that claim. A direct extension: link the community-based cohort (n = 498,095) to its geographic coordinates and compute, for each census unit or sub-district, the delta between current ophthalmic access (2SFCA-derived catchment) and the MES-screening coverage offered by primary-care labs. This would produce the first spatial triage gap map for blinding eye diseases — showing exactly where the blood-test pathway adds most value and where specialist coverage already suffices. It would also let the paper engage the literature on healthcare access disparities head-on (see Corpus ref. below) by modeling cost-constrained communities as the primary beneficiaries.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct extension of my 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility work into a new clinical domain: ophthalmology. The MES test defines a new supply source (primary-care labs performing routine blood tests), and R2SFCA can quantify whether that supply is spatially matched to demand (undiagnosed at-risk patients). It also seeds a GeoAI pipeline where spatial predictors (proximity to specialists, broadband access, socioeconomic indices) are appended to the blood-feature vector to further personalize triage thresholds.

② Replace static SHAP feature selection with a causal, counterfactual variable selection

Extending this paperThe paper uses SHAP to rank and reduce blood-test features — an associative method that captures marginal attribution but cannot distinguish causal biomarkers from confounders (e.g., a biomarker correlated with age might be selected because age predicts eye disease, not because the biomarker itself is mechanistically involved). Extending the paper: reframe feature selection as a causal inference problem — use do-calculus or counterfactual prediction to identify blood parameters that are causally upstream of each blinding condition, drawing on the inflammation-oxidative-stress pathways the authors discuss (refs 31–33, 49). This produces a more robust, biologically interpretable reduced panel that maintains performance under distribution shift (e.g., different ethnic groups or comorbidity profiles).

Added value to my researchThis mirrors my work on explainable machine learning for spatial prediction: SHAP-based importance in geospatial models similarly conflates correlation with causation. A causal-variable-selection layer before SHAP visualization would make both my spatial models and this clinical model more defensible for policy — identifying which intervention levers (blood markers, spatial features) are actionable versus merely predictive.

Cross-ref (corpus): Causal inference and counterfactual prediction in machine learning for actionable healthcare — 10.1038/s42256-020-0197-y

③ Audit and mitigate demographic bias before population-scale deployment

Extending this paperThe MES framework shows robust performance across age and comorbidity subgroups, but the paper does not report disaggregated performance by sex, ethnicity, or socioeconomic status — all of which influence blood parameters and eye disease prevalence. At population scale (n = 498,095), even small systematic biases compound into large absolute numbers of missed or over-triaged cases. A critical extension: apply algorithmic fairness auditing and bias-mitigation techniques (e.g., the reinforcement-learning debiasing framework in the corpus) to the MES classifiers, ensuring the blood-test-to-referral pathway does not replicate or amplify existing disparities in ophthalmology access for underserved groups.

Added value to my researchMy research on health geography and accessibility — particularly telehealth and digital-divide analysis — is directly implicated: if the MES system performs worse for populations with limited primary-care access (because their blood-test records are sparser or systematically different), it could widen rather than narrow the screening gap. Embedding spatial accessibility indices as fairness constraints in the ML training loop would bridge my accessibility modeling with clinical AI auditing.

Cross-ref (corpus): Algorithmic fairness and bias mitigation for clinical machine learning with deep reinforcement learning — 10.1038/s42256-023-00697-3

④ Generalize the MES blueprint to other screening bottlenecks using a multi-disease foundation model

Extending this paperThe MES framework's architecture — routine lab input, binary + multiclass cascade, SHAP reduction, multi-cohort validation — is disease-agnostic in principle. Extending the paper: train a multi-panel foundation model on the same blood-test feature space that can detect risk across multiple specialty domains simultaneously (ophthalmology, nephrology, thyroid, haematology) by sharing representations across disease heads. The paper's own references to parallel work (thyroid dysfunction from routine labs, ref. 15; CKD progression, ref. 16) suggest a natural multi-disease corpus exists. Foundation models for clinical lab data would also address the reliability challenges inherent in single-task models trained on small, homogeneous cohorts.

Added value to my researchFoundation models trained on tabular clinical data parallel geospatial foundation models that I am fine-tuning for urban analytics. The same transfer-learning and fine-tuning methodology — pre-train on large heterogeneous corpora, adapt to local (specialty-specific or city-specific) tasks — applies directly. Cross-pollination of foundation-model evaluation standards between clinical AI and GeoAI is a concrete research bridge.

⑤ Pair MES with large-scale non-invasive screening to build a two-tier equity-first referral chain

Extending this paperThe MES test operates at tier one (blood tests at primary care); a natural tier two is a non-specialist, non-invasive secondary screen for positives flagged by MES. The corpus contains a parallel example in prenatal testing (NIPT deployed at population scale in Hebei province, China — n = 108,500 pregnancies), which shares key structural features: non-invasive, routine-sample-based, large-scale publicly funded rollout, and primary-care delivery. Extending the MES paper: design a two-tier referral pathway where MES-positive patients receive a low-cost retinal photo (AI-interpreted) before full ophthalmic consultation — analogous to the NIPT architecture — and model the equity implications of that pathway using real geographic coverage data (which facilities have fundus cameras vs. only labs).

Added value to my researchThis multi-tier screening design maps directly onto my work on spatial accessibility of primary care: each tier has a different facility-type catchment (labs at primary care, fundus cameras at community health centers, ophthalmologists at tertiary hospitals), and 2SFCA can compute composite multi-tier accessibility scores. Embedding cost and geographic constraints into the referral chain would create a spatially-explicit, equity-optimized health pathway model — a novel application of facility-location and service-area methods.
