Group identities can undermine social tipping after intervention

Sönke Ehret · Sara M. Constantino · Elke U. Weber · Charles Efferson · Sonja Vogt Published 2022-09-22 DOI 10.1038/s41562-022-01440-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Social tipping — the rapid cascade of behaviour change driven by coordination incentives — is powerful but unreliable. In a pre-registered online coordination experiment run around the 2020 US elections (N = 805 participants, 68 experimental groups), a pay-off-irrelevant relabelling of choice options with partisan political images destroyed social tipping entirely: identity-linked groups failed to transition to a new norm after an incentivizing intervention and accumulated substantial opportunity costs, whereas groups with neutral labels tipped rapidly. Even a trivial symbolic link between choice and group identity is sufficient to prevent endogenous norm change that would otherwise occur.

★

Why It Matters

  • Provides causal evidence for a foundational policy risk. While theory has long proposed that group identities can block norm tipping, this is among the first incentivized, pre-registered experiments demonstrating the mechanism causally — and the effect size was total elimination of tipping, not just attenuation.
  • Reveals a dual role of identity as both accelerant and blocker. The same partisan labels that sped up norm convergence before intervention completely prevented norm change after intervention — a nuanced two-edged dynamic that prior theoretical work had not cleanly documented.
  • Reframes how policymakers should sequence interventions. The findings imply that an "intervention before the intervention" — first decoupling identity from the target behaviour — may be necessary before expecting social tipping to operate in politically polarized contexts (e.g., climate action, masking).
  • Quantifies real monetary costs of identity-driven norm lock-in. Participants in identity sessions paid measurable opportunity costs (Fig. 5), translating an abstract social concept into concrete economic losses — a lever for cost-benefit analysis of identity-aware policy design.
  • Generalizes across domains. The experiment is domain-neutral (coordination game), making the finding applicable to public health, climate, resource conservation, and any setting where socially visible choices carry group-identity valence.

Core Methods

  • Repeated coordination game experiment: groups of 12 participants played up to 45 periods of a 2-choice coordination game with random within-group matching each period; monetary pay-offs rewarded coordination on either option, creating a pure coordination problem without a prescribed "correct" choice.
  • Two-phase design (pre- and post-intervention): the pre-intervention phase established a "status quo norm" (≥90% agreement or 20 periods); the intervention then applied a new pay-off matrix to 50% of players (targeted) to favor the alternative choice, while non-targeted players retained original incentives.
  • Treatment manipulation — pay-off-irrelevant identity labelling: neutral treatment used abstract symbols (@ / #); identity treatment used partisan political images of Biden and Trump embedded in the same buttons — all other aspects of the experiment were identical between treatments.
  • Spillover measurement: the primary pre-registered outcome was a normalized spillover metric Θ ∈ [−1, 1] quantifying how common the alternative choice became relative to the size of the intervention, capturing endogenous social amplification beyond direct intervention effects.
  • OLS regression (linear probability models): spillovers analyzed by OLS with robust standard errors; individual choices analyzed with linear probability models including treatment × targeting × phase interaction terms, with cluster-robust standard errors at the group level.
  • Affective polarization measurement: Biden/Trump feelings thermometers and social dominance orientation collected at recruitment as covariates; a priming experiment used as an instrumental variable to separate pre-existing from endogenously activated identities.
  • Exploratory analyses: comparison of behavior before vs. after the November 7, 2020 election outcome; inequality aversion modeled using the Fehr–Schmidt framework; convergence speed analyses to disentangle pre-existing identity from endogenous dynamics.

① Map identity-tipping interactions onto geographic political sorting to predict where urban interventions will fail

Extending this paperThis paper demonstrates that partisan identity destroys tipping in a lab setting, but it does not tell policymakers where in space norm-change campaigns are likely to fail. A direct extension: couple the paper's experimental spillover model with fine-grained geographic political sorting data (e.g., the 180-million-voter partisan sorting dataset in the corpus) to map the expected "tipping probability" surface across US neighborhoods. Areas with high partisan residential segregation would be identified as high-risk zones where norm-change interventions need identity-decoupling pre-steps before any social tipping strategy is deployed.

Added value to my researchThis translates directly into my geospatial AI agenda: building an explainable ML model that predicts intervention success probability as a function of observable spatial covariates — partisan sorting index, neighborhood socioeconomic homogeneity, social network density from mobility data. This is a spatial variant of heterogeneous treatment effect modeling, and links directly to my work on MGWR/GWR to capture spatially varying coefficients of identity-intervention interaction.

③ Agent-based simulation of identity-constrained social tipping on empirical mobility-derived contact networks

Extending this paperThe paper uses random within-group matching, abstracting away from real social network structure. Extending it: implement the paper's coordination game dynamics (with identity-weighted payoffs calibrated from the experimental results) on empirical contact networks derived from GPS trajectories and mobile phone data. This would reveal whether the "chronic disagreement" state the paper observes in small lab groups (n = 12) persists or breaks down at realistic network scales and topologies, and whether network bridge nodes between partisan clusters can serve as catalysts for cross-group tipping.

④ Quantify the spatial heterogeneity of identity-tipping interference for climate behaviour change using mobility-derived social influence networks

Extending this paperThe paper focuses on US partisan identity but its mechanism is general to any strong group identity (religious, ethnic, class-based). A natural extension: replicate the analytical logic at scale by using mobile-phone-derived social contact matrices and co-location networks to proxy the "identity homophily" of social influence graphs across cities. Cities or neighborhoods where within-group mobility dominates would be predicted (via the paper's model) to show lower endogenous norm-change amplification in climate-related behaviour campaigns, even when material incentives are identical — providing a spatial "identity resistance map" for climate communicators.

⑤ Design identity-aware norm-change interventions for healthcare behaviour using edutainment decoupling strategies

Extending this paperThe paper's Discussion explicitly proposes an "intervention before the intervention" — first decoupling identity from choice, then applying the norm-change nudge — citing the CNN face-mask ad and substitute-behaviour strategies for female genital cutting. A corpus companion on edutainment campaigns against gender-based violence (delivered via radio in LMICs) demonstrates this strategy at scale. The extension: systematically compare decoupling-first vs. direct intervention sequences in a multi-arm experiment, measuring how much the identity-decoupling pre-step reduces the identity-driven suppression of spillovers that this paper quantifies.

Added value to my researchIn my health geography work on telehealth adoption and digital-divide interventions, uptake barriers often have identity-valence (e.g., rural communities viewing telehealth as a threat to traditional care relationships).
