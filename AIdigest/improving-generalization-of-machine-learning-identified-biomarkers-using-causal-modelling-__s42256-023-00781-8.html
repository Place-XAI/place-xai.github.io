Improving generalization of machine learning-identified biomarkers using causal modelling with examples from immune receptor diagnostics

Milena Pavlović · Ghadi S. Al Hajj · Chakravarthi Kanduri · Johan Pensar · Mollie E. Wood · Ludvig M. Sollid · Victor Greiff · Geir K. Sandve Published 2024-01-24 DOI 10.1038/s42256-023-00781-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

ML-based diagnostics trained on high-dimensional molecular data — here, adaptive immune receptor repertoires (AIRRs) — fail to generalize when confounders, selection biases, or batch effects differ between training and deployment populations. A causal modelling perspective formalizes these failure modes through directed acyclic graphs and do-calculus, enabling principled identification and correction of spurious associations. Simulation experiments confirm that accounting for causal structure substantially improves balanced error rates under distribution shift, establishing causal reasoning as an essential component of robust biomarker discovery.

Core Methods

  • Causal graphical modelling (DAGs): biological and experimental variables (immune state, AIRR, HLA, age, batch, selection node) are encoded as directed acyclic graphs; edges represent direct causal mechanisms. The causal structure determines which biases arise and how to correct them.
  • Do-calculus (Pearl's framework): used to formalize identifiability of diagnostic causal effects under confounding, selection bias, and covariate shift — distinguishing stable (causal-direction) from unstable (anticausal-direction) prediction tasks.
  • Structural causal models: each simulation experiment instantiates a specific structural causal model, allowing exact control of confounder distributions, selection mechanisms, and batch effect magnitudes.
  • k-mer frequency representation: TCRβ CDR3 sequences are encoded as 3-mer frequency vectors; L1-regularized logistic regression is used as the ML classifier throughout all experiments.
  • Batch-effect correction: linear regression on k-mer frequencies removes batch effects in experiment 3; performance compared against uncorrected and control (no batch effects) scenarios.
  • Performance metric: balanced error rate (BER) reported as median across 30 repetitions, enabling comparison across class-balanced and imbalanced settings.
  • Reporting standards proposal: the paper derives concrete study-design guidelines and MiAIRR-compliant metadata reporting standards from the causal analysis framework.

① Translate the paper's causal DAG audit to spatial health datasets with geographic confounders

Extending this paperThe paper focuses on biological confounders (HLA, age, sex) and experimental batch effects in AIRR diagnostics. However, the same causal DAG framework applies wherever unmeasured population-level variables correlate with both predictor and outcome — a situation endemic in spatial health data. An extension would construct causal DAGs for geographic confounders (neighborhood deprivation, built environment, environmental exposures) that co-determine both health outcomes and the features used by ML classifiers. The paper's approach of encoding the confounder as a DAG node and using do-calculus to specify adjustment sets could be directly translated to a "spatial-causal diagnostic" pipeline that audits whether geographic covariates act as confounders, precision covariates, or mediators — mirroring the HLA role analysis of Fig. 3 of this paper.

Added value to my researchMy explainable ML work on spatial health outcomes (healthcare accessibility disparities, digital-divide predictors) faces exactly the same confounding problem: neighborhood socioeconomic status co-determines both the "treatment" (e.g., telehealth availability) and the "outcome" (health utilization). Adopting this paper's causal DAG audit before fitting GWR/MGWR or ML ensemble models would formalize which variables to condition on vs. leave open, replacing ad hoc covariate selection with principled backdoor-criterion adjustment — directly improving causal credibility in spatial health diagnostics.

② Apply stable learning to spatially heterogeneous ML models to isolate environment-invariant health signals

Extending this paperThe paper proposes invariant prediction — learning features that produce stable predictions across different environments — as one path to robust biomarkers. In the AIRR domain, "environments" are study cohorts from different hospitals or countries. An extension would operationalize invariant risk minimization (IRM) or stable learning directly within the AIRR simulation pipeline: rather than correcting observed confounders via do-calculus, learn representations whose predictive relationship to immune state is invariant across cohort-environments (e.g., across age-stratified or geography-stratified subgroups), and benchmark against the paper's logistic-regression baseline under the same BER metric.

Added value to my researchIn my spatiotemporal health models (e.g., predicting mental health service utilization across counties), the "environments" are naturally defined by administrative regions or census tracts with heterogeneous socioeconomic contexts. Applying stable learning to identify spatial predictors that are invariant across geographic environments would produce health models that generalize across cities rather than overfitting to local confounders — enabling transferable GeoAI models for health equity analysis directly relevant to my 2SFCA/R2SFCA research.

③ Combine the paper's causal audit with active experimental design to prioritize which confounders to measure

Extending this paperThe paper identifies that unmeasured confounders (e.g., geographic environment as an unobserved DAG node) are the most dangerous because they cannot be adjusted for without measurement. However, measuring every potential confounder is expensive. An extension would integrate the paper's causal graph with active learning for intervention design: given a partial DAG and a budget for additional measurements, sequentially query the most informative confounder to observe, maximizing the reduction in bias of the ML diagnostic. This would make the paper's framework actionable for prospective AIRR study design, not just retrospective diagnosis of what went wrong.

Added value to my researchIn my health geography studies, collecting ground-truth accessibility data (e.g., actual travel times to clinics by mode) is expensive relative to proxy measures (straight-line distance, census statistics). An active-learning confounder-selection strategy derived from this paper's causal framework would indicate which spatial variables are most worth measuring in a new city or region, optimizing field data collection budgets for 2SFCA accessibility and telehealth-divide studies.

④ Stress-test the paper's correction strategies against OOD deployment using functionally invariant path engineering

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that batch-effect correction (linear regression on k-mer frequencies) and confounder adjustment reduce BER under moderate distribution shifts, but does not characterize how much shift the corrections can tolerate before breaking. An extension would build an OOD stress-test suite: fix the correction strategy, then systematically increase the magnitude of distribution shift and measure when each correction fails. Pairing this with "functionally invariant path" engineering — identifying the subset of model parameters consistent across environments — would isolate which components of the AIRR classifier are genuinely causal-direction stable vs. fragile, providing a principled model hardening step.

Added value to my researchMy ML ensemble models for spatial prediction (e.g., predicting EV charging demand or hospital utilization across cities) face similar OOD stress: a model trained in one city is deployed in a city with very different land-use structure. This paper's BER-under-shift experiments provide a methodological template for my own OOD benchmarking, and the "invariant path" framing suggests a way to identify which spatial features of my ensemble are genuinely transferable vs. city-specific artifacts.

⑤ Adapt the paper's causal DAG batch-node framework to multi-modal spatial data fusion

Extending this paperThe paper treats batch effects as a DAG node whose edges to the AIRR node represent systematic protocol-level biases corrupting the biological signal. This is formally identical to the problem of fusing data from heterogeneous sensors or platforms — remote sensing vs. mobile phone vs. social media — where each source introduces its own systematic "batch" bias (spatial resolution, temporal sampling, population representativeness). An extension would map each data source to a "batch node" in a causal graph and ask: when does fusing heterogeneous sources introduce spurious associations that corrupt a spatial ML predictor, and how can do-calculus guide the adjustment? The paper's simulation experiments could be adapted to spatial feature vectors to benchmark multi-source fusion strategies.

Added value to my researchMy data-fusion research combines mobile-phone records, social media, remote sensing, and POI data to predict urban health outcomes. Each source has known biases (mobile-phone data over-represents young urban males; social media is geographically clustered near city centers). Treating each source as a "batch variable" in a causal DAG — exactly as this paper treats sequencing protocol as a batch node — gives a principled framework for diagnosing and correcting cross-source confounding in my multi-source GeoAI models, improving both reproducibility and spatial generalizability of my FAIR open-science pipelines.
