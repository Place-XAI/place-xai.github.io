Infrastructure for regional food systems to feed cities at a time of deglobalization

Sarah Elton · Donald Cole · Krishna Bahadur KC · Evan Fraser Published 2026-03-18 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00413-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

As globalization retreats and climate change threatens long supply chains, cities cannot assume uninterrupted global produce flows. Using Canada's largest wholesale produce market — the Ontario Food Terminal (OFT) — as a working example, the authors show that scaled-up regional food system infrastructure, combined with protected nearby agricultural land, reliably connects farmers to urban consumers and underpins food security. The policy imperative: protect regional foodsheds and the physical infrastructure (markets, terminals, farmland) that lets a city feed itself when trade is disrupted.

Why It Matters

  • Reframes food security as an infrastructure question. Most "local food" debates fixate on farmers' markets and short chains; this work shows that an at-scale wholesale terminal is the missing physical backbone that lets regional produce actually reach a metropolis of millions.
  • Directly relevant to the deglobalization moment. With tariffs, trade restrictions and climate shocks now plausible, the paper asks the urgent planning question: can a city still be fed if global produce flows are interrupted?
  • Links land protection to urban resilience. The OFT only works because of nearby agricultural land; the paper turns "protect farmland" from sentiment into a concrete urban-resilience argument tied to a functioning supply node.
  • Offers a transferable, decades-proven template. Rather than a hypothetical model, it documents infrastructure that has functioned for decades — a reference design planners elsewhere can study and adapt.

Core Methods

  • Case-study research design: an in-depth study of a single, decades-old piece of regional food infrastructure (the OFT) used as an exemplar of scaled regional supply.
  • Qualitative fieldwork: interviews and field visits with actors across the produce value chain (farming, wholesale distribution, retail) to reconstruct how the terminal links farmers to urban consumers.
  • Spatial / GIS mapping: choropleth mapping of farms supplying the OFT Farmers' Market by census subdivision, and land-use mapping of supplying areas, using public Agriculture and Agri-Food Canada data.
  • Foodshed & supply-chain analysis: situating the terminal within its regional foodshed to connect agricultural land protection to urban food security.
  • Evidence-to-policy synthesis: translating the mixed evidence into a planning argument for protecting regional foodsheds and food-system infrastructure.

① Turn the foodshed into a quantitative accessibility surface

Extending this paperThe paper establishes qualitatively that the OFT plus regional farmland feeds the city, and maps supplying farms by census subdivision (Fig. 2) — but never quantifies how accessible fresh produce is to residents, nor where access gaps lie. A direct extension: build a network-based produce-access surface from farms → terminal → retailers → neighborhoods, exposing food-desert pockets the choropleth cannot show and stress-testing them under a trade-disruption scenario.

② Resilience stress-testing of the single-terminal architecture

Extending this paperThe paper celebrates a centralized terminal that has "functioned for decades," but a single dominant node is also a single point of failure under flood, fire, labor action or pandemic. Extending it: model the OFT-centered supply network and run node/edge-removal stress tests, quantifying how quickly urban produce access degrades and whether distributed mini-hubs would harden the system — converting the paper's success story into a robustness analysis.

④ Couple the foodshed with intra-urban production (rooftops, urban agriculture)

Extending this paperThe paper's resilience story is entirely about peri-urban farmland feeding the terminal; it ignores produce that could be grown inside the city. Extending it: add an intra-urban supply layer (rooftop and vacant-lot agriculture) to the regional model and ask how much it shifts the foodshed boundary and the self-sufficiency margin under disrupted trade.

Extending this paperThe paper stops at the terminal-to-retail wholesale stage and does not analyze the last mile into neighborhoods — where equity and carbon costs concentrate.
