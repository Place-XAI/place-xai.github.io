Partially shared multi-modal embedding learns holistic representation of cell state

Zhang, Xinyi · Shivashankar, G. V. · Uhler, Caroline Published 2026-02-25 DOI 10.1038/s43588-025-00948-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

APOLLO (Autoencoder with Partially Overlapping Latent space via Latent Optimization) is a general computational framework that learns to automatically separate shared and modality-specific information across diverse single-cell data types — including paired RNA-seq/ATAC-seq, scRNA-seq/protein, and multiplexed imaging — without manual feature alignment. By explicitly disentangling what is jointly captured across modalities from what is modality-exclusive, APOLLO enables accurate cross-modality prediction (predicting unmeasured protein stains from chromatin images) and provides an interpretable, holistic view of cell state that existing integration methods cannot achieve.

★

Why It Matters

  • Solves a fundamental ambiguity in multi-modal biology. Current methods either process modalities separately (losing cross-modal information) or integrate them into a single shared space (losing modality-specific signal). APOLLO is the first autoencoder framework to explicitly partition the latent space into shared and private components, making the contributions of each data type traceable.
  • Enables prediction of unmeasured modalities at single-cell resolution. By routing information through the shared latent space as a bridge, APOLLO accurately predicts protein fluorescence images from chromatin images alone, with performance on downstream phenotype classification matching that of real protein images.
  • Biologically interpretable by design. PC analysis of the partitioned latent spaces reveals that cell-cycle genes dominate RNA-specific dimensions, transcription-factor activity genes dominate shared dimensions, and heterochromatin volume — a marker of aging — is exclusively captured in the shared imaging space; distinctions invisible to black-box integration methods.
  • Broadly applicable beyond single-cell genomics. The framework is directly applicable to any paired multi-modal data, including medical imaging biobanks combining diverse modalities (MRI, omics, clinical records) at the individual level, wherever partial information sharing between data types is expected but unknown.

Core Methods

  • Partially overlapping autoencoder (APOLLO): one autoencoder per modality; latent space partitioned into a large shared subspace and smaller per-modality private subspaces. Architecture is modality-agnostic: fully connected layers for sequencing data, convolutional layers for imaging.
  • Two-step latent optimization: Step 1 jointly trains decoders and directly optimizes latent coordinates as free parameters via reconstruction loss (BCE for both seq and image) plus L2 regularization and Gaussian noise injection. Step 2 trains modality-specific encoders to generalize to held-out cells by regressing onto Step-1 latent coordinates via MSE.
  • Cross-modality prediction: a sample from modality j is encoded to its latent space; the shared dimensions are extracted and decoded through the target-modality decoder — enabling prediction of unmeasured proteins from chromatin images in held-out patients.
  • Conditional multi-protein imaging model: a trainable 64-dimensional protein-ID embedding concatenated to encoder hidden layers and decoder inputs allows a single model to handle multiple protein channels.
  • Latent space interpretation: PCA of each latent subspace; cells binned along each PC; differential gene expression (Scanpy rank_genes_groups) or differential image-feature analysis (234 predefined morphological features from ref. 43); GO enrichment analysis. Feature ablation via ResNet-18 phenotype classifiers.
  • Benchmarking: Seurat WNN, standard multi-modal autoencoder (single-step), and image inpainting model. Metrics: cell-type classification accuracy, cross-modal prediction L1 loss, phenotype classification accuracy (ResNet-18 on 4 diagnoses from 40 patients).

① Scale APOLLO's disentanglement to foundation-model cell embeddings

Extending this paperAPOLLO trains from scratch on each paired dataset; for small cohorts the latent optimization in Step 1 is data-hungry and cell-type coverage may be sparse. A direct extension: replace the modality-specific encoders with a pre-trained single-cell foundation model (such as a cell-atlas transformer) and fine-tune only the shared/private decoder heads. This lets APOLLO inherit universal gene-expression priors from hundreds of millions of cells while still learning the partial-sharing structure specific to the target paired dataset — dramatically reducing the data needed to disentangle new modality combinations.

Added value to my researchThis mirrors my agenda of fine-tuning geospatial foundation models for domain-specific tasks. In place of cell-type embeddings, a pre-trained spatiotemporal encoder (trained on GPS, mobile-phone, or remote-sensing streams) could serve as a fixed backbone, with APOLLO-style shared/private heads learning what is jointly captured by, say, satellite imagery and social-media activity vs. what is unique to each data stream — directly improving my multi-source data fusion pipeline for urban health analysis.

② Benchmark APOLLO rigorously within the emerging autoencoder ecosystem for omics

Extending this paperAPOLLO is validated by direct ablation against Seurat WNN and a standard multi-modal autoencoder, but it is not positioned against the broader landscape of autoencoder architectures (variational, beta-VAE, contrastive, diffusion-decoder variants) recently systematically compared for biological molecular profiling. Running APOLLO through the AUTOENCODIX benchmarking framework would objectively locate where APOLLO's two-step latent optimization adds value over simpler architectures on reconstruction quality, clustering accuracy, and cross-modality transfer, and identify regimes where it may be outperformed.

Added value to my researchThe same benchmarking logic applies to my spatiotemporal prediction tasks. A systematic comparison of autoencoder variants for fusing heterogeneous geospatial data (GPS trajectories, POI counts, remote-sensing spectral bands) would clarify whether a partial-sharing design beats standard multi-input VAEs for producing interpretable spatial representations — directly informing which architecture to adopt for my explainable geospatial ML pipeline.

Cross-ref (corpus): AUTOENCODIX: a generalized and versatile framework to train and evaluate autoencoders for biological representation learning and beyond — 10.1038/s43588-025-00916-4

③ Adapt APOLLO to federated multi-site settings without raw data sharing

Extending this paperAPOLLO requires co-measured paired data on the same cell; many clinical datasets collect overlapping but never fully paired modalities across sites, and raw data cannot be pooled due to privacy constraints. Extending the paper: incorporate APOLLO's latent-optimization strategy into a federated learning setting where each site trains its modality-specific encoder/decoder locally and only the shared latent coordinates (or gradient updates) are communicated across sites — following the federated disentangled representation approach already demonstrated for unsupervised brain anomaly detection across hospitals.

Added value to my researchIn my telehealth and health-geography work, electronic health records, mobile-phone mobility data, and remote-sensing environmental exposures are held by different custodians under strict access constraints. A federated APOLLO-style architecture would let me learn what is jointly captured by mobility and clinical records vs. what is environment-specific, without centralizing sensitive data — enabling privacy-compliant digital-divide and healthcare accessibility analysis across health systems.

Cross-ref (corpus): Federated disentangled representation learning for unsupervised brain anomaly detection — 10.1038/s42256-022-00515-2

④ Extend APOLLO to spatial transcriptomics: disentangle tissue context from intrinsic cell state

Extending this paperThe Discussion explicitly flags extension to unpaired measurements (e.g. spatial transcriptomics and multiplexed protein stains on adjacent tissue slices) as a key future direction and sketches a distribution-matching approach. APOLLO currently operates on isolated single cells; applying it to spatially resolved data would add a neighborhood-context modality — disentangling what a cell's microenvironment contributes vs. what is intrinsic to the cell — a distinction APOLLO's shared/private design is perfectly suited to capture but has not yet been applied to spatial data.

Added value to my researchThe analogy to my urban-analytics work is direct. In spatiotemporal analysis of public-health outcomes, the "intrinsic" risk of a location (local socio-economic deprivation) vs. its "contextual" risk (spillover from neighboring areas) is precisely the shared/private decomposition APOLLO models. Adapting this framework to decompose place-based vs. neighborhood-spillover health signals in spatial panel data would enrich my 2SFCA accessibility models with a mechanism for separating supply-side access from demand-side contextual effects.

⑤ Feed APOLLO's interpretable latent axes into hierarchical single-cell clustering

Extending this paperAPOLLO produces interpretable, disentangled latent spaces, but the paper stops at PC-level interpretation and binary k-means clustering. A natural extension: feed the separate shared and modality-specific embeddings into a hierarchical autoencoder that first clusters within each subspace and then aligns the resulting hierarchical cluster trees across modalities — revealing cell-state hierarchies driven by shared biology (e.g. lineage) vs. modality-specific processes (e.g. cell cycle captured only in RNA). This gives APOLLO a richer downstream analysis layer beyond UMAP visualization.

Added value to my researchIn my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and community-detection work, the analogous challenge is decomposing spatial clusters that reflect universal urban structure (shared across cities) from those that reflect local context (specific to each city's economy or history). An APOLLO-style shared/private decomposition at the cluster level would improve my network-community detection pipeline by first aligning cross-city patterns before modeling city-specific deviations — directly relevant to my urban agglomeration and regional-collaboration research.
