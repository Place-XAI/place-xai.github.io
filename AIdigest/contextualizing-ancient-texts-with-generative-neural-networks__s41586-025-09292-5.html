Contextualizing ancient texts with generative neural networks

Yannis Assael · Thea Sommerschield · Alison Cooley · Brendan Shillingford · John Pavlopoulos · Priyanka Suresh · Bailey Herms · Justin Grayston · Benjamin Maynard · Nicholas Dietrich · Robbe Wulgaert · Jonathan Prag · Alex Mullen · Shakir Mohamed Published 2025-07-23 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09292-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Aeneas, a multimodal generative neural network, can contextualize, restore, date, and geographically attribute Latin inscriptions at a level that meaningfully augments expert historians. In a 23-historian evaluation, Aeneas-retrieved parallels were deemed useful research starting points in 90% of cases, improved historians' confidence by 44%, and reduced chronological dating error to a 13-year distance from ground truth — demonstrating that human–AI collaboration substantially outperforms either humans or AI alone on core epigraphic tasks.

Why It Matters

  • Transforms a labor-intensive scholarly workflow. Finding textual parallels for a single inscription traditionally takes days; Aeneas retrieves historically grounded parallels in seconds, allowing historians to focus on interpretation rather than discovery.
  • Solves a genuinely hard AI problem. Arbitrary-length text restoration — recovering text when the amount missing is itself unknown — had not previously been addressed for ancient languages; Aeneas introduces and benchmarks this capability.
  • Quantifies human–AI synergy rigorously. The study is the largest historian–AI collaborative evaluation to date (23 epigraphers, 60 inscriptions, 3-stage protocol), providing statistically significant evidence that pairing humans with AI yields results superior to either alone.
  • Ships as open, reusable infrastructure. The Latin Epigraphic Dataset (LED, 176,861 inscriptions), the Aeneas model weights, and a public interface at predictingthepast.com are all publicly released, seeding future Digital Humanities research.
  • Demonstrates broad generalizability. The architecture is designed to extend to any ancient language and written medium (papyri, manuscripts, coinage) — making it a platform, not a one-off tool.

Latin Epigraphic Dataset (LED) — 176,861 deduplicated Latin inscriptions totalling 16 million characters, spanning the 7th century BCE to the 8th century CE and covering Roman provinces from Britannia and Lusitania (west) to Aegyptus and Mesopotamia (east). Partitioned into training (last digit ≠ 3,4), validation (digit = 4), and test (digit = 3) sets.

Three openly licensed epigraphy databases merged: Epigraphic Database Roma (EDR) — zenodo.org/records/3575495; Epigraphic Database Heidelberg (EDH) — zenodo.org/records/3575155; EDCS_ETL (Clauss-Slaby) — zenodo.org/records/7072337. All under CC BY 4.0.

Images sourced from EDR and EDH; approximately 5% of inscriptions have a corresponding image (exact count not stated in the article). Images are greyscale, 224×224 pixels, quality-filtered to remove drawings, squeezes, and blurry scans.
Human evaluation
23 epigraphers (masters students to professors; 11 male, 12 female; roughly equal early-career and senior researchers) evaluated 60 inscriptions drawn from the LED test set in a 3-stage protocol (solo → parallels → parallels + predictions). Ethics approval from the University of Nottingham Faculty of Arts Research Ethics Committee.
Onomastics baseline

Inscriptions span 800 BCE to 800 CE; 62 Roman provinces used as geographic attribution labels; historical case studies include the Res Gestae Divi Augusti (emperor Augustus, 14 CE) and a votive altar from Mainz (211 CE).

Core Methods

  • Multimodal transformer architecture (Aeneas): a T5-derived decoder ("torso") with rotary positional embeddings processes character sequences up to 768 characters; a ResNet-8 vision network processes greyscale images. Specialized MLP heads handle restoration, arbitrary-length restoration, 62-class geographical attribution, and decade-level chronological attribution.
  • Arbitrary-length restoration: a binary auxiliary head predicts at each decoding step whether one or multiple characters are missing (signalled by the # placeholder), enabling restoration of lacunae of unknown length — a first for ancient languages. Candidate restorations generated by beam search (beam width = 100).
  • Historically-enriched embeddings for contextualization: intermediate torso representations are aggregated into a unified embedding vector that encodes chronological and geographical signal. Cosine similarity retrieval against the 141,000-inscription training LED yields ranked parallel texts — outperforming fuzzy string matching and standard T5 embeddings (demonstrated via UMAP visualization).
  • Multi-task training with Bayesian loss weighting: four task losses combined as L = 3Lrestoration + Lunknown + 2Lregion + 1.25Ldate; trained for 1 week on 64 TPU v5e chips (Google Cloud) with 1 million steps, LAMB optimizer, and extensive data augmentation (up to 75% text masking, word deletion, image rotation/brightness).
  • Onomastics baseline: fully automated extraction of Roman personal name n-grams (uni/bi/trigrams) from LED to derive geographical and chronological priors — used as a proxy for traditional historian methods and for comparison with Ithaca (prior state-of-the-art on ancient Greek).
  • Saliency maps for explainability: character-level and image-level saliency maps identify which textual features and image regions most influenced each prediction, providing historians with interpretable evidence for model outputs.
  • Evaluation: character error rate (CER) and top-20 accuracy for restoration; top-1/top-3 accuracy among 62 provinces for attribution; mean years-from-range for dating. Statistical significance via permutation tests.

① Adapt Aeneas' historically-enriched embeddings for geospatial document retrieval

Extending this paperAeneas constructs embeddings that jointly encode textual content and task-specific contextual signals (temporal, geographic) — far richer than standard T5 embeddings. A direct extension: apply the same multi-task embedding design to other ancient document corpora (papyri, cuneiform, medieval charters) to test whether the contextual enrichment transfers across languages and media. The paper calls for this explicitly in its future-directions section. The retrieval mechanism could also be augmented with geographic metadata (findspot coordinates) to produce a spatially-aware contextualization system, linking inscription networks to the physical Roman landscape.

Added value to my researchThe multi-task embedding paradigm directly informs my work on geospatial foundation model fine-tuning: just as Aeneas enriches text embeddings with historical-geographic tasks, fine-tuning a geospatial foundation model with spatial prediction tasks (facility accessibility, health outcome attribution) would produce richer embeddings for spatiotemporal retrieval and few-shot transfer to new cities or health regions.

② Use Aeneas' saliency maps as a template for spatial explainability in GeoAI models

Extending this paperAeneas produces character-level and image-level saliency maps that historians find interpretable — identifying archaizing orthography, personal names, and iconographic elements as chronological or geographical markers. Extending this: a systematic audit of Aeneas' saliency maps across the full LED test set could reveal which linguistic features are most diagnostic by province and period, building a data-driven catalog of epigraphic "fingerprints." This directly addresses the paper's acknowledged limitation that saliency explanations for the image modality are "often less interpretable by domain experts."

Added value to my researchThe Aeneas saliency paradigm — input-feature attribution for structured predictions in a domain with sparse expert labels — mirrors my use of explainable ML (SHAP, LIME, GWR) for spatial prediction. Specifically, the idea of learning which features are "chronologically diagnostic" maps onto learning which socio-spatial features are predictive of health outcomes across geographic regions, informing spatially non-stationary models (MGWR) and their interpretability for policy audiences.

③ Extend Aeneas' human–AI evaluation protocol to measure AI-assisted spatial data annotation

Extending this paperThe paper's 3-stage historian–AI evaluation — solo baseline, then parallels, then predictions — is a rigorous template for measuring human–AI co-performance. Extending the paper: apply this exact protocol to a secondary set of newly discovered inscriptions, measuring how Aeneas' assistance changes over time as historians gain familiarity with the tool, and whether parallels or predictions contribute more to long-term skill development. The paper's finding that historians accepted 1.5 Aeneas parallels on average (range 0–6) suggests large individual variation worth characterizing.

Added value to my researchThe 3-stage evaluation design translates directly to crowdsourced or expert-assisted spatial data labeling in my work: e.g., measuring how AI-generated accessibility predictions (from 2SFCA/R2SFCA models) change health geography researchers' confidence in classifying underserved areas, using the same solo → AI parallels → AI predictions structure to isolate the contribution of each AI output type to analyst judgment.

④ Replace Aeneas' cosine similarity retrieval with a learned re-ranking stage

Extending this paperAeneas retrieves parallels by cosine similarity over its historically-enriched embedding space — a fully unsupervised retrieval step. No feedback from historian judgments is fed back to refine future retrievals. Extending the paper: introduce a lightweight re-ranker trained on historians' accept/reject decisions (the evaluation generated binary parallel-utility labels for each retrieved inscription). This would create a human-in-the-loop retrieval loop where Aeneas' parallel quality improves with expert feedback — a reinforcement-from-human-feedback (RLHF) variant for historical document retrieval.

Added value to my researchThe same human-feedback re-ranking design applies to open-science data fusion in my work: when fusing mobile phone records, social media, and remote sensing for urban analytics, there is no ground truth for which data combination is most informative — an expert re-ranking loop (analogous to historian feedback) could iteratively improve multi-source feature selection, connecting to my theme of reproducible, FAIR multi-source geospatial analysis.

⑤ Map inscription networks reconstructed by Aeneas onto actual Roman spatial networks

Extending this paperAeneas identifies networks of epigraphically related inscriptions (parallel texts) without access to spatial or archaeological context — yet its parallels frequently share findspot provinces (as in the Mainz beneficiarius altar case study). Extending the paper: systematically overlay Aeneas-inferred epigraphic networks onto reconstructed Roman road and trade networks, testing whether linguistic-cultural diffusion follows physical infrastructure. This would transform Aeneas from a text tool into a spatial diffusion detector and connect epigraphy to network geography.

Added value to my researchReconstructing latent spatial networks from text-derived similarity is structurally identical to my use of network community detection (e.g., job-housing networks, mobility flow graphs) to identify spatial clusters. Aeneas-like embedding-based network construction, applied to spatiotemporal health data (e.g., mobile-phone co-location → social network → disease transmission), would enhance my agent-based modeling and SIR-model work by providing data-driven network topology rather than assumed connectivity.
