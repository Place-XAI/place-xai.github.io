A videogame for perceived risk of harm from opioid misuse in adolescents: a randomized controlled trial

Tyra Boomer · Lily Hoerner · Kaitlyn Larkin · Kaitlin Maciejewski · Tassos C. Kyriakides · Lynn E. Fiellin Published 2025-12-01 DOI 10.1038/s44360-025-00010-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

PlaySmart, a theory-driven videogame, did not significantly raise adolescents' perceived "great risk of harm" from opioid misuse at the primary 3-month endpoint (29% vs 23% control; P = 0.14). However, it produced a transient gain in risk perception at 6 weeks (immediately post-gameplay; P = 0.047) and durable improvements in opioid knowledge (P < 0.001) and negative expectancies (P = 0.001), with high engagement and satisfaction. The effect on risk perception decayed by 3 months — pointing to the need for booster content rather than a one-shot dose.

★

Why It Matters

  • Targets a lethal, fast-rising harm in youth. Opioid toxicity accounted for over 10% of deaths in US 15–19-year-olds in 2021, and 12% of high-school students report lifetime prescription-opioid misuse — yet almost no rigorously evaluated opioid-specific prevention games exist.
  • A rare RCT of a "serious game". Most digital-health games are evaluated in small pre–post studies; this is a two-arm, placebo-controlled, superiority RCT with 532 high-risk adolescents and 87–90% follow-up — a credible efficacy benchmark.
  • An informative null with a signal. The primary outcome was not met, but the 6-week spike and durable knowledge/attitude gains localize where digital prevention works and when it fades — actionable design intelligence, not just a negative result.
  • Scalability is the point. A web/mobile game side-steps the staffing, training and fidelity barriers that have shrunk school-based prevention programs (75%→65% of schools, 2011–2019), offering a low-marginal-cost delivery channel.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Study design
Two-arm parallel superiority, unblinded RCT with a placebo (attention/time) comparator; intention-to-treat analysis. Registered at ClinicalTrials.gov NCT04941950; Yale IRB protocol #2000030553.
Sample & setting
532 participants (PlaySmart n = 269; control n = 263) of 1,062 screened and 839 eligible; mean age 16.6 yr, 47% female; racially/ethnically diverse (45% Black, 34% White, 38% Hispanic). Recruited at 15 Connecticut high schools.
Eligibility
Ages 16–19, no prior opioid misuse, and "high-risk" by past-30-day non-opioid substance use OR a modified PHQ-2 ≥1 OR modified GAD-2 ≥1.
Time period
Enrolment and follow-up 21 Oct 2021 – 27 Feb 2024. Self-reported assessments at baseline, 6 weeks (end of gameplay) and 3 months (primary endpoint); 6- and 12-month data forthcoming.
Follow-up rates
3-month primary outcome completed by 87% (231/266) PlaySmart and 90% (234/261) control; overall completeness ≥85% (below the 16% loss assumed in power calculation).
Instruments
Perceived risk: 8 items from Monitoring the Future (MTF) (5 heroin, 3 prescription opioid). Secondary: DURSE self-efficacy, intentions scale, 30-item knowledge composite (max 38), 15-item attitudes/expectancies scale.
Data availability
De-identified participant data to be deposited (post-publication, indefinitely) in NIDA's NAHDAP archive (NIDA HEAL Prevention Cooperative); restricted access via data-use agreement, ~4–6 week review. No external geospatial/remote-sensing datasets used.
Code availability
No custom analysis code (analyses in SAS 9.4). PlaySmart distributed via Playbl (playbl.com); game source code not released (IP restrictions).

Core Methods

  • Intervention design: PlaySmart is a theory-driven (theory of planned behaviour, health belief model, social cognitive theory) narrative web/mobile game with 6 storylines and 6 minigames, built via user-centred design with adolescents; control arm = 9 content-neutral commercial games (e.g., The Sims) as an attention/time placebo.
  • Randomization: REDCap-based, block sizes 2 and 4, stratified by sex and grade (9/10 vs 11/12); scheme generated by the senior biostatistician and implemented by an independent team; outcome team blinded until database lock.
  • Dosing: supervised after-school sessions (~60 min, 1–2×/week, ~6 weeks) on research iPads; mean play 211 min PlaySmart vs 226 min control (of 300-min target).
  • Primary analysis: χ² test of the proportion endorsing "great risk of harm" on all eight MTF items at 3 months; difference in proportions with 95% CIs.
  • Supplemental models: logistic/linear mixed-effects models (random participant; random participant-within-school cluster) adjusted for sex, grade and baseline risk; secondary outcomes via least-squares means under missing-at-random (no imputation).
  • Power: designed to detect a 15% absolute difference at 3 months (90% power, α = 0.05), n = 454 inflated to 532 for 16% attrition.

① Geocode the trial: turn 15 schools into a spatial efficacy surface

Extending this paperThe trial treats the 15 Connecticut high schools only as a clustering variable in mixed models, discarding their geography. A direct extension: link each school to its catchment's spatial accessibility to substance-use treatment, school-based health centres and pharmacies, and test whether place-based deprivation moderates PlaySmart's 6-week risk-perception lift. This converts an "average null" into a map of where a scalable digital intervention adds the most marginal value.

② Model and pre-empt the decay: a booster-timing optimizer

Extending this paperThe paper's central empirical fact is a significant 6-week gain that vanishes by 3 months; the authors call for an unspecified "booster." Extending it: fit a per-participant memory-decay / survival model of risk perception from the three timepoints (and forthcoming 6- and 12-month waves), then derive the optimal booster timing that maximizes durable effect under a fixed engagement budget — a quantitative answer to the paper's open question.

③ Adaptive, personalized gameplay via a learned policy

Extending this paperPlaySmart currently delivers fixed storylines with no required dosage or adaptivity (mean 211 of 300 target minutes). ④ Fuse the game with the environment that shapes risk

Extending this paperPerceived risk of harm is treated as a purely individual, in-game construct. Extending it: enrich the trial with contextual exposures — neighbourhood urbanicity, greenspace, deprivation — to test whether the environment an adolescent returns to after each session erodes the game's 6-week gain. A multi-source design would explain the decay as a person × place interaction, not just memory loss.
