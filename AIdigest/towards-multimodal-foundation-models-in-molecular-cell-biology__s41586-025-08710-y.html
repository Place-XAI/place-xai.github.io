Towards multimodal foundation models in molecular cell biology

Haotian Cui · Alejandro Tejada-Lapuerta · Maria Brbić · Julio Saez-Rodriguez · Simona Cristea · Hani Goodarzi · Mohammad Lotfollahi · Fabian J. Theis · Bo Wang Published 2025-04-16 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-08710-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The exponential growth of high-throughput omics data demands a new generation of multimodal foundation models pretrained jointly on genomics, transcriptomics, epigenomics, proteomics, metabolomics, and spatial profiling. Through context-specific transfer learning, such models could produce holistic maps of cells, genes, and tissues, enabling novel cell-type discovery, biomarker identification, gene regulation inference, and in silico perturbation experiments — launching an AI-empowered paradigm for understanding molecular cell biology.

Core Methods

  • Multimodal pretraining framework: joint pretraining on heterogeneous omics modalities via transformer-based architectures adapted from NLP, with each modality mapped to a token space (gene expression values, chromatin accessibility peaks, protein abundance vectors, metabolite profiles).
  • Cross-modal attention: tokens from distinct modalities interact through attention layers, learning co-variation between e.g. transcriptomics and epigenomics, enabling imputation of missing modalities.
  • Context-specific transfer learning: pretrained foundation model is fine-tuned for specific biological contexts (disease states, tissue types, perturbation conditions) with limited labeled data, following the NLP few-shot paradigm.
  • Lab-in-the-loop (active learning): model uncertainty and information-gain estimates guide prioritization of the next wet-lab experiment, iteratively improving the model while reducing cost.
  • In silico perturbation: causal reasoning within the model predicts cellular responses to genetic or chemical perturbations without physical experimentation, building on scGen and CPA architectures.
  • Holistic cell-state mapping: integrating all modalities produces continuous representations of cell states beyond discrete labels, supporting trajectory inference, pseudo-time analysis, and gene regulatory network inference.

① Apply the multimodal pretraining blueprint to geospatial foundation models

Extending this paperThe paper's core argument — that siloed unimodal models fail when biological reality is inherently multimodal — maps directly onto the geospatial domain. Remote sensing, mobility traces, social media, POIs, and census records are the "omics modalities" of GeoAI, currently pretrained in isolation. A concrete extension: design a geospatial multimodal foundation model pretrained jointly on raster imagery, vector networks, tabular population attributes, and textual place descriptions, using cross-modal attention analogous to the biological tokenization scheme the authors propose in Fig. 3.

② Transfer the "lab-in-the-loop" active-learning engine to spatial health-data acquisition

Extending this paperThe lab-in-the-loop framework (Fig. 2) proposes that the foundation model selects the next most informative biological experiment, reducing wet-lab cost. The paper sketches this conceptually; a concrete extension would implement a Bayesian experimental-design layer on top of the pretrained omics model — with quantified uncertainty estimates over cell-state predictions guiding which tissue samples or perturbation conditions to sequence next — and benchmark the reduction in labels required to match fully supervised performance.

This is a principled active-learning wrapper for GeoAI health studies that would dramatically reduce data-collection costs while improving equity coverage.

⑤ Scale spatial multi-omics integration toward a "Spatial Cell Atlas of Cities"

Extending this paperThe paper highlights spatial profiling as the most data-hungry omics modality to integrate, because it must co-register molecular readouts with micrometer-scale tissue coordinates. SWITCH addresses cross-modal co-registration for spatial multi-omics, and Geneformer's expanded pretraining corpus (>100 million single-cell profiles) demonstrates what scale buys. A direct extension: establish a benchmark suite for spatial multi-omics integration — defining standard metrics for spatial alignment quality, modality imputation accuracy, and downstream cell-type annotation improvement — giving the field evaluation infrastructure it currently lacks.

Added value to my researchSpatial profiling in biology — resolving molecular states at micrometer precision across tissue — is conceptually parallel to my urban analytics goal of resolving socioeconomic and health states at street-block precision across a city. An "urban cell atlas" pretrained on fine-grained spatiotemporal records from millions of locations across many cities could serve as a geospatial foundation model for spatial health prediction, 15-minute city accessibility modeling, and healthcare facility siting. This bridges my spatial-equity research with the geospatial FM fine-tuning theme and the SWITCH-style cross-layer data fusion methods.
