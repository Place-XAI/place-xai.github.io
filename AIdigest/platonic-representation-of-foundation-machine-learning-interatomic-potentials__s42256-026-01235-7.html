Platonic representation of foundation machine learning interatomic potentials

Zhenzhu Li · Aron Walsh Published 2026-05-07 DOI 10.1038/s42256-026-01235-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Seven independently developed machine learning interatomic potentials (MLIPs) — spanning equivariant and non-equivariant, conservative and non-conservative architectures — converge toward a shared latent geometry of atomic environments, consistent with the "platonic representation hypothesis." By projecting each model's embeddings through an anchor-based cosine-similarity transform, a unified common space emerges that preserves chemical periodicity, enables cross-model arithmetic and zero-shot model stitching, and provides a ground-truth-free measure of structural atypicality. Physical supervision (energy and force targets) — not training data distribution alone — is required for this convergence to occur.

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the silo between incompatible foundation models. Different MLIPs encode atomic environments in incommensurable latent spaces; this work provides the first practical, architecture-agnostic framework to unify and directly compare those spaces without retraining.
  • Reveals a physical principle behind convergence. The platonic organization requires energy and force supervision: a generative model trained on identical structures but without physical targets fails to reproduce it, demonstrating that physics, not data, drives representational alignment.
  • Enables zero-shot model stitching and embedding arithmetic. Cross-model product/reactant embeddings can be algebraically combined, opening modular reuse of pretrained potentials — a direct route to composable foundation models for materials.
  • Provides a label-free diagnostic tool. Manifold distance in the platonic space quantifies structural atypicality (out-of-distribution detection) and signals physical prediction failures such as symmetry breaking in non-equivariant architectures — without any ground-truth property labels.
  • Clarifies architectural trade-offs. Non-equivariant models (Orb-v3) show near-zero local-neighbourhood agreement with equivariant models, and propagate symmetry violations into phonon predictions — directly useful for model selection and architecture design.

Core Methods

  • Anchor-based platonic projection: select K representative anchor vectors from the embedding manifold via DIRECT sampling (Birch clustering + stratified centroid selection to maximise Silhouette score), then project each atomic embedding into a model-agnostic space of dimension K via pairwise cosine similarities: z_i = [cos(e_i, a_1), ..., cos(e_i, a_K)]. Scale invariance is inherent (cosine similarity, bounded [−1, 1]).
  • DIRECT anchor sampling: anchors selected from the MACE-small embedding distribution; validated across three random seeds and anchor counts K ∈ {3, 8, 20, 50, 100, 200, 400}; K = 100 chosen as the convergence point.
  • Representational alignment metrics: (1) Procrustes score — global alignment after optimal rotation; (2) mutual k-nearest neighbour (mKNN) score — Jaccard index of local neighbourhoods; (3) normalized optimal transport (OT) cost via Sinkhorn algorithm — distributional distance; combined into a composite SuperScore = ½(Procrustes + mKNN) × e−OT.
  • Dummy model negative control: MACE-small architecture with random weights (3.85 M parameters, no training) to confirm platonic geometry requires learned physics, not architectural priors.
  • Two-nearest-neighbour (2-NN) equivariance test: for each atom, measure d₁/d₂ in representation space; fraction with d₁ = 0 quantifies symmetry preservation across four representative space groups (Pm̅3m, Fm̅3m, I4/mmm, P6₃/mmc).
  • Embedding arithmetic and zero-shot stitching: material centroid z_Mater, polymorph difference z_Morph = z_M1 − z_M2, and reaction embedding z_React = Σz_products − Σz_reactants; cross-model stitching by pairing reactant embeddings from one model with product embeddings from another.
  • Manifold-distance typicality scoring: classify query structures into dense/sparse interior and near/far exterior regions of the reference MP-20 manifold; provides a ground-truth-free OOD measure for generated materials and non-equilibrium configurations.

② Use the paper's manifold-distance typicality score as a geospatial OOD detector

Extending this paper The paper partitions the platonic space into dense/sparse interior and near/far exterior regions relative to an MP-20 reference manifold, then classifies query structures by distance from this manifold without any property labels. This same approach generalises beyond materials: replace MP-20 with a reference corpus of typical urban configurations (e.g., typical POI distributions, street-network topologies, or health-facility catchment geometries). Any new city or neighbourhood is then scored by its manifold distance from the reference — the paper already validates the approach for generative-model outputs (GNoME, MatterGen) and non-equilibrium rattled structures; the logic transfers directly to spatial data.

Extending this paper The paper defines a reaction embedding as z_React = Σz_products − Σz_reactants and demonstrates cross-model agreement for BaTiO₃ synthesis (c-sim ≥ 0.7), then shows zero-shot stitching by mixing reactant embeddings from one model with product embeddings from another. ④ Adapt the 2-NN equivariance diagnostic to audit spatial invariance in physics-informed GNNs

Extending this paper The paper's 2-NN equivariance test measures the fraction of atoms with d₁ = 0 (identical first and second nearest-neighbour distances in representation space under rotation), exposing which MLIP architectures genuinely preserve physical symmetry. SevenNet — despite formally incorporating E(3)-equivariant layers — fails at strict numerical tolerance, revealing truncation artefacts invisible to standard performance benchmarks. A direct extension: apply the same diagnostic to graph neural networks for spatial data, testing whether they correctly respect geographic invariants (e.g., rotational invariance of distance-based accessibility metrics, translation invariance of spatial density patterns). The test is cheap, label-free, and architecture-agnostic.
