The decreasing housing utilization efficiency in China's cities

Lifeng Shi · Tobias Leichtle · Xianjin Huang · Michael Wurm · Hannes Taubenböck Published 2025-01-02 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00177-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Beyond the dramatic but rare "ghost city," China's far more pervasive problem is the quiet underutilization of urban housing. The authors introduce Housing Utilization Efficiency (HUE) — the ratio of actual population to theoretical population capacity — and measure it at the subdistrict level for 108 cities. Overall HUE fell from 84% (2010) to 78% (2020); central old-town areas show the lowest and fastest-declining efficiency while outer layers fare better; and four evolution types capture distinct couplings of population movement, housing growth and HUE change across China's urbanization stages.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reframes a fuzzy indicator into a measurable one. China has no standardized housing vacancy rate (HVR) and surveys are costly; HUE (actual population ÷ population capacity) sidesteps the impossible task of enumerating "vacant" units and makes supply–demand gaps computable from open data.
  • Resolves a chronic resolution–scale–time trade-off. Prior work was either single-city/single-date at fine resolution, or multi-date but coarse (city/grid). This study delivers subdistrict-level, two-date (2010, 2020), nationwide coverage simultaneously.
  • Locates the oversupply in space. It shows underutilization is concentrated in old urban cores and in lower-tier cities (4th-tier cities fell to ~70% by 2020), linking the pattern to land-finance incentives and "build-then-populate" urbanization.
  • Transferable framework. The HUE pipeline — boundary harmonization, census population, crawled real-estate capacity — can be adapted to other countries lacking authoritative vacancy statistics.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Spatial coverage
108 selected Chinese cities (urban population rate >60%; 4 first-tier, 15 second-tier, 30 third-tier, 59 fourth-tier), comprising 503 highly urbanized districts and 4,626 highly urbanized subdistricts. Cities chosen via the First Financial–New First-tier City Research Institute (FF-NFTCRI) "Cities' Business Attractiveness" ranking.
Administrative boundaries
National Platform for Common Geospatial Information Services (Tianditu) — 2020 vectorial subdistrict boundaries; manually adjusted to a consistent 2010–2020 extent using division-change announcements from the Ministry of Civil Affairs.
Population data
Sixth (2010) and Seventh (2020) National Census of China — permanent residents and average household size per subdistrict; urban population derived via census urbanization rates.
Housing capacity
Web-crawled real-estate platforms (e.g., Anjuke, Beike, Fangtianxia), collected Apr & Dec 2020 — per-neighborhood location, number of housing units, and construction year; cross-verified across platforms. 2010 housing reconstructed backward from construction years.
Spatial unit / window
Subdistrict (typical area 3–5 km² in cores, ~10 km² in outer layers); two time stages, 2010 and 2020.
Stated limitations
Informal/self-built housing not captured (e.g., Guangzhou HUE likely 4–5% lower than estimated; other cities <4%); no intermediate years feasible due to frequent division changes and missing subdistrict population data.

Core Methods

  • HUE definition: HUE = actual population ÷ population capacity within a spatial unit (the conceptual inverse of HVR but measured without enumerating vacancies); capped at 100% where informal dwellings push actual above capacity.
  • Population capacity: per subdistrict, capacity = (Σ housing units across neighborhoods) × average household size; units counted (not floor area) to avoid "shared-area" ambiguity.
  • Boundary harmonization: tracked and merged subdistricts altered by administrative changes so 2010 and 2020 share an identical spatial reference, mitigating census miscounts.
  • Intraurban layering: monocentric city model; each city's subdistricts split by distance to center into four equal-count rings — center, expansion, suburb, periphery — with center identified via main rail station(s) or CBD.
  • Evolution typing: compute within-city standard deviations of urban-population-change (UPC), urban-housing-change (UHC) and HUE-change (HUEC) rates; grade by interquartile intensity and combine into four interpretable evolution types (28 / 26 / 34 / 20 cities).
  • Plausibility check: cross-compare HUE-implied occupancy against independent HVR estimates (survey-based and big-data) since no authoritative reference exists.

① Replace crawled housing capacity with building-level remote sensing

Extending this paperThe paper's population-capacity denominator rests on web-crawled real-estate listings — incomplete for informal/older stock and unavailable before 2010. A direct extension: substitute or fuse in a satellite-derived building-function map to count and characterize residential units consistently, removing platform-coverage bias and enabling capacity estimation for lower-commercialization areas the paper had to drop. This would also let HUE be back-cast more rigorously than the construction-year heuristic.

Added value to my researchThis is core multi-source data fusion for me: pairing crawled POI/listing data with a remote-sensing building layer is exactly the supply-side denominator my 2SFCA/R2SFCA healthcare-access models need where facility-capacity data are missing.

② Turn static two-date HUE into a migration-driven dynamic model

Extending this paperThe paper explains falling central-core HUE through outward relocation + rural–urban migration, but only at two snapshots and without an explicit flow model. Extending it: couple HUE to net domestic migration / city-rank dynamics so capacity and occupancy co-evolve, predicting where oversupply will next appear rather than describing where it already is. This converts the four evolution "types" into a trajectory model.

Added value to my researchFeeds my job-housing network and urban-agglomeration work: a flow-aware HUE is a demand signal I can route through network community detection to detect mismatched supply across an agglomeration.

Cross-ref (corpus): Domestic migration and city rank dynamics — 10.1038/s44284-024-00170-1

③ Explain HUE decline, not just map it, with interpretable spatial ML

Extending this paperThe paper's typology is descriptive; it attributes decline to land finance and policy narratively. An extension: fit an explainable spatial model (MGWR / SHAP-on-ensembles) with subdistrict covariates (density, distance-to-center, tier, household-size shift, land-transfer reliance) to quantify which drivers move HUE and how their effects vary across space — testing the monocentric assumption the authors flag as a simplification.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my explainable ML for spatial prediction and MGWR agenda: HUE is a clean, policy-relevant target with strong non-stationarity, ideal for demonstrating spatially varying coefficients.

④ Reconnect HUE to service accessibility and public health

Extending this paperHUE measures whether housing is occupied, but low-HUE peripheral new towns are also where the paper notes services were "relocated" to lure residents. Extending it: overlay HUE with public-transport and facility accessibility to test whether underutilization tracks accessibility deficits — turning a housing metric into an early-warning layer for under-served, half-empty districts.

⑤ From measuring oversupply to optimizing redistribution under equity

Extending this paperThe paper diagnoses oversupply but stops short of intervention. Extending it: pose a facility/function-relocation optimization — given fixed housing stock, where should schools, transit and administrative functions (the levers the paper says cities use) be placed to lift low-HUE cores and peripheries together, balancing efficiency vs. equity across tiers? Affordable-housing allocation evidence supplies the equity objective.
