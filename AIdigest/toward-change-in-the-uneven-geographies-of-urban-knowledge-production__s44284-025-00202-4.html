Toward change in the uneven geographies of urban knowledge production

Martin Müller · Quentin Rihoux · Laura Neville Published 2025-02-14 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00202-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Although 84% of the world's urban population lives in the Global South and East, urban knowledge is still made and curated in the North. Across 25 leading Anglophone journals and 9 handbooks, only 42% of journal authors (17% of handbook authors) and just 15% of journal editors (10% of handbook editors) are based outside the North. The more powerful the gatekeeping role, the fewer non-Northern scholars — and a Northern PhD is almost a prerequisite for editorship (91% of gatekeepers hold one). Authorship is more diverse than gatekeeping; journals with less Northern boards and a less theoretical orientation publish more non-Northern authors, pointing to concrete structural levers for change.

★

Why It Matters

  • Quantifies a long-asserted bias. The "decentering" debate in urban studies has been largely qualitative; this work measures the actual geography of knowledge across 14,582 journal authors and 252 handbook authors, turning critique into evidence.
  • Separates creators from gatekeepers. By distinguishing authors from editors and board members, it shows the bias is not uniform — gatekeeping is far more Northern than authorship, locating where power actually concentrates.
  • Identifies actionable predictors. Regression models reveal that board composition, journal size, planning (vs. urban-studies) home discipline, and low theory orientation predict more non-Northern authorship — these are levers editors and publishers can pull.
  • Exposes the "aspiration gap." A journal's mission statement (decolonial/postcolonial wording) does not correlate with who actually gets published — stated intentions are not achieved facts.
  • Speaks to every quantitative urbanist. If most urban theory and data infrastructure are produced in the North, models and "universal" findings inherit a geography that may not transfer to where most cities actually are.

Core Methods

  • Affiliation mapping: classify every author/editor/board member as North or South-and-East by country of main institutional affiliation, then compare distributions against the world urban population.
  • Academic-trajectory tracing: reconstruct the Bachelor's → Master's → PhD → current-affiliation path of all 123 editors and visualize mobility with a Sankey diagram (Fig. 4) to surface diasporic scholarship.
  • Inferential statistics: two-tailed Welch t-tests (unequal variances) for mean differences (e.g., theory-oriented vs. other journals); correlation between board and authorship Northern shares (r = 0.62, P = 0.001).
  • Regression modeling: four log10-transformed OLS models (Board / Journal / Remit / Consolidated) of the proportion of non-Northern authors, with diagnostics for normality, homoscedasticity and multicollinearity (VIF < 2).
  • Mission-statement & theory coding: ordinal coding of journals' inclusivity statements (0 "Not Concerned" → 3 "De-/Postcolonial") and binary theory-orientation flags.
  • Qualitative triangulation: grounded-theory coding of editor interviews (Atlas.ti, deductive + inductive codes, memoing) to interpret why some journals outperform others.

① From affiliation labels to NLP-mined epistemic location at scale

Extending this paperThe authors candidly note their core proxy — single current institutional affiliation in binary North/non-North form — flattens "complex epistemic terrains" and is collected by hand for 14,582 authors. A direct extension: use large language models on funded research proposals and full texts to mine richer signals (study region, cited literatures, co-author networks, language of cases) and learn a continuous epistemic-location score rather than a binary flag — exactly the open-data-plus-LLM pipeline already demonstrated for cross-national smart-city research.

② Treat gatekeeping as a network and detect the structure of power

Extending this paperThe paper measures aggregate shares (15% non-Northern editors, r = 0.62 board–author correlation) but stops short of modeling the editor–board–handbook co-membership network that it explicitly warns about ("North-centered ego networks"). Extending it: build the bipartite scholar–outlet graph and apply community detection to reveal whether non-Northern scholars sit in peripheral cliques, and simulate which board additions would most rewire the network toward inclusion.

④ Connect knowledge geography to the data that get studied

Extending this paperThe paper maps where authors are based but, by design, not which cities they study. A powerful extension: cross the affiliation corpus with a geocoded index of study sites and the underlying cultural/remote-sensing data to test whether Northern gatekeeping co-produces a Northern bias in case selection — i.e., are South/East cities under-studied as well as under-authored?
