Infrastructure, regulation and the experiences of delivery cyclists in Australian cities

Amelia Thorpe · Marilyn Johnson · Courtney Hercus · Thomas Rudge · Soufiane Boufous · Derek Chong Published 2024-10-11 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00145-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Centering the missing voice of delivery riders in Sydney and Melbourne, this first comprehensive Australian study finds that riders' dominant safety concern is not the gig economy but the road environment — fast, distracted and inattentive drivers, doorings, and disconnected bike-lane networks. The change riders want most is connected, separated cycling infrastructure plus better road rules and enforcement, with delivery cyclists uniquely exposed to inclement weather they cannot opt out of. Riders are not waiting: they already repurpose underused public space to stitch together safer routes, modelling how cities could become more permeable.

★

Why It Matters

  • Restores the missing voice. Australian public, media and policy debate fixated on the gig economy and on cyclist "lawlessness"; this is the first comprehensive study to ask riders themselves what endangers them — and the answer reframes the problem.
  • Reframes the policy target. By showing the built environment (not employment status) is the primary danger, it redirects intervention toward connected separated infrastructure, road rules and enforcement — concrete, planner-actionable levers.
  • Names a delivery-specific hazard. Unlike commuters, delivery riders cannot opt out of storms and searing heat; the study foregrounds weather and heat strain as an occupational and urban-design problem.
  • Counters a harmful stereotype. Roadside video shows respectful, careful behaviour (e.g., >80% of riders exiting a lane crossed on the pedestrian phase), pushing back on racialized "risk-taker" narratives about migrant riders.
  • Turns lived expertise into design knowledge. Riders' informal route-hacks reveal where the network is broken and where latent public space could host new infrastructure.

Core Methods

  • Grounded theory: no a-priori hypothesis; data collection and analysis run concurrently, iteratively grouping responses into themes and categories to build theory from evidence.
  • In-depth interviews: semi-structured (45–90 min) covering risks, route selection, road environment, regulation, near-misses and suggested change; coded in NVivo by ≥2 team members with refinement in group meetings.
  • Naturalistic cycling study: handlebar GoPro capturing video + GPS across three real delivery shifts per rider; GPS routes mapped in QGIS over Google basemaps (Fig. 1).
  • Roadside observation: fixed cameras at high-volume city junctions (incl. pop-up bike lanes) recording all road users; behaviour counts (e.g., dooring, lane-blocking, pedestrian-phase crossing).
  • Statistical testing: chi-square tests (SPSS v27.0) on demographic/experience differences between food-delivery riders and couriers; descriptive statistics throughout.
  • Reflexive recruitment design: after a failed survey, pivoted to paid interviews + snowballing, working with the Migrant Justice Institute and Transport Workers Union to reach a distrustful, hard-to-sample cohort (acknowledged as a convenience-sample limitation).

① Turn the handlebar/roadside video into a quantitative GeoAI risk surface

Extending this paperThe study's 104 h of GoPro and 216 h of roadside footage are coded mostly by hand into thematic counts. A direct extension: apply computer-vision detection and trajectory extraction to the same footage to produce a continuous, geolocated map of conflict events (doorings, lane-blocking, near-overtakes) along the GPS routes in Fig. 1 — converting rich but qualitative observation into a reproducible, city-scale cycling-risk surface that planners can act on.

Added value to my researchThis feeds my GPS-trajectory and multi-source-fusion line directly: street-level video becomes another sensing stream alongside POIs and mobile-phone data, and the conflict-event surface is exactly the kind of fine-grained exposure layer I need for spatiotemporal kernel-density and explainable spatial-prediction models of road-safety risk.

② Build a rider-centred accessibility metric for the delivery network

Extending this paperThe paper shows riders stitch together safe routes across a "disparate, disconnected" lane network. Extending it: combine the mapped GPS routes with a routable street graph to compute, for each origin–destination pair, the gap between the safest and the app-suggested route — quantifying where infrastructure incompleteness forces riders into traffic, and ranking the missing links whose construction would most reduce exposure.

Added value to my researchThis reframes my 2SFCA/R2SFCA service-area work for a worker population: instead of patients reaching care, it is riders reaching destinations safely, with a safety-weighted impedance replacing simple travel time — a transferable template for equity-of-access analyses in active transport.

③ Optimize where to place separated infrastructure and rider refuges

Extending this paperRiders both demand more separated lanes and reveal latent public space (traffic islands, plazas) they already repurpose, plus a need for sheltered "hubs." Extending the paper: pose a facility-location / network-design optimization that allocates a fixed infrastructure budget across candidate lane segments and refuge sites to maximize safety gain — using the observed risk hotspots and route demand as inputs.

Added value to my researchThis is a near-isomorph of my EV-charging efficiency-vs-equity optimization: swap chargers for protected-lane segments and rider hubs, keep the equity constraint (migrant/LMIC riders, who fear enforcement, get priority coverage), and pair it with explainable ML to attribute which built-environment features drive the safety payoff.

④ Couple delivery cycling with the platform economy's system-level footprint

Extending this paperThe paper deliberately separates "the road" from "the gig economy," but the two are coupled: platform routing, pay-per-trip incentives and demand peaks shape when and where riders are exposed. Extending it: link rider-level risk to platform operational data and city-scale delivery volumes to model how dispatch and incentive design change aggregate exposure and emissions — turning individual experience into a system lever.

Added value to my researchThis connects to my urban-analytics and space–human coupling agenda: agent-based modelling of platform dispatch over the city graph, with riders as agents whose route/timing choices (and weather avoidance) emerge from incentives — a testbed for policy simulation that also informs public-health exposure mapping.

⑤ Model heat and storm exposure as an occupational-health surface for riders

Extending this paperA signature finding is that delivery riders cannot opt out of heat and rain, with high activity in 37 °C conditions and on slippery surfaces. Extending the paper: fuse the observed activity-by-hour spikes with gridded temperature/precipitation and surface-treatment data to build a spatiotemporal heat-and-weather exposure index for riders, identifying when and where heat-strain risk concentrates.

Added value to my researchThis sits squarely in my health-geography line: it merges remote-sensing/weather layers with worker mobility to produce an environmental-exposure surface for a vulnerable, mostly-migrant workforce — feeding spatial accessibility-to-shelter analysis and explainable models linking the built environment to health outcomes.
