Equitable impact of an AI-driven breast cancer screening workflow in real-world US-wide deployment

Leeann D. Louis · Edgar A. Wakelin · Matthew P. McCabe · Annie Y. Ng · Jiye G. Kim · Christoph I. Lee · Diana S. M. Buist · A. Gregory Sorensen · Bryan Haslam Published 2025-11-17 DOI 10.1038/s44360-025-00001-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A multistage AI-driven workflow — combining a computer-aided detection/diagnosis (CADe/x) device with an AI-triggered safeguard review — deployed across 109 US imaging sites and 96 radiologists in four states increased the cancer detection rate by +21.6% over standard of care (5.6 vs 4.6 per 1,000 exams, N = 579,583), while also improving positive predictive value by +15.0%. Critically, CDR gains were statistically equivalent across racial/ethnic subgroups (Black, Hispanic, white, non-Hispanic) and breast density categories — demonstrating that AI-driven screening can expand early detection without widening existing health disparities.

★

Why It Matters

  • Fills a critical US-specific evidence gap. Prior large AI screening trials were exclusively European (double-reading, full-field digital mammography, biennial). This is one of the first studies of comparable scale tailored to the US paradigm — annual, single-reading, digital breast tomosynthesis (DBT) — covering over half a million exams.
  • Proves equitable impact at national scale. The CDR improved consistently across Black, Hispanic, and white non-Hispanic women, and across dense and non-dense breast categories, with no statistically significant disparity in outcomes — directly addressing a key concern that AI could amplify existing screening inequities.
  • Demonstrates efficiency through targeted second review. Only 8% of exams required safeguard re-read by a breast-imaging specialist, yet the system achieved nearly the full CDR benefit of double-reading all exams — a compelling cost-efficiency argument for health systems.
  • Estimates population-level impact. The observed CDR increase extrapolates to approximately 34,097 additional cancers detected annually if applied to the US screening population (~43 million mammograms/year), framing AI adoption as a major public health intervention.
  • Real-world, not controlled-trial, evidence. Prospective deployment across geographically diverse outpatient settings (California, Delaware, Maryland, New York) generates evidence with high external validity, enabling regulatory and clinical adoption decisions.

Core Methods

  • Multistage AI workflow: (1) DeepHealth Breast AI CADe/x assigns a 4-level cancer suspicion score and localized bounding boxes to every DBT exam; (2) SafeGuard Review routes high-suspicion, non-recalled exams to a breast imaging specialist, who can trigger radiologist review and recall — only 8% of exams required this second stage.
  • Prospective consecutive case series design: pre-deployment (standard-of-care) vs. post-deployment (AI workflow) cohorts, with a 2-month learning curve excluded; not a randomized trial because blinding was infeasible in real-world deployment.
  • Unadjusted analysis: CDR, RR, and PPV1 computed per BCSC definitions; one-sided chi-squared tests with Agresti–Coull 95% CIs; no correction for multiple comparisons (outcomes are correlated).
  • Adjusted analysis via GEE: generalized linear models with GEE accounting for age, race/ethnicity, breast density, and interpreting radiologist clustering; marginal effects reported to assess robustness to confounding and test subpopulation interaction terms.
  • Subpopulation equity analysis: pre-specified primary subgroups — women with dense breasts, Black non-Hispanic women — plus secondary subgroups (Hispanic, white non-Hispanic, non-dense breast, other race); powered for CDR detection in all primary subgroups.
  • Radiologist eligibility criteria: only radiologists meeting MQSA volume thresholds in both periods were included, and only sites present in both cohorts — to reduce radiologist-level confounding and ensure comparable practice composition.
  • AI suspicion level analysis: distribution of cancer cases across the four CADe/x suspicion categories compared between cohorts to check whether the AI changed which cancers were found.

② Diagnose and mitigate dataset bias before AI deployment at scale

Extending this paperASSURE demonstrates that the deployed AI does not widen CDR disparities post-hoc, but the paper cannot assess whether the AI model's training data over- or under-represented dense-breast or minority-race cases. A causal extension: apply a structured dataset-bias audit using counterfactual reasoning to the training corpus of DeepHealth Breast AI, identifying confounders (scanner vendor, site workflow, radiologist skill mix) that could cause the model to generalize inequitably to new sites — before the next deployment. This converts the paper's retrospective equity check into a prospective design tool for equitable AI development.

③ Replace the fixed SafeGuard threshold with a learned, patient-adaptive routing policy

Extending this paperThe current workflow routes exams to safeguard review using a fixed DeepHealth Breast AI threshold, meaning the same suspicion cutoff applies to all patients regardless of prior screening history, age trajectory, or breast density trajectory over time. Extending ASSURE: recast the routing decision as a reinforcement learning problem where the threshold is a policy conditioned on longitudinal patient state (prior mammogram result, density change, age) — learning the optimal routing cutoff per patient profile to maximize CDR gain per additional second-read radiologist hour. Only 8% of exams were re-read; a learned policy could selectively concentrate that 8% on the highest-yield cases while preserving equity.

The learned routing policy produces a spatiotemporal decision surface (when, where, and which patient-risk stratum warrants second review), which is directly compatible with my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation methods for hotspot detection in health service utilization.

④ Extend to multi-cancer AI screening with a foundation model

Extending this paperASSURE is deliberately narrow: one AI system, one modality (DBT), one cancer type (breast). However, the study's workflow architecture — CADe/x triage + selective expert second-read — is modality-agnostic. A forward extension: replace DeepHealth Breast AI with a multi-organ foundation model trained on CT, enabling simultaneous breast and lung cancer screening from a single non-contrast CT scan within the same workflow infrastructure. The equity analysis framework developed in ASSURE (pre-specified racial/density subpopulations, GEE adjustment, BCSC-style metrics) could then be directly applied to assess whether multi-cancer AI screening scales equitably — a natural sequel study in this line of evidence.

⑤ Map hospital bypass and utilization inequality to identify where AI deployment yields the greatest equity dividend

Extending this paperASSURE establishes that the AI workflow improves CDR equitably at 109 sites that already had patients showing up for mammography. It cannot address the deeper equity challenge: many high-risk women do not access screening at all. Extending the study: link the site-level CDR improvements observed in ASSURE to mobile-phone-derived hospital utilization data (travel behavior, bypass patterns, socioeconomic barriers) to identify the geographic and socioeconomic profiles of non-screenees — then simulate where deploying the ASSURE workflow at new sites or mobile units would generate the largest marginal equity benefit in CDR for underserved populations.

This fuses my spatial accessibility methods with clinical AI impact quantification.
