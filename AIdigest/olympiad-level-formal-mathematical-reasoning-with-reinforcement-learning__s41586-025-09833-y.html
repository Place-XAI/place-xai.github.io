Olympiad-level formal mathematical reasoning with reinforcement learning

Thomas Hubert · Rishi Mehta · Laurent Sartran · Miklós Z. Horváth · Goran Žužić · Eric Wieser · Aja Huang · Julian Schrittwieser · Yannick Schroecker · Hussain Masoom · et al. (DeepMind) Published 2025-11-12 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09833-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

AlphaProof, an AlphaZero-inspired agent trained via reinforcement learning (RL) in the Lean formal proof assistant, achieves silver-medal performance at the 2024 International Mathematical Olympiad (IMO) — the first AI system to reach any medal level at that competition. By auto-formalizing ~80 million problems for a self-play RL curriculum and deploying test-time RL (TTRL) that generates millions of problem variants at inference, AlphaProof solves 3 of 5 non-geometry IMO problems, including the competition's hardest (P6, solved by only 5 of 609 human contestants). The work demonstrates that grounded, verifiable experience at scale produces agents with sophisticated mathematical reasoning far beyond prior automated systems.

Why It Matters

  • First medal-level AI at the IMO. Before AlphaProof, even the best AI systems could solve only a small fraction of the easiest historical IMO problems; achieving 28/42 points — the silver-medal threshold — is a qualitative leap, not an incremental improvement.
  • Formal verification closes the "trust gap." Unlike LLMs that produce plausible-looking but potentially incorrect proofs, every step AlphaProof produces is kernel-verified by Lean, guaranteeing correctness — essential for AI to be a reliable mathematical collaborator.
  • Test-time RL is a new inference paradigm. TTRL shows that running RL during inference on problem-specific variants can unlock solutions no amount of standard search could find, adding a qualitatively new scaling axis beyond larger models or more search budget.
  • Auto-formalization at scale democratizes formal math. The pipeline that converts ~1 million natural-language problems into ~80 million Lean problems dramatically expands the training frontier, showing that formal math need not wait for slow human-expert curation.
  • Benchmark infrastructure contribution. The paper introduces formal-imo (258 historical IMO problems in Lean) and corrects miniF2F, giving the community higher-quality evaluation testbeds.

Core Methods

  • Lean RL environment: theorem proving cast as a sequential decision process — states are Lean tactic states, actions are tactic strings, rewards are −1 per tactic (incentivizing short proofs). AND-OR tree structure handles multi-subgoal decomposition; every proof is kernel-verified for soundness.
  • Proof network (3B-parameter encoder–decoder transformer): takes the Lean tactic state as input; outputs a policy (top-K tactics) and a value function (estimated remaining proof length). Architecture similar to T5/PaLM-style encoder–decoder with a categorical value head.
  • AlphaZero-inspired tree search: PUCT-based selection, AND-node handling for subgoal decomposition, progressive sampling to broaden exploration along promising paths, single search tree retained across an entire proof attempt without intermediate commits.
  • Auto-formalization pipeline: Gemini 1.5 Pro fine-tuned on ~70,000 expert (problem, chain-of-thought, Lean statement) triplets via an iterative STaR-style self-improvement loop; generates ~80 million valid Lean problems from ~1 million natural-language sources.
  • Main RL (AlphaZero loop): distributed actor–learner architecture with a matchmaker that prioritizes "interesting" problems (mixed success rate) and adaptively allocates simulation budgets. Policy trained on 90% self-generated proofs + 10% Mathlib SFT data; ~80,000 TPU days.
  • Test-time RL (TTRL): at inference, generates hundreds of thousands of Lean variants of the target problem (simplifications, generalizations, lemma proposals, analogies) via Gemini + programmatic methods + evolutionary refinement (up to 15 generations). Runs a focused RL phase on these variants to adapt the generalist agent to the specific target; once the target is solved, TTRL stops.
  • Inference scaling: two complementary axes — (a) scaling tree search budget (from 2 TPU minutes to 12 TPU hours, yielding +10 pp on formal-imo and PutnamBench-test); (b) TTRL (yielding a further +15 pp beyond extensive search). Perfect score on miniF2F-valid at full TTRL budget.

① Apply TTRL's variant-generation curriculum to spatial optimization problems

Extending this paperAlphaProof's TTRL generates hundreds of thousands of formal variants of a hard target problem — simplifications, generalizations, lemma proposals, analogies — to build a problem-specific curriculum that bridges the gap between what the generalist agent can solve and what it needs to learn. The paper is exclusively applied to mathematical theorem proving, but the TTRL framework is domain-agnostic: any problem with a verifiable solver (a feasibility checker, a simulation) could substitute for Lean's kernel. A direct extension is to apply TTRL to combinatorial spatial optimization problems — e.g., facility siting under equity constraints — where exact feasibility can be checked and the "variant" is a perturbation of the problem's geographic or demographic parameters.

Added value to my researchMy EV-charging and primary-care accessibility optimization problems are precisely the kind of constrained combinatorial problems TTRL could attack: generate variants by perturbing demand distributions, road network connectivity, or budget constraints, run focused RL on those variants, and converge on solutions to the original siting problem that resist memorization of a single scenario. This would give my facility-location work an inference-time adaptation mechanism analogous to what AlphaProof uses for P6, unlocking solutions to equity-constrained configurations that standard greedy or branch-and-bound methods cannot reach.

③ Transfer the paper's two-axis inference scaling (search + TTRL) to spatiotemporal prediction models

Extending this paperAlphaProof identifies two orthogonal compute axes at inference: scaling tree search (fast, general, diminishing returns) and TTRL (slow, problem-specific, unlocks qualitatively harder problems). This decomposition — a generalist baseline boosted by problem-specific adaptation — maps naturally onto spatiotemporal modeling. An extension is to cast test-time fine-tuning of a spatial foundation model on the specific city/region being predicted as the "TTRL" axis, and ensemble beam search over geographic scenarios as the "tree search" axis. The two axes would be evaluated jointly to characterize inference-compute trade-offs in spatial prediction tasks.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and MGWR work currently treats each city or region as an independent run with no inference-time adaptation. Borrowing TTRL's logic — generate geographic variants of the target area (adjacent census tracts, analogous cities), run short adaptation RL or fine-tuning on those variants, then evaluate on the target — would let my geospatial foundation model fine-tuning work adapt to out-of-distribution regions without full retraining. This is especially valuable for telehealth digital-divide prediction in data-sparse rural areas.

④ Recast the matchmaker's "interestingness" criterion as an active-learning sampler for GeoAI training

Extending this paperThe paper's matchmaker assigns training problems to actors based on an "interestingness" criterion — problems with a mixed recent success rate (solved some but not all of the last N attempts) get priority, while consistently solved or consistently unsolved problems are deprioritized. This is a form of curriculum RL anchored to zone of proximal development (ZPD) theory. The paper applies it to proof search, but the criterion is general: any binary-outcome learning task with a replay buffer can use the same adaptive curriculum. An extension is to benchmark this matchmaker strategy against standard curriculum learning baselines (SPL, self-paced, SPCL) in GeoAI settings.

Added value to my researchMy multi-source data fusion work (mobile phone, social media, remote sensing) involves training on highly heterogeneous samples: some city–year combinations are data-rich and easy; others are data-sparse and hard. A matchmaker that identifies "interesting" training examples — those at the agent's frontier, neither trivially solved nor persistently intractable — would serve as an active learning sampler for my health-geography prediction models, concentrating training compute on the examples that actually drive generalization improvement in underserved spatial contexts.

⑤ Leverage AlphaProof's AND-OR tree search for hierarchical spatial reasoning

Extending this paperAlphaProof's AND-OR tree structure handles theorem subgoal decomposition: an AND node requires all subgoals to be solved (the return is the minimum over branches — incentivizing balanced difficulty splitting). This is structurally identical to hierarchical spatial planning problems where a global accessibility objective decomposes into independent regional sub-problems that must all be satisfied simultaneously. A paper extension is to apply AlphaProof's AND-OR tree search, with its PUCT formula prioritizing the hardest unsolved subgoal, to hierarchical facility siting — decompose a national-scale coverage problem into regional AND-nodes, solve each with a focused agent, and backpropagate the binding constraint.

Added value to my researchMy network community detection and agent-based modeling work already reasons about hierarchical spatial structures (communities within cities, cities within regions). Importing AlphaProof's AND-OR search architecture into a spatial-planning agent would let me model multi-level facility planning (e.g., primary care deserts at the census-tract level, county level, and state level simultaneously) as a single tree search where the hardest under-served geography drives the global allocation — a principled, compute-scalable alternative to my current sequential optimization approach.
