Machine learning for data-centric epidemic forecasting

Alexander Rodríguez · Harshavardhan Kamarthi · Pulak Agarwal · Javen Ho · Mira Patel · Suchet Sapre · B. Aditya Prakash Published 2024-09-27 DOI 10.1038/s42256-024-00895-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The COVID-19 pandemic catalyzed a data-centric revolution in epidemic forecasting: new digital streams — mobility records, online symptom surveys, wastewater RNA, genomic data, retail signals — now complement traditional clinical surveillance, enabling more expressive and timely models. A taxonomy of methods spanning statistical, deep learning, and hybrid mechanistic–AI approaches reveals that no single model dominates across all settings; ensemble methods consistently outperform individual approaches. Persistent challenges — data quality, calibrated uncertainty, real-world deployment, and bridging forecasts to actionable decisions — define the open frontier for the field.

Core Methods

  • Conceptual pipeline taxonomy: three-stage framework — (1) data processing and target definition, (2) model training and validation, (3) utilization and decision-making — providing a unifying scaffold for the literature.
  • Classical statistical and ML methods: autoregressive models (ARIMA variants), nearest-neighbour regression, elastic nets, multi-task Gaussian processes, dynamic Poisson autoregression, minimum-description-length methods; text-based methods (word embeddings, topic models, Twitter-to-SIR stage mapping).
  • Deep learning methods: recurrent neural networks (LSTM/GRU) and transformers for temporal modelling; graph neural networks (GNNs) for spatiotemporal propagation incorporating mobility-flow graphs; clustering-based architectures for temporal-similarity transfer; transfer learning and meta-learning for data-scarce emerging pathogens.
  • Probabilistic / uncertainty quantification: kernel density estimation, delta density, Gaussian processes, non-parametric deep generative models learning forecast distributions as probabilistic combinations of historical segments; proper scoring rules (weighted interval score, log score).
  • Hybrid mechanistic–ML models: data assimilation (ensemble Kalman filters integrating digital streams into SIR/SEIR compartmental models); differentiable simulators enabling gradient-based parameter calibration; discrepancy modelling (ML corrects mechanistic residuals); physics-informed neural networks (EINNs); tensor factorization with epidemiological regularization (STELAR).
  • Ensemble / wisdom-of-crowds methods: equally weighted average ensembles, adaptive weighting, representative clustering, Bayesian model averaging, prediction markets, expert elicitation — consistently the best-performing operational approach across FluSight, COVID-19 Forecast Hub, and Ebola challenges.

① Spatially explicit epidemic forecasting with health-accessibility as a structural covariate

Extending this paperThe review identifies spatial dynamics and hierarchical relationships as a key open challenge, and notes that demographic and healthcare-vulnerability features have been used as static covariates in some models. However, the paper does not leverage healthcare facility accessibility (how quickly people can reach a clinic) as a structured spatial prior that modulates both exposure and care-seeking behavior. Extending this paper: incorporate 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility scores for primary care and testing facilities as dynamic graph-node features in a GNN epidemic model — operationalizing the paper's call for "more principled methods using spatial dynamics and hierarchical relationships."

Added value to my researchMy R2SFCA and 2SFCA pipelines produce spatial accessibility surfaces for healthcare facilities at fine resolution. Encoding these as node attributes in a graph-based epidemic forecasting model (the GNN architectures reviewed in this paper) would create a bidirectional linkage: accessibility shapes epidemic spread, and epidemic burden reveals accessibility shortfalls. This directly bridges my health-geography and GeoAI agendas, and could feed into telehealth/digital-divide analyses by distinguishing communities with vs. without physical fallback access during surges.

② Physics-informed graph learning for multi-pathogen spatiotemporal co-dynamics

Extending this paperThe review covers hybrid physics-informed neural networks (EINNs) for single-pathogen dynamics but does not address the co-circulation of multiple respiratory pathogens — a setting explicitly emerging in post-COVID years where influenza, RSV, SARS-CoV-2, and other viruses compete for the same susceptible population. Extending this paper: build a multi-pathogen physics-informed GNN where each compartmental model (SIR/SEIR per pathogen) acts as a constraint on the latent dynamics, and shared mobility-graph structure captures cross-pathogen spatial coupling — as called for in the paper's "multi-scale behavioural models" and "phylodynamics" sections.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly onto my physics-informed graph-learning research agenda: the SIR/SEIR ordinary differential equations serve as the "physics prior," and the GNN learns residual cross-pathogen interactions that the mechanistic model cannot capture. The approach also provides a natural test bed for my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation methods to characterize uncertainty in multi-pathogen spread surfaces across urban agglomerations.

③ Machine-learned mobility maps as transferable epidemic features

Extending this paperThe review discusses mobility data extensively as a covariate but treats mobility networks as given — they are sourced from GPS traces, cell towers, or Google Mobility Reports and inserted directly into models. However, the paper explicitly calls for transfer learning approaches that generalize across pathogens and geographies. Extending it: learn a canonical mobility representation — a latent graph embedding of human movement patterns — from historical inter-epidemic periods, then adapt it via meta-learning to new pathogen emergence settings where mobility data may be unavailable or biased, closing the paper's gap on "limited data" settings for emerging pandemics.

Added value to my researchMy work on multi-source data fusion (mobile phone, GPS trajectories, social media) and spatiotemporal analysis positions me to build these transferable mobility embeddings. A learned mobility graph that generalizes across cities would also enrich my job-housing network and urban agglomeration analyses by providing a principled, epidemic-aware mobility prior — connecting urban analytics and public health forecasting in a shared representational framework.

④ Explainable ML for feature attribution in multi-source epidemic models

Extending this paperThe review identifies explainability as a critical open challenge for public health decision-making: deep learning models incorporating mobility, surveys, wastewater, and genomics achieve better predictive accuracy but offer little intuition about which signals matter when and where. The paper calls for methods that "bridge forecasts and decision-making" and notes linear/mechanistic models as the current explainability baseline. Extending this paper: apply spatially-aware Shapley-value attribution (spatial SHAP) to multi-source GNN epidemic models to produce county-level feature-importance maps — which data stream (mobility vs. wastewater vs. social media) drove each forecast, varying across space and epidemic phase.

Added value to my researchExplainable ML for spatial prediction is a core method in my toolkit (MGWR, GWR, ML ensembles with SHAP). Applying spatial SHAP to epidemic forecasting models would generalize my explainability pipeline from static health-geography outcomes (e.g., mental-health accessibility determinants) to dynamic spatiotemporal processes — and would directly produce policy-ready attribution maps that public health agencies can use to decide which surveillance investments have the highest forecast return.

⑤ Linking spatial epidemic forecasts to equity-aware resource allocation

Extending this paperThe review discusses decision-making at strategic and tactical scales — lockdown policies, ventilator allocation — but treats these as downstream consumers of forecasts rather than co-optimized objectives. The paper notes fairness in ML for health as an open challenge. Extending it: couple a GNN epidemic forecaster with an equity-aware facility-location optimization layer that dynamically reallocates testing sites, vaccine distribution centers, or ICU capacity in response to probabilistic forecasts — minimizing expected burden while explicitly penalizing spatial disparities in access (efficiency × equity Pareto optimization informed by real-time predictions).

Added value to my researchThis is the intersection of my EV-charging efficiency-vs-equity optimization work and epidemic forecasting: the same multi-objective facility-location formulation generalizes from electric vehicle infrastructure to public-health resource siting. Forecast uncertainty becomes a risk parameter in the optimization (robust facility location under stochastic demand), directly leveraging the paper's uncertainty-quantification methods and my Monte Carlo simulation expertise. This could yield a generalizable GeoAI decision-support tool for health policy under pandemic conditions.
