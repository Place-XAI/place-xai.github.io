Neighborhood street connectivity and hippocampus volume in older adults

Govinda R. Poudel · Perminder S. Sachdev · Maria V. Soloveva · Miguel A. Molina · Ralf-Dieter Schroers · Wei Li · Luke D. Knibbs · Vibeke Sorensen Catts · Kaarin J. Anstey · Wei Wen · Bin Jalaludin · Henry Brodaty · Ester Cerin Published 2026-03-10 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00408-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

In a longitudinal cohort of 500+ community-dwelling Sydney residents aged >70 with up to three MRI scans over six years, older adults living in more highly interconnected neighborhoods (higher street-intersection density within walkable buffers) had a larger hippocampal tail — a subregion central to spatial navigation. Paradoxically, these same residents also showed a steeper decline in tail volume over time, with a slight rebound after age 85. The association is subregion-specific (tail, not head/body), suggesting connected street networks may engage and shape navigation-related brain structure, and that age-friendly street design could be a lever for brain health.

Why It Matters

  • Links objective urban form to brain structure in ageing. Most built-environment-and-cognition work uses cognitive test scores or self-report; here the outcome is structural MRI of hippocampal subregions, giving a mechanistic, anatomical readout of how the city may touch the brain.
  • Connects "the Knowledge" to the everyday street. Classic studies showed London taxi drivers' navigation expertise enlarges the hippocampus; this asks whether simply living in a navigationally rich (well-connected) neighborhood leaves a measurable trace in older adults.
  • Longitudinal, not cross-sectional. Up to three repeated scans over six years let the authors separate a level effect (larger tail) from a trajectory effect (steeper decline), exposing a non-trivial, non-monotonic story rather than a simple "good/bad" verdict.
  • Actionable for age-friendly design. Street-intersection density is a directly modifiable planning variable (zoning, connectivity standards), so findings feed straight into how cities can be designed to support cognitive ageing.

Core Methods

  • Hippocampal subregion segmentation: Bayesian longitudinal segmentation with subject-specific atlases to quantify head, body and tail volumes from repeated 3T structural MRI.
  • Neighborhood connectivity exposure: compute street-intersection density within walkable network buffers around each geocoded residence using OSMnx; complement with street-network entropy as an orientation-order measure.
  • Level vs. trajectory modeling: linear mixed-effects models estimate both the cross-sectional association (person-level average SID → volume) and the longitudinal association (SID → rate of volume change), including age-related non-linearity (rebound after ~85).
  • Within-person change analysis: model how within-person changes in neighborhood connectivity track with hippocampal trajectory, reducing between-person confounding (Extended Data Tables).
  • Robustness checks: complete-case, outlier-removed, and entropy-based sensitivity analyses test whether the tail-specific effect is stable.

Expanded Ideas — this paper's extensibility & added value to my research

Each idea stays anchored to this paper: first how the paper itself could be extended (drawing on our corpus), then the added value for my research lines (migis.net — GeoAI, 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility, explainable & physics-informed graph learning, spatiotemporal analysis for urban health).

① Move from intersection-count to a graph-native exposure on the street network

Extending this paperThe paper collapses neighborhood form into two scalars — intersection density and orientation entropy — within a walkable buffer. But the street network is literally a graph, and the hippocampus encodes network topology, not raw counts. A direct extension: feed the per-home OSMnx subgraph (with betweenness, choice, meshedness, route complexity) into a graph neural network that predicts hippocampal-tail level and trajectory, testing whether topological richness — not just density — drives the tail effect and its paradoxical decline.

Added value to my researchThis is my physics-informed graph-learning agenda applied to health: a GNN over residential street subgraphs becomes a reusable "neighborhood-form → health-outcome" operator, and SHAP-style attribution tells me which topological features carry the signal — directly transferable to my explainable spatial-prediction work.

Cross-ref (corpus): From the betweenness centrality in street networks to structural invariants in random planar graphs — 10.1038/s41467-018-04978-z

② Disentangle connectivity from exposure: add mobility and realized navigation

Extending this paperThe paper measures residential street form as a static buffer, but the mechanism it invokes — spatial navigation engaging the hippocampal tail — depends on actually moving through connected space. Extending it with GPS trajectories or mobile-phone mobility would let the authors test a dose–response: does realized navigational complexity (route entropy travelled), not just available connectivity, predict tail volume? This could also explain the steeper-decline paradox (high-connectivity residents who stop navigating decline fastest).

Added value to my researchFusing residential form with GPS/mobile-phone trajectories is exactly my multi-source-data-fusion line; it turns a static accessibility surface into a behavior-weighted exposure, and feeds my spatiotemporal-KDE methods for mapping where older adults actually accumulate "navigational dose."

Cross-ref (corpus): Countrywide natural experiment links built environment to physical activity — 10.1038/s41586-025-09321-3

③ Reframe connectivity as an accessibility resource and fold in service access

Extending this paperStreet connectivity is also what makes destinations reachable. The paper treats SID as a navigation proxy but never connects it to access to services (clinics, parks, social venues) that themselves protect cognition. Extending it: layer a 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility surface over the same homes and ask whether connectivity's protective level-effect is partly mediated by better access to health-promoting destinations — a mediation model the current design can support.

Added value to my researchThis bridges the paper into my core spatial-accessibility work: it gives a concrete health-outcome anchor for R2SFCA service-area delineation in an ageing population, and motivates connectivity-aware catchments rather than Euclidean buffers.

Cross-ref (corpus): Visitation patterns reveal service access disparities for ageing populations in the USA — 10.1038/s41562-025-02285-4

④ Control and contrast against co-located environmental exposures

Extending this paperWell-connected neighborhoods often coincide with denser traffic, higher air pollution, and less greenery — plausible drivers of the paradoxical steeper tail decline. The paper adjusts for PM via land-use regression, but a fuller extension would jointly model greenness dose, air pollution, and connectivity as competing/interacting exposures, clarifying whether connectivity helps the brain on net or merely co-travels with harm.

Added value to my researchMulti-exposure modeling with interaction terms is squarely my explainable-ML / MGWR territory: it lets me map where in the city connectivity is net-protective vs net-harmful, supporting place-specific urban-health policy rather than a single global coefficient.

Cross-ref (corpus): A national cohort study (2000–2018) of long-term air pollution exposure and incident dementia in older adults — 10.1038/s41467-021-27049-2

⑤ Scale the single-city finding into a transferable, generalizable model

Extending this paperThe result is from one city (Sydney); its policy promise ("design age-friendly street networks") requires transferability. A natural extension: pool comparable cohorts (e.g., AIBL, PATH, international ageing studies) and fit a multi-city hierarchical / domain-adaptive model, testing whether the tail-specific connectivity effect and its late-age rebound replicate across urban morphologies and demographics — exactly the universality test the paper's claim needs.

Added value to my researchThis matches my interest in fine-tuning geospatial foundation models and FAIR/reproducible multi-cohort analysis: a connectivity-to-brain-health model that domain-adapts across cities is a candidate for a transferable GeoAI urban-health operator, with uncertainty reported per city.
