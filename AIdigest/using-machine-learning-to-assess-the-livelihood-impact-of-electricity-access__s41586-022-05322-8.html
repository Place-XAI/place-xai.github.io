Using machine learning to assess the livelihood impact of electricity access

Nathan Ratledge · Gabe Cadamuro · Brandon de la Cuesta · Matthieu Stigler · Marshall Burke Published 2022-11-16 DOI 10.1038/s41586-022-05322-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Combining satellite imagery, computer vision, and ML-based causal inference yields reliable village-level livelihood measurements in data-sparse environments. Applied to Uganda's electrical grid expansion (2011–2012), the method estimates that grid access improves rural asset wealth by up to 0.15 standard deviations — more than doubling the growth rate relative to unelectrified areas. The approach is low-cost and generalizable to future infrastructure policy evaluation across sub-Saharan Africa and beyond, addressing a long-standing data gap in development economics.

Why It Matters

  • Solves the data-gap problem at country scale. In low-income settings, national statistics are infrequent and coarse; this work shows how satellite imagery can substitute for expensive surveys, generating annual village-level wealth measurements across all of Uganda from publicly available inputs alone.
  • Advances credible causal identification. Matrix completion (MC) and synthetic control with elastic net (SC-EN) outperform difference-in-differences when pre-treatment trends are heterogeneous — a setting pervasive in development contexts — validating ML as a more reliable quasi-experimental tool.
  • Delivers a country-scale treatment-effect estimate with policy relevance. Electrification roughly doubles the village-level wealth growth rate, providing direct evidence that grid infrastructure investment yields measurable livelihood returns, informing large-scale programs like the World Bank's Energy Sector Management Assistance Program.
  • Generalizable pipeline. The CNN + bias-correction + ML-inference workflow is explicitly designed for transfer to other infrastructure shocks (roads, water, telecom) and other countries in sub-Saharan Africa with similar data scarcity.

Core Methods

  • Convolutional neural network (CNN) for wealth prediction: a ResNet-style CNN trained on DHS asset wealth indices from 25 SSA countries (640,000+ households, 27,174 villages) predicts community-level asset wealth annually from satellite imagery. A custom quintile-bias penalty (λb) corrects prediction bias across the wealth distribution, trading modest overall R² for a bias-corrected slope coefficient near 1.
  • Asset wealth index construction: a composite index is built from a battery of household-level DHS asset variables (appliances, building materials, ownership items); robustness checks show alternative index specifications are highly correlated (Extended Data Figs. 3–4).
  • Causal identification — Matrix Completion (MC): treats the counterfactual of each electrified village as a missing entry in a panel matrix and recovers it via low-rank matrix factorization. Simulation experiments confirm MC is less biased than DiD when treatment-correlated pre-trends exist (Extended Data Fig. 7).
  • Causal identification — Synthetic Control with Elastic Net (SC-EN): constructs a synthetic control for each treated village as a regularized linear combination of control units. Cross-validation confirms SC-EN and MC recover held-out control values with mean absolute error of approximately 0.017–0.018 (Extended Data Fig. 6).
  • Difference-in-Differences (DiD) benchmark: used as a traditional comparison; the paper shows pre-trend violations inflate false-positive rates, motivating ML alternatives.
  • Falsification tests: road investment and cellular coverage are shown to be uncorrelated with grid expansion timing and location, ruling out confounding co-investments (Extended Data Figs. 10–11).
  • Bootstrap confidence intervals: all treatment effect estimates are based on 100 bootstrapped model runs; 2-km, 3-km, and 4-km buffer robustness checks confirm stability.

① Transfer the CNN wealth pipeline to urban healthcare accessibility measurement

Extending this paperThe paper's CNN is trained on rural DHS clusters in SSA and optimized to predict village-level asset wealth; it was not designed for informal urban settlements where the built environment is heterogeneous at much finer spatial scales. A direct extension: retrain the same bias-penalized CNN architecture on DHS clusters in African cities — particularly informal settlements now mapped in detail (see cross-ref below) — to generate annual, sub-district wealth estimates. This would fill the urban data gap the paper's authors note as a limitation, and the quadrant-bias correction trick generalizes to any skewed urban wealth distribution.

Added value to my researchThis CNN wealth pipeline would serve as a demand-surface generator for my 2SFCA/R2SFCA models: instead of relying on census-based population counts (often outdated in fast-growing African cities), satellite-derived wealth estimates would weight health-facility demand by socioeconomic vulnerability — producing a need-adjusted accessibility score directly relevant to my health geography work on telehealth and primary care deserts.

② Extend from grid-line buffers to a full spatial accessibility index of electricity as a health determinant

Extending this paperThe paper's treatment assignment is a binary indicator (within/outside a 2 km grid buffer), collapsing continuous distance-to-grid into a threshold. A richer extension: compute a continuous electricity accessibility score using the paper's own infrastructure GIS data — analogous to a 2SFCA score where "supply" is grid capacity and "demand" is household count — and model the dose-response relationship between this score and CNN-predicted wealth. This would recover heterogeneity in treatment intensity that the binary instrument discards, and the paper's cross-validation framework already supports such a graded outcome model.

Added value to my researchThis directly imports my R2SFCA service-area delineation toolkit into energy access research: distance-decay functions from my healthcare accessibility models (Gaussian, kernel-density) are immediately applicable to electricity coverage, and the resulting continuous access index would replace the arbitrary buffer threshold with a theoretically grounded spatial measure — an idea transferable to any infrastructure-access study.

③ Apply explainable ML to audit and attribute the CNN's wealth predictions spatially

Extending this paperThe paper's CNN is a black-box predictor: it reports aggregate R² and slope coefficients but does not identify which visual features — building density, rooftop materials, road surface quality, agricultural land use — drive wealth predictions in different spatial regimes. Extending it: apply SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) or GradCAM attribution maps to the trained CNN to decompose predictions village by village. This would validate the model's spatial logic (does it "look at" meaningful development proxies?), reveal where the model fails (e.g., dense informal settlements vs. dispersed rural areas), and pinpoint which satellite features mediate the electrification-wealth pathway.

Attribution maps would also inform feature engineering for spatiotemporal kernel density models of infrastructure access — a methodological bridge between remote-sensing-based wealth prediction and my spatiotemporal analysis toolkit.

④ Replace the panel-matrix counterfactual with a physics-informed graph model of infrastructure diffusion

Extending this paperThe paper's MC and SC-EN estimators treat villages as independent time series arranged in a matrix, learning counterfactuals from statistical similarity. This ignores the network topology of the grid: electricity diffuses along transmission lines, so nearby villages are correlated through a physical graph structure. An extension: encode the grid network as a graph and learn a physics-informed GNN counterfactual model where the message-passing operator is constrained by Kirchhoff's voltage law analogs — ensuring that wealth predictions respect the electrical network's spatial propagation structure. This would improve counterfactual accuracy, particularly for villages just outside the 2 km buffer where spillovers are plausible.

Added value to my researchThis is precisely the kind of physics-informed graph learning architecture I am developing for spatiotemporal prediction of health outcomes: the infrastructure network encodes a physical prior that regularizes the GNN, analogous to how road-network topology constrains my graph-learning models for job-housing accessibility. A Uganda electricity GNN would be a compelling proof-of-concept for my broader physics-informed GeoAI agenda.

⑤ Scale the causal pipeline to multi-country infrastructure evaluation using multi-source data fusion

Extending this paperThe paper validates its method on Uganda and gestures at generalizability, but the actual estimation is Uganda-specific: the grid GIS data, the DHS survey rounds, and the buffer thresholds are all Uganda-tailored. A systematic extension: assemble a multi-country panel covering all SSA countries with completed rural electrification programs — drawing on the same DHS corpus (27,174 villages, 25 countries already in the training set) — and run a pooled MC/SC-EN meta-analysis with country-fixed counterfactuals. This would answer whether the 0.15 s.d. estimate is Uganda-specific or representative of SSA electrification in general, and identify which country-level moderators (baseline access rate, grid topology, urbanization) amplify or attenuate the effect.

Added value to my researchThis matches my multi-source data fusion and open science/FAIR research directions: combining DHS, satellite, and GIS grid data across 25 countries requires exactly the kind of harmonized, reproducible pipeline I develop. The country-level moderator analysis would deploy my MGWR/GWR spatially varying coefficient models — asking whether electrification effects are spatially stationary or vary with local context, a question directly transferable to healthcare infrastructure investments across administrative units.
