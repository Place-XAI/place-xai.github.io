Affordable public housing and intergenerational mobility

Sumit Agarwal · Yi Fan · Wenlan Qian · Tien Foo Sing Published 2026-01-08 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00375-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using Singapore's large-scale public housing program as a quasi-natural experiment, the paper shows that affordable public housing raises intergenerational mobility for lower-socioeconomic-status families. Across 147,560 parent–child pairs (1995–2018), housing-consumption mobility splits into three regimes: upward mobility for the bottom-50 percentile, high persistence at the top-20 ranks, and downward mobility for the rest. A difference-in-differences design built on the public-housing supply shock finds that children whose parents benefited from the program are 9.5% more likely to surpass their parents in housing status — because affordable housing relaxes budget constraints and frees parents to invest more in children's human capital.

Why It Matters

  • Names a new policy lever for mobility. Most mobility research treats schooling, neighborhoods, or income transfers as the levers; this paper identifies affordable housing supply itself as a causal pathway to upward mobility.
  • Moves from correlation to causation. By exploiting a large public-housing supply shock, it delivers a credible difference-in-differences estimate rather than the usual rank–rank correlations that plague the mobility literature.
  • Quantifies the mechanism. It traces the effect through a concrete channel — relaxed housing-consumption budget constraints enabling higher educational expenditure — connecting a built-environment policy to human-capital investment.
  • Speaks to a global affordability crisis. With housing unaffordability rising worldwide, evidence that broad-based affordable housing can lift the bottom half offers a transferable template for cities far beyond Singapore.

Core Methods

  • Intergenerational rank–rank mobility: match parents and children and place each on a housing-consumption percentile rank, then estimate the rank–rank slope to characterize persistence vs. mobility (Fig. 1, Extended Data Fig. 1).
  • Three-regime decomposition: identify upward mobility (bottom-50), high persistence (top-20), and downward mobility for the middle from the joint density of parent–child ranks.
  • Difference-in-differences (DiD): exploit the BTO public-housing supply shock as a quasi-natural experiment, comparing children in public vs. private housing estates before/after the launch to estimate a causal effect (+9.5% chance of surpassing parents).
  • Event study: trace the dynamic effect of public housing on educational expenditure over event time, isolating the human-capital investment channel (Extended Data Fig. 5).
  • Robustness & placebo: 1,000-iteration permutation placebo randomizing BTO timing (Extended Data Figs. 3–4), sub-sample and robustness DiD checks (Extended Data Tables 1–2).
