Underlying rules of evolutionary urban systems in Africa

Gang Xu · Mengyan Zhu · Bin Chen · Muhammad Salem · Zhibang Xu · Patrick Brandful Cobbinah · Xuecao Li · Neema Simon Sumari · Xiaoling Zhang · Limin Jiao · Peng Gong Published 2025-02-18 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00208-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 1950–2020, Africa's urban systems obey three simple statistical rules under one unified continental city definition. Zipf's law shows population increasingly concentrating in large cities — a shift from dispersed toward centralized city-size distributions. Gibrat's law holds since the 1990s: urban growth has become independent of initial city size. The scaling law reveals an unexpected super-linear relationship between built-up area and population (lower land-use efficiency in large cities, especially in Eastern and Western Africa), though the overall decline of scaling exponents over time signals maturing economies of scale.

Why It Matters

  • The next century of urbanization is African. Africa is set to drive global urban growth, yet the long-run evolution of its urban systems has been understudied — this work fills that gap with a 70-year continental record.
  • Three classical laws, one coherent diagnosis. By jointly applying Zipf's, Gibrat's and the urban scaling law on a single consistent dataset, the paper turns scattered descriptive statistics into a structured, comparable diagnosis of urban-system imbalance.
  • A surprise that flips intuition. Built-up area scales super-linearly with population (exponent > 1), meaning large African cities consume disproportionately more land per capita — the opposite of the sub-linear infrastructure economies seen in mature systems elsewhere.
  • Actionable for land and planning policy. Identifying where land-use efficiency is lowest (Eastern and Western Africa) and tracking whether economies of scale are improving gives planners an evidence base for compact-growth and land-management decisions.

Core Methods

  • Unified urban definition: apply one consistent morphological/agglomeration definition (Africapolis) across the continent so city-size distributions are comparable between countries and over time.
  • Zipf's law (rank-size): fit the rank–size distribution of city populations per year and track the Zipf exponent to quantify the shift from dispersed to centralized (top-heavy) distributions.
  • Gibrat's law (law of proportionate growth): test whether a city's population growth rate is statistically independent of its initial size, finding independence holds from the 1990s onward.
  • Integration of Zipf + Gibrat: couple the two laws to expose imbalances in urban-system structure driven by differential growth among small, medium and large cities.
  • Urban scaling law: regress (log) built-up area on (log) population to estimate the scaling exponent β; β > 1 indicates super-linear land consumption. Track β across regions (Eastern/Western/etc.) and over time.
  • Temporal trend analysis: examine the decline of scaling exponents over decades as evidence of improving economies of scale as urban systems mature.

① Explain the super-linear land-scaling residuals, don't just report β

Extending this paperThe paper estimates a single scaling exponent β per region/year and flags Eastern and Western Africa as low-efficiency, but the residuals from the scaling fit — which cities over- or under-consume land relative to their size — are left unexplained. A direct extension: feed each agglomeration's population, growth history, GDP-per-capita and form into an explainable ML model (SHAP-attributed gradient boosting / MGWR) to attribute the built-up scaling residual to interpretable drivers, turning the paper's descriptive β into a diagnostic of why particular cities sprawl.

② From rank-size snapshots to a generative growth equation

Extending this paperThe paper validates Zipf and Gibrat separately as empirical regularities, but never fits a single stochastic process that generates the observed evolving rank-size distribution. Extending it: calibrate a Gibrat-with-migration growth equation (à la the Nature "growth equation of cities") to the 1950–2020 African series, so the centralization trend and Zipf-exponent drift emerge from one mechanistic model that can be projected forward.
