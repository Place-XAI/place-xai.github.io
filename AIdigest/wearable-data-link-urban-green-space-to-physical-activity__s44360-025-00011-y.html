Wearable data link urban green space to physical activity

Yougeng Lu · Markus Reichert · Anne D. Guerry · Quan Yuan · Yingjie Li · Anders Rydström · Tong Wu · Iris Reinhard · Johanna Rehder · Abby C. King · Ann W. Hsing · Kari Nadeau · Chethan Sarabu · Andreas Meyer-Lindenberg · Gretchen Daily · Lisa Mandle Published 2025-11-24 DOI 10.1038/s44360-025-00011-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using objectively measured daily step counts from 7,013 Fitbit users across 53 US metropolitan areas (2017–2019), multilevel modelling reveals that a 10% increase in park accessibility is associated with 107 additional daily steps, whereas overall vegetation greenness (NDVI) shows no significant step-count association. The effect is strongest for elderly, Black, and Latino residents and in western and southern US cities — pointing to an equigenic benefit of accessible parks that could disproportionately help already under-served populations.

★

Why It Matters

  • First nationwide, longitudinal wearable study linking park access to steps. Prior research was mostly cross-sectional, single-city, or relied on self-report; this study spans 53 US cities over up to 3 years of objective device data.
  • Separates park accessibility from general greenness. NDVI (vegetation amount) does not predict step counts — only accessible parks do, clarifying the policy target: it is not "more green" in the abstract but park proximity and walkability that drive behaviour.
  • Quantifies a clinically meaningful signal. Moving from the 25th to the 75th percentile of park accessibility corresponds to ~128 additional daily steps — within thresholds linked to reductions in all-cause and cardiovascular mortality.
  • Exposes geographic and demographic heterogeneity. Western and southern cities show stronger park–step relationships; cities with older, more racially diverse, and less active populations benefit most — directing planners toward where park investment yields greatest returns.
  • Supports an equigenic mechanism. The results align with the principle that green space access can partially offset physical activity disadvantages for under-served groups, grounding equity arguments in large-scale behavioural data.

Core Methods

  • Wearable data quality control: excluded participants with fewer than 180 days of data; removed step-count outliers (<100 or >20,000 steps/day); discarded months with <50% data completeness; applied Median Absolute Deviation (MAD × 3) filtering — resulting in removal of ~6.5% of raw records in total.
  • Park accessibility index: Gaussian distance-decay function (search radius 1 km) weighting park area by distance from each CBG centroid, multiplied by the EPA walkability index of each CBG — then aggregated to three-digit zipcode level.
  • Multilevel modelling (3-level HLM): daily step counts nested within participants (level 2), nested within metropolitan areas (level 3). Fixed effects include park accessibility, NDVI, temperature (linear + quadratic), precipitation (level 1); age, sex, race, education, income, employment (level 2); walkability, population density, AQI (level 3). Random slopes for park accessibility at both individual and city levels.
  • Within-person design: the park accessibility predictor operates at city level (level 3), isolating the between-city effect while the individual random slopes characterize how each person's steps vary with park access — effectively a natural-experiment design.
  • Park–step slope derivation: city-level random slopes extracted from the mixed model to produce per-city park–step effect estimates, then correlated with city demographic and climatic characteristics (Pearson's r) to reveal spatial and sociodemographic heterogeneity.
  • Sensitivity analyses: parallel multilevel models for lightly active, fairly active, and very active minutes to validate step-count findings; intraclass correlation coefficients reported (participant ICC = 0.76; city ICC = 0.02).

① Replace the paper's static CBG-level exposure with individual GPS activity spaces

Extending this paperThe paper acknowledges its core limitation: park accessibility is measured at the three-digit zipcode level, not the individual level — a classic uncertain geographic context problem (UGCoP). The paper's own within-person multilevel design is the right scaffold; what is missing is individual-level GPS traces linking each person to the parks they actually visit. Extending the paper: couple the NIH All of Us Fitbit data with geolocation-enabled wearable records or ecological momentary assessment to construct behaviour-based activity spaces for each participant, then re-estimate the park accessibility–step model at true individual resolution. The corpus paper on countrywide natural experiments and built-environment effects demonstrates the feasibility of nationwide, objective environment–behaviour linkage.

Added value to my researchThis directly maps onto my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation work: individual GPS trajectories can define dynamic catchment areas that replace fixed administrative units in 2SFCA frameworks, producing demand-weighted, behaviour-grounded accessibility scores for parks, clinics, and other public facilities — a pivot from supply-side proximity to realized demand-side access.

② Model the park–step heterogeneity surface with spatially varying coefficient regression

Extending this paperThe paper reports regional heterogeneity in park–step slopes as post-hoc city-level Pearson correlations with temperature and demographics (Figs. 4–5). This is a descriptive step, not a model. A direct extension: treat the city-level park–step slopes as the dependent variable and fit a Geographically Weighted Regression (GWR) or MGWR surface over the 53 cities, with temperature, racial composition, baseline activity, and built-environment density as inputs. This converts the paper's scattered r-values into a continuous, spatially smooth predictive surface of "where parks matter most," and reveals whether the western/southern enhancement is a regional climate effect, a land-use effect, or a sociodemographic residual — separating mechanisms the paper currently conflates.

Added value to my researchMGWR/GWR is one of my core methods. Applying it to park–step slope data from this paper creates a concrete calibration dataset: a national map of "park-activity responsiveness" that can inform adaptive bandwidth selection and benchmark explainable-ML spatial prediction models I develop for public health and facility siting.

③ Extend the equigenic finding into a park-equity optimization model

Extending this paperThe paper's equigenic observation — that cities with older, non-White, and less active populations show stronger park–step slopes — is framed as a descriptive epidemiological finding. It can be converted into a spatial optimization problem: given a fixed budget for new park construction or accessibility improvements, where should they be located to maximize total step-count gain subject to equity constraints (e.g., prioritizing CBGs with high proportions of elderly or Black and Latino residents)? The paper's own park accessibility formula (equation 4) and Gaussian decay model provide the exact objective function. Adding a second objective (equity gain per dollar) yields a Pareto front of efficiency-versus-equity trade-offs across US cities — a direct policy tool.

Added value to my researchThis is a natural extension of my EV charging-station efficiency-vs-equity optimization work into the domain of green public infrastructure. The same multi-objective spatial optimization framework I use for charging station siting can absorb the paper's park accessibility function as its spatial objective, producing city-specific park investment blueprints with transparent equity–efficiency trade-off curves.

④ Fuse the paper's wearable-step signal with multi-source urban sensing for a GeoAI health observatory

Extending this paperThe paper uses a single wearable data stream (Fitbit steps) and a relatively sparse set of environmental covariates. A richer extension: fuse the All of Us Fitbit longitudinal records with social media check-ins at parks (revealed preferences), mobile-phone mobility traces (actual park visitation rates), remote-sensing green-space change (NDVI time series from Sentinel-2 instead of NOAA AVHRR at 0.05°), and street-view walkability scores derived from computer vision — then train an explainable ensemble model to predict step-count responses to park changes across US cities. This converts the paper's fixed multilevel model into an adaptive GeoAI health observatory.

⑤ Generalize the distance-decay park accessibility model to healthcare facility access

Extending this paperThe paper's park accessibility index (equation 4) is a Gaussian-decay weighted sum of facility area and walkability — functionally identical in structure to a Two-Step Floating Catchment Area (2SFCA) supply score. The paper validates this model at population scale using objective behavioural outcomes (step counts), providing empirical calibration for the decay parameter d₀ = 1 km under walking-mode assumptions. Extending it: use the paper's empirically validated decay function as a prior for healthcare facility accessibility models, replacing the typically assumed Gaussian or inverse-power decay in 2SFCA with a behaviorally grounded form derived from park-visit distance patterns across diverse urban contexts.

Added value to my researchBehaviorally calibrated distance-decay is the key missing ingredient in my R2SFCA and 2SFCA work for primary care, mental health, and telehealth accessibility. Importing the paper's empirically estimated decay — and extending it to transport-mode-specific variations across urban density gradients — would directly sharpen my healthcare accessibility models and the spatial equity analyses built on them, particularly for populations (elderly, low-income, racial minorities) who share the same demographic profile as those showing the strongest park–step slopes in this study.
