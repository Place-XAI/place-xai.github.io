Global spatial assessment of potential for new peri-urban forests to combat climate change

Saverio Francini · Gherardo Chirici · Leonardo Chiesi · Paolo Costa · Guido Caldarelli · Stefano Mancuso Published 2024-03-27 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00049-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Globally, 141–322 Mha of land around cities are potentially available for tree restoration. Mapped at 500 m resolution, these peri-urban areas could host between 34 and 241 billion trees depending on land-availability scenario (and on whether croplands are excluded). Crucially, almost 80% of that capacity is concentrated in just 20 countries. Peri-urban forests are no substitute for cutting emissions, but they offer a spatially explicit, decision-ready lever for carbon removal plus co-benefits (cooler cities, cleaner air, reduced heat islands).

Core Methods

  • Peri-urban delineation: use GAIA impervious-area time series to identify cities and define their surrounding peri-urban belt, the geographic frame for the whole analysis.
  • Multi-layer suitability screen: overlay land cover (WorldCover), potential natural vegetation/biome (PNV), and an existing-forest mask (PALSAR FNF) to retain only land that could plausibly support new forest.
  • Three land-availability scenarios: from permissive to restrictive land assumptions, bracketing the 141–322 Mha range; a separate variant excludes current croplands to protect food security.
  • Tree-count estimation: translate available area into potential tree numbers (241→106 billion; 101→34 billion without croplands) and aggregate to country level (~80% in 20 countries).
  • Accuracy assessment & policy comparison: validate predicted restorable areas (Supplementary Data 1) and benchmark results against Bonn Challenge national commitments (Supplementary Data 2).
  • Reproducible cloud pipeline: implemented end-to-end in Google Earth Engine producing a 500 m global map and downloadable intermediate products.

① Turn the static restoration map into a health-co-benefit accessibility surface

Extending this paperThe paper maps where peri-urban forests could go and counts trees, but stops short of asking who would actually benefit. A direct extension: overlay the 500 m restoration-potential layer with gridded population and convert each candidate site into a service-area / accessibility score for green-space exposure — so the map ranks sites not only by carbon but by the number of residents brought within walking distance of a new forest, exactly the cooling/air-quality co-benefits the paper invokes.

Extending this paperThe paper's pipeline is a deterministic suitability screen (land cover ∩ biome ∩ non-forest). Extending it: train an explainable ML ensemble on the multi-source rasters to predict per-cell survival probability and expected co-benefit, with SHAP-style attribution exposing which environmental drivers govern peri-urban restoration potential.
