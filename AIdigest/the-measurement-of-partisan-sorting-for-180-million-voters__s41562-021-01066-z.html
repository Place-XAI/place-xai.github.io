The measurement of partisan sorting for 180 million voters

Jacob R. Brown · Ryan D. Enos Published 2021-03-08 DOI 10.1038/s41562-021-01066-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using the exact residential addresses of 180+ million registered US voters and a novel spatially weighted exposure measure, this study demonstrates that partisan segregation in the United States is pervasive and severe: a large proportion of voters live with virtually no residential exposure to members of the opposing party. High partisan isolation exists across all densities and place types, is distinct from racial and ethnic segregation, and persists even when Democrats and Republicans reside in the same city or neighbourhood — evidence that residential sorting by party operates at the finest geographic scales.

★

Why It Matters

  • First individual-level national measurement of partisan segregation. Prior measures relied on aggregate geographic units; this is the first spatially weighted, individual-level accounting for every voter in the US — a methodological breakthrough in segregation science.
  • Quantifies the scope of partisan bubbles. Nationwide, 10% of Democrats have a cross-party residential exposure below 0.05 (fewer than 5 in 100 neighbours from the other party); a majority of both Democrats and Republicans exceed the 0.60 isolation threshold commonly used to identify severe racial segregation.
  • Demonstrates sorting within neighbourhoods, not just cities. Even after conditioning on same census tract, partisan sorting produces 11-percentage-point differences in partisan environment — ruling out the urban–rural divide as a sole explanation.
  • Disentangles partisan from racial segregation. For non-Hispanic white voters, partisan sorting is independent of racial sorting; for minority voters, the overlap of racial and partisan segregation compounds isolation further.
  • Implications for democratic governance. Extreme residential partisan isolation restricts exposure to competing political viewpoints, may amplify affective polarization, shape campaign strategy, and contribute to geographically biased electoral representation.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Primary dataset
L2 commercial voter file — 180,735,645 registered voters in the United States as of June 2018, obtained from L2 (a commercial data vendor). After geocoding, 180,660,202 records were retained. L2 aggregates voter registration data from all 50 state governments.
Partisanship
Recorded at registration in 30 states + DC; imputed for the remaining ~50% of voters using (1) most recent partisan primary ballot, (2) third-party ideological alignment, (3) Bayesian imputation using 2016 precinct-level presidential vote share and individual demographics. Validated via a survey of 12,221 voters (77% accuracy).
Geography & routing
Voter residence latitude/longitude geocoded by L2. Geographic units (state, CBSA, county, city/town, ZIP code, census tract) drawn from US Census Bureau 2010+ definitions. Precincts from MIT Election Lab (electionlab.mit.edu) and individual state election administrations.
Spatial processing
Geohash (z-order curve) spatial indexing; k-nearest-neighbour analysis (k=1,000) run on Amazon Web Services across ten Postgres/PostGIS instances developed with Harvard's Center for Geographic Analysis. Total compute: ~4 weeks continuous (AWS), plus 200+ CPU-hours for weighted averaging in R.
Replication data
Anonymized replication data and all code available at Harvard Dataverse doi.org/10.7910/DVN/A40X5L.
Density classification
CityLab density schema (D. H. Montgomery), applied to US Census Tract population per square mile. Four tiers: high, medium, low, very low.
Time window
Voter file as of June 2018; precinct-level presidential vote share from 2016 general election.

Core Methods

  • Geohash spatial indexing: latitude/longitude coordinates encoded as z-order strings enabling efficient k-nearest-neighbour (k=1,000) retrieval across 180 million points using PostGIS on AWS.
  • Spatially weighted exposure/isolation index: for each voter, a distance-inverse-weighted average of neighbours' partisan probability across the 1,000 nearest residential neighbours — weight = 1/(d+1) where d is geodesic distance in metres.
  • Partisanship imputation (three-step Bayesian): (1) primary ballot history; (2) third-party ideological coding; (3) Bayesian posterior from 2016 precinct vote share + demographics. Validated via Brier score on 12,221 survey respondents.
  • Relative exposure index: within any geographic unit, the difference between a party's average exposure to the out-party and the out-party's average isolation — quantifies sorting within places beyond compositional effects.
  • Multi-scale analysis: measures computed and aggregated at state, CBSA, county, city/town, ZIP code, and census-tract levels without rerunning the underlying individual calculations.
  • Racial–partisan decomposition: spatial exposure recomputed restricting neighbours to same racial/ethnic group, comparing with all-neighbour exposure to isolate partisan sorting net of racial sorting.
  • Robustness checks: sensitivity to k (k=50,000 random sample), distance-weight exponent a, constant c, and discrete vs. probabilistic partisan assignment (Supplementary Information, 27 figures, 20 tables).

① Extend the spatially weighted exposure index to experienced (mobility-based) partisan segregation

Extending this paperThis paper measures residential partisan segregation using fixed home addresses. A direct extension of the paper: replace static residential locations with GPS-traced daily mobility trajectories, computing a time-weighted, activity-context-aware version of the same distance-inverse exposure index at workplaces, transit stops, and commercial zones. This would reveal whether the residential partisan bubble persists in daily activity spaces — or whether people escape it at work or in consumption environments — and would connect the paper's spatial framework to emerging work on experienced urban segregation.

Added value to my researchThe mobility-based exposure index is structurally analogous to the 2SFCA/R2SFCA demand-side catchment: both aggregate a weighted sum of group-member encounters across a spatial kernel. Replacing the static residential kernel with a spatiotemporal, GPS-derived kernel translates directly into a dynamic service-area delineation approach — where the "supply" is cross-party exposure and the "demand" is daily activity. This mobility extension feeds my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation methods and multi-source data fusion (GPS + social media) pipelines.

④ Model partisan sorting dynamics with an agent-based or spatial interaction model

Extending this paperThis paper's cross-sectional snapshot confirms that partisan sorting exists at fine geographic scales, but explicitly flags that it cannot capture the dynamics of how sorting evolves. A natural extension: use the paper's individual exposure scores as ground-truth calibration data for an agent-based model (ABM) or a spatial interaction model in which agents make residential choices conditioned on the partisan composition of candidate neighbourhoods (Schelling-type dynamics with empirically calibrated thresholds). The paper itself notes that even small initial clustering can drive extreme segregation through a positive feedback mechanism.

⑤ Transfer the spatially weighted individual exposure method to measure multi-dimensional urban diversity

Extending this paperThe paper's spatially weighted exposure index is formally general: it measures any group-membership-based exposure across k nearest residential neighbours. An extension of the paper: apply the same individual-level k-NN framework to multi-dimensional urban diversity — computing simultaneous exposure scores across income, race, age, and political identity layers in a single computation, then constructing a composite spatial diversity index. This would generalise the paper's partisan measure into a multi-attribute segregation tensor, enabling researchers to ask which dimensions of diversity co-cluster or trade off in residential space.
