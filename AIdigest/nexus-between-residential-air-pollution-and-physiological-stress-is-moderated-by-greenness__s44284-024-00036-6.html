Nexus between residential air pollution and physiological stress is moderated by greenness

Ka Yan Lai · Sarika Kumari · John Gallacher · Chris Webster · Chinmoy Sarkar Published 2024-02-16 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00036-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

In a UK-wide cohort of 190,200 adults, higher residential air pollution was associated with higher composite physiological stress (CPS), while higher residential greenness lowered it. People in the "double-impacted" group (high pollution + low greenness) had 13–22% higher odds of being in the worst CPS quartile than the least-impacted group, with synergistic interactions between coarse particulates / NOx and greenness. Effects varied considerably between cities — pointing to nature-based interventions (optimizing urban greenness) as a lever for healthier, lower-stress cities.

Why It Matters

  • Measures stress biologically, not by survey. CPS is built from 13 biomarkers across three physiological functions and two organs (an allostatic-load-style index), moving "stress" from self-report to a multi-system, objective readout.
  • Tests a modifier, not just a main effect. Most work studies air pollution or greenness in isolation; this quantifies how greenness buffers pollution-linked stress, including synergistic interactions (PM10, PM2.5–10, NOx × greenness) on the risk of high CPS.
  • Names a vulnerable population. The "double-impacted" group (high pollution + low greenness) carries measurably higher physiological stress — a concrete equity target for planners.
  • Translates to policy. Considerable between-city variability plus a green-buffering effect support nature-based interventions and equitable greenspace provision as a population-health strategy.

Core Methods

  • Composite physiological stress index: aggregate 13 biomarkers into CPS (and a 9-biomarker allostatic-load variant) to capture multi-system wear-and-tear objectively.
  • Non-linear exposure–response: restricted cubic splines (Harrell's knots at the 10th/50th/90th percentiles) for air pollutants and NDVI greenness vs CPS (Fig. 2, Extended Data Fig. 2).
  • Joint / interaction analysis: stratify into 9 groups by air pollutant (Q1, Q2–Q4, Q5) × NDVI greenness (Q1, Q2–Q4, Q5); reference = low-pollution/high-greenness; logistic regression for odds of CPS in the 4th quartile (Figs. 3, 5).
  • Additive & multiplicative interaction: RERI (relative excess risk due to interaction) for additive synergy; P-interaction for multiplicative effects.
  • Confounder adjustment: sex, age, education, employment, income, healthy-lifestyle score, sleep, noise, urbanicity, stressful life events, and biological age (telomere-length residual adjusted for comorbidities).
  • City-cluster stratification: repeat associations by UK Biobank city cluster to expose between-city heterogeneity (Figs. 4–5).

① Turn the between-city heterogeneity into an explainable spatial model

Extending this paperThe paper reports "considerable between-city variability" in the pollution–greenness–CPS nexus but treats city clusters as stratified subgroups, leaving the drivers of that variability unexplained. A direct extension: model CPS risk with MGWR / explainable ML (e.g., gradient boosting + SHAP) so the greenness-buffering coefficient is allowed to vary continuously over space, and attribute why some cities show stronger buffering (greenspace type, density, deprivation) instead of just reporting that they differ.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my explainable-spatial-prediction agenda: a spatially varying "green-buffer" surface feeds directly into healthy-city diagnostics and lets me rank neighborhoods by marginal health return on added greenness.

② Move from residential proximity to mobility-based exposure

Extending this paperExposure here is fixed to the residential address (0.5 m NDVI around home + modeled background pollution), so it misses the pollution and greenness people actually encounter while commuting, working, and recreating. Extending it with GPS trajectories / mobile-phone records would yield activity-space exposure, testing whether the green-buffer effect on CPS strengthens when daily mobility is accounted for.

③ Re-cast the green buffer as a dose–response design target

Extending this paperThe paper shows greenness inversely associated with CPS and uses cubic splines, but stops short of a generalizable dose–response curve that planners could read as "how much greenness, of what type, buys how much stress reduction." Extending it: fit a transferable greenness-dose → CPS-response function (with the greenspace-typology data already in Extended Data Fig. 5) to convert the buffering effect into a planning standard.

Added value to my researchA dose–response target plugs directly into my accessibility framing: I can pose greenspace provision as a 2SFCA-style supply–demand problem where "demand" is stress-burdened, pollution-exposed population and "supply" is dose-effective greenness.

④ Optimize greenness placement for equity, not just average effect

Extending this paperThe "double-impacted" group (high pollution + low greenness) is identified but not targeted: the paper does not ask where new greenness should go to maximally shrink that group. Extending it: pose a spatial optimization that allocates greening interventions to minimize the population in the double-impacted/high-CPS state under a fixed budget — turning the descriptive finding into a prescriptive equity tool.

Added value to my researchThis mirrors my EV-charging efficiency-vs-equity optimization: same machinery (constrained facility/resource placement, Pareto efficiency–equity trade-off) applied to greenspace siting for stress reduction.

⑤ Tighten causal claims and stress-test the pollution–stress link

Extending this paperThe design is cross-sectional and association-based; the synergy claim could be confounded by residential self-selection and unmeasured deprivation. Extending it with negative-control / quasi-experimental designs (and longitudinal CPS where re-measured) would sharpen whether greenness causally moderates pollution-linked physiological stress, especially at exposure levels below regulatory standards.
