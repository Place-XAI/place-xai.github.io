Integrative deep learning of spatial multi-omics with SWITCH

Zhongzhan Li · Sanqing Qu · Haixin Liang · Ruohui Tang · Xudong Zhang · Fan Lu · Jiani Yang · Ziling Gan · Shaorong Gao · Yanping Zhang · Guang Chen Published 2025-10-29 DOI 10.1038/s43588-025-00891-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

SWITCH (Spatially Weighted Multi-omics Integration and Cross-modal Translation with Cycle-mapping Harmonization) is a deep generative model that integrates unpaired spatial multi-omics data and performs cross-modal prediction without requiring paired training data. Its cycle-mapping mechanism generates reliable pseudo-pairs that supply supplementary supervision signals, enabling SWITCH to outperform nine competing methods by up to 59% in overall integration score and achieve more precise spatial domain delineation — including resolving cortical layers in mouse brain at higher resolution than prior methods. The validated cross-modal translations unlock downstream analyses (differential expression, trajectory inference, gene regulatory network reconstruction) from single-modality spatial data.

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the co-profiling bottleneck. Acquiring co-profiled spatial multi-omics data is expensive and technically limited; SWITCH lets researchers infer comprehensive multi-omics landscapes from much cheaper single-modality data already collected.
  • First spatially-aware unpaired integration framework. Existing single-cell integration methods (Seurat, GLUE, MaxFuse, etc.) discard spatial coordinates; SWITCH is the first to leverage spatial proximity graphs across all integration and translation steps, which is decisive for accurately delineating spatial domains.
  • Built-in uncertainty quantification. The probabilistic decoder reports imputation confidence per spot, enabling researchers to flag unreliable predictions rather than treating all imputed values equally — a safety feature missing from most competing tools.
  • Extends to cross-technology scenarios. SWITCH can integrate spatial transcriptomics with dissociated single-nucleus ATAC-seq, effectively transferring spatial context to technologies that inherently lack it, broadening the utility far beyond co-profiled datasets.
  • Fully open and reproducible. Source code and Jupyter notebooks are on GitHub (github.com/zzli123/SWITCH); processed datasets are deposited at Zenodo, satisfying FAIR data principles.

Core Methods

  • Spatial proximity graph construction: for each modality, spots are connected based on Euclidean distance below threshold r, forming an undirected neighbor graph (with self-loops) that encodes the spatial microenvironment.
  • Graph Attention Network (GAT) encoder: modality-specific GAT encoders with Leaky ReLU activations and an attention mechanism over spatial neighbors produce low-dimensional latent embeddings as multivariate normal (MVN) distributions; a final linear layer mitigates over-smoothing.
  • Variational reparameterization: latent variables are sampled via reparameterization trick (z = μ + σ ⊙ ε) enabling end-to-end gradient flow through stochastic sampling.
  • Two-stage adversarial alignment: (1) adversarial alignment guided by a biology-informed knowledge graph (GLUE-style guidance graph encoding gene–peak regulatory relationships via genomic proximity); (2) iterative refinement through minimization of discrepancy between pseudo-pair embeddings and original embeddings.
  • Cycle-mapping mechanism: cross-modal translations are re-encoded by the target modality's encoder and decoded back to the source modality to enforce consistency — a self-supervised signal that obviates the need for paired data.
  • Probabilistic decoder: modality-specific decoders generate feature distributions using a Negative Binomial (NB) model, with per-spot library-size scaling, producing uncertainty estimates alongside imputed values.
  • Composite loss function: total loss = reconstruction loss + guidance graph reconstruction loss + adversarial loss + cycle-mapping loss + embedding-alignment loss (cosine similarity) + KL regularization; all components are weighted by tunable hyperparameters.
  • Downstream enabling methods: UMAP for visualization; Monocle3 for trajectory inference; Moran's I and ICC for spatial cluster quality; FOSCTTM / FOSKNN for spot-level alignment accuracy; motif enrichment and peak–gene linking for gene regulatory network inference.

A direct extension would adapt SWITCH to spatiotemporal multi-source human data: treat mobility records and digital health signals as two "unpaired modalities" sharing a spatial coordinate, and learn a cycle-consistent fusion that fills gaps where one signal is missing. The paper's spatial proximity graph and GAT encoder would translate directly; the guidance graph could be replaced by knowledge of behavioral correlations across data types.
