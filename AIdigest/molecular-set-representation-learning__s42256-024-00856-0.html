Molecular set representation learning

Maria Boulougouri · Pierre Vandergheynst · Daniel Probst Published 2024-07-05 DOI 10.1038/s42256-024-00856-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Treating molecules as unordered sets of atom invariants — rather than as explicit graphs — is both a competitive alternative and a powerful extension to graph neural networks (GNNs) for molecular machine learning. The simplest set model (MSR1) matches or surpasses D-MPNN and GIN on 5–9 of 11 MoleculeNet benchmarks without any explicit bond topology, and replacing a GNN's global-pooling layer with a set representation layer (SR-GINE) improves performance across chemistry, biology, and materials science tasks — including pharmacokinetics, protein–ligand binding, and reaction-yield prediction — without additional precomputed descriptors.

★

Why It Matters

  • Challenges the graph-as-default assumption. The paper shows that many molecular benchmarks reward graph-based GNNs not because of topology but because the datasets are insensitive to it — set-only models can match or beat GNNs, suggesting the field may be over-investing in complex graph architectures for certain tasks.
  • Elegant fix for fuzzy chemical bonds. Ionic, metallic, delocalized, and π-stacking interactions cannot be cleanly encoded as edges; the set formulation avoids this problem entirely by dropping explicit connectivity, which turns out to be especially useful for conjugated molecules and protein–ligand complexes.
  • Plug-in upgrade for any existing GNN. Replacing the global-pooling layer with a RepSet layer is a drop-in modification (SR-GINE outperforms GINE in virtually all benchmarks), giving practitioners an immediate performance boost without redesigning their pipeline.
  • Unified framework spanning four task domains. A single paradigm — molecular set representation — is shown to work for quantum-chemical property prediction (QM7/QM8/OCELOT), pharmacokinetics (Biogen ADME), protein–ligand binding affinity (PDBbind), and reaction-yield prediction (Buchwald–Hartwig HTE/ELN).
  • Open, pip-installable codebase. All code and data (except PDBbind, which requires free registration) are MIT-licensed on GitHub (daenuprobst/molsetrep), lowering the barrier for adoption.

Core Methods

  • Molecular set representation: each molecule is encoded as a multiset of one-hot-encoded atom-invariant vectors (133-dimensional, encoding degree, atomic number ≤100, formal charge, hybridization, chiral tag, ring membership, hydrogen count) — no explicit bonds, no graph edges.
  • MSR1 (single-set model): atom-invariant multiset → RepSet permutation-invariant layer → MLP regressor/classifier. Essentially ECFP radius-0 passed through a set network.
  • MSR2 (dual-set model): two parallel RepSet layers for atom and bond invariants respectively; outputs concatenated before MLP.
  • SR-GINE (set-enhanced GNN): standard GINE graph convolution (GINEConv) with global mean-pooling replaced by a RepSet layer — the key plug-in enhancement tested across all benchmarks.
  • SR-BIND (binding-affinity model): dual-set architecture encoding ligand and protein atom subsets within radius r = 5.5 Å of each other, enabling implicit geometric information without 3D coordinates.
  • MSR2-RXN (reaction model): dual-set on ECFP (r=3, 2,048 bits) fingerprint vectors of reactant and product molecule sets.
  • Set representation layer — RepSet: chosen over DeepSets and Set-Transformer based on empirical performance and interpretability; configured with 128 hidden sets × 64 elements each.
  • Benchmark evaluation: Murcko scaffold splits (MoleculeNet), 5-fold CV (OCELOT), predefined splits (Biogen ADME, PDBbind, Buchwald–Hartwig); paired t-tests for significance; MolFormer large language model finetuned as an additional baseline.

① Transfer the set-pooling upgrade from molecules to spatial facility networks

Extending this paperThe paper's central finding — that replacing global-pooling with a RepSet layer in a GNN (SR-GINE) consistently improves performance — was demonstrated on molecular graphs. A direct extension would apply the same plug-in to heterogeneous spatial graphs where node types differ qualitatively (e.g., facility types, population cells, road junctions): rather than mean-pooling over all neighbors during graph convolution, a RepSet layer could learn a permutation-invariant aggregation that is sensitive to the composition of the neighborhood, potentially improving spatial prediction tasks beyond molecular chemistry.

Added value to my researchIn my GeoAI work on 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility modeling, the service-area aggregation step (summing supply over catchment cells) is essentially a fixed-weight global pooling. Replacing it with a learned RepSet-style aggregation over the set of facilities within a catchment could yield a learned, permutation-invariant accessibility integrator — sensitive to the mix of facility types and capacities rather than their mere sum, directly applicable to primary-care or mental-health accessibility delineation.

② Use the set-of-atoms analogy to rethink spatial POI representation

Extending this paperThe paper treats a molecule as a multiset of atoms without requiring explicit bond topology, on the grounds that bonds are often approximate or undefined. An analogous argument applies to urban point-of-interest (POI) ensembles: a neighborhood's "identity" may emerge from the unordered composition of its service types rather than a fixed spatial graph connecting them. Extending this paper: replace the paper's atom invariants with POI category embeddings and apply MSR1/MSR2-style architectures to predict neighborhood-level outcomes (health metrics, mobility demand), treating each neighborhood as a multiset of co-located POIs.

Added value to my researchMy work on urban analytics and healthy cities — especially 15-minute neighborhood scoring and public-facility accessibility — currently encodes neighborhood context through fixed spatial buffers or fixed graph connectivity. A set-based POI representation would be invariant to the order in which POIs are encountered and robust to missing connectivity data (e.g., in rapidly developing cities with incomplete road network data), directly improving spatial prediction in my multi-source data fusion pipelines.

③ Replace the MolFormer baseline with a geospatial foundation model comparison

Extending this paperThe paper's comparison with MolFormer (a large language model pretrained on ~100 million molecules) showed no significant advantage for MolFormer over SR-GINE on recent benchmarks — a notable finding given MolFormer's scale. An extension would replicate this set-vs-pretrained-LLM head-to-head in a spatial domain: compare a RepSet-based neighborhood encoder against a fine-tuned geospatial foundation model (e.g., Urban-FM or GeoCLIP) on urban property prediction tasks, testing whether the same "small-but-well-structured set model" principle holds geographically.

Added value to my researchOne of my active directions is fine-tuning geospatial foundation models. This paper's methodology offers a principled lightweight alternative: before committing to large-model fine-tuning, test whether a RepSet over well-chosen spatial invariants already captures most of the predictive signal — a valuable null hypothesis for my foundation-model benchmarking work and a potential efficiency argument for data-scarce health-geography settings.

④ Adapt SR-BIND's radius-based dual-set to model patient–facility interaction zones

Extending this paperSR-BIND creates two parallel sets — ligand atoms within radius r of the protein, and protein atoms within radius r of the ligand — enabling implicit geometric encoding without 3D coordinates. An extension of this paper would generalize the dual-set radius formulation to arbitrary proximity graphs: instead of atomic radius, use a travel-time or network-distance threshold, producing dual neighborhood sets for any two interacting spatial entities. This turns SR-BIND's insight into a general proximity-aware dual-set encoder for spatial interaction problems.

Added value to my researchIn my healthcare accessibility and telehealth research, a patient population cell and a healthcare facility both have neighborhood contexts (nearby competing facilities, road infrastructure, demographic composition). A dual-set encoder parameterized by the 2SFCA catchment radius could capture the mutual context of patient and provider — an expressively richer accessibility score than scalar supply-to-demand ratios, applicable to both physical and digital (telehealth) access measurement.

⑤ Challenge benchmark datasets in spatial ML the same way this paper challenges MoleculeNet

Extending this paperOne of the paper's most impactful contributions is the demonstration that MoleculeNet may not be well-suited to reveal the advantages of GNNs, because simpler set models match or surpass GNNs on those datasets. The paper proposes more modern datasets (OCELOT, Biogen ADME) as better discriminators. Extending this framework: conduct a systematic benchmark audit for spatial prediction tasks (e.g., crime, health outcomes, mobility demand) to test whether graph-based spatial models actually outperform non-graph baselines, or whether the commonly used datasets similarly fail to discriminate model complexity.
