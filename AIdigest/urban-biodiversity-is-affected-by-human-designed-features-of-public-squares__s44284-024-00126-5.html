Urban biodiversity is affected by human-designed features of public squares

Andrew J. Fairbairn · Sebastian T. Meyer · Maximilian Mühlbauer · Kirsten Jung · Beate Apfelbeck · Katherine Berthon · … · Wolfgang W. Weisser Published 2024-09-11 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00126-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 103 public squares in Munich, the way humans design a square strongly determines which species can live there. Overall taxon richness and abundance rise with a square's "greenness", but different taxa respond to different specific features — the proportion of lawn, shrub volume and tree density matter most, while artificial light at night (ALAN) and the number of people/pets suppress nocturnal and disturbance-sensitive taxa. There is no single design optimal for all biodiversity; "green" is not a monolith, so biodiverse-city planning must be multifaceted, balancing the needs of humans and other taxa.

★

Why It Matters

  • Decomposes "green" into actionable design levers. Instead of treating green cover as a single number, it shows that lawn vs. shrub vs. tree vs. flower bed each drives different taxa — giving planners and landscape architects specific knobs rather than a vague mandate to "add green."
  • Targets the built-up city, not just parks. By studying public squares — spaces designed for humans, central to urban life — it shows biodiversity can be cultivated in the everyday fabric of the city, where conservation competes with transport, housing and economic priorities.
  • Quantifies human–wildlife trade-offs. ALAN and human presence (both wanted for safety and use) measurably reduce small-mammal and bat activity, naming concrete design tensions (e.g., wildlife-friendly lighting) rather than assuming win-wins.
  • A multi-taxon, multidiversity blueprint. Simultaneously sampling eight taxa (birds, pollinators, arthropods, bats, small/pest mammals, bryophytes, spontaneous vegetation) under one framework is rare and supports a transferable "design-for-many-taxa" approach.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Study area & sample
City of Munich, Germany (48.1372, 11.5761). From 354 public squares ("Platz"), stratified random sampling by size yielded 95, plus 8 manually added (tree-free underrepresented) = 103 squares, ranging 0.086–6.71 ha.
Biodiversity scope
Eight taxa sampled with taxon-specific methods: birds (feral pigeon separate), pollinators, other arthropods, bats, small mammals, pest mammals, bryophytes, spontaneous vegetation; summarized as a multidiversity index. Richest square ≈ 8× the richness of the poorest.
Time period
Field sampling 2017–2018 (arthropods/bats/birds/bryophytes/mammals 2017–18; pollinators 2018; spontaneous vegetation surveyed 2020). Square photos for human counts taken across three days/times.
Square features
14 final predictors (after removing collinear ones, VIF ≤ 5) including lawn proportion, shrub volume (m³), tree density/richness, flower beds, square size, distance to city center, number of people, number of streets, ALAN, water presence; up to 25 tree species per square (Norway maple n=1,179; small-leaved linden n=1,044 most abundant).
Greenness (NDVI)
NDVI ≥ 0.2 computed per 20 cm pixel from aerial imagery to derive square greenness and a 1,000 m buffer green proportion around each square.
Night lights (ALAN)
Average gray value per square from Luojia-1 nighttime satellite imagery.
Base maps / tools
Square delineation and characterization via Google Earth, ArcGIS, aerial images and land-register maps; basemap tiles by Stamen Design, map data © OpenStreetMap contributors; taxon icons from PhyloPic (CC0). Analysis in R 4.1.1 (caret, randomForest, vegan, usdm).
Availability
Square features + biodiversity data openly available on Dryad (10.5061/dryad.bcc2fqznq). No dedicated code repository stated (methods describe the analytical pipeline).

Core Methods

  • Stratified square sampling: log-normal size distribution split into five equal classes; random draw + manual top-up to span the green-to-gray gradient.
  • Multi-taxon field sampling: taxon-tailored protocols — suction/knockdown for arthropods, ultrasonic Batcorders for bats, transect counts for birds, phytometer observations for pollinators, footprint tunnels for mammals, substrate surveys for bryophytes, surface surveys for spontaneous vegetation.
  • Multidiversity index: average proportional species richness across taxa, each scaled to its maximum observed value so high-richness groups don't dominate.
  • Greenness linear models: per-taxon linear regression of abundance/richness on proportion of green (with covariates: domestic animals for birds, flower richness/abundance for pollinators).
  • Random forest feature importance: 1,001-tree forests per response (multidiversity + each taxon), 10-fold CV ×3, mtry tuned, collinear predictors removed (VIF ≤ 5); variable importance = % increase in MSE under permutation; effect direction read from partial-dependence plots. RF models explained ≈2× the variance of greenness-only models (r² 0.433 vs 0.239).
  • Community composition: non-metric multidimensional scaling (NMDS, vegan::metaMDS) with feature vectors fitted via envfit.

① Scale the 103-square case to a city-wide, remote-sensing-driven design model

Extending this paperThe paper's design–biodiversity link rests on intensive field sampling of 103 squares — powerful but unscalable to a whole city or to other cities the authors call for. Extend it by training the random-forest importance structure as a predictive map: use high-resolution remote sensing (the same 20 cm NDVI, plus canopy/3-D structure) to impute square features everywhere, then predict multidiversity for all public spaces, validating against the surveyed 103.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my multi-source data fusion + explainable ML for spatial prediction agenda: fuse remote sensing, OSM and survey labels into a city-scale biodiversity surface, with SHAP-style attribution mirroring the paper's importance scores — a reusable GeoAI mapping pipeline.

② Replace per-taxon random forests with a multi-task, spatially explicit graph model

Extending this paperThe paper fits a separate forest per taxon and treats squares as independent, discarding both cross-taxon correlation and the connectivity it repeatedly flags as important (e.g., bats). Extend it: build a graph of squares (edges from proximity / green-corridor connectivity within the 1 km buffer) and learn all taxa jointly with a multi-task GNN, so shared green-feature signals and spatial spillovers are modeled explicitly.

Added value to my researchDirectly feeds my physics-informed graph learning line: encode dispersal/connectivity as message passing with ecological priors, and reuse the same square-network machinery I use for facility-access networks — biodiversity as a graph-prediction problem.

③ Couple biodiversity supply with human-accessibility demand

Extending this paperThe paper measures the supply of biodiverse squares but never who can reach them; its human variable is on-square head-counts, not catchment access. Extend it by overlaying a 2SFCA-style accessibility layer: which residents live within a walkable catchment of high-multidiversity squares, and is that access equitably distributed across neighborhoods?

Added value to my researchThis merges urban ecology with my R2SFCA service-area methods: treat biodiverse squares as "supply" and population as "demand," producing a biodiversity-exposure-equity index — a natural extension of my green-amenity accessibility work.
