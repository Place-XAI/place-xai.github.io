Role of community and sexual contacts as drivers of MPXV clade I

Hiroaki Murayama · Toshiaki R. Asakura · Borame L. Dickens · Dorothy Boyle · Jen Han Foo · Shihui Jin · Patrick K. Mukadi · Keisuke Ejima · Sung-mok Jung · Akihiro Nishi · Kiesha Prem · Audry M. Wakamba · Diafuka Saila-Ngita · David Niyukuri · Akira Endo Published 2026-04-06 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00080-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

MPXV clade Ib has a higher outbreak potential than clade Ia because it has established a sexual transmission route among high-activity individuals — particularly female sex workers (FSWs) and their clients — on top of the usual community contact pathway. Mathematical modelling shows that this additional sexual route accounts for 14–62% of the effective reproduction number (highest in the Kamituga health zone), pushing Ry above the epidemic threshold of 1. Prioritising FSW-focused vaccination, even at small absolute dose numbers, substantially reduces the doses required among the general adult population for control — a finding that mirrors the role of MSM networks in the globally circulating clade IIb.

Why It Matters

  • Explains the unprecedented exponential growth of clade Ib. The paper is the first to quantify, via a validated next-generation matrix model, how much of clade Ib's reproduction number is driven by sexual versus community contacts — resolving a key open question during the WHO public-health emergency declared in August 2024.
  • Links outbreak potential to a specific key population. By identifying FSWs as the bridge group, it gives public health agencies an actionable target group, directly comparable to MSM for clade IIb — enabling transferable, equity-centred response frameworks.
  • Quantifies vaccination trade-offs under scarcity. The contour-plot analysis (Fig. 2c) shows that focused FSW vaccination can dramatically reduce the mass-vaccination doses needed for control — critical intelligence under current global vaccine shortages.
  • Warns of growing baseline risk from demographic ageing. Projections show clade Ia's Ry rising from ~0.82 to ~0.90 in the DRC between 2015 and 2024 as smallpox-immune cohorts age — meaning even minor additional transmission routes suffice to breach the epidemic threshold.
  • Open science design. All case data, code (Julia 1.9.3 + R 4.3.1), and model outputs are publicly available via GitHub, enabling community-wide replication and extension.

Core Methods

  • Next-generation matrix (NGM) framework: the age-structured NGM is the core mathematical object — its dominant eigenvector gives the stationary age distribution of cases and its dominant eigenvalue gives the effective reproduction number Reff. Modelling the observed case age/sex distribution as multinomial proportional to the dominant eigenvector enables likelihood-based fitting.
  • Age-dependent susceptibility estimation: two free parameters — σ0 (relative susceptibility of under-5s) and ε (net smallpox vaccine protection) — are estimated from the historical Tshuapa clade Ia dataset via Bayesian importance sampling, then validated on hold-out 2024 data (KL divergence < 0.01).
  • Sexual contact block extension for clade Ib: the NGM is extended with proportionate-mixing heterosexual blocks for high-activity groups (FSWs and clients), parameterised with age-stratified proportions {pa}, {qa} and mean sexual contact rates. Parameters estimated by Markov chain Monte Carlo (No-U-Turn Sampler, 2,000 samples, effective sample size ≥ 500).
  • Mean-neighbour-degree ratio: using a configuration-network model, the ratio wF/vF = 1 + σ²/μ² = 2.17 is derived analytically from FSW sexual degree distributions, capturing the superspreading structure without explicit network simulation.
  • Model averaging: four contact matrix variants are combined using Watanabe–Akaike information criterion (WAIC) weights, pooling parameter estimates across models and propagating uncertainty.
  • Demographic projections: Ry is projected from 2010 to 2030 by shifting the smallpox-immunized cohort age boundary and correcting contact matrices for changing population age structure (density-correction method of Arregui et al.).
  • Vaccination scenario analysis: contour plots of Reff under combinations of FSW-focused (20–39-year-old high-activity females) and mass (20–39-year-old general adults) vaccination, using JYNNEOS effectiveness of 86% against infection.

① Spatially explicit heterogeneity: mapping FSW network density across health zones

Extending this paperThe model estimates a single set of sexual-contact parameters per geographical unit (Kivus, Burundi, etc.), but the paper itself acknowledges that Kamituga's gold-mining commercial-sex economy drives a dramatically higher sexual contribution (62%) than other South Kivu zones (14%). A direct extension would disaggregate the analysis to the health-zone level — mapping how FSW density, bar density, and economic activity spatially predict the relative contribution of sexual contacts to Ry. This would turn the paper's scalar parameter estimates into a spatially continuous surface, enabling targeted allocation of FSW vaccination by zone.

Added value to my researchThis is a natural application of my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and GWR/MGWR methods to epidemic modelling: instead of a fixed susceptibility or sexual contact rate per province, estimate it as a spatially varying coefficient driven by local socio-economic covariates (bar density, mining activity, FSW prevalence). It links my spatial regression toolkit directly to infectious disease epidemiology, extending my health geography work beyond accessibility into outbreak response geography.

② Replace proportionate mixing with a learned sexual network structure

Extending this paperThe paper assumes proportionate (random) heterosexual mixing among high-activity individuals — a significant simplification the authors flag in the Discussion. Extending it: fit an empirical sexual network with age-assortative mixing and power-law degree heterogeneity (using FSW client-frequency data beyond the DRC survey), then re-derive the dominant eigenvalue framework for this structured network. The paper's own proof-of-concept configuration network (Supplementary Figs. 10–12) hints at this but does not pursue it for the main estimates.

Added value to my researchLearning network structure from mobility and encounter data is central to my GeoAI agenda. GPS-derived co-location networks (from mobile phone records) could approximate contact heterogeneity at finer spatial scales — adapting my mobile-phone data fusion methods from commuting/urban analytics to epidemic contact inference. This also directly feeds my community-detection methods: identifying densely connected FSW network communities is a network community-detection problem analogous to job-housing network delineation.

③ Machine-learning surrogate for real-time outbreak parameter estimation

Extending this paperThe paper's Bayesian MCMC fitting requires dedicated model runs per geographical setting and time window. As clade Ib spreads to new countries (29 globally as of the study), re-running the full NGM estimation for each setting is computationally and logistically expensive. A direct extension: train a neural surrogate that takes as inputs the observed case age-sex distribution (a <20-dimensional vector), the population age structure, and a few proxy covariates, and outputs posterior estimates of the sexual contact contribution — enabling near-instant outbreak characterisation for any new country without full MCMC.

Added value to my researchThis maps onto my explainable machine learning for spatial prediction agenda: the surrogate model's input features are directly interpretable (age distribution skewness, sex ratio of cases, child-to-adult ratio), enabling SHAP-based attribution of which case-distribution features signal a sexual-transmission-dominant outbreak. My experience with ML ensembles for spatially structured data would directly transfer to this cross-country epidemic prediction setting.

④ Integrate healthcare accessibility barriers into the FSW vaccination coverage model

Extending this paperThe paper's vaccination scenario analysis (Fig. 2c) models uptake among FSWs as a continuous free parameter, abstracting away the spatial accessibility barriers that make reaching FSWs difficult in practice — the authors acknowledge stigma and social barriers but do not model them spatially. Extending it: couple the NGM vaccination model with a 2SFCA-based accessibility surface for vaccination sites, where FSW catchment populations are defined by the commercial-sex hotspot locations (health zone-level) and travel impedance to JYNNEOS distribution points.

Added value to my researchThis is exactly the intersection of my 2SFCA/R2SFCA spatial accessibility work and public health intervention modelling. Mapping JYNNEOS vaccine access for FSWs in the DRC is structurally identical to mapping primary-care accessibility for underserved groups — the same service-area delineation, distance-decay, and equity decomposition framework applies. It would constitute the first application of 2SFCA-class methods to mpox vaccination equity in sub-Saharan Africa.

⑤ Agent-based simulation to stress-test the NGM assumptions and explore stochastic extinction

Extending this paperThe paper's NGM is a deterministic mean-field model that cannot capture stochastic extinction, cluster-level heterogeneity, or the role of superspreader events. The Discussion acknowledges that its estimates may not generalise to settings with different sociobehavioural contexts. Extending it: build an agent-based model (ABM) seeded by the paper's estimated NGM parameters, with explicit FSW nodes, household clusters, and geographic mobility between health zones — testing whether the paper's Ry ~ 1.5 estimate is consistent with observed stochastic outbreaks, or whether superspreading alone could explain observed patterns without requiring a 42% sexual contribution.

Added value to my researchAgent-based modelling is one of my explicit methodological directions. Constructing an ABM for mpox in the DRC would leverage my Monte Carlo simulation experience, spatial network construction from GPS/mobility data, and spatiotemporal KDE for initialising infectious seed locations. The ABM's spatial component (heterogeneous health zone sizes, travel between zones) would also connect to my urban agglomeration and regional-collaboration research — treating health zones as the analogue of functional urban areas.
