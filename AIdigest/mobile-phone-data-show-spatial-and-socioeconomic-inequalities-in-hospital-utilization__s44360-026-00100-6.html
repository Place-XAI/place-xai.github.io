Mobile phone data show spatial and socioeconomic inequalities in hospital utilization

Jizhe Xia · Pei Ye · Longyan Pan · Di Zhang · Zihao Wu · Qiye Deng · Xiana Chen · Qingquan Li · Carlo Ratti · Michael Batty · Ziqian Ye · Chen Zhong · Yang Yue · Wei Tu Published 2026-04-24 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00100-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 96 million mobile phone users in 11 Chinese cities, 80.63% of patients bypass their nearest hospital, incurring 211.84% more travel distance. Low-SES patients show a paradox: they bypass less often yet skip more hospitals when they do, because their 5.9-fold stronger willingness to travel for quality acts as a compensatory mechanism against structural spatial deficits in local medical resources. This bypass-driven spatial sorting unintentionally reduces experienced social segregation within hospitals by 42.15% for low-SES groups — a finding that challenges strict bypass-control reforms and calls instead for redistribution of high-level clinical capacity into underserved districts.

Why It Matters

  • First population-scale, multi-city measurement of hospital bypass. Prior work relied on specific disease cohorts, narrow hospital sets, or small surveys; this study covers 96 million users and 8 million hospital visits across a stratified sample of 11 cities spanning China's full urban hierarchy.
  • Uncovers a policy-relevant paradox. Low-SES patients are not bypassing less because they care less about quality — they have 5.9× stronger willingness to travel — but because poor residential location forces high-intensity compensatory travel to reach minimally acceptable care.
  • Reveals an unintended equity benefit of bypass. Hospital bypass organically reduces experienced social segregation by 42.15% for the lowest-SES group; suppressing bypass (as China's Hierarchical Diagnosis and Treatment reform intends) could eliminate this patient-driven equity mechanism.
  • Reframes the accessibility policy debate. Standard 2SFCA-type accessibility analyses show low-SES areas have worse access, yet bypass behaviour nearly equalizes realized utilization — implying that access scores alone underestimate effective healthcare equity.
  • Delivers a new monitoring metric. The Nth Nearest Hospital Index (NNHI) is a directly computable, population-scale indicator of both bypass prevalence and intensity, usable to track reform success without clinical records.

Core Methods

  • Hospital visit identification: spatiotemporal filtering of GPS trajectories — visits defined by AOI intersection + dwelling time ≥30 min; staff excluded via visit-frequency threshold (≥1/3 of days); companion travellers removed via two-stage graph-theoretic contact-network analysis.
  • Nth Nearest Hospital Index (NNHI): for each patient, hospitals are ranked by road-network distance from home; NNHI = rank of the actually visited hospital (NNHI = 1 → no bypass; NNHI > 1 → bypass).
  • SES proxy: residential property value (CNY m−2) at a 200 × 200 m grid level, sourced from real-estate platforms; patients classified into high (top 20%), middle (20–80%), low (bottom 20%) SES groups within each city.
  • E2SFCA accessibility: Enhanced Two-Step Floating Catchment Area method with Gaussian distance-decay (β = 440) and 15/30/60 min travel-time thresholds; supply = hospital bed capacity, demand = population.
  • Concentration curves (CCs) and Concentration Index (CI): World Bank-endorsed approach to quantify whether health access/bypass is pro-rich or pro-poor.
  • Structural Equation Modelling (SEM): decomposes SES effects on bypass through mediating pathways — home–hospital distance, hospital reputation, and tertiary hospital concentration in the neighbourhood.
  • Instrumental Variable (IV) analysis: school-district quality as IV for residential property value, isolating exogenous SES variation from potential confounding by health status / disease severity.
  • Experienced Segregation (ES) and Experienced Socioeconomic Class (ESC) indices: adapted from Abbiasov and Xu — measure within-hospital socioeconomic mixing at the level of each originating community.
  • Mixed-logit discrete-choice model: hospital-selection utility function including grade, bed capacity, Fudan CHLT reputation, and cubic polynomial distance decay; estimated via maximum simulated likelihood on an 8% random sample; SES-stratified models quantify willingness-to-travel (WTT).

① Replace the paper's fixed-radius E2SFCA with a learned, bypass-calibrated distance-decay

Extending this paperThe paper's hospital accessibility measure relies on E2SFCA with a Gaussian decay fixed at β = 440 and thresholds at 15/30/60 min — parameters borrowed from prior literature rather than estimated from the observed bypass trajectories. The paper's own mixed-logit model already recovers a cubic polynomial distance-decay from the same GPS data, but this richer decay is used only for WTT analysis and never fed back into the accessibility score. A direct extension: replace the static Gaussian in E2SFCA with the SES-stratified empirical decay estimated from the discrete-choice model, producing a behaviour-calibrated R2SFCA that reflects how patients actually discount distance rather than how planners assume they do. This would sharpen the concentration-curve analysis and likely reveal that the measured access inequality is even more pronounced than reported, because low-SES patients face a steeper effective decay at short distances before their high WTT kicks in.

Added value to my researchThis is a natural upgrade to my 2SFCA/R2SFCA toolkit: substituting the assumed Gaussian with a data-driven, SES-conditional decay function — estimated from GPS trips rather than travel surveys — turns the floating catchment into a behaviorally grounded accessibility measure. Combined with MGWR, the spatially varying decay exponent can expose which neighbourhoods systematically over- or under-estimate access under the standard fixed-bandwidth assumption.

② Extend the NNHI framework to quantify intercity and telehealth bypass

Extending this paperThe paper focuses on intra-city hospital bypass — the NNHI ranks all hospitals within a city. But the same GPS data that reveal intra-city travel could reveal intercity patient flows: when is a city-level "nearest" hospital bypassed for a hospital in a different city? The corpus documents that China's intercity healthcare system is highly unequal, with a few hub cities attracting disproportionate patient flows from peripheral cities. Extending NNHI to cross-city pairs would expose the spatial hierarchy of bypass, showing how intra-city quality deficits cascade into intercity migration of patients — and whether the same low-SES compensatory pattern holds at the regional scale. The SEM decomposition could then test whether inter-regional bypass is driven by the same quality-accessibility pathway or by fundamentally different structural factors (insurance portability, referral networks).

Added value to my researchIntercity healthcare flows are a direct extension of my work on telehealth and digital-divide accessibility. Mapping cross-city hospital bypass would complement analysis of teleconsultation demand, identifying which patient populations substitute physical travel with telemedicine and which do not — a critical input for equity-aware telehealth infrastructure planning.

③ Use the paper's WTT heterogeneity to design an explainable spatial targeting policy

Extending this paperThe paper identifies that low-SES patients have 5.9× higher WTT but are compelled to invoke it as compensation for local quality deficits. The policy recommendation — redistribute high-level clinical capacity to underserved districts — is directionally correct but not operationally specific: which districts, which specialties, how many beds? The paper's own mixed-logit coefficients and the spatial SES distribution (200 m grid) contain all the inputs for a facility-location optimisation: place satellite clinics or rotating senior-physician slots to minimise the aggregate compensatory travel burden of low-SES patients, subject to a budget constraint on physician-hours. Adding an explainability layer (SHAP on the SES-grade-distance features) would reveal which spatial features of a neighbourhood most drive the compensatory travel burden — prioritising intervention sites more precisely than any access-score ranking.

Added value to my researchThis is the healthcare analogue of my EV-charging efficiency–equity optimisation: instead of charging-station locations, the decision variable is satellite-clinic placement, and the equity criterion is minimising the NNHI gap between SES groups. The explainable ML layer maps directly onto my agenda for transparent spatial prediction — attributing facility-need to specific neighbourhood features rather than a black-box score.

④ Test whether the bypass-driven desegregation persists under experienced-mobility lens

Extending this paperThe paper's ES index measures socioeconomic mixing within hospitals: low-SES patients who bypass encounter high-SES co-patients, reducing their experienced segregation. But the paper stops at the hospital gate — the journey to the hospital (public transit, shared ride, walking) may itself be segregated, meaning the net desegregation experienced in daily life is smaller than the hospital-level index suggests. Extending the paper: apply the same GPS trajectories to compute an experienced economic isolation index along the travel route (analogous to the campus/student isolation literature) for each hospital visit, and compare it with the within-hospital ES index. This would reveal whether bypass behaviour genuinely reduces experienced segregation or merely shifts it from the residential neighbourhood to the clinic.

Added value to my researchThe mobility-based segregation concept connects directly to my work on spatiotemporal exposure and health geography. Measuring experienced segregation along healthcare-seeking trajectories — rather than at fixed residential or hospital locations — is a methodological advance I could apply to any large-city GPS dataset, building a richer picture of how spatial sorting in daily mobility shapes health and social equity.

⑤ Build a GeoAI transfer model of bypass risk from GPS mobility features

Extending this paperThe paper estimates the mixed-logit model separately per SES stratum using a city-pooled 8% random sample — a computationally tractable but theoretically limited approach that assumes preference parameters are stable across 11 cities. A richer extension: treat each city × SES stratum combination as a separate observation and ask whether a spatiotemporal graph neural network can predict NNHI distributions (and WTT curves) from observable city features (hospital network topology, housing price surface, transport network density) without re-estimating the discrete-choice model from scratch for each new city. The paper's 11-city dataset serves as the supervised training set; the GNN learns to transfer the structural relationship between spatial quality mismatch and compensatory bypass intensity. City features derivable from OpenStreetMap and public housing data make this fully operationalisable even in data-sparse settings.

Added value to my researchThis is the GeoAI angle I care about most: using physics-informed or structure-aware graph learning to make health-behaviour models transferable across cities without repeated costly data collection. The hospital network (nodes = hospitals, edge weights = patient-flow volume by SES tier) is a natural spatial graph for a message-passing GNN, and the bypass intensity gradient across SES groups provides a rich, interpretable supervision signal. Combined with fine-tuning on a geospatial foundation model, this could generalise hospital-bypass prediction to cities without mobile-phone data.
