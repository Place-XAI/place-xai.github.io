Inter-brain neural dynamics in biological and artificial intelligence systems

Zhang, Xingjian · Phi, Nguyen · Li, Qin · Gorzek, Ryan · Zwingenberger, Niklas · Huang, Shan · Zhou, John L. · Kingsbury, Lyle · Raam, Tara · Wu, Ye Emily · Wei, Don · Kao, Jonathan C. · Hong, Weizhe Published 2025-07-02 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09196-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

During social interaction, the multi-dimensional neural activity of each individual's dorsomedial prefrontal cortex decomposes into a shared subspace — synchronized across brains — and a unique subspace private to each animal. GABAergic neurons carry a disproportionately large shared subspace driven by both self- and other-behaviour, whereas glutamatergic neurons do not. Critically, the same shared-subspace phenomenon arises spontaneously in multi-agent reinforcement-learning (MARL) agents as social behaviour emerges, and selectively silencing the shared neural dimensions abolishes social actions — demonstrating that shared neural dynamics are a functionally necessary and generalizable feature of interacting neural systems.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reveals a universal organizing principle of social brains. The shared-vs.-unique subspace decomposition is replicated across cell types, sex, interaction partners, and time windows in mice — and then independently recovered in artificial agents — providing the strongest evidence yet that inter-brain synchrony is mechanistically necessary, not epiphenomenal.
  • Assigns a specific cell-type role to social cognition. GABAergic (inhibitory) neurons, not glutamatergic, dominate the shared subspace. This selectivity points to an unexpected circuit mechanism: inhibitory neurons coordinate social dynamics across brains.
  • Bridges biological and artificial intelligence research. By applying the same mathematical framework to MARL agents, the paper creates a two-way translation: insights from mice illuminate AI agent design, and AI experiments allow causal perturbations impossible in vivo.
  • Provides a causal, not correlational, test. Disrupting shared dimensions computationally in MARL agents reduces social actions without affecting non-social behaviour — turning a descriptive finding into a causal claim.
  • Opens a new technology direction. Shared subspace structure could inform brain-computer interface design, assistive social-communication devices, and interpretable AI agents for social tasks.

Core Methods

  • Inter-brain correlation (IBC): Pearson correlation of aggregate (mean) neural activity between two simultaneously recorded animals, compared against separation sessions and shuffled/phase-randomized controls.
  • Partial Least Squares Correlation (PLSC): Decomposes the joint neural activity of two animals into shared (PLSC) dimensions that maximize covariance across brains and unique (U) dimensions that are independent — the paper's core dimensionality-reduction framework.
  • Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA): Applied as a robustness check against PLSC; high cosine similarity between PLSC and CCA shared dimensions confirms results are method-independent.
  • SVM decoding: Linear support vector machines trained to decode annotated behaviors from neural population activity, quantifying how much behavioral information is encoded in shared versus unique subspaces.
  • GLM behavioral regression: Generalized linear model regressing individual behavior features onto population neural activity to attribute shared-subspace variance to self-behaviors and partner behaviors separately.
  • Neuronal ensemble detection: Identifies co-active neuron assemblies during social versus non-social moments; quantifies their overlap with shared/unique subspace contributors.
  • MARL + subspace perturbation: Trains competitive and cooperative agents in a custom Ray environment; applies the same PLSC decomposition to agent hidden states; selectively disrupts shared dimensions and measures the behavioral consequence on social action frequency.

① Transfer the shared-subspace framework from brains to urban mobility networks

Extending this paperThe paper's PLSC decomposition partitions a pair of individuals' neural spaces into shared (cross-brain) and unique (within-brain) dimensions. An immediate extension of the paper: apply the same cross-entity PLSC to dyadic mobility trajectories — e.g., pairs of commuters or neighborhoods — to identify shared spatiotemporal movement patterns (analogous to shared neural dynamics) versus idiosyncratic ones. Just as the paper shows the shared subspace encodes partner-behavior information, shared mobility dimensions may encode structural coupling between origin–destination zones or correlated demand shocks, providing a new decomposition tool for origin–destination flow modeling.

Added value to my researchThis directly enriches my spatiotemporal analysis toolkit: applying PLSC to paired GPS or mobile-phone trajectory datasets would decompose job-housing flow networks into a shared connectivity subspace (driven by land-use coupling) and a unique subspace (driven by individual preference), enabling a more interpretable factorization than PCA alone and informing my R2SFCA catchment delineation with structurally grounded demand clusters.

② Use the paper's causal perturbation paradigm to audit AI agent interpretability

Extending this paperThe paper's key causal result is that selectively zeroing the shared subspace components of MARL agents' hidden states reduces social actions without affecting non-social tasks. This is a form of mechanistic interpretability by subspace ablation — a generalization the paper does not fully develop. Extending it: apply the same perturbation protocol systematically to probe which shared dimensions encode which social behaviors (approach vs. attack vs. retreat), building a causal map of social computation inside MARL policies — turning the paper's single-perturbation test into a full interpretability audit of social AI.

③ Encode inter-entity coupling as a graph inductive bias for spatial interaction modeling

Extending this paperThe paper's PLSC reveals that the shared neural subspace arises jointly from intra-brain coupling (within-animal co-firing) and inter-brain coupling (cross-animal coordination). This two-level coupling structure — within-node and between-node — mirrors the architecture of a graph neural network where node-level aggregation (intra-brain) and edge-level message passing (inter-brain) jointly determine the learned representation. Extending the paper: reformulate the shared-subspace discovery as a graph learning problem, where each animal is a node, each social interaction is an edge, and the GNN learns to predict the shared subspace from connectivity alone — enabling transfer to unseen animal pairs or tasks without re-running PLSC.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct entry point into my physics-informed graph learning agenda: the intra-brain / inter-brain coupling hierarchy is formally identical to the within-zone / between-zone coupling in a spatial interaction graph (e.g., census-tract-level health demand driven by local demographics and inter-tract flow). Encoding both coupling levels as soft constraints in a GNN layer would let my spatiotemporal models learn which inter-zone connections genuinely drive health disparities, making the graph structure interpretable rather than learned end-to-end.

④ Extend the shared-subspace metric to measure social cohesion across urban neighborhoods

Extending this paperThe paper quantifies social coupling strength as the number of significant shared PLSC dimensions and their variance-explained fraction — a principled, dimensionality-based metric of inter-individual synchrony. This turns the paper's inter-brain synchrony score into a quantitative urban social cohesion metric with direct policy implications for regional collaboration and agglomeration analysis.

⑤ Leverage GABAergic-cell specificity to design cell-type-targeted social AI architectures

Extending this paperThe paper's most specific biological finding is that inhibitory (GABAergic) neurons — not excitatory ones — preferentially carry the shared neural subspace. In neural circuit terms, inhibitory interneurons coordinate population-level dynamics via global inhibition. Extending the paper: test whether MARL architectures that incorporate an explicit inhibitory-population module (a separate sub-network that provides global, non-specific suppression of all action dimensions, analogous to GABAergic interneurons) spontaneously produce larger shared subspaces and more robust social behavior — directly testing whether the biological specificity of inhibition is load-bearing for social computation.

Added value to my researchThe inhibitory-population metaphor maps onto attention/gating mechanisms in spatial transformers: a "GABAergic-inspired" spatial gate that globally downweights irrelevant location features could improve the interpretability of my geospatial foundation model fine-tuning, ensuring that socially shared spatial patterns (e.g., cross-city regularities in healthcare access) are captured by a dedicated sub-network rather than diffused across all attention heads — a biologically motivated architectural prior for GeoAI.
