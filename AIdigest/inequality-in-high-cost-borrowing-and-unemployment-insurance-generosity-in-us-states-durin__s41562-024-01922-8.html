Inequality in high-cost borrowing and unemployment insurance generosity in US states during the COVID-19 pandemic

Lawrence M. Berger · Meta Brown · J. Michael Collins · Rachel E. Dwyer · Jason N. Houle · Stephanie Moulton · Davon Norris · Alec P. Rhodes Published 2024-07-11 DOI 10.1038/s41562-024-01922-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

More generous state unemployment insurance (UI) benefits were inversely associated with high-cost credit use during the first seven quarters of the COVID-19 pandemic. This protective effect was concentrated among consumers in low-income households, who are also most likely to access costly alternative financial services (AFS) loans. The findings confirm a credit–welfare state trade-off: when the safety net weakens, the most financially vulnerable turn to high-cost debt — deepening inequality during crises.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reveals the hidden cost of stingy safety nets. When states offered less generous UI benefits, low-income consumers were substantially more likely to take out payday-style AFS loans — the most predatory form of credit available — widening economic inequality during the pandemic.
  • Distinguishes credit types for the first time at scale. Prior research lumped all unsecured debt together. By separating credit cards, personal finance loans, and AFS loans using a 1% national Experian sample (16.7 million consumer-quarter observations), the study shows that income heterogeneity in policy response only becomes visible when credit types are disaggregated.
  • Resolves a theoretical ambiguity. Higher-income consumers actually increased credit use as UI generosity grew (a consumptive response), while lower-income consumers decreased it (a substitutive response) — showing that aggregate models mask opposing mechanisms.
  • Informs durable UI policy design. The results support expanding UI eligibility and benefit levels beyond the pandemic context, especially to cover gig workers and low-wage earners most exposed to high-cost credit markets.
  • Quantifies a k-shaped recovery mechanism. The paper provides empirical grounding for why uneven state UI generosity translated into uneven debt accumulation, contributing to the observed divergence between high- and low-income households during COVID-19.

Core Methods

  • Two-way fixed effects (TWFE) linear probability models: state and quarter-year fixed effects isolate within-state variation in UI generosity over time, controlling for time-invariant state characteristics and period-specific macro shocks (stimulus checks, federal student loan pause, etc.).
  • Income-stratified interaction design: UI generosity interacted with Q4 2019 household income quartile to expose heterogeneous substitutive vs. consumptive mechanisms that aggregate models obscure.
  • Longitudinal balanced panel: 2,385,373 consumers present in all eight study quarters; baseline characteristics fixed at Q4 2019 to avoid pandemic endogeneity.
  • Three distinct credit-type outcomes: binary indicators for opening a new credit card, personal finance loan, or AFS loan in a given quarter — allowing disaggregation by cost tier and income access.
  • Robustness battery: (1) alternative UI measure — replacement ratio; (2) minimum benefit specification; (3) lagged dependent variable controls; (4) credit inquiry (application) outcomes; (5) outstanding debt balance outcomes; (6) high credit utilization rate; (7) SNAP Emergency Allotment supplement controls.
  • Staggered treatment leverage: variation exploited from states' differential early termination of FPUC supplements starting Q2 2021, providing exogenous policy timing variation across states.
  • Stata MP Version 15; standard errors clustered at the consumer level.

① Map the spatial ecology of AFS lender access as a mediating variable

Extending this paperThe paper treats UI generosity as the explanatory variable and credit uptake as the outcome, but the spatial distribution of AFS lenders is itself a structural mediator: consumers in states with fewer payday storefronts may be unable to act on their demand even if UI is low. Extending this paper: merge the paper's Experian/Clarity panel with geocoded AFS lender locations to test whether the negative UI–AFS association is stronger where AFS lenders are more spatially accessible — i.e., whether place amplifies or dampens the policy effect. The corpus paper on banks vs. alternative institutions in US cities maps exactly this spatial–temporal ecology of racial inequality in access to predatory lenders, providing the conceptual and data template for such an extension.

Added value to my researchMy R2SFCA spatial accessibility methods, developed for healthcare facility access, transfer directly here: AFS lender coverage areas could be modeled as supply-side service areas, and 2SFCA could quantify the "predatory credit accessibility score" of each ZIP Code — enabling me to build a spatial layer that explains residual variance in the paper's regressions and creating a new GeoAI application for financial equity research.

② Disaggregate by race using spatial proxies to address the paper's acknowledged limitation

Extending this paperThe authors explicitly note race and ethnicity data are absent from credit records — a fundamental gap since UI has historically been distributed in racially unequal patterns and AFS lending is concentrated in majority-Black and Hispanic ZIP Codes. Extending this paper: augment the Experian panel with ZIP Code-level racial composition (already present as a control variable in the paper) and apply Bayesian Improved Surname Geocoding (BISG) or small-area estimation to impute race probabilities at the individual level. This would allow a race-stratified replication of Fig. 4 and test whether the income gradient in the UI–credit trade-off is confounded by racial geography. The corpus paper on socio-spatial inequality and COVID transmission in US cities provides a methodological precedent for using spatial proxies to unpack individual-level race effects in administrative data.

Added value to my researchBISG and small-area spatial imputation are generalizable to my GeoAI work: I could build spatially-explicit race/ethnicity imputation models using Geographically Weighted Regression or MGWR calibrated at the ZIP Code level, providing a reproducible pipeline for equity analyses that lack individual demographic identifiers — a method gap that spans healthcare access, housing, and financial inclusion studies.

Cross-ref (corpus): Socio-spatial inequality and the effects of density on COVID-19 transmission in US cities — 10.1038/s44284-023-00008-2

③ Extend the credit–welfare trade-off to housing cost burden and utility-access deprivation

Extending this paperThe paper focuses on unsecured credit (credit cards, personal loans, AFS loans) but the authors acknowledge that retrospective obligations — past-due utility bills, unpaid rent — are common among the most vulnerable and are invisible in credit report data. Extending it: link the Experian/Clarity panel to the Urban Institute's rental arrears data and the corpus paper on housing crisis and water access deterioration in US cities, testing whether low-UI states show simultaneous increases in AFS borrowing AND housing/utility deprivation — revealing whether high-cost credit is a substitute or a complement to service deprivation, not just a substitute for UI income.

Added value to my researchThis framing — financial coping strategies as spatial choices between different types of deprivation — connects directly to my urban accessibility research on housing prices and public-facility access. I can model this as a joint spatial optimization problem using spatial entropy and network community detection, where households in poorly-served areas face a portfolio of deprivations (healthcare inaccessibility, high-cost credit, utility shutoffs) that co-cluster spatially and respond jointly to safety net policy variation.

④ Apply explainable ML to identify which state-level features drive heterogeneous UI policy effects

Extending this paperThe paper's TWFE approach absorbs all time-invariant state heterogeneity into fixed effects — a conservative but black-box approach that cannot tell us which state features make the UI–credit trade-off stronger or weaker. Extending it: fit a state-level SHAP analysis on the paper's predicted probabilities, using state-level features (labor market tightness, AFS regulation index, racial composition, housing cost index, governor's party) as predictors of the income-specific UI effect size. This would convert the paper's fixed effects into a ranked attribution, revealing which policy levers amplify or mute the credit–welfare trade-off and allowing counterfactual simulation. The behavioral changes during COVID paper demonstrates how state-level mobility and income-diversity patterns can be characterized using panel observational data in ways that feed directly into such feature-importance models.

Added value to my researchSHAP decomposition of fixed-effect heterogeneity is directly transferable to my explainable GeoAI pipeline: in healthcare accessibility studies, I can replace opaque MGWR bandwidth selection with SHAP-ranked spatial features that explain which place-level characteristics drive variations in 2SFCA service-area catchments — producing policy-legible explanations for accessibility inequality analogous to what this extension would produce for credit inequality.

⑤ Generalize the credit–welfare framework to intergenerational mobility via childhood exposure

Extending this paperThe paper studies short-term financial coping during the pandemic but leaves open the long-run consequences of low-income households taking on AFS debt when UI is stingy. Extending it: link the paper's state-level UI generosity index to intergenerational income mobility data at the commuting zone level (e.g., the Chetty Opportunity Atlas), testing whether states with chronically low UI generosity — and thus higher AFS debt exposure for low-income families during COVID-19 — show lower subsequent upward mobility for children growing up in those households. The corpus paper on childhood exposure to local wealth inequality and intergenerational income mobility provides exactly the right conceptual scaffold and empirical target variable for this extension.
