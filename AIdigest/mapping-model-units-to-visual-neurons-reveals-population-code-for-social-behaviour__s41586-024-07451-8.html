Mapping model units to visual neurons reveals population code for social behaviour

Benjamin R. Cowley · Adam J. Calhoun · Nivedita Rangarajan · Elise Ireland · Maxwell H. Turner · Jonathan W. Pillow · Mala Murthy Published 2024-05-22 DOI 10.1038/s41586-024-07451-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

By introducing knockout training — a method that perturbs a deep neural network during training to mirror real genetic silencing of neurons — the authors establish a one-to-one mapping between 23 model units and 23 lobula columnar (LC) neuron types in the Drosophila visual system. Contrary to the prevailing channel-specific hypothesis, the model reveals that combinations of LC neuron types, including those previously linked only to non-social behaviours, form a rich population code that drives male courtship pursuit and song. The framework maps the full path from visual stimulus to neuron type to behaviour, and is designed to incorporate future connectome wiring diagrams.

★

Why It Matters

  • Overcomes a fundamental interpretability gap in systems neuroscience. Standard deep network models of sensory systems predict neural responses but cannot explain how individual neurons causally drive behaviour. Knockout training closes this gap by aligning model units to real neurons through causal perturbation, not just correlation.
  • Challenges the dominant channel-coding hypothesis. Prior work assumed each LC neuron type encodes one visual feature to drive one specific behaviour. This study demonstrates that combinations of LC types — even those associated with escape and non-social behaviours — collectively encode female motion and drive courtship, fundamentally revising the circuit-level picture.
  • Demonstrates zero-shot neural prediction from behaviour alone. The 1-to-1 network was never shown real neural recordings during training, yet it predicts calcium imaging responses of LC neurons to novel stimuli with a noise-corrected R2 of ~0.35 — an independent test of model correctness.
  • Provides a unified, extensible platform. The model consolidates behavioural effects from 23 different genetic perturbations into a single framework and naturally accommodates incorporation of the full Drosophila connectome (FlyWire) as a structural constraint for future experiments.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Behavioural data
Courtship recordings from 459 male–female fly pairs across 24 conditions: 23 different LC neuron-type silencing lines plus a spGAL4 control. Each session ran up to 30 min or until copulation. Recorded at 60 Hz video and 10 kHz audio. Dataset deposited at DANDI Archive dandiset/000951.
Neural recordings
Two-photon calcium imaging (GCaMP6f, 920 nm, 50 Hz) of 5 LC neuron types (LC6, LC11, LC12, LC15, LC17) across 25 head-fixed male flies (4–6 flies per type) viewing a suite of artificial and naturalistic visual stimuli. A total of 27 responsive LC-type / stimulus-sequence combinations (out of 45) were retained for model validation.
Visual stimuli
Reconstructed greyscale images (64 × 228 pixels) of a fictive female, generated frame-by-frame from tracked joint positions of both flies. Input to the model: sequences of the 10 most recent frames (~300 ms). Pose tracking used SLEAP (deep learning multi-animal pose estimation).
Song segmentation
Audio labelled into sine, Pfast and Pslow song using the MurthyLab FlySongSegmenter. Training dataset comprised ~11.6 million frames after balancing across LC type conditions.
Connectome
FlyWire whole-brain connectome (adult female Drosophila, public release version 630, codex.flywire.ai). Analysed synaptic connectivity for 57 LC/LPLC neuron types; identified 538 presynaptic and 956 postsynaptic partner cell types to the 28 LC/LPLC types studied.
Fly genetics
23 LC split-GAL4 driver lines (provided by M. Reiser, A. Nern, G. Rubin); UAS-TNT-C from Bloomington Stock Center. Flies aged 4–7 days, reared at 25 °C / 60% relative humidity, 12:12 light:dark cycle.
Code & models
Model weights, example stimuli, and training code at github.com/murthylab/one2one-mapping. Implemented in Keras/TensorFlow. SLEAP available at sleap.ai.

Core Methods

  • Knockout training: the central methodological contribution. During DNN training on courtship data from LC-silenced flies, the model unit corresponding to the silenced LC type is set to zero to match the biological perturbation. This causal constraint forces each model unit to carry the same functional role as its matched real neuron.
  • 1-to-1 network architecture: a three-component DNN — (1) a convolutional vision front-end (optic lobe proxy), (2) a 23-unit LC bottleneck layer with ReLU activations (one unit per LC type), and (3) a dense decision network mapping LC activations to 6 behavioural outputs (forward/lateral/angular velocity; sine, Pfast, Pslow song probability).
  • Visual input reconstruction: frame-by-frame greyscale images (64 × 228 px) of a fictive female are generated from tracked joint positions, giving an image-computable model that receives raw visual input rather than hand-coded features.
  • Zero-shot neural prediction: model LC unit responses are compared to two-photon calcium imaging recordings of real LC neurons using a noise-corrected R2 metric that accounts for finite-repeat noise, without ever exposing the model to neural data during training.
  • Cumulative inactivation procedure (CLIP): a greedy ablation method that iteratively silences model LC units (setting activity to mean) in order of least contribution, tracing necessity and sufficiency curves for each of the 6 behavioural outputs across stimulus conditions.
  • Variance decomposition: response variance of each model LC unit is decomposed into components attributable to female size, position, rotation, and their pairwise/triple interactions, quantifying distributed vs. feature-specific encoding.
  • Connectome analysis: FlyWire synaptic connectivity matrices are computed for pre- and postsynaptic partners of the 28 LC/LPLC types; agglomerative clustering (cosine similarity, complete linkage) groups LC types by shared input/output connectivity.
  • Statistics: permutation tests throughout (1,000 runs, two-sided unless noted); Benjamini–Hochberg FDR correction for multiple LC-type comparisons; bootstrapped 90% confidence intervals for neural response traces.

① Extend knockout training from genetic silencing to in-silico lesion studies of spatial neural networks

Extending this paperThe paper's knockout training is currently constrained to the genetic toolkit available in Drosophila: 23 LC types that can each be bilaterally silenced. A direct extension would apply the same principle to the full 57-type LC/LPLC inventory using the FlyWire connectome as a virtual perturbation substrate — silencing any combination of neuron types in silico without needing new fly lines. The connectome-constrained architecture from closely related work (Connectome-constrained networks predict neural activity across the fly visual system) provides exactly this scaffold: embedding FlyWire synaptic weights as constraints within a DNN so that in-silico knockout predictions can be grounded in real circuit anatomy and subsequently validated by targeted optogenetic experiments.

Added value to my researchThis lesion-to-prediction pipeline is structurally identical to spatial ablation studies in GeoAI: removing a service node (hospital, transit hub) from a spatial accessibility network and predicting the downstream change in population access. My physics-informed graph learning work can adopt knockout training's causal framing — rather than predicting neural responses, the model predicts catchment-area redistribution after facility closure, with the causal constraint encoded by actual closure events (hospital mergers, COVID-era shutdowns) used to train and validate the graph operator.

② Replace the fixed bottleneck architecture with a learned, compact representation of distributed coding

Extending this paperThe 1-to-1 network imposes a hand-designed bottleneck of exactly 23 units corresponding to known LC types. An extension of the paper would ask: what is the minimum bottleneck size that still reproduces the observed population code, and do compact representations better generalise to held-out stimuli or behaving conditions? A companion Nature paper on compact deep neural network models of the visual cortex develops exactly this question for the primate visual cortex — using model compression and efficiency metrics to derive parsimonious descriptions of visual computation. Applying those techniques to the fly's LC bottleneck would answer whether the 23-unit code is already near-minimal or whether the effective dimensionality of the population code is much lower.

Added value to my researchThe same compression question applies to my spatiotemporal foundation models: geospatial embeddings for cities are typically high-dimensional and over-parameterised. Adopting a compact-bottleneck design informed by this paper's knockout constraint would yield interpretable, minimal representations of urban human mobility — where each latent unit maps to a specific spatial process (commuting direction, time-of-day rhythm), directly supporting explainable ML for spatial prediction and enabling causal attribution of model outputs to concrete urban mechanisms.

③ Transfer the population-code framework to a foundation model of neural activity across stimulus domains

Extending this paperThe 1-to-1 network is trained and validated on a single behavioural context: male-female courtship in a small arena with a specific camera setup. Its predictions degrade when head-fixed imaging conditions differ from free-courtship training conditions, because internal state (arousal, motivation) is not modelled. The natural extension is to pre-train a foundation model of LC activity across many stimulus domains (escape, flight, feeding, courtship) and then fine-tune with knockout-training losses on domain-specific perturbation data — matching the strategy of the foundation model of neural activity that predicts response to new stimulus types. This would convert the 1-to-1 mapping from a single-behaviour tool into a generalizable decoder of visual circuit function.

Added value to my researchThe transfer learning strategy directly mirrors my work on fine-tuning geospatial foundation models: a large pre-trained spatial encoder is adapted with small, domain-specific labelled datasets (e.g., telehealth access surveys in a single metro area). The knockout training loss — aligning model units to causal perturbation outcomes — is the neural science equivalent of my region-specific calibration step, and demonstrates that causal fine-tuning signals are more informative than standard supervised labels.

④ Align the paper's neuron-to-behaviour map with human visual representations to test cross-species population coding

Extending this paperThe paper establishes that Drosophila LC neuron types form a distributed population code for social behaviour, overturning the channel-coding hypothesis for insects. A key unanswered question is whether this population-code structure shares geometric properties with visual representations in vertebrates. A direct extension would embed the paper's 23-dimensional LC population responses and a DNN model of human visual cortex into a shared representational similarity space — using the methods of the paper on aligning machine and human visual representations across abstraction levels — to test whether population-coding geometry is conserved across evolutionary distance and whether the same knockout training framework can reveal analogous distributed codes in the primate visual hierarchy.

⑤ Incorporate arousal and internal state as a continuous modulator of the LC population code

Extending this paperThe authors acknowledge a key limitation: the 1-to-1 network is trained on freely-moving courtship but validated on head-fixed imaging, and behavioural state (arousal level, motivational drive) is not modelled. The paper on arousal as a universal embedding for spatiotemporal brain dynamics shows that a low-dimensional arousal signal explains a large fraction of variability in whole-brain activity across species. Incorporating a latent arousal variable — inferred from the fly's own movement statistics — as a multiplicative gain on LC unit activities would let the 1-to-1 network generalise across behavioural contexts, resolving mismatches between courtship training and imaging validation, and making quantitative predictions about state-dependent shifts in the population code.

Added value to my researchIn urban analytics, "internal state" corresponds to contextual modifiers of human mobility — time of day, weather, special events, season — that shift the effective distance-decay function in accessibility models. Treating these as latent gain factors on spatial interaction terms is precisely the extension needed for my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and R2SFCA models: rather than fixing decay parameters, a learned arousal-analogue (contextual state embedding) modulates how strongly each spatial unit contributes to aggregate flow, yielding a temporally adaptive accessibility metric grounded in causal first principles from neuroscience.
