Quantifying the energy impact of heat mitigation technologies at the urban scale

Shamila Haddad · Wanni Zhang · Riccardo Paolini · Kai Gao · Muzahim Altheeb · Abdulrahman Al Mogirah · Abdullatif Bin Moammar · Tianzhen Hong · Ansar Khan · Constantinos Cartalis · Anastasios Polydoros · Mattheos Santamouris Published 2024-01-11 DOI 10.1038/s44284-023-00005-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A city-scale field-and-simulation study in Riyadh, Saudi Arabia combines daytime radiative coolers and super-cool reflective materials with irrigated / non-irrigated greenery across eight holistic mitigation scenarios. The best scenario cuts peak ambient temperature by up to 4.5 °C — described as the highest reported urban ambient reduction — and lowers annual cooling degree hours by up to 26%. Across 3,323 buildings, mitigation alone saves up to 16% of cooling energy, and combining mitigation with building-side energy-adaptation measures cuts cooling demand by up to 35%.

Why It Matters

  • Moves heat mitigation from material lab to whole-city evidence. Most cool-material and greenery studies stay at the surface or block level; here the chain runs from material radiative properties → mesoscale microclimate → city-scale building energy for thousands of real buildings.
  • Targets an extreme, under-studied climate. Riyadh is a hot, arid Gulf megacity where cooling dominates electricity demand — exactly where heat mitigation has the largest stakes and where water-use trade-offs (irrigated vs. dry greenery) are decisive.
  • Quantifies a record-level cooling benefit. A 4.5 °C peak-temperature drop and up to 35% cooling-demand reduction when mitigation is paired with building adaptation give planners concrete, large-magnitude levers.
  • Compares technologies, not just one fix. Eight scenarios spanning reflective, very-reflective, green, very-green, irrigated and dry combinations let decision-makers weigh cooling gain against irrigation water cost.

Core Methods

  • Scenario design: eight holistic combinations of radiative coolers, reflective (super-cool) materials and irrigated / non-irrigated greenery, spanning a reflective-to-green and wet-to-dry design space.
  • Mesoscale climate modeling: simulate how each scenario reshapes the urban microclimate, yielding modified ambient and land-surface temperature fields relative to a baseline.
  • Climatic-impact metrics: compute peak ambient temperature reduction and cooling degree hours (annual sum of temperature differences above a standard base) per scenario.
  • City-scale building energy simulation: propagate the modified microclimate into urban building energy models for 3,323 buildings to estimate cooling-load and cooling-energy changes.
  • Energy-adaptation coupling: combine heat mitigation with building-side energy-adaptation measures to quantify the joint cooling-demand reduction (up to 35%).
  • Composite heat-risk indicator: integrate remote-sensing-derived heat indicators to map the distribution of heat risk across the city (Fig. 3).

① Add an equity layer to the eight cooling scenarios

Extending this paperThe paper reports city-average peak-temperature and energy gains but never asks who in Riyadh receives the 4.5 °C relief. A direct extension: intersect the per-scenario microclimate and cooling-load fields with neighborhood socio-economic and building-vintage strata, and re-rank the eight scenarios by an equity-weighted cooling benefit rather than the mean — surfacing whether super-cool materials versus irrigated greenery distribute relief differently across vulnerable districts.

Extending it: train a surrogate/emulator mapping (material albedo, radiative-cooling power, greenery fraction, irrigation state, building geometry) → (ΔT, cooling degree hours, cooling load), so the design space becomes continuous and optimizable instead of an eight-point grid.

Extending it: add an explicit irrigation-water budget and trace the Pareto frontier of cooling-energy saved per litre of water — critical in arid Riyadh — to identify where greenery beats reflective materials and where it cannot be afforded.
