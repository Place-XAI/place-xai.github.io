A flexible digital compute-in-memory chip for edge intelligence

Anzhi Yan · Jianlan Yan · Penghui Shen · Yihan Fu · Enyi Zhang · Jingkai Song · Qinghang Zhang · Ziqi He · Xin Li · Zecheng Pan · Ding Li · Yu Dong · Xiaowei Xu · Feng Qi · Tianqi Shao · Bonan Yan · Yi Yang · Houfang Liu · Tian-Ling Ren Published 2026-01-28 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09931-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

FLEXI is a flexible, digital, compute-in-memory AI chip fabricated using commercial low-temperature polycrystalline silicon (LTPS) thin-film transistor technology. By co-optimizing process, circuit, and algorithm, it achieves up to 12.5 MHz clock speed and as little as 2.52 mW power at sub-dollar unit cost, surviving 40,000+ bending cycles. A one-shot on-chip neural-network deployment reaches 99.2% accuracy in arrhythmia detection and 97.4% accuracy in daily activity monitoring from multimodal physiological signals — demonstrating that high-performance AI inference is achievable directly on conformable hardware.

★

Why It Matters

  • Closes the gap between AI and body-conformable hardware. Prior flexible electronics were mostly analogue or memory-only; FLEXI is the first fully digital, reconfigurable compute-in-memory architecture on a flexible substrate capable of real neural-network inference.
  • Sub-dollar cost at commercial yield. Achieving 70–92% operational circuit yield on an existing LTPS foundry process at sub-dollar cost removes the bespoke-fabrication barrier that has stalled deployable flexible AI.
  • One-shot deployment eliminates write latency. Traditional on-chip neural networks require sequential weight writing; FLEXI's one-shot deployment cuts that overhead entirely, making inference near-instantaneous after programming.
  • Clinically relevant benchmarks. 99.2% accuracy on the MIT-BIH arrhythmia dataset and 97.4% activity classification from multimodal physiological signals on a 1-kb chip are performance levels competitive with much larger rigid hardware.
  • Mechanically extreme robustness. Operation after 40,000 bending cycles (down to 1 mm radius) and 6+ months of stable performance set a new durability bar for flexible AI chips in continuous health monitoring.

Core Methods

  • Process–circuit–algorithm co-optimization: jointly design the LTPS thin-film transistor (TFT) fabrication process, the SRAM-based compute-in-memory circuit architecture (RLPU — reconfigurable logic processing unit), and the quantized neural-network algorithms to match hardware constraints.
  • Digital dynamically reconfigurable compute-in-memory (CiM): SRAM cells function both as memory (weight storage) and as compute units via in-situ vector–matrix multiplication (VMM), activating one row per compartment for parallel execution without data movement to separate compute blocks.
  • One-shot on-chip neural network deployment: weights are written simultaneously in a single pass, eliminating sequential write latency; this is enabled by single-ended SRAM writing with 10 ns low-latency switching.
  • Quantized neural networks: binary and multi-bit quantization adapted to the 1-kb SRAM capacity; network weight distributions are mapped to rows by reuse frequency (more frequently reused weights placed closer to RLPUs for best performance).
  • Benchmark evaluation: neural networks trained offline and deployed on chip; accuracy benchmarked on MIT-BIH arrhythmia, MNIST, GSC, FMNIST, and multimodal activity monitoring; confusion matrices and energy breakdowns reported.
  • Mechanical characterization: automated bending (0°–180°, bending radius ≥1 mm, 30–40 cycles/min) and rolling tests (glass rods, radius ≥2 mm) with electrical measurement before and after to quantify performance stability.

Expanded Ideas — this paper's extensibility & added value to my research

① Replace offline-trained quantized networks with active-learning-driven on-chip adaptation

Extending this paperFLEXI currently deploys weights trained entirely offline and written in one shot to the chip; the inference is fixed thereafter. However, physiological signals drift over time (electrode impedance, user-specific baselines) and arrhythmia patterns vary by patient. A direct extension: integrate a Bayesian active learning loop that uses the chip's in-memory compute to identify uncertain predictions and selectively request label refinement, then rewrite weights in a single new shot. This couples FLEXI's one-shot deployment strength with continual adaptation, extending the paper's chip family to a genuinely personalized edge-AI system without cloud round-trips.

Added value to my researchIn my GeoAI work, labeling is expensive (field surveys, healthcare records) and spatially non-uniform. The same Bayesian active-learning hardware paradigm translates to on-device, edge-deployable geospatial models that actively query for ground-truth labels only in high-uncertainty spatial zones — reducing annotation cost for spatiotemporal prediction tasks (disease surveillance, healthcare accessibility mapping) without sacrificing model quality.

② Compress spatiotemporal geospatial models into FLEXI's 1-kb CiM using random sketching

Extending this paperFLEXI's 1-kb SRAM capacity severely limits the network depth deployable on a single chip; the paper stacks multiple chips (FLEXI-1 through FLEXI-4) to scale capacity. A complementary extension: apply random sketch learning to compress the neural-network weights before deployment, so that the effective model capacity exceeds the raw SRAM size. The paper's one-shot write mechanism is naturally compatible with compressed, low-rank weight matrices — potentially enabling FLEXI-1 to serve tasks currently requiring FLEXI-4, reducing cost and power for body-area sensor networks.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal prediction models (graph neural networks for health geography, ML ensembles for accessibility) are too large for edge deployment. Random sketch compression applied to these models, combined with FLEXI-class hardware, opens a path to privacy-preserving, on-device inference for sensitive health and location data — fitting my interest in digital equity and telehealth accessibility in low-resource settings.

Cross-ref (corpus): Random sketch learning for deep neural networks in edge computing — 10.1038/s43588-021-00039-6

③ Fuse multimodal physiological streams with spatiotemporal context via in-memory reservoir computing

Extending this paperFig. 4 shows FLEXI processing multiple physiological channels (ECG, EMG, IMU) independently through a 1D CNN — the modalities are concatenated but not jointly embedded. Extending the paper: replace the fixed CNN backbone with an in-memory reservoir computing layer that non-linearly mixes the multimodal streams into a high-dimensional reservoir state, then reads out a small linear classifier stored in the remaining SRAM. Reservoir computing's sparse, fixed random connectivity is ideal for FLEXI's reconfigurable RLPU, and the zero-shot generalization demonstrated for reservoir computing maps directly onto FLEXI's deployment constraint of one-shot weight writing.

Added value to my researchMy research increasingly fuses heterogeneous data streams — mobile phone records, remote sensing, social media, POIs. An in-memory reservoir layer on conformable hardware that performs sensor-side multimodal fusion before uplink is directly applicable to spatiotemporal data collection in field studies, reducing bandwidth and preserving user privacy in health-geography surveys and GPS-trajectory analytics.

Cross-ref (corpus): Energy-efficient multimodal zero-shot learning using in-memory reservoir computing — 10.1038/s43588-024-00762-w

④ Add spiking-neural-network (SNN) dynamics to FLEXI's CiM for ultra-low-power temporal event coding

Extending this paperFLEXI uses standard quantized rate-coded neural networks. Its arrhythmia task is inherently temporal and event-driven (heartbeat timing, inter-beat intervals), suggesting that spiking representations would be more natural and power-efficient. Extending the paper: map biologically inspired integrate-and-fire neuron dynamics onto the RLPU's digital logic — since the RLPU already supports configurable Boolean operations, SNN temporal coding can be emulated at the circuit level without analog components, keeping FLEXI's all-digital advantage while gaining sparse spike-based computation that further reduces active power below the reported 2.52 mW.

Added value to my researchSparse, event-driven processing is precisely what is needed for continuous ambient health monitoring in urban and peri-urban settings relevant to my health-geography work. An SNN-enabled FLEXI embedded in a wearable patch could perform long-term, low-power arrhythmia surveillance in underserved communities — connecting hardware innovation directly to spatial health equity research.

Cross-ref (corpus): Deep learning incorporating biologically inspired neural dynamics and in-memory computing — 10.1038/s42256-020-0187-0

⑤ Exploit FLEXI's reconfigurability for continual-learning GeoAI models across spatial domains

Extending this paperFLEXI's dynamically reconfigurable CiM allows different weight sets to be loaded in one shot for different tasks — the paper demonstrates this across arrhythmia, MNIST, GSC, and activity monitoring. This reconfigurability is architecturally equivalent to functionally invariant path traversal in neural networks: a single hardware substrate executing multiple task-specific subnetworks without retraining from scratch. Extending the paper: implement structured weight-masking during the one-shot deployment step so that FLEXI maintains non-interfering subnetworks for each task, enabling true continual learning across healthcare tasks (arrhythmia → activity → blood oxygen) on a single chip without catastrophic forgetting.

Added value to my researchMy explainable GeoAI models must generalize across cities, regions, and populations with heterogeneous spatial distributions — a form of continual multi-domain learning. The idea of functionally invariant paths offers a principled way to build multi-city, multi-task geospatial models (accessibility, mobility, health outcomes) that share a common weight structure but adapt to local spatial context, directly advancing my work on fine-tuning geospatial foundation models across diverse urban environments.
