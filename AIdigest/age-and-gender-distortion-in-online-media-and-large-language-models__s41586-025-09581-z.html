Age and gender distortion in online media and large language models

Douglas Guilbeault · Solène Delecourt · Bhargav Srinivasa Desikan Published 2025-10-08 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09581-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Despite no systematic age differences between women and men in the US workforce (per Census), women are represented as younger than men across nearly 1.4 million images and videos (Google, Wikipedia, IMDb, Flickr, YouTube) and in nine language models. This age gap is starkest for high-status, high-earning occupations. The Google Image search engine and ChatGPT amplify this bias: Googling occupation images shifts hiring-age beliefs, and ChatGPT generates younger, less-experienced resumes for women — rating older male applicants as highest quality. The distortion is a culture-wide statistical artifact, not an industry-specific anomaly.

★

Why It Matters

  • First large-scale multimodal ground-truth test of stereotype accuracy. By anchoring gender stereotypes to biological age — an objective, verifiable quantity — the study escapes the usual impasse of unverifiable ground truth and demonstrates that age–gender associations online are systematically distorted relative to US Census realities.
  • Quantifies algorithmic amplification causally. A nationally representative pre-registered experiment (n = 459) shows that simply Googling occupation images shifts participants' age estimates by ~5.5 years between female and male images, and meaningfully biases hiring preferences — a direct causal chain from internet content to human judgement.
  • Audits ChatGPT as a hiring tool at scale. Generating and evaluating ~40,000 resumes across 54 occupations reveals that ChatGPT encodes gendered ageism structurally: female names receive younger, less-experienced resumes and lower quality scores, while older male applicants receive the highest ratings.
  • Reveals a cross-modal bias layer. The same age–gender distortion holds in images, videos, and text embeddings (9 models), ruling out modality-specific explanations (e.g., cosmetics, image filters) and pointing to a systemic cultural bias in internet training data.
  • Bridges social science and AI safety. Provides concrete methodology — word-embedding geometry, crowdsourced face annotation, resume audit prompts — that other researchers can apply to audit intersectional biases in other AI systems.

Core Methods

  • Large-scale crowdsourced face annotation: 6,392 Amazon Mechanical Turk coders (3 per face) classified gender and age (7 ordinal bins) of faces from Google and Wikipedia images using OpenCV face-cropping for standardization; validation confirmed coder reliability and absence of gender-based coding bias.
  • Ground-truth age/gender benchmarking: IMDb–Wiki and CACD datasets provide time-stamped celebrity photographs with known birth dates — enabling objective measurement of on-screen ages without relying on perception or ML. This distinguishes stereotype distortion from perceptual artifacts.
  • Word-embedding geometry of culture: for each of 9 LLMs, a gender dimension (female pole: "she/her/girl/woman/female" sentences; male pole: "he/his/boy/man/male" sentences; 50 sentences per pole) and an age dimension (young/old poles) are projected via cosine distance; each of 3,495 WordNet categories is placed on a 0–1 (female–male) × 0–1 (young–old) grid.
  • Pre-registered human experiment (n = 459): between-subjects design — image condition (participants Google occupation images and report age estimates) vs. control condition (images of unrelated objects, then age/gender belief elicitation); occupation and participant fixed effects used in regression analyses.
  • ChatGPT resume audit: prompt-engineering audit adapted from Armstrong et al. (2024); three generation conditions (control / control-gender / treatment with named applicants); ~39,560 resumes generated and each scored by ChatGPT on 1–100 quality scale; OLS regression with name/occupation fixed effects; robustness checks across temperature settings.
  • Correlation analysis & statistical controls: Pearson's correlation (two-tailed), Student's t-tests, linear regression with fixed effects; robust to word frequency, polysemy, coder demographics, image visual features and geographic variation in Google search.

① Extend the audit to spatial and demographic intersections: mapping where LLM bias concentrates geographically

Extending this paperThe paper audits age–gender distortion nationally (US Census benchmarks) but does not spatialize the bias. A direct extension: re-run the paper's Google Images collection and ChatGPT resume audit for occupation categories disaggregated by metro area or region, then compare the local LLM-estimated age gap to local labor-market gender pay gaps from the American Community Survey. This tests whether cities with greater structural gender inequality also show larger algorithmic distortion — linking the paper's national finding to subnational spatial heterogeneity and identifying geographic hotspots of algorithmic harm.

② Build an intersectional bias monitor using the paper's geometry-of-culture framework as a continuous audit pipeline

Extending this paperThe paper's geometry-of-culture word-embedding analysis is run at a single point in time on static model snapshots. A key extension: deploy the same cosine-distance age–gender measurement as a longitudinal pipeline that re-evaluates model bias after each fine-tuning or RLHF update, producing a bias trajectory for GPT-series and open-source models over release versions. This would empirically track whether debiasing interventions (e.g., safety RLHF) actually reduce age–gender distortion or merely displace it to other demographic dimensions — a claim currently unverifiable because the before/after audit infrastructure does not exist.

③ Apply the paper's census-benchmarking design to spatial accessibility data: does AI distort which neighborhoods are portrayed as served?

Extending this paperThe paper's core methodological move — comparing algorithmic representations to an objective ground truth (Census age distributions) — is generalizable to any domain where objective data exist.
