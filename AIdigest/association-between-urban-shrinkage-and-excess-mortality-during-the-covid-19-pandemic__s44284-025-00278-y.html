Association between urban shrinkage and excess mortality during the COVID-19 pandemic

Gukhwa Jang · Saehoon Kim · Jae Seung Lee Published 2025-07-18 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00278-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 1,142 US counties during the COVID-19 pandemic (Mar 2020–Feb 2023), shrinking counties suffered 165% higher excess deaths and 142% more mortality peaks than growing counties. Counties experiencing simultaneous demographic and economic contraction (declining population and GRDP) fared worse than those declining on a single dimension, and excess deaths rose proportionally with the severity of shrinkage. Socioeconomic disadvantages concentrated in shrinking places — lower income, more older adults, lower education, higher unemployment — were also linked to elevated mortality, underscoring the need for place-based public health strategies.

Why It Matters

  • Reframes urban shrinkage as a public-health risk factor. Depopulation and economic decline are usually studied as planning or fiscal problems; this work quantifies them as measurable drivers of excess mortality, connecting demographic trajectory to lives lost.
  • Distinguishes patterns of decline, not just presence/absence. By separating four urban types (growing; shrinking on both axes; population-growth/economy-decline; population-decline/economy-growth), it shows that compound contraction is the most lethal — a nuance generic vulnerability indices miss.
  • Establishes a dose–response signal. Excess deaths scale with the severity of demographic and economic contraction, giving policymakers a graded, not binary, risk picture.
  • Provides an evidence base for targeting. The concentration of socioeconomic disadvantage in shrinking counties argues for geographically tailored rather than one-size-fits-all crisis response and resource allocation.
  • Fully reproducible. Organized county-level data and R/Python code are released on GitHub, enabling replication and extension.

Core Methods

  • Excess-death estimation: compute monthly observed vs. expected deaths per 100,000 per county; expected baselines derived (R code) from pre-pandemic mortality (from Jan 2015), with excess = observed − expected.
  • Mortality-peak detection: count the number of peaks in each county's monthly excess-death time series (Python), capturing the intensity/recurrence of mortality surges, not just the total.
  • Urban-shrinkage typology: classify counties by the CAGR of population and CAGR of GRDP (2010–2020) into four urban types (growing; shrinking on both; mixed population/economy directions).
  • Group comparison: Kruskal–Wallis tests with Bonferroni post-hoc comparisons of excess deaths and peak counts across urban types.
  • Mixed-effects models: regress monthly excess deaths / peaks on shrinkage severity and socioeconomic covariates (income, older-adult share, education, unemployment, race, density) to estimate dose–response associations while accounting for county-level structure.

① Open the black box: explain why shrinking counties died more

Extending this paperThe paper's mixed-effects models establish that shrinkage and socioeconomic disadvantage are associated with excess deaths, but the relative, possibly non-linear contribution of each driver — and their spatial interactions — stays hidden in additive coefficients. Extending it: refit the 1,142-county panel with an explainable ML ensemble (SHAP-attributed gradient boosting) alongside MGWR, so the model recovers local, non-stationary attributions of how income, older-adult share and compound contraction drive mortality county by county.

② Add a healthcare-access mechanism between shrinkage and mortality

Extending this paperThe paper treats shrinkage as a structural exposure but omits the obvious mediating pathway: as counties depopulate, hospitals and clinics close and travel times to care lengthen. Extending it: insert a measured spatial-accessibility-to-care covariate (2SFCA on facility supply vs. county demand) into the mixed-effects model and test it as a mediator of the shrinkage→excess-death link, converting a correlation into a partially mechanistic chain.

③ Disentangle the health–economy trade-off inside shrinking places

Extending this paperThe paper shows compound (population + GRDP) decline is most lethal, but cannot separate whether deaths track spontaneous economic contraction or the policy/behavioral responses bundled with it. Extending it: merge county mobility and policy-stringency data and apply a causal decomposition of the economic-shrinkage axis, isolating how much excess mortality is attributable to the economy collapsing versus to the protective/harmful behaviors that accompany it.

④ Model the mortality peaks as a spatiotemporal contagion process

Extending this paperThe paper counts mortality peaks per county but treats them as independent county attributes, ignoring that surges propagate across the county network.
