Predicting emergence of crystals from amorphous precursors with deep learning potentials

Muratahan Aykol · Amil Merchant · Simon Batzner · Jennifer N. Wei · Ekin Dogus Cubuk Published 2024-12-18 DOI 10.1038/s43588-024-00752-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A computational framework called a2c (amorphous-to-crystal) uses universal deep learning interatomic potentials to predict, with high accuracy, which crystal polymorphs nucleate from amorphous precursors — a problem previously beyond the reach of molecular modeling or ab initio methods. By sampling tens of thousands of local structural motifs in an amorphous configuration and relaxing periodic subcells under a graph-neural-network force field, a2c identifies the correct crystallization products across 12 diverse inorganic material systems, including oxides, nitrides, carbides, fluorides, chalcogenides and metal alloys, and achieves up to a ~6-fold improvement in selectivity over random structure search.

Why It Matters

  • Breaks a long-standing barrier in materials science. Predicting the crystallization product of an amorphous precursor — governed by nucleation kinetics, not thermodynamics — has been inaccessible to both ab initio molecular dynamics (too slow) and classical crystal structure prediction (ignores amorphous starting state). a2c bridges this gap.
  • Universality across chemical space. A single GNN potential trained on ~7.4 million DFT calculations generalizes across the entire periodic table, enabling polymorph predictions in oxides, nitrides, carbides, fluorides, chlorides, chalcogenides, and metallic glasses without system-specific tuning.
  • Atomic-level explainability. The method does not just output a structure — it reveals which local motifs in the amorphous phase seed each polymorph, giving mechanistic insight aligned with classical nucleation theory and Ostwald's rule of stages.
  • Resolves outstanding experimental mysteries. a2c solved the crystal structure of BiBO3 polymorph (II) — previously synthesized but structurally unsolved — and reconciled the decomposition pathway of metallic glass Fe80B20.
  • Computationally practical at scale. The entire workflow (melt-quench MD + subcell relaxation) runs on a single GPU (P100 or A100), making it applicable to high-throughput materials discovery without supercomputer access.

Core Methods

  • Melt-and-quench molecular dynamics (MQMD): generate representative amorphous configurations by (1) randomly placing ~100 atoms in a cubic box at ~15% volumetric expansion over the stable phase, (2) briefly relaxing with soft-sphere repulsion to remove overlaps, then (3) running three-stage NVT MD (equilibrate melt at Thigh, quench to Tlow, equilibrate at Tlow) using the NequIP GNN force field with a Nosé–Hoover thermostat and 2 fs timestep.
  • Subcell extraction and periodic relaxation (a2c core step): exhaustively enumerate all orthorhombic parallelepipeds (subcells) on an ngrid=10 (default) point grid from the amorphous supercell (~1.7×105 total); apply stoichiometry and atom-count filters to reduce to 103–104 subcells; relax all degrees of freedom (atomic positions, cell shape, cell size) using the FIRE algorithm for ≥150 steps.
  • Universal NequIP GNN interatomic potential (GNoME): E(3)-equivariant GNN with 3 message-passing layers, lmax=2 irreps (multiplicities 64/32/16 for l=0,1,2), 2-layer radial MLP with 64 neurons over 8 Bessel functions, 5 Å cutoff, ~2.4M parameters, trained on ~7.4M DFT calculations with Huber loss using Adam optimizer.
  • Comparison with random structure search (RSS): generate ~10,000 random configurations per system with bond-distance constraints via the AIRSS package, relax with the same GNN force field, compute "acceleration factor" (a2c precision / RSS precision) — found to range from ~1.2× to ~6× across 7 benchmarked systems.
  • Structure analysis and matching: crystal matching via pymatgen StructureMatcher and SpacegroupAnalyzer; structure visualization via VESTA; experimental validation via comparison with diffraction patterns and radial distribution functions.
  • DFT validation: MP2020 Materials Project DFT settings used to validate exact a2c-identified relaxation paths from initial subcell to final target structure, confirming computational accuracy.

① Replacing the static GNoME potential with an active-learning-updated interatomic potential

Extending this paperThe a2c framework relies on a static universal potential trained on GNoME data, which achieves 0.04 eV/atom on MatBench Discovery but may degrade for chemical spaces underrepresented in training (e.g., ternary nitrides, multi-component chalcogenides). A direct extension would embed an uncertainty-driven active learning loop: when a subcell relaxation trajectory enters a high-uncertainty region of configuration space (detected via ensemble disagreement or Bayesian uncertainty), trigger targeted DFT single-point calculations and retrain on-the-fly. This would make a2c self-improving for unexplored chemical systems without full retraining from scratch, extending the paper's applicability beyond the 12 validated systems.

Added value to my researchThis mirrors the coverage challenge in geospatial foundation models: models pretrained on dense urban regions perform poorly in data-sparse rural or developing-country contexts. An analogous uncertainty-driven active data collection loop — where model uncertainty over underrepresented geographic regions triggers targeted survey augmentation — would directly improve my spatiotemporal prediction models for healthcare accessibility in underserved areas, aligning with my telehealth and digital divide research.

② Transferring the "local motif seeds global order" principle to urban structure prediction

Extending this paperThe central a2c insight is that global crystalline order emerges from local structural motifs sampled in the amorphous phase — no global optimization is needed, only local subcell relaxation. An extension of the paper would test whether this holds for non-periodic targets: metallic glass decomposition into multiple competing compositions (as shown for Fe80B20), organic molecular crystals, or hybrid perovskites. The subcell enumeration on an orthogonal grid could be replaced by an equivariant GNN that learns which local motif types most reliably seed each polymorph, enabling a predictive map from amorphous composition to nucleation product without exhaustive subcell enumeration.

Added value to my researchThe conceptual transfer to urban analytics is direct: emergent urban functional zones (analogs to crystal polymorphs) arise from local land-use and mobility configurations (analogs to local structural motifs). My physics-informed graph learning work can adopt this paradigm — building GNN models that predict emergent urban patterns (healthy-neighborhood clusters, job-housing structure) from local neighborhood features alone, rather than requiring city-wide optimization, enabling scalable urban analytics without global census data.

③ Building a multi-potential ensemble for consensus-based crystallization prediction

Extending this papera2c currently uses a single NequIP/GNoME potential. A natural extension is to run the same MQMD + subcell relaxation workflow with an ensemble of foundation machine learning interatomic potentials (GNoME, CHGNet, M3GNet, MACE) and take a consensus vote on the predicted lowest-energy crystallization product. Cross-potential agreement would strongly indicate reliable predictions; disagreement would flag uncertain cases for experimental follow-up. This "Platonic representation" approach — checking whether architecturally diverse potentials converge on the same basins — would dramatically increase confidence in a2c predictions for novel materials systems not well covered by any single training set.

Added value to my researchMulti-model ensemble consensus is directly applicable to my explainable ML ensemble methods for spatial prediction: rather than selecting one best spatial model (MGWR, XGBoost, GNN), I can use cross-model disagreement as a spatially resolved uncertainty map, identifying geographic regions where additional data collection is most needed — precisely analogous to how potential disagreement in a2c flags chemically uncertain predictions needing DFT validation.

④ Coupling a2c with crystal stability scoring to enable synthesis-first materials discovery

Extending this paperThe paper benchmarks a2c's selectivity (does it find the correct crystallization product?) but does not address stability (is that product thermodynamically viable for applications?). A direct extension would connect a2c in a two-stage pipeline: (1) use a2c to generate kinetically accessible polymorph candidates from the amorphous precursor, then (2) pass those candidates through a ML crystal stability evaluator (e.g., via MatBench Discovery-style hull-distance scoring) to rank them by both kinetic accessibility and thermodynamic stability. This closes the loop from amorphous precursor to synthesizable, application-ready material in a fully computational pipeline.

Added value to my researchThis two-stage generate-then-score pattern maps directly onto my EV charging station placement and public facility optimization research: first generate candidate facility configurations (analogous to a2c subcell enumeration), then apply ML-based accessibility-equity-efficiency scoring to filter to the Pareto-optimal set. The a2c paper demonstrates that a constrained generate-then-score pipeline dramatically outperforms global random search — a generalizable lesson for spatial facility-location optimization.

⑤ Scaling a2c to spatially heterogeneous nanomaterials via heterogeneous graph architectures

Extending this papera2c as presented targets bulk crystallization from homogeneous amorphous precursors. However, many technologically important crystallizations occur at surfaces, grain boundaries, or in spatially heterogeneous nanoparticle cores — where interfacial energy dominates over bulk thermodynamics. The paper itself notes this limitation for a-TiO2 thin films where brookite appears due to surface effects. An extension would incorporate a physics-infused heterogeneous graph with separate node types for bulk vs. surface vs. defect atomic environments, each with type-conditioned message passing, enabling a2c-style motif search in nano-confined or surface-dominated geometries.

Added value to my researchThe heterogeneous-graph extension maps cleanly to my multi-source data fusion and spatiotemporal graph learning agenda: just as an a2c heterogeneous graph fuses bulk and surface atomic environments with distinct node semantics, my urban GNN models must fuse semantically distinct node types (residential zones, hospitals, transit hubs, population grids) with different physical roles. The NequIP-derived principle of type-conditioned equivariant message passing provides an architectural template for GeoAI graph networks that respect the semantic heterogeneity of urban spatial systems.
