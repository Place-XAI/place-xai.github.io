Unequal roles of cities in the intercity healthcare system

Pengjun Zhao · Juan Li · Mengzhu Zhang Published 2025-01-02 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00185-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using cell-phone trajectories of 300+ million users, the paper reconstructs China's intercity healthcare system (IHS) from ~2 million nonlocal patient visits and shows that cities play deeply unequal roles. Only 8% of cities are national/regional hubs that absorb the healthcare shortfall of others, while 63% are "unnoticed" destinations that quietly serve returning migrant workers ("pseudo patient mobility") denied healthcare rights in megacities. The IHS produces a Matthew effect: the few resource-rich cities (12%) get richer in local provision, while many shortage cities (35%) fall further behind — new structural inequalities emerge even as the system "fixes" the spatial mismatch of supply and demand.

★

Why It Matters

  • Names a hidden system. It conceptualizes the intercity healthcare system as a real, measurable structure behind nationwide patient mobility — not just isolated "patient travel," but an interdependent web of provision and demand among cities.
  • Surfaces "pseudo patient mobility." A novel category: migrant workers excluded from megacity welfare (via the hukou system) return to healthcare-deprived home cities for care — 19% of all cross-city utilization. This reframes intercity healthcare as a justice problem, not just an efficiency one.
  • Quantifies a Matthew effect in healthcare access. It shows that the very mechanism meant to repair spatial mismatch (encouraging mobility to hubs) amplifies inequality — resource-rich cities benefit most.
  • Directly challenges current policy. China's reform invests in regional hub cities but overlooks the unnoticed pseudo-destination cities; the paper argues these deserve attention and investment to advance equity.

Core Methods

  • Patient-mobility identification (3 steps): infer residence city as the longest nighttime (21:00–08:00) stay over 12 months; flag a cross-city utilization when a user dwells >30 min inside a non-residence tertiary-A hospital AOI; define such users as nonlocal patients.
  • Purpose classification: separate flows into "best", "better", and "pseudo" mobility using a push–pull framework plus migrant-worker logic — pseudo flows are residents of 28 major host cities returning to healthcare-deprived home cities; richness thresholds set via Jenks natural breaks on tertiary-A bed capacity.
  • Dual-role clustering: bisecting k-means on 7 attributes (service scope, catchment depth/diversity, deprivation & economy of partner cities, mobility-type mix) clusters destinations and origins into four types each (elbow + silhouette → k=4).
  • Benefit structure: combine provision × demand roles into 9 compound modes; quantify gains via a trade-style revealed comparative advantage index (Vollrath) — relative import vs. export advantage → "relative gains" per city.
  • Inference & stats: Mann–Whitney U tests across types; Shapiro–Wilk normality checks; network indegree centrality for transport accessibility.

① Turn the IHS into a demand-aware, congestion-sensitive accessibility model

Extending this paperThe paper measures realized patient flows but stops at a typology; it never closes the loop into an accessibility model that asks how reachable quality care is for each city's population once competition for hub beds is accounted for. Extending it: feed the observed origin–destination flows and the 1,404-hospital bed capacities into a spatial-interaction / 2SFCA formulation so the "Matthew effect" can be re-expressed as an accessibility deficit map, and so policy scenarios (e.g., new regional hubs) can be simulated rather than only described.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct gift to my R2SFCA agenda at national scale: instead of assuming catchments, the distance-decay and catchment radius are calibrated against real telecom-derived patient flows, yielding an empirically grounded intercity service-area delineation for tertiary care.

② Replace bisecting k-means roles with network-community + explainable typing

Extending this paperThe four-type role taxonomy comes from attribute-space clustering (bisecting k-means) that ignores the actual flow topology — two cities can land in the same cluster while sitting in entirely different positions in the patient-flow network. Extending it: build the directed patient-flow graph and apply community detection + role/blockmodel analysis, then use an explainable classifier to learn which city attributes (beds, GDP, rail centrality) predict each network role, validating "hub / pseudo-destination / outsider" as topological rather than just statistical categories.

Added value to my researchThis fuses my network community detection and explainable ML for spatial prediction lines: a reusable pipeline that classifies cities' functional roles from mobility graphs and tells you, feature by feature, why a city is a hub or a refuge.

③ Add transport-accessibility scenarios for the disadvantaged "immobile" cities

Extending this paperType-3 cities are flagged as immobile — poor rail accessibility limits residents' ability to reach hub care. Extending it: couple the IHS with a transport-accessibility model and simulate how new high-speed-rail links or PT investment move refuges/caretakers toward "trader" status.

④ Generalize the IHS typology into transferable urban-system rules

Extending this paperThe paper's typology and Matthew-effect claim are China-specific. Its deeper contribution — that cities specialize into provision vs. demand roles in a healthcare urban system — invites a test for universal scaling/structural rules (do hub counts follow a rank-size law? does pseudo mobility scale with migrant share?). Extending it cross-nationally would convert a national case into a transferable theory of intercity service systems.

Added value to my researchThis feeds my urban agglomeration & regional collaboration theme: a framework where healthy-city and healthcare-collaboration policy is grounded in measurable structural laws of the city system, not anecdote — and a template for cross-country FAIR comparison.

⑤ Couple the IHS with epidemic risk and a learned flow generator

Extending this paperThe same intercity patient flows that distribute care also distribute contagion — concentrating patients into a few hub hospitals is an efficiency choice with epidemiological consequences the paper does not examine. Extending it: overlay a metapopulation/SIR layer on the IHS network and use a learned (deep-gravity-style) flow model to generate counterfactual mobility, asking whether decentralizing hubs trades some efficiency for lower systemic outbreak risk.

Added value to my researchThis unifies my SIR / agent-based modeling and physics-informed graph-learning strands: a healthcare-mobility GNN that both predicts patient flows and serves as the substrate for epidemic and resilience stress tests — a dual-use accessibility-and-risk operator.
