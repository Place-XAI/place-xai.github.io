High-speed quadrupedal locomotion by imitation-relaxation reinforcement learning

Yongbin Jin · Xianwei Liu · Yecheng Shao · Hongtao Wang · Wei Yang Published 2022-12-14 DOI 10.1038/s42256-022-00576-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A two-step imitation-relaxation reinforcement learning (IRRL) framework trains a quadruped locomotion controller achieving a stable running speed of 5.0 m s−1 on a MIT-MiniCheetah-like robot — a 35% improvement over the prior state-of-the-art. By first imitating a kinematic reference (unimodal reward surface, fast convergence) and then relaxing toward a full multi-objective reward (convex path to global optimum), IRRL escapes local optima that defeat standard one-step training. A companion entropy-based stochastic stability metric quantifies robustness, sensitively detects period-doubling bifurcation and chaos, and successfully bridges the simulation-to-reality gap.

Why It Matters

  • Breaks a long-standing speed barrier for legged robots. The 5.0 m s−1 trot speed (Froude number 8.5) surpasses the previous RL-controlled quadruped record and narrows the gap with animals of equivalent body mass, demonstrating that RL-based control can rival biological agility.
  • Resolves the multi-objective conflict theoretically and practically. The paper provides a geometric explanation for why standard one-step reward optimization fails at high speed (multiple local maxima on the hyperplane), then proposes a principled two-step fix whose convergence is guaranteed by the unimodal and convex structure of the IRRL path.
  • Introduces a novel, quantitative stability metric. The entropy decreasing rate κ is more sensitive than push-recovery tests or Lyapunov exponents; it detects impending chaos and period-doubling bifurcations before they cause physical failures, providing an actionable tool for controller development.
  • Demonstrates zero-shot sim-to-real transfer to diverse terrains. The controller generalizes across rubber tracks, lawns, concrete, bricks, rock trails, bridges, and stairs without fine-tuning, validating the robustness of the IRRL + dynamics-randomization strategy.
  • Open code and data. Full experimental data and code at github.com/WoodenJin/High_Speed_Quadrupedal_Locomotion_by_IRRL, enabling reproducibility and community extension.

Core Methods

  • Imitation-Relaxation RL (IRRL) — two-step training: Step 1 (imitation) trains the LSTM controller using only the kinematic mimic reward rm, whose reward surface is unimodal, guaranteeing fast convergence to Θm. Step 2 (relaxation) initializes from Θm and optimizes the full multi-objective reward (mimic + balance + velocity + torque), following the convex monotone path on the parametric hyperplane to the global optimum Θf.
  • Characteristic hyperplane visualization: the multi-objective reward landscape is sampled on a 2D ternary hyperplane spanned by three independently optimized controllers, at grid spacing Δ = 0.01 (≈5,151 evaluation points), providing geometric insight into reward surface topology.
  • Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO): continuous-space RL solver; policy modeled as a multivariate Gaussian distribution over joint target positions; maximum 200 M iterations per step; learning rates 0.001 (imitation) and 0.0005 (relaxation).
  • LSTM controller architecture: two fully connected layers (48 units each) with LSTM recurrence; 35-D input (command + gait phase + proprioception); 12-D output (joint target positions ∈ ℝ12); proportional-derivative joint driver (kp = 40 N m rad−1, kd = 1 N m s rad−1); runs at 500 Hz on commercial hardware.
  • Entropy-based stochastic stability: Shannon entropy of the 6D body state (height, roll, pitch, and their velocities) is computed over 10,000 parallel perturbed simulation trajectories. The stage-II entropy decreasing rate κ quantifies controller stability; κ ≈ 0 or κ > 0 flags period-doubling and chaos.
  • Robustness diagnostics: systematic sweep of feedback latency (0–10 ms) and friction coefficient (0.05–1.0) via stochastic stability maps; Poincaré return maps; recurrence plots for orbital periodicity diagnosis.
  • Sim-to-real transfer: dynamics randomization (mass, friction, inertia), observation noise injection (Supplementary Table 3), latency reduction from 6 ms to <2 ms through hardware-level SPI optimizations.

① Extend IRRL’s two-step curriculum to bio-inspired adaptive gaits

Extending this paperIRRL relies on a single manually designed trot reference trajectory, limiting both speed range and terrain diversity. A direct extension: replace the static reference with a bio-inspired, adaptive gait library (trot, bound, gallop), each providing a distinct unimodal imitation target, and let the relaxation step navigate among gaits conditioned on speed and terrain load. This would generalize IRRL’s two-step logic from one gait to a hierarchical multi-gait framework — directly addressing the paper’s acknowledged constraint that “there is no single ideal reference trajectory that suits all speed ranges.”

Added value to my researchThe stage-wise curriculum in IRRL is structurally analogous to coarse-to-fine spatial smoothing in my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation pipeline. Transporting this logic to agent-based urban mobility simulation — first train agents on idealized reference routes, then relax to realistic OD demand distributions — could accelerate convergence and reduce reward hacking in multi-objective urban transport optimization.

② Apply entropy stability as a transferable robustness diagnostic in spatiotemporal models

Extending this paperThe paper’s entropy decreasing rate κ is applied post-hoc to evaluate a trained controller. A natural extension: integrate κ as a differentiable online shaping term during training, penalizing entropy increase over a rolling window of parallel rollouts. This would convert a diagnostic into an active signal, enabling IRRL to avoid period-doubling during training — closing the loop between stability analysis and policy optimization rather than using stability only for post-training evaluation.

Added value to my researchShannon entropy evolution is directly applicable as a stability diagnostic for ensemble spatiotemporal models. When running Monte Carlo ensembles for SIR epidemic spread or mobile-phone trajectory prediction, κ computed over the ensemble distribution at each time step would flag diverging predictive uncertainty — a principled early-warning signal that RMSE-based diagnostics cannot provide, and a natural add-on to my Monte Carlo SIR work.

Cross-ref (corpus): Reinforcement learning in artificial and biological systems — 10.1038/s42256-019-0025-4

③ Replace the LSTM controller with a morphology-agnostic graph neural network policy

Extending this paperThe IRRL paper uses a fixed-architecture LSTM that does not generalize across robot morphologies. A direct extension: represent the robot as a kinematic graph (nodes = joints/links, edges = rigid connections) and train a graph neural network (GNN) policy via IRRL, so the imitation step exploits morphological symmetry and the relaxation step learns morphology-conditioned gaits. A single trained policy could then transfer to new limb lengths, payload masses, or bipedal morphologies without re-architecture — the paper already hints at bipedal generalization in its Supplementary but handles it manually.

Added value to my researchMy physics-informed graph learning agenda directly benefits: techniques for enforcing physical constraints (torque limits, energy budgets) in GNN robot controllers directly transpose to enforcing supply–demand conservation in graph-based R2SFCA accessibility models over road networks — making accessibility predictions physically consistent at the network level.

Cross-ref (corpus): Efficient and scalable reinforcement learning for large-scale network control — 10.1038/s42256-024-00879-7

④ Augment IRRL with a learned world model to close the residual sim-to-real gap

Extending this paperIRRL is entirely model-free (PPO + dynamics randomization), yet the paper acknowledges a remaining sim-to-real gap. A targeted extension: augment IRRL with a learned world model (Dyna-style) that explicitly captures residual dynamics errors — actuator back-EMF nonlinearity, joint elasticity, contact stiffness — and refines the imitation reference to match real hardware. The paper’s stochastic stability framework could then validate model quality before any physical deployment, eliminating trial-and-error hardware tuning.

Added value to my researchA learned world model that reconciles a privileged but imperfect physics simulator with noisy sensor observations is structurally identical to multi-source data fusion in GeoAI: combining a physics-constrained graph prior with ground-truth GPS trajectories. The residual-modelling strategy would sharpen how I reconcile land-use model predictions with mobile-phone trace observations in spatiotemporal transport models.

⑤ Transfer IRRL’s multi-objective decomposition to urban facility-location optimization

Extending this paperIRRL’s core insight is that a multi-objective RL problem becomes tractable by decomposing it into sequential single-objective stages whose reward surfaces are individually well-conditioned. Extending the paper to multi-terrain locomotion: first imitate on smooth ground (unimodal, easy), then relax terrain-by-terrain in increasing difficulty order, compounding robustness incrementally — a principled extension of the paper’s current single-terrain relaxation beyond the treadmill.

Added value to my researchIRRL’s decomposition principle is directly transferable to multi-criteria EV charging-station siting and healthcare facility optimization where efficiency, equity, and resilience objectives conflict. Adopting IRRL logic — first optimize pure spatial accessibility (unimodal, efficient), then relax toward equity and resilience — could avoid the combinatorial local optima that plague Pareto-front methods; the entropy stability metric could simultaneously monitor solution robustness under demand shocks, turning a robot locomotion tool into a GeoAI facility-planning diagnostic.
