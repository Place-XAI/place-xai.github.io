City racial segregation statistics are robust to aggregation bias

Jacob R. Brown · Christopher T. Kenny · Tyler Simko Published 2026-06-16 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00459-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

By simulating millions of alternative Census tract boundary configurations using redistricting software, this study finds that standard racial segregation indices computed from official Census tracts are not systematically biased relative to the distribution of plausible alternatives. Aggregation-induced variability is primarily governed by the number of spatial units: small cities show wide spread across boundary configurations while large cities converge. These findings confirm the practical robustness of Census tracts as units of segregation measurement and provide a general Monte Carlo framework for diagnosing and correcting spatial aggregation error in any spatial statistics context.

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a long-standing methodological anxiety in urban sociology. The Modifiable Areal Unit Problem (MAUP) has shadowed segregation research for decades; this paper is the first to empirically quantify how much official tract boundaries actually distort the major indices (Dissimilarity, Entropy/Theil H, Isolation), finding no systematic distortion.
  • Transforms the MAUP from a theoretical concern into a measurable quantity. By generating probabilistic distributions of segregation scores, practitioners can now report confidence intervals around their official estimates — a direct upgrade in statistical rigor.
  • Reveals the key moderator: city size. Small-city estimates are substantially noisier than large-city estimates, offering a practical rule of thumb for researchers about when to worry most about boundary choice.
  • Provides a general-purpose framework. The redistricting-simulation approach is domain-agnostic: the same logic applies to school attendance zones, health service areas, or any spatial partition used in aggregate statistics.
  • Open data and code. All analysis data and replication code are deposited at the Harvard Dataverse (10.7910/DVN/DLAT2J), enabling immediate extension to other segregation dimensions or geographies.

Core Methods

  • Redistricting simulation via SMC and ReCom: The redist package implements Sequential Monte Carlo (McCartan & Imai, 2023) and merge-split / forest-recombination Markov chain algorithms (DeFord, Duchin & Solomon, 2021) to draw large ensembles of Census-tract-like partitions that respect population-balance and contiguity constraints — the same machinery used to detect partisan gerrymandering, here repurposed for the MAUP.
  • Probabilistic distribution of segregation indices: For each simulated alternative map, Dissimilarity, Entropy (Theil H), and Isolation indices are computed, yielding full empirical distributions. Official tract values are located in those distributions to measure bias (mean deviation) and variance.
  • Aggregation-error decomposition: Variability across simulated plans is partitioned to identify the principal driver — number of spatial units — enabling the paper to characterize when the MAUP matters most (small cities with few tracts).
  • MCMC convergence diagnostics: R-hat statistics (Vehtari et al., 2021) are used to confirm that simulated plan ensembles are well-mixed and represent the target distribution of tract configurations.
  • Bias testing: Official segregation values are compared to the mean of the simulated distribution; absence of systematic offset confirms no directional bias. Optimized extreme maps (maximum/minimum segregation plans) bound the plausible range.

① Apply the same redistricting simulation framework to health service areas and 2SFCA catchments

Extending this paperThis paper restricts its MAUP diagnostic to racial segregation indices computed within Census tract boundaries. The framework — simulate millions of alternative spatial partitions satisfying design constraints, compute a statistic for each, examine the resulting distribution — is domain-agnostic. A direct extension would apply the same pipeline to health service area boundaries (HSAs, HRRs) or to the fixed catchment radii used in 2SFCA accessibility measurement, asking: does the choice of service area shape systematically bias measured healthcare accessibility just as Census tract design might bias segregation? Given that HSA boundaries are arguably more arbitrary than Census tracts, the MAUP may be even more severe there.

Added value to my researchMy R2SFCA and 2SFCA work assumes fixed or smooth catchment shapes. Importing the redistricting-simulation logic would let me generate probabilistic confidence bands around accessibility scores — "this block has accessibility score 0.4 ± 0.12 depending on plausible catchment designs" — directly upgrading the statistical rigor of healthcare access mapping. Combining with MGWR would reveal whether catchment-boundary uncertainty is spatially non-stationary.

② Use the simulated distribution of segregation indices to study implicit racial bias formation

Extending this paperThis paper validates that official tract-based segregation estimates are not biased on average, but shows that small-city estimates carry substantial variance. A natural extension is to feed the full probabilistic distribution of segregation estimates (rather than the single official point estimate) into social-science models that treat segregation as a predictor of implicit bias, economic inequality, or political attitudes. Measurement error in an independent variable induces attenuation bias; the paper's simulated distributions make it possible to apply errors-in-variables correction using the actual empirical error distribution rather than an assumed parametric form.

Added value to my researchIn spatial regression models (GWR/MGWR) that use segregation or socioeconomic context as covariates, MAUP-induced measurement error propagates into coefficient estimates. Having the paper's probabilistic uncertainty bands on the right-hand-side variable would let me implement simulation-based measurement-error-corrected spatial regression — a methodological contribution at the GeoAI / spatial econometrics boundary.

③ Extend the framework to economic and social-network segregation indices

Extending this paperThe paper focuses exclusively on racial composition (Census 2020 race/ethnicity counts). Yet income segregation and social-network segregation — which compound spatial inequality — are measured using the same tract-level aggregates and therefore equally subject to MAUP. Extending the redistricting simulation to income quartile Dissimilarity and network-based segregation indices (which require linking social graph edges to geographic units) would test whether the paper's reassuring finding generalizes or whether economic/social segregation metrics are more boundary-sensitive than racial ones, potentially because income is more spatially autocorrelated at sub-tract scales.

Bringing the redistricting-simulation diagnostic to economic network partitions would let me quantify how much of the community structure I detect is a boundary artifact — a direct robustness check grounded in this paper's methodology.

④ Train a surrogate model to predict aggregation-induced variance from city morphology features

Extending this paperThe paper shows that the number of spatial units (a function of city size) is the dominant predictor of MAUP variance, but city shape, racial spatial autocorrelation, and network topology likely contribute additional variance. Running full redistricting simulations for every city is computationally expensive. An extension would use the paper's simulation results for a sample of US cities to train a lightweight surrogate (XGBoost or GNN) that predicts the simulated variance distribution from cheaper observables — city population, number of tracts, spatial entropy of racial composition, street network statistics — enabling fast MAUP uncertainty estimation for cities where full simulation is infeasible.

Added value to my researchBuilding an explainable ML surrogate for MAUP variance directly aligns with my agenda on explainable spatial prediction. SHAP values on the trained surrogate would reveal which urban morphology features drive aggregation sensitivity — an actionable insight for both census design and for choosing spatial units in GeoAI pipelines.

⑤ Link the paper's probabilistic segregation estimates to temporal trend analysis

Extending this paperThe paper uses only 2020 Census data. A longitudinal extension would apply the redistricting simulation framework to 1990, 2000, 2010, and 2020 Census blocks jointly, generating time series of simulated segregation distributions. This would enable a first rigorous test of whether changes in segregation over time — the central object of study in residential segregation trends research — are robust to boundary choice or whether apparent convergence/divergence in the literature is partly a tract-redesign artifact (Census tracts are redrawn each decade).

Added value to my researchSpatiotemporal analysis is central to my research on urban dynamics. A panel-robust version of this paper's framework — where the measurement-error distribution is estimated separately for each Census year and then differenced — would provide a template for separating genuine spatial change from measurement-artifact change in any longitudinal spatial dataset, including mobile-phone trajectory studies where geographic cells change across data vintages.
