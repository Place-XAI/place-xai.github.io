Unlocking urban climate adaptation imaginaries

Maria Loroño-Leturiondo · Marta Olazabal · William Lewis · Ana Terra Amorim-Maia · Aiora Zabala Published 2025-12-05 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00354-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

There is no single, universal idea of urban climate adaptation. Using an image-based Q methodology with 31 artist-produced illustrations sorted by 79 local adaptation actors across 37 countries, the study surfaces four distinct and sometimes conflicting imaginaries — Green City, Sustainable Lifestyles, Climate Preparedness, and Top Down and Technology Driven. Some echo dominant Western, technocratic paradigms while others call for transformative system change, exposing the limits of one-size-fits-all solutions and arguing that adaptation is a political process requiring negotiation, pluralistic epistemologies, and the centring of local communities. The work also shows that art can surface tacit knowledge that survey language usually hides.

★

Why It Matters

  • Makes the invisible measurable. Imaginaries normally stay hidden inside the beliefs of adaptation actors; this study renders them empirically distinct and comparable, showing that "adaptation" means materially different things to different people.
  • Debunks "common-sense" universal adaptation. By documenting four competing visions — and a global-south skew toward the Top Down and Technology Driven imaginary — it exposes how standardized, Western-modelled solutions can reinforce existing vulnerabilities and maladaptation.
  • Reframes adaptation as politics, not engineering. The findings push planners from "doing adaptation for people" to co-design with people, foregrounding social justice, open governance, and grounded, place-specific goals.
  • Demonstrates a transferable, language-free method. Image-based Q plus bespoke illustrations lets actors across languages and knowledge systems engage emotionally and reveal tensions — a portable engagement instrument for any city.
  • Flags a collective blind spot. All four imaginaries reject loss and damage, migration, maladaptation and innovation, revealing a shared reluctance to confront negative outcomes that the field must address.

79 valid Q sorts from local adaptation actors in 37 countries (100 responses collected; 21 discarded for lacking qualitative validation). Each participant ranked 31 illustrations on a forced quasi-normal grid from +4 to −4, plus open-ended comments on their most/least representative images.

Core Methods

  • Image-based Q methodology: an exploratory, semiquantitative technique that clusters individual discourses into shared viewpoints; participants forced-sort 31 illustrations from "most" to "least" representative of their adaptation vision.
  • Concourse → Q-set construction: 335 crowdsourced statements distilled in a team workshop to 31 concepts spanning adaptation outcomes (e.g., blue/green infrastructure, retrofitting) and processes (e.g., participation, transparency).
  • Art–science translation: each concept rendered as a brief-driven illustration (orange = adaptation-relevant element, blue = background), iteratively de-Europeanized through a 15-expert pilot.
  • Factor extraction: principal component analysis on the Q-sort correlation matrix, Varimax-rotated to four factors using the R qmethod package; factor choice validated via Kaiser–Guttman eigenvalues, inter-factor correlations, explained variance, and 3- vs. 4- vs. 5-factor comparison.
  • Mixed-method interpretation: z scores combined with participants' qualitative comments (29 of 31 illustrations explained) and adaptation-debate expertise to label and characterize each imaginary.
  • Descriptive demographic profiling: for the flagged (most representative) sorts, patterns across gender, organization, experience, north/south, and city size (n = 65).

Expanded Ideas — this paper's extensibility & added value to my research

Each idea stays anchored to this paper: first how the paper itself could be extended (drawing on our corpus), then the added value for my research lines (migis.net — GeoAI, health geography & accessibility, explainable spatial ML, open/FAIR multi-source data fusion).

① Geolocate the imaginaries — couple Q factors with spatial context

Extending this paperThe four imaginaries are currently aspatial: each Q sort carries only coarse north/south and city-size tags. A direct extension is to attach each respondent's city to measurable spatial covariates (heat exposure, flood risk, green-space share, density, adaptation-plan maturity) and model which imaginary is favored where. This would convert "imaginaries vary by context" from assertion into a mapped, testable geography — and reveal whether the global-south skew toward Top Down and Technology Driven tracks actual risk or governance structures.

Added value to my researchThis is a clean template for my space–human coupling work: pair latent attitudinal factors with remote-sensing and POI layers via MGWR/GWR to map where adaptation preferences diverge from adaptation needs — a perception-vs-exposure mismatch index for urban health policy.

Cross-ref (corpus): Progress and gaps in climate change adaptation in coastal cities across the globe — 10.1038/s44284-024-00106-9

② Scale the Q-set with explainable ML on text + image corpora

Extending this paperThe concourse (335 statements → 31 concepts) was distilled by a one-day human workshop, which caps coverage and reproducibility. Extending the paper: build the concourse with NLP topic models and vision-language embeddings over policy documents, social media, and image banks, then use explainable clustering to propose candidate Q items — making the elicitation auditable and repeatable across regions and languages.

Added value to my researchDirectly feeds my multi-source data-fusion and explainable-ML lines: a pipeline that fuses social-media text, news, and imagery into transparent, FAIR-documented latent themes — the same machinery I use to surface health-access narratives from heterogeneous geospatial text.

Cross-ref (corpus): A systematic map of machine learning for urban climate change mitigation — 10.1038/s44284-025-00328-5

③ Turn imaginaries into procedural-justice indicators

Extending this paperThe paper argues adaptation is political and that people-centred views appear in only half the imaginaries — but stops at qualitative interpretation. Extending it: operationalize each imaginary's stance on participation, transparency, and equity into a procedural-justice scorecard, then link those scores to whether a city's actual adaptation plans embed procedural justice, testing if dominant imaginaries predict thin participation.

Added value to my researchReinforces my equity-vs-efficiency work (e.g., EV-charging optimization): a way to encode procedural as well as distributive equity into spatial allocation objectives, so accessibility models account for whose voice shaped the plan, not just who is near a facility.

Cross-ref (corpus): Cities incorporate equity in their climate policies but overlook procedural justice in decision-making — 10.1038/s44284-024-00167-w

④ Link the "health and well-being" blind spot to access geography

Extending this paperA striking result is that health and well-being scores low across all imaginaries despite the established climate–health link. Extending the paper: pair this attitudinal gap with measured spatial accessibility of health and care services in respondents' cities, asking whether actors under-imagine health because their cities already have access — or in spite of severe gaps. That reframes a perception finding as an actionable health-adaptation priority.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my health-geography lane: deploy 2SFCA/R2SFCA to quantify primary-care and mental-health access in adaptation hotspots, and overlay it on imaginary maps to flag cities where climate planning is structurally ignoring health vulnerability.

Cross-ref (corpus): Cities need an integrated and holistic approach to health adaptation in climate planning — 10.1038/s44284-025-00364-1

⑤ Stress-test imaginaries against the maladaptation / gentrification they suppress

Extending this paperAll four imaginaries reject loss and damage, migration and maladaptation — the paper calls this a blurred line between the imagined and the desired. Extending it: confront each imaginary with empirical evidence of its own downside (e.g., green/blue interventions driving gentrification) in a second-round, scenario-based Q, measuring whether exposure to outcomes shifts the imaginaries toward more grounded, negotiation-ready positions.

Added value to my researchConnects to my urban-analytics and agent-based-modeling work: simulate how a favored adaptation imaginary, once implemented, reshapes housing prices, displacement, and facility access — closing the loop between stated preferences and the spatial inequities they produce.
