Foundation models for fast, label-free detection of glioma infiltration

Kondepudi, Akhil · Pekmezci, Melike · Hou, Xinhai · Scotford, Katie · Jiang, Cheng · Rao, Akshay · Harake, Edward S. · Chowdury, Asadur · Al-Holou, Wajd · Wang, Lin · Pandey, Aditya · Lowenstein, Pedro R. · Castro, Maria G. · Koerner, Lisa Irina · Roetzer-Pejrimovsky, Thomas · Widhalm, Georg · Camelo-Piragua, Sandra · Movahed-Ezazi, Misha · Orringer, Daniel A. · Lee, Honglak · Freudiger, Christian · Berger, Mitchel · Hervey-Jumper, Shawn · Hollon, Todd Published 2024-11-13 DOI 10.1038/s41586-024-08169-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

FastGlioma is a visual foundation model pretrained on ~4 million label-free optical microscopy images that detects tumour infiltration in fresh, unprocessed brain tissue in under 10 seconds, achieving an average AUROC of 92.1 ± 0.9% across a prospective, international cohort of 220 patients with diffuse glioma. In a head-to-head comparison (n = 129), FastGlioma outperformed standard-of-care image-guided and fluorescence-guided surgical adjuncts by a wide margin and reduced the rate of high-risk tumour miss errors from 24.0% to 3.8%. The model also generalizes zero-shot to non-glioma brain tumour diagnoses, demonstrating the transformative potential of medical foundation models in cancer surgery.

★

Why It Matters

  • Addresses a global surgical crisis. Safely resectable residual tumour is found in the majority of glioma patients after surgery; corrective procedures cost an estimated >US$1 billion annually in the United States. FastGlioma directly targets this outcome by providing real-time, bedside decision support within seconds.
  • Label-free at scale. By training on stimulated Raman histology (SRH) — a rapid, dye-free optical imaging method — FastGlioma removes the bottleneck of tissue processing and staining that limits intraoperative conventional microscopy, enabling deployment by a single technician using a portable, touchscreen-controlled imager.
  • Demonstrates the "few labels, broad generalization" promise of foundation models. The fine-tuning dataset is ~100× smaller than the pretraining set, yet FastGlioma generalizes across patient demographics, three continents, all WHO diffuse glioma subtypes, and zero-shot to paediatric and other adult brain tumour diagnoses.
  • Clinical safety signal is concrete and large. Reducing the patient-level tumour miss rate from 24% to 3.8% — a 6.3× decrease in relative risk — is a clinically actionable, prospectively measured effect size, not merely a benchmark improvement.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Pretraining corpus
SRH foundation dataset: 2,799 patients, 11,462 whole-slide SRH images, ~4 million unique 300×300 pixel patches from 13 medical centres across the United States and Europe, spanning the full spectrum of CNS tumours and some extra-CNS cancer types. Collected "in the wild" since June 2015 (University of Michigan IRB HUM00083059).
Fine-tuning dataset
SRH tumour-infiltration dataset: 161 surgical specimens from 35 patients, annotated on a 0–3 ordinal infiltration scale by three independent expert neuropathologists, with downstream H&E/IDH immunohistochemistry confirmation. Source: previously published study (ref. 4). Approximately 100× smaller than the pretraining set.

Core Methods

  • Stimulated Raman Histology (SRH): label-free, sub-micrometre optical microscopy generating two-channel images at Raman shifts 2845 cm−1 (lipid-rich structures) and 2930 cm−1 (protein/nucleic acid-rich regions). Fast mode (10× speed) acquires a single-channel, downsampled image, enabling <10 s whole-slide imaging.
  • Two-stage self-supervised pretraining: (1) Hierarchical discrimination (HiDisc) — contrastive patch tokenization using the patch-slide-patient hierarchy as the source of positive pairs, trained on a ResNet-34 backbone; (2) VICReg whole-slide encoder — a vision transformer trained by generating two random views of each whole slide (splitting, cropping, masking patch tokens) and minimizing variance–invariance–covariance loss.
  • Ordinal metric learning for efficient fine-tuning: a data-efficient fine-tuning strategy that enforces pairwise ordering of whole-slide images via binary cross-entropy on score differences, replacing standard cross-entropy or regression losses. Trained on only 161 specimens (35 patients), achieving 88.7 ± 1.6% mAUROC on hold-out validation.
  • Few-shot visualizations for interpretability: cosine-similarity comparison between query SRH patches and a small physician-selected support set (~10 examples each of tumour and normal tissue). No model retraining required; generalizes zero-shot to non-glioma tumour types.
  • Prospective clinical trial design: single-arm, non-inferiority diagnostic trial with pre-specified sample size (n ≥ 1,130 specimens); head-to-head prospective comparison against MRI neuronavigation and 5-ALA fluorescence-guided surgery as standard-of-care controls.
  • Evaluation metrics: mean AUROC (mAUROC) across three binary tasks (0 vs 123, 01 vs 23, 012 vs 3); Spearman correlation (ρ) of continuous infiltration scores with ground-truth ordinal labels; patient-level false-negative (tumour miss) rate for the clinically high-stakes score-0 vs score-3 classification.

① From single-site SRH to multimodal spatial pathology across healthcare systems

Extending this paperFastGlioma's foundation model was pretrained exclusively on SRH images from a single modality. The paper notes that the SRH dataset was generated "in the wild" across 13 centres — a strength for diversity, but the model has no concept of the spatial provenance of specimens (which surgical margin, which hemisphere, which tumour zone). A direct extension: fuse FastGlioma's patch-level features with neuronavigation coordinates and preoperative MRI volumetrics using a multimodal generative AI copilot architecture, enabling the model to simultaneously reason over tissue microscopy and spatial tumour anatomy. This would convert FastGlioma from a per-specimen classifier into a spatially-aware surgical guidance system that can reason about residual tumour topology.

Added value to my researchThe challenge of fusing heterogeneous data from multiple institutions at different spatial resolutions is directly analogous to my work on multi-source data fusion (mobile phone records, remote sensing, POIs, social media) for GeoAI tasks. The FastGlioma multimodal architecture would inform how I integrate spatiotemporal layers of different provenance — for example, coupling fine-grained GPS trajectories with coarse census-level health outcomes — to build spatially grounded predictive models for health geography and telehealth accessibility.

② Fine-tuning geospatial foundation models via ordinal metric learning

Extending this paperThe paper's most technically novel contribution is ordinal metric learning — a data-efficient fine-tuning strategy that enforces pairwise score ordering without requiring dense labels. FastGlioma achieved state-of-the-art performance on just 161 annotated specimens (35 patients). This strategy is agnostic to domain: it requires only that downstream labels have a natural ordinal structure. A direct extension: apply ordinal metric learning to fine-tune whole-slide pathology foundation models for other ordinal clinical tasks (tumour grading, treatment response, fibrosis staging), testing whether the 100× annotation efficiency generalizes across cancer types and imaging platforms.

Added value to my researchMy agenda explicitly includes fine-tuning geospatial foundation models. Many GeoAI regression targets have natural ordinal structure — deprivation indices, accessibility quintiles, healthcare facility quality ratings, air pollution tiers. Ordinal metric learning offers a direct blueprint: pretrain a spatial encoder (e.g., on satellite imagery or street-view data) and fine-tune with ordinal labels drawn from public health or urban analytics datasets, achieving good performance with far fewer annotated samples than standard supervised regression. This reduces the annotation burden for spatially heterogeneous training data — a persistent bottleneck in my MGWR/GWR-based spatial prediction pipelines.

③ Hierarchical self-supervision as a template for spatiotemporal health data pretraining

Extending this paperThe paper's HiDisc pretraining leverages the inherent patch-slide-patient hierarchy of SRH images to define positive pairs for contrastive learning — a principled alternative to random augmentation-based positives. This works because SRH data has a natural multi-level structure. Extending it: test whether a time–census-tract–city hierarchy of mobile phone or clinical visit records produces equivalently powerful representations when used as the positive-pair definition in a spatiotemporal contrastive pretraining framework. The paper already shows that HiDisc outperforms ImageNet transfer on biomedical data — the same principle may hold for geospatial data.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and agent-based modeling work relies on high-quality representations of human mobility patterns. A HiDisc-style pretraining on GPS trajectory data — where positive pairs are defined by the visit-location-user hierarchy (visit from the same user to the same POI category) — could yield transferable mobility embeddings for downstream spatial accessibility prediction, 2SFCA catchment delineation, or telehealth utilization modeling. This would reduce my dependence on manually engineered spatiotemporal features.

④ Zero-shot generalization as a benchmark for equity across patient demographics and healthcare settings

Extending this paperFastGlioma explicitly reports that its performance remained high across sex, age, race, and medical centre — and achieves zero-shot generalization to non-glioma diagnoses. However, the paper does not probe whether its performance is equitable across patients from lower-resource centres, or whether the foundation model's training data (predominantly US and European) encodes biases that would degrade performance in low-to-middle-income countries (LMICs). A direct extension: deploy FastGlioma in a wider international cohort including LMIC centres, quantify performance gaps by centre-level resource proxies (SRH imager age, technician training), and use few-shot adaptation to close any identified equity gaps with minimal local annotation effort.

Added value to my researchEquity across settings is central to my work on telehealth digital divides and spatial accessibility of mental health services. The FastGlioma framework offers a concrete methodology: (1) pretrain a foundation model on high-resource settings, (2) measure zero-shot performance degradation in under-resourced areas, (3) quantify how many locally annotated samples are needed to close the gap. This maps directly to my R2SFCA research question of how many local calibration points are needed for a globally-pretrained accessibility model to generalize to a rural or underserved region.

⑤ Translating the paper's bedside AI adjunct model to a real-time spatial accessibility decision tool

Extending this paperFastGlioma's architectural contribution is not only the foundation model — it is the end-to-end point-of-care AI adjunct paradigm: lightweight foundation model inference (<10 s) + interpretable visualization + actionable binary decision (continue/stop resection). This paradigm could be extended to whole-slide pathology foundation models for other cancer types, as the paper itself suggests (lung, prostate, breast). A key remaining challenge is developing the pathology foundation model for cancer diagnosis and prognosis prediction that integrates FastGlioma-style intraoperative decisions with post-operative molecular and survival prediction, closing the loop between real-time surgical AI and long-term patient outcomes.

Added value to my researchThe "bedside adjunct" paradigm translates architecturally to a real-time GeoAI accessibility decision tool for healthcare facility planning: a lightweight geospatial foundation model that can be queried in near-real-time to output an accessibility score and an interpretable heatmap of underserved zones — analogous to FastGlioma's few-shot tumour heatmaps. My work on 2SFCA/R2SFCA spatial accessibility and healthcare facility optimization would benefit from such a fast-inference design pattern, enabling planners to interactively explore "what-if" facility placement scenarios at the bedside of policy decisions.
