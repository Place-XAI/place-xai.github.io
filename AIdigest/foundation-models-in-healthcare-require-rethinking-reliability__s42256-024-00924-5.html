Foundation models in healthcare require rethinking reliability

Thomas Grote · Timo Freiesleben · Philipp Berens Published 2024-11-11 DOI 10.1038/s42256-024-00924-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Foundation models violate three core assumptions of the standard machine learning reliability paradigm — unseen test data, stable data distribution, and adequate output functionality — making existing evaluation methods conceptually inadequate, not merely practically challenging. New research infrastructure, more rigorous reasoning-capacity tests, and prospective clinical validation are needed before warranted trust in healthcare foundation models can be established.

Why It Matters

  • Diagnoses a conceptual gap, not just a practical one. The authors show that the problems with evaluating foundation models in healthcare are structural and principled — not engineering gaps that more data or larger benchmarks will fix. This reframing elevates the stakes for the entire clinical AI community.
  • Unpacks three broken assumptions. The argument is precise: (1) "unseen test data" is incoherent for models trained on the whole internet; (2) without a defined data source, OOD detection loses its anchor; (3) open-ended generative outputs cannot be judged by classification accuracy alone — each point invalidates a specific tool in the reliability toolkit.
  • Calls out a pervasive evaluation shortcut. The paper warns that recent foundation-model studies sidestep rather than address these conceptual problems, and that relying on medical licensing questionnaires tests memorization, not clinical reasoning or coherence.
  • Proposes actionable steps. Three concrete recommendations emerge: systematic transparency in training data reporting, comprehensive reasoning-capacity testing (coherence, consistency, modal robustness), and prospective evaluation across a broad range of tasks and metrics — providing a checklist for reviewers and regulators.

② Build a prospective benchmark that tests the three reliability conditions simultaneously

Extending this paperThe paper proposes prospective evaluation but provides no concrete protocol. A practical extension: design a three-axis benchmark — (1) genuinely withheld data from facilities not in the training corpus, (2) explicit distributional provenance labels per sample, (3) task-specific adequacy metrics beyond accuracy — then run existing clinical foundation models through it and report where each condition fails. This converts the paper's conceptual audit into a reproducible leaderboard, supplying the "community-wide infrastructure" it calls for.

③ Replace medical licensing questionnaires with coherence-stress tests grounded in causal reasoning

Extending this paperThe paper argues that USMLE-style benchmarks test memorization rather than reasoning. A concrete extension: develop coherence-stress test sets that present the same clinical scenario in semantically equivalent but syntactically varied prompts, then check whether the model's output distribution is invariant (consistency) and whether counterfactual interventions produce causally correct responses (modal robustness). This directly implements the paper's abstract call for "norms of rationality" as a benchmark artifact.

Added value to my researchCausal consistency testing for LLMs mirrors my use of explainable ML with causal constraints in spatial prediction — both ask whether a model's output changes appropriately under a controlled perturbation. Applying such perturbation-invariance tests to health-AI fairness (across race, geography, and socioeconomic strata) aligns with my telehealth/digital-divide research and the spatial equity lens I bring to GeoAI.
