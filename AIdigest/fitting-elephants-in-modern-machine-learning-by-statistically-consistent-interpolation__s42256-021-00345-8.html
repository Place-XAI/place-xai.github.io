Fitting elephants in modern machine learning by statistically consistent interpolation

Partha P. Mitra Published 2021-05-19 DOI 10.1038/s42256-021-00345-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Interpolating noisy training data — once dismissed as textbook overfitting — can be statistically consistent: provably optimal in the large-data limit. The wiNN algorithm (weighted interpolating nearest neighbours) demonstrates this rigorously, explaining why overparameterized deep networks generalize well despite fitting every training point exactly. This reframes modern ML as a third epistemology distinct from physics (sparse priors, few parameters) and biological brains (rich innate mesoscale circuitry), one that succeeds purely through empirical data accumulation but structurally lacks explanatory power and extrapolation ability.

Core Methods

  • wiNN estimator (weighted interpolating nearest neighbours): a Nadaraya-Watson kernel estimator with singular weights w(x, x(i)) = (r(k+1)/r(i))δ − 1, 0 ≤ δ < d/2; the singularity at the origin enforces exact interpolation of training labels.
  • Statistical consistency proofs: bias-variance decomposition of excess risk yields optimal sample-size-dependent k ~ n2α/(2α+d); regression risk O(n−2α/(2α+d)) matches minimax lower bounds for Holder-α functions in dimension d.
  • Double-descent analysis: multivariate linear regression with random design matrix Y = Xβ (m observations, p parameters) explains why generalization error diverges at m = p (square, singular matrix) then improves in the overparameterized regime p > m.
  • Estimator landscape taxonomy (Fig. 3): conceptual mapping of known estimators — wiNN, Hilbert kernel interpolator, simplex interpolation, kernel machines, infinitely wide nets — onto the (consistent × interpolating × parametric/non-parametric) axes.

② Use SCI's adversarial-island mechanism to spatially map label-noise fragility in healthcare accessibility models

Extending this paper The paper proves that any interpolating learner creates misclassification islands in the immediate neighbourhood of mislabelled training points — and in regions with finite density of mislabelled points, adversarial examples are dense in the large-sample limit. An extension: map these adversarial regions geographically for spatial ML models by identifying zones where label noise in training data (e.g. incorrect POI categories, mismatched census boundaries, geocoding errors) creates brittle local predictions. This operationalizes the paper's theoretical insight as a spatial uncertainty diagnostic.
