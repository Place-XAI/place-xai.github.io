A randomized controlled trial evaluating the effects of nurse-led triage of 911 calls

Kevin H. Wilson · Rebecca A. Johnson · Chrysanthi Hatzimasoura · Robert P. Holman · Ryan T. Moore · David Yokum Published 2024-05-24 DOI 10.1038/s41562-024-01889-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Washington, DC's "Right Care, Right Now" nurse triage line — embedding registered nurses directly in the 911 dispatch loop — safely and significantly reduced emergency ambulance dispatches from 97% to 56% and ambulance transports from 73% to 45% among 6,053 randomized low-acuity callers. Within 24 hours, Medicaid beneficiaries in the treatment group were more than three times as likely to visit a primary care physician (8.2% vs. 2.5%), demonstrating that nurse-led triage can redirect non-emergent demand toward more appropriate care without measurable safety harms. Longer-term effects (six months) on emergency department visits and Medicaid expenditures were not statistically detectable, likely due to call-level randomization complexities.

★

Why It Matters

  • First large-scale RCT of embedded nurse triage in a 911 system. Prior evidence was entirely observational (before-after designs); call-level randomization here isolates the causal effect from confounding temporal trends — a methodological leap for emergency-care policy.
  • Achieves equitable care redirection, not just cost-cutting. Among Medicaid beneficiaries (50.7% of callers, predominantly Black adults), PCP uptake tripled within 24 hours — showing the intervention can function as an equity lever, channeling underserved callers into appropriate primary care.
  • Safety held under real-world conditions. All 13 callers later diagnosed with time-sensitive illness were rapidly triaged to ambulance response; only 7 of 3,182 (0.2%) experienced care delays exceeding two minutes — an important proof of safety that was lacking in observational literature.
  • Quantifiable system-level relief. Approximately 900 ambulance transports avoided, saving ~186,000 minutes of ambulance availability and an estimated US$764,000 over 10.5 months in a single jurisdiction — readily scalable across the 240 million annual US 911 calls.
  • Pre-registered, transparent design. The analysis plan was publicly registered before data collection (osf.io/xderw), and code is openly published — exemplary open-science practice for health-policy RCTs.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Trial setting
Washington, DC — one US city with among the highest per-capita 911 medical-response volumes nationally. Trial period: 19 April 2018 to 1 March 2019 (~10.5 months).
Sample size
6,053 callers randomized 1:1 (treatment: n = 3,030; control: n = 3,023). Initially screened 8,761 eligible calls; 2,708 excluded (primarily outside operating hours or cancelled).
CAD dispatch data
DC Fire and Emergency Medical Services (FEMS) Computer-Aided Dispatch (CAD) system — time-stamped event sequences for all enrolled calls, used to determine ambulance dispatch and transport outcomes. Provided by DC's Office of the Chief Technology Officer.
Medicaid claims data
DC Department of Health Care Finance (DHCF) — claims records for 50.7% of callers (n = 3,067) matched to Medicaid via SOUNDEX fuzzy matching on name/date-of-birth. Used to track ED visits, PCP utilization, and health expenditures at 24 hours and 6 months. Data contain protected health information (PHI); not publicly posted.
ED visit classification
NYU Center for Health and Public Service Research ED utilization coding scheme — ICD-10 codes classified into non-emergent, emergent/PCP-treatable, emergent–preventable, and emergent–not-preventable categories.
Chief complaint criteria
Criteria-Based Dispatch system (King County, WA, 2013) — ~46 eligible chief complaints (e.g., allergic reactions without breathing difficulty, migraines, panic attacks) curated by medical experts; list publicly available in pre-analysis plan Appendix B.
Pre-registration
OSF pre-registration: osf.io/xderw (17 April 2018); ClinicalTrials.gov: NCT05589168 (registered retroactively 2022). Code: GitHub (thelabdc).
Analysis software
Python v.3.8.6 and R v.4.1.2.

Core Methods

  • Embedded randomized controlled trial (RCT): the CAD system uniformly randomized eligible calls 1:1 at the point of dispatch — call-level (not patient-level) randomization. The "warm hand-off" to a nurse was the treatment; business-as-usual ambulance dispatch was the control.
  • Eligibility criteria: adult, non-incarcerated, first-party caller; chief complaint in ~46 approved low-acuity categories; nurse available. Eligibility expanded from 16-hour to 24-hour operation in January 2019.
  • Primary statistical model: logistic regression of binary outcomes on a treatment indicator (y_c ~ t_c) with two-tailed z-test; linear regression for log-transformed Medicaid expenditures. Bonferroni correction applied across eight hypotheses (α = 0.05, power = 0.80).
  • Medicaid linkage via fuzzy matching: DC DHCF used exact date-of-birth matching and SOUNDEX phonetic name matching to link callers to Medicaid enrollment records — enabling downstream claims-based outcome measurement.
  • ICD-10-based outcome classification: NYU coding scheme used to classify ED visits as non-emergent or PCP-treatable; DC DHCF's own scheme for PCP visit identification.
  • Safety audit: 100% of nurse call audio reviewed for the first six months; ongoing 10% random audio review plus 100% review of selected diagnosis categories thereafter; all 3,182 treatment calls reviewed at study conclusion.
  • Power analysis: based on historical call data; targeted n = 5,400 (achieved n = 6,053 after two extensions from the original 6-month window).

① Spatial stratification of triage effectiveness: where does nurse redirection work best?

Extending this paperThe trial treats Washington, DC, as a single spatial unit, aggregating call outcomes across all neighborhoods. But low-acuity 911 calling patterns, proximity to FQHCs, and Medicaid enrollment rates are spatially heterogeneous. A direct extension: geocode caller locations from the CAD data, join with spatial access to FQHCs (2SFCA-based reachability scores), and model triage success rate (nurse-to-PCP diversion) as a function of local healthcare-access geography. This would reveal whether the nurse triage effect is larger or smaller in neighborhoods with poor primary-care proximity — and whether spatial gaps in FQHC coverage systematically undermine the trial's intent to provide "right care, right now."

Added value to my researchThis is the natural application of my 2SFCA/R2SFCA healthcare accessibility framework to emergency-care triage. The spatial access score around each caller's location becomes the moderating variable explaining heterogeneous treatment effects — directly connecting my accessibility measurement work to a causal inference context and providing a principled way to identify where NTL programs need FQHC co-investment. The result would be a spatial effectiveness map that could guide facility-location decisions for federally qualified health centers.

② From call-level to patient-level analysis using mobile phone mobility traces

Extending this paperThe paper's key limitation is that call-level randomization precludes reliable patient-level tracking — the same individual can be in both treatment and control across different calls, and without robust name/DOB data, repeat callers cannot be identified. An extension: supplement CAD/Medicaid data with opt-in mobile phone location data (where legally permissible) to link repeated calls to persistent individuals, enabling a proper intent-to-treat analysis at the patient level. This would also allow measuring the paper's unobserved outcomes — "did the caller subsequently establish a PCP relationship?" — using mobility patterns to infer clinic visits even outside Medicaid coverage.

Added value to my researchThis aligns with my multi-source data fusion work (mobile phone records, GPS trajectories). The mobility-linkage approach would allow me to study healthcare-seeking behavior trajectories — a spatiotemporal analysis of how a single emergency-triage contact changes subsequent spatial patterns of care utilization over time. Combined with Medicaid claims, this creates a rare longitudinal window into the spatial health-seeking dynamics of urban Medicaid populations.

Cross-ref (corpus): Mobile phone data show spatial and socioeconomic inequalities in hospital utilization — 10.1038/s44360-026-00100-6

③ Scaling the nurse triage model across US cities using intercity healthcare system structure

Extending this paperThe paper's evidence base is a single city; the authors explicitly call for multi-jurisdiction replications to inform meta-analyses. A structural extension: before running new trials, use intercity healthcare network analysis to predict which cities are most likely to replicate DC's results. Cities with high per-capita EMS volume, strong Medicaid penetration, dense FQHC networks, and low baseline primary-care utilization by the low-acuity 911-calling population are most promising candidate sites. This would prioritize trial investments by modeling the structural preconditions for NTL success across US cities.

My spatiotemporal kernel density methods could map the geographic concentration of non-emergent 911 demand nationally, identifying priority intervention zones before any new trial is launched.

④ Replacing the nurse with an AI triage agent — and measuring the equity gap

Extending this paperThe DC NTL relied on human nurses using proprietary software (Global Medical Response's nurse triage system). A timely extension: compare nurse-led triage against an LLM-powered triage agent embedded in the same dispatch loop, using the paper's own pre-registered outcomes as benchmarks. The comparison can be designed as a three-arm trial (business-as-usual, nurse, LLM-agent) with the same call-level randomization, measuring not only utilization shifts but the safety and equity profile of AI triage — particularly for the Medicaid/Black caller majority in the original trial who may face differential AI performance.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my interest in explainable AI for health policy. An LLM triage agent needs to be spatially aware — it should know the caller's approximate location, the nearest open FQHC, available non-emergent transport, and likely travel time before recommending a care pathway. This marries my geospatial foundation model fine-tuning agenda with a consequential clinical application: a location-conditioned triage recommender whose decisions can be explained and audited by race, neighborhood, and acuity.

⑤ Embedding the NTL within a telemedicine access network to close the digital-divide gap

Extending this paperThe paper shows the nurse line was most effective within 24 hours but effects fade over six months — suggesting the intervention creates a "first contact" but does not durably connect callers to ongoing primary care. An extension: after the nurse interaction, route callers directly into a telemedicine follow-up pathway — a scheduled video or phone consultation within 72 hours with the same FQHC. This would test whether coupling the NTL with an integrated telehealth step extends the short-term diversion effect into durable primary-care engagement, especially for callers who lack transport or face language barriers for in-person follow-up.

Added value to my researchThis addresses my core telehealth and digital divide research theme. The NTL creates a natural experiment: callers diverted to telehealth follow-up vs. in-person FQHC vs. no follow-up allow me to model the spatial digital divide in telehealth uptake — whether broadband access at the caller's home address predicts successful telemedicine engagement. My 2SFCA framework can be extended to measure effective telehealth accessibility (combining broadband coverage, device ownership, and appointment slot availability) as a predictor of which NTL callers durably shift to primary care.
