Disruption of the oak tree microbiome with urbanization

Kathryn F. Atherton · Chikae Tatsumi · Isabelle Frenette · David Heaton · Ian A. Smith · Lucy R. Hutyra · Pamela H. Templer · Jennifer M. Bhatnagar Published 2025-10-03 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00322-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Urbanization reshapes the oak tree microbiome across leaves, roots and soils: it reduces mutualistic symbionts (beneficial root/leaf fungi) while boosting decomposers and pathogens — including microbes relevant to plant, animal and human health. These shifts track urban stressors (heat, drought, atmospheric aerosol deposition) and come with altered biogeochemistry: greater potential for nitrogen loss as the greenhouse gas N2O and reduced methane consumption. Overall microbiome diversity drops — especially among non-pathogens — potentially eroding the ecological and health benefits that urban trees are meant to provide.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reframes "green = healthy" for cities. Planting trees is a cornerstone of urban climate and health policy, but this work shows the invisible half of the tree — its microbiome — is degraded by the very urban conditions trees are planted into, so canopy cover alone may overstate the ecosystem benefit delivered.
  • Connects urban form to greenhouse-gas fluxes. Urban tree soils show higher N2O-production potential and lower methane uptake — meaning urbanization may quietly turn a carbon/climate asset into a partial source, with direct implications for city-scale GHG accounting.
  • A One Health early-warning signal. Enrichment of plant, animal and even zoonotic pathogens in urban tree microbiomes links the urban environment to potential human and ecosystem exposure pathways — a tangible biological cost of urbanization.
  • Mechanistic, not just descriptive. Shifts correlate with named stressors (heat, drought, aerosol deposition), giving planners candidate levers (irrigation, air-quality, microclimate) rather than a black-box "cities are bad for trees" result.

Core Methods

  • Gradient sampling design: compare oak microbiomes along an urban-to-rural gradient across three host compartments (leaves, roots, soils) to isolate the urbanization signal.
  • Amplicon (marker-gene) sequencing: characterize bacterial and fungal community structure and composition from each compartment.
  • Functional-group classification: partition the community into mutualists, decomposers and pathogens (incl. plant/animal/zoonotic pathogens) to quantify urbanization-driven enrichment/depletion.
  • Functional/biogeochemical inference: estimate cycling potential — N2O production (nitrogen loss) and methane consumption — from community profiles.
  • Diversity analysis: compare alpha/beta diversity urban vs rural, separating pathogenic from non-pathogenic taxa.
  • Stressor correlation: relate observed microbiome shifts to measured urban stressors (heat, drought, atmospheric aerosol deposition).

① Scale the single-gradient design to a continental, multi-city microbiome map

Extending this paperThe study resolves the urbanization signal along one Boston-area gradient, which leaves the "universality" of the disruption untested. A natural extension is to harmonize this oak-microbiome design with continental urban-soil surveys and ask whether urban microbiomes homogenize or diverge across cities and climates — replacing a city case study with a transferable, multi-city model of microbiome disruption.
