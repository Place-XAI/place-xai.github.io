A novel machine learning framework for automated biomedical relation extraction from large-scale literature repositories

Lixiang Hong · Jinjian Lin · Shuya Li · Fangping Wan · Hui Yang · Tao Jiang · Dan Zhao · Jianyang Zeng Published 2020-06-08 DOI 10.1038/s42256-020-0189-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

BERE, a novel ML framework combining latent tree learning (Gumbel Tree-GRU), Bi-GRU, and multi-head self-attention, automatically extracts drug–drug and drug–target interactions from biomedical literature without manual annotation via distant supervision. On the DDI’13 benchmark it achieves an F1 of 73.9% (best among six baselines), and on a new distantly supervised DTI dataset it reaches AUPRC 0.524 / F1 0.625. Applied to ~2.2 million PMC full-text articles, BERE identified two novel kinase targets of nintedanib—JAK2 and EGFR—subsequently confirmed by wet-lab assays, demonstrating its capacity to advance drug discovery beyond what exists in current databases.

★

Why It Matters

  • Closes the gap between unstructured literature and structured knowledge bases. Over 30 million biomedical articles exist; BERE automates the costly expert-curation step that currently bottlenecks databases such as DrugBank, CTD, and SIDER.
  • Eliminates the need for manual annotation via distant supervision. By aligning known relation facts in DrugBank against nearly 20 million PubMed abstracts, BERE generates a large training corpus automatically—a scalable alternative to expensive human labeling.
  • Jointly captures sentence syntax and semantics. The hybrid Gumbel Tree-GRU + Bi-GRU + self-attention architecture encodes both local constituency structure and long-range dependencies, outperforming CNN- and plain RNN-based models that ignore one dimension.
  • Closes the loop with wet-lab validation. Predictions of nintedanib inhibiting JAK2 (IC50 = 290.1 nM) and EGFR (IC50 = 3,463.5 nM) were confirmed by mobility-shift assays—a rare end-to-end demonstration from text to biology.
  • Publicly reproducible. Code and datasets are released on GitHub and archived on Zenodo, enabling community benchmarking and extension.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

DDI’13 dataset
Manually annotated single-sentence corpus drawn from 784 DrugBank texts and 233 MedLine abstracts; five DDI relation types (NA, ADVICE, EFFECT, MECHANISM, INT); ~77% training / 23% test split after negative-sentence filtering.
DTI dataset (new)
Distantly supervised drug–target interaction corpus built by aligning DrugBank relation facts against ~20 million PubMed abstracts; six relation types (NA, substrate, inhibitor, agonist/antagonist, unknown, other); sentences >64 words or with >64 repetitions of the same DTI were removed.
PMC prediction set
Unlabelled set of ~2.2 million PubMed Central full-text articles (abstracts excluded to prevent trivial extraction); sentences co-mentioning nintedanib and any kinase were retrieved by dictionary-based NER for novel DTI discovery.
Word embeddings
Pre-trained on PubMed and PMC texts using word2vec (exact corpus size not stated in article); fine-tuned jointly during BERE training. POS embeddings initialized randomly and updated during training.
DrugBank
Primary knowledge base supplying DTI facts for distant annotation and the drug/target name dictionary for NER. Ambiguous names removed to improve recognition precision. Version not specified in article.

Core Methods

  • Hybrid encoding network: input words are represented by concatenating pre-trained word embeddings and randomly initialized POS embeddings. A multi-head self-attention layer with residual connection captures long-range word dependencies; a Bi-GRU captures local sequential context.
  • Gumbel Tree-GRU (latent tree learning): a greedy, end-to-end differentiable method that implicitly learns constituency-based parse trees without an external parser. Adjacent vectors are composed by a shared Tree-GRU cell; the best composition is selected via a scoring function using the Gumbel–Softmax estimator to ensure differentiability during training. After M−1 steps for an M-word sentence, a single root vector encodes both syntactic and semantic features.
  • Entity-context concatenation: GRU output vectors for Entity1 and Entity2 are concatenated with the Gumbel Tree-GRU root vector, forming a 6u-dimensional sentence representation that amplifies associations between target entities.
  • Multi-instance learning with attention-based sentence aggregation: for a bag of N sentences co-mentioning an entity pair, an attention weight βi is computed per sentence; the bag is represented as a weighted sum that filters irrelevant sentences and surfaces the most diagnostic evidence.
  • Distant supervision: all PubMed/PMC sentences co-mentioning a drug–target pair are automatically labeled with the DTI fact in DrugBank, eliminating manual annotation. Assumes at least one sentence in a bag actually expresses the relation.
  • Classification and optimization: aggregated bag features are passed through a two-layer feed-forward network (ReLU) and a softmax classifier, optimized with cross-entropy loss via Adam with mini-batch training. Dropout applied after input representation and before classifier to reduce overfitting.
  • Dictionary-based NER: drug and target names recognized using a DrugBank-derived dictionary (ambiguous terms removed) for both DTI dataset construction and novel DTI discovery from the PMC corpus.
  • Evaluation: micro-averaged F1 on DDI’13; precision–recall curve, AUPRC, and F1 on the distantly supervised DTI dataset. Wet-lab kinase mobility-shift assays used for biological validation of predicted novel DTIs.

① Replace word2vec sentence encoding with a biomedical pre-trained language model

Extending this paperBERE’s input layer uses word2vec embeddings pre-trained on PubMed/PMC—a strong baseline in 2020, but contextual embeddings from models such as BioBERT or PubMedBERT have shown substantial gains on biomedical NLP tasks. A direct extension is to replace BERE’s encoding layer with a transformer-based contextual encoder, feeding its contextualized token representations into the Gumbel Tree-GRU. This would let the latent-tree syntactic learning operate on richer, sense-disambiguated representations, potentially raising the DDI’13 F1 well above 73.9% without changing the multi-instance learning head. Combining this with retrieval-augmented synthesis of the literature at inference time—fetching evidence from newly published papers—would also address BERE’s training-data temporal cut-off problem.

Added value to my researchFor GeoAI text mining, the same principle applies: combining transformer-based geoparse models with spatial graph networks could extract location–health-outcome relations from clinical notes, social media, and policy documents at scale—directly feeding my spatial accessibility and health geography pipelines with automatically extracted evidence on facility utilization patterns and population health needs in underserved areas.

② Replace the static DrugBank oracle with a continuously updating graph knowledge base

Extending this paperBERE’s distant supervision relies on a fixed snapshot of DrugBank as its annotation oracle, meaning any drug–target pair absent at training time remains unlabelled. A natural extension is to couple BERE with a dynamic knowledge graph that is updated as BERE itself discovers and validates new relations—creating a self-reinforcing loop: new wet-lab confirmations (like JAK2/EGFR) are added to the oracle, new distant labels are generated, and the model is periodically re-trained. Molecular graph neural networks (such as MolCLR) could supply structure-based plausibility priors to score newly predicted DTIs before investing in wet-lab validation, reducing experimental cost substantially.

Added value to my researchAnalogous feedback loops exist in spatial data science: POI databases (analogous to DrugBank) are incomplete and lag behind on-the-ground reality. A similar active-learning framework—where my spatial models flag high-uncertainty predictions for targeted field verification and then update the training set—could continuously improve healthcare facility accessibility maps, benefiting my 2SFCA-based primary care and mental health accessibility analyses in data-sparse regions.

③ Extend multi-instance learning from bag-of-sentences to hierarchical bag-of-documents

Extending this paperBERE aggregates sentences within a bag via a single attention layer but treats each sentence as an isolated unit—losing cross-sentence coreference and discourse context. The paper explicitly acknowledges misclassifications caused by referent ambiguity across sentences (e.g., PLK1 and AXL). A structural extension is a second-level hierarchical attention operating over sentences within a document and then over documents within the bag, capturing coreference chains, entity salience signals, and discourse structure. This addresses BERE’s identified failure mode without changing the core Gumbel Tree-GRU architecture, and aligns with how protein–ligand affinity GNNs handle multi-level graph hierarchies.

Added value to my researchHierarchical multi-source fusion is precisely what my multi-source data fusion agenda requires: combining mobile phone records, social media posts, and POI reviews to predict spatial health outcomes needs exactly this—weighting evidence across heterogeneous text sources at both the record level and the source level. A hierarchical attention module trained on biomedical multi-instance learning transfers to geolocated urban sensing with minimal architectural modification.

④ Apply explainability techniques to audit BERE’s sentence scoring for clinical trust

Extending this paperBERE ranks sentences by attention weight and surfaces the top-scoring sentence as “representative” evidence for each predicted relation (Tables 2–3). However, attention weights are not faithful explanations—high attention does not guarantee causal relevance. A rigorous extension would apply post-hoc explainability methods (integrated gradients, LIME on the Bi-GRU layer, or probing classifiers on Gumbel Tree-GRU intermediate nodes) to validate which syntactic constituents actually drive each prediction. This is especially important for clinical deployment where domain experts must audit why BERE asserted a drug–target inhibitory relation before committing resources to laboratory assays.

Added value to my researchExplainable ML for trust and auditability is central to my GeoAI work—specifically, using SHAP or LIME to decompose spatial predictions and identify which features (population density, transit network, facility type) drive accessibility scores or health-outcome predictions. BERE’s sentence-level attention scoring is an analogue of tabular feature importance, and lessons from high-stakes biomedical explainability directly inform how I design interpretable outputs for urban planners and health policymakers who must act on my model predictions in policy-relevant contexts.

⑤ Adapt BERE’s architecture for spatial-knowledge relation extraction from urban policy texts

Extending this paperBERE’s full pipeline—distant supervision from a structured knowledge base, syntactic–semantic hybrid encoding, and multi-instance bag aggregation—is domain-agnostic. A compelling extension is to apply it to urban and public-health policy corpora: extracting relations between spatial entities (neighborhoods, facilities, transit stops) and outcome entities (health indicators, access barriers, demographic vulnerabilities) from planning documents, public-health reports, and EHR discharge summaries. The “DrugBank oracle” role would be filled by curated spatial knowledge graphs such as OpenStreetMap facility taxonomies or national healthcare-facility registries, and the PMC corpus role by repositories of urban planning grey literature and policy documents.
