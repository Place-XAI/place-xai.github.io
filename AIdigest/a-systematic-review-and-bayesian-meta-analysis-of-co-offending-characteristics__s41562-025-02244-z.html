A systematic review and Bayesian meta-analysis of co-offending characteristics

Ruslan Klymentiev · Dayle Harvey · Luis E. C. Rocha · Christophe Vandeviver Published 2025-06-23 DOI 10.1038/s41562-025-02244-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 110 studies from 17 countries, roughly 29% of offences and 50% of offenders involved co-offending, with an average group size of 2.73. Property crimes show higher co-offence prevalence driven by practical collaboration, while violent crimes have larger group sizes as a by-product of group dynamics. Young and female offenders co-offend more persistently, but cross-study comparison remains hard due to inconsistent reporting — underscoring the urgent need for standardized co-offending metrics.

★

Why It Matters

  • First global quantitative synthesis of co-offending. No prior meta-analysis synthesized co-offence prevalence and group size across this breadth — nearly 46 million offences and 3.5 million offenders from 17 countries collapsed into a single posterior estimate.
  • Policy implication: arresting one offender may not reduce crime. The finding that half of all offenders co-offend at some point directly challenges incarceration-focused strategies, since co-offenders can recruit replacements or redistribute roles.
  • Age and crime-type nuances reshape interventions. Youth offenders are 30 percentage points more likely to co-offend and form larger groups, pointing prevention resources toward early peer-influence disruption rather than individual deterrence.
  • Standardization call with concrete measurement recommendations. The paper proposes reporting co-offence participation rates alongside prevalence and granular group-size counts — actionable guidance for the research community.
  • Open and reproducible science. All data, code, and R notebooks are deposited on the Open Science Framework (OSF: RUF9K), enabling replication and updating.

Core Methods

  • PRISMA 2020 systematic review: four databases searched (Scopus, Web of Science, ProQuest, PubMed) using Boolean search strings; semi-automated screening with ASReview machine-learning tool; two independent reviewers with interrater reliability (κ = 0.32).
  • Bayesian mixed-effects meta-analysis: logit-linked Bayesian hierarchical models (brms R package, 5 chains × 6,000 iterations) to estimate pooled co-offence prevalence, co-offender prevalence, and average group size with full posterior distributions and 95% credible intervals (CrIs).
  • Bayesian meta-regression: fixed effects for crime type (property/violent/other/all), sex (male/female/both), age group (youth/adults/all) estimated separately; Bayes factors (BF12) used for evidence strength following Jeffreys' scale.
  • Two-way interaction model: sex × age group interaction model built for violent co-offender prevalence (the only subgroup with sufficient data for a full interaction).
  • Skew-normal model for group size: a right-skewed Bayesian fixed-effects model (SkewNormal residuals with shape parameter α) fitted to average group size data; predictive posterior distribution generated for future reference values.
  • Sensitivity analysis: models re-run with wide non-informative priors to verify robustness; convergence checked via R-hat (<1.05) and bulk/tail effective sample size (ESS).
  • Data harmonization: crime labels manually harmonized into three high-level categories (violent, property, other); group-size averages computed from reported frequencies where raw averages were unavailable.

① Add a spatial layer: where co-offending happens, not just how often

Extending this paperThe paper synthesizes who co-offends and how many, but strips all geography from its meta-analytic framework. A direct extension would add a third outcome variable — the spatial clustering coefficient of co-offence incidents extracted from the underlying official records — and model it as a fixed or random effect alongside crime type and age. This would test whether co-offending is an ecological phenomenon driven by urban density gradients (hot spots, social disorganization zones) rather than purely a social-network property. The subset of included studies that provide incident-level geo-coordinates could supply a first spatial meta-regression pilot.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly onto my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and GWR/MGWR work: the meta-analytic posterior for co-offence prevalence by crime type could serve as a theoretically grounded prior for a Bayesian spatially-varying co-offending surface, allowing me to fuse aggregate study-level estimates with fine-grained incident-point data from a single city. This would be a concrete use case for my multi-source data fusion pipeline (mobile phone + official records + social media) applied to crime geography.

② Extend the posterior predictive distribution into a dynamic agent-based co-offending model

Extending this paperThe paper produces a posterior predictive distribution for average group size (centred at 2.66, 95% CrI 2.14–3.68) and notes that its results can serve as reference parameters for computational models — but stops there. The direct extension is to plug these calibrated Bayesian posteriors into an agent-based model (ABM) of criminal collaboration: agents carry empirically-posterior-calibrated co-offending propensities stratified by age, sex, and crime type, and the ABM tests how incapacitation of one member cascades through the co-offending network. Such a model would directly test the paper's policy implication that "arresting one offender may not reduce crime rates."

Added value to my researchAgent-based modelling is already in my methods toolkit. The paper provides the population-level priors I need to calibrate a co-offending ABM with defensible empirical inputs rather than ad hoc parameters. More broadly, the same posterior-to-ABM pipeline could be applied to my public-health accessibility work: calibrate agent mobility parameters from meta-analytic literature rather than from a single city dataset, improving generalizability of health-access simulations across settings.

③ Build an explainable ML predictor of study-level co-offending estimates from reporting characteristics

Extending this paperThe paper identifies three sources of variation in co-offending estimates (data type, what is reported, how it is reported) but only accounts for crime type, sex, and age group as meta-regression predictors. A rich set of study-level moderators remains unused: observation window length, country GDP, legal system type, data collection era, and definitional choices. An explainable gradient-boosting meta-regression (e.g., XGBoost with SHAP decomposition) fitted on the 110-study extracted dataset could rank which study-design features most inflate or deflate co-offending estimates — directly answering the paper's own call for better reporting standards by telling researchers which design choices matter most.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my explainable ML toolkit to a social-science meta-analytic problem. SHAP values would reveal whether, for instance, observation window length systematically biases co-offender prevalence — the paper already runs a sub-analysis on this and finds a negligible effect, but an XGBoost model could explore higher-order interactions the linear regression cannot. The resulting feature-importance map would constitute an empirical style guide for future co-offending studies.

④ Integrate urban scaling theory to test whether co-offending prevalence scales with city size

Extending this paperThe paper aggregates co-offending rates to the country level, masking a potentially important urban variable: city size. Urban scaling theory predicts that violent and property crime both scale superlinearly with population — but co-offending, being a social-network phenomenon, may scale differently from solo offending, possibly following the same superlinear regime or exhibiting saturation. By cross-linking the subset of included studies that report city-level data with urban population figures, the paper's existing dataset could be repurposed to test whether co-offending prevalence (or group size) is a superlinear urban function — a hypothesis invisible in the current country-level analysis.

Added value to my researchUrban scaling is directly relevant to my urban analytics theme. If co-offending co-varies superlinearly with city population, then intervention efficiency (cost per co-offence prevented) would itself be a function of city scale — an insight that could be incorporated into my spatial accessibility and urban-policy optimization work. It would also suggest that the paper's pooled estimates are systematically biased toward large-city patterns because large cities contribute more offences to the total sample.

⑤ Cross-link co-offending networks with mobility data to model criminal contagion diffusion

Extending this paperThe paper frames co-offending as a social-network phenomenon and notes that group crime can trigger social contagion of delinquency. Yet co-offending network structure (nine studies with network measures were identified but excluded from the main analysis) remains unintegrated with human mobility. An extension of the paper: combine the co-offending network topology metrics (density, centrality of "recruiters") with mobile-phone-derived movement flows between city zones to model how criminal co-offending spreads spatially — turning the paper's aggregate posterior into a diffusion process anchored in the real mobility infrastructure of cities.

Added value to my researchFusing co-offending network data with GPS-trajectory or mobile-phone-record mobility data is a concrete application of my multi-source data fusion and spatiotemporal analysis expertise. The social contagion framing maps onto my SIR-model experience: treat co-offending recruiters as "infectious nodes" and model spread through the mobility contact network, with the paper's posterior group-size distribution calibrating the reproduction number R0 for criminal co-offending diffusion — a genuinely novel public-health-style framing of crime prevention.
