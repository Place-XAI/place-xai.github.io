Tumour recurrence, gastrointestinal symptoms and inflammation associated with microplastic-positive intestinal tumours

Honghong Chen · Zhaohui Liu · Wenhui Qiu · Yuping Zhou · Elvis Genbo Xu · Jason T. Magnuson · Jian Zhao · Wenjing Zhao · Shengwei Hou · Huahong Shi · Baoshan Xing · Chunmiao Zheng Published 2026-05-14 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00123-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Microplastics (MPs) were detected in situ within 56.4% of 188 clinical intestinal tumour specimens, with polyethylene terephthalate (PET) as the dominant polymer. MP-positive tumours displayed localized inflammation and histopathological changes, and patients with MP-positive tumours showed a trend toward higher tumour recurrence rates and significantly elevated gastrointestinal symptom severity over a mean follow-up of 34.1 months, implicating MPs as a potential contributor to the colorectal cancer burden.

Why It Matters

  • First direct in situ evidence in intestinal tumours. Using three complementary high-resolution techniques (confocal micro-Raman spectroscopy, Raman imaging–SEM, hyperspectral imaging), the study provides among the most rigorous in situ detection of MPs in human diseased tissue to date.
  • Links environmental contamination to clinical outcomes. The association between MP positivity and both tumour recurrence trend and gastrointestinal symptom burden extends the MP health risk story from mere detection to patient prognosis.
  • Quantifies the MP-inflammation axis in human cancer tissue. Elevated inflammatory biomarkers co-localised with MPs bridge the mechanistic gap between environmental exposure and tumour microenvironment disruption.
  • Raises the bar for exposure research. By combining spectroscopy, imaging, and a 34-month clinical follow-up in 188 patients, the paper moves beyond ecological associations toward causal inference, motivating stronger regulatory scrutiny of plastic packaging and food-contact materials.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Clinical cohort
188 intestinal tumour specimens collected from patients undergoing colonoscopic surgery; MPs detected in 106 cases (56.4%). Mean clinical follow-up: 34.1 ± 17.4 months. Patient enrollment and eligibility schematic provided in Extended Data Fig. 1.
Tissue samples
Excised intestinal tumour tissue sections from Shenzhen Second People's Hospital / First Affiliated Hospital of Shenzhen University Health Science Center and affiliated sites (China). Both tumour and paired adjacent-normal tissue analysed where available.
MP characterization
Three complementary techniques: (1) confocal micro-Raman spectroscopy for polymer-type identification; (2) Raman imaging–scanning electron microscopy (RISE) for in situ spatial mapping; (3) hyperspectral imaging (HSI) for particle counting and morphology. Predominant polymer: polyethylene terephthalate (PET).
Inflammatory biomarkers
Histopathological staining and quantification of inflammatory markers co-localised with MP particles in tumour tissue sections. Specific biomarkers reported in main text and Supplementary Figs. 1–10.
Clinical endpoints
Tumour recurrence rate (primary trend outcome) and Gastrointestinal Symptom Rating Scale (GSRS) scores comparing MP-positive vs. MP-negative groups over the follow-up period.
MP size/count method
A size-probability distribution method for converting MP counts to mass (Chen et al., Environ. Sci. Technol., 2026) applied to ensure accurate quantification.

Core Methods

  • Multi-modal spectroscopic detection: confocal micro-Raman spectroscopy for polymer identification, RISE (Raman imaging–SEM) for spatially resolved in situ detection, and hyperspectral imaging (HSI) for quantitative particle characterization — three orthogonal techniques applied to the same specimens.
  • MP quantification: size-probability distribution method (Chen et al. 2026) to convert particle count distributions to mass, enabling systematic comparison across specimens.
  • Histopathological analysis: tissue staining and quantification of inflammatory biomarkers co-localised with MP particles to characterise the tumour microenvironment.
  • Clinical outcome comparison: MP-positive vs. MP-negative patient groups compared on tumour recurrence rate (trend analysis) and GSRS-scored gastrointestinal symptom severity over a mean 34.1-month follow-up.
  • Statistical design: group comparisons between MP-positive (n = 106) and MP-negative (n = 82) cases; statistical details (tests, adjustments) reported in Supplementary Information. Study design schematic in Extended Data Fig. 1.

① Spatial exposomics: map the geographic drivers of MP burden in tumour patients

Extending this paperThe paper characterizes MPs within tissue but does not link patient MP burden to where they live, eat, or work. A direct extension: geocode each patient's residential address and integrate spatial layers of plastic exposure (proximity to industrial zones, food-contact material use, drinking-water source quality) to build a spatial exposome of MP ingestion risk. The companion corpus paper on pesticide-cancer spatial Bayesian frameworks provides a ready methodological template for multi-exposure spatial modeling at patient scale.

② Explainable ML model to predict MP positivity from clinical and dietary features

Extending this paperThe paper establishes association between MP detection and clinical outcomes but does not build a predictive model. A direct next step: train a gradient-boosted or random forest classifier on the 188 patients' baseline characteristics (Extended Data Table 1) — including dietary habits, water source, plastic-contact occupation, and tumour subtype — to predict MP positivity at intake. Explainability tools (SHAP) would quantify which exposure features most strongly predict tissue MP burden, enabling targeted screening or dietary intervention before surgery.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my explainable ML for spatial/clinical prediction agenda: training a health-risk model where feature importance maps onto actionable exposure pathways. The model's spatial features (residential environment variables) would bridge my GeoAI toolkit with clinical oncology — demonstrating how spatially stratified SHAP values can guide environmental health interventions at the neighborhood level.

Cross-ref (corpus): Microplastics and nanoplastics in brain tumours and the healthy human brain — 10.1038/s44360-026-00091-4

③ Gut microbiome as mediator: extend the MP-inflammation axis to the microbiota layer

Extending this paperThe paper documents co-localised inflammation but does not measure gut microbiome composition. Given that MPs alter gut microbiota in animal models (Yan et al. 2022; Zhang et al. 2023, both cited in the paper), a natural extension is to add 16S rRNA or metagenomics profiling of patient stool alongside tissue MP characterization, testing whether microbiome dysbiosis mediates the MP → inflammation → recurrence pathway via structural equation modeling. The corpus paper on urbanization, diet change, microbiome, and colorectal cancer risk in South Africa provides a ready comparison framework.

This opens a methodological bridge between my graph-learning tools and molecular epidemiology.

④ Quantify the population-scale economic burden of MP-associated cancer recurrence

Extending this paperThe paper shows a trend toward higher recurrence rates in MP-positive patients over 34 months but does not translate this into health-economic terms. An extension: combine the paper's observed recurrence rate difference with health-augmented macroeconomic modeling (190 countries, 2020–2050) — as deployed in the corpus paper on the global economic burden of digestive-tract diseases — to estimate the attributable economic cost of MP-driven recurrence under varying exposure-reduction scenarios. This would directly inform plastic-regulation cost-benefit analyses.

⑤ Mobile-phone and wearable-sensor data to reconstruct MP exposure trajectories at scale

Extending this paperThe paper captures a single-timepoint tissue snapshot with no exposure history. A methodological leap: leverage GPS trajectory data from mobile phones to reconstruct each patient's historical movement through high-MP-exposure environments (food courts, plastic-heavy workplaces, areas with poor drinking-water quality) and correlate these exposure trajectories with tissue MP burden and clinical outcomes. This could reveal whether cumulative spatial exposure — not just a cross-sectional assessment — predicts who becomes MP-positive and at what burden level.
