Social-ecological and technological factors moderate the value of urban nature

Bonnie L. Keeler · Perrine Hamel · Timon McPhearson · Maike H. Hamann · Marie L. Donahue · Kelly A. Meza Prado · Katie K. Arkema · Gregory N. Bratman · Kate A. Brauman · Jacques C. Finlay · Anne D. Guerry · Sarah E. Hobbie · Justin A. Johnson · Graham K. MacDonald · Robert I. McDonald · Nick Neverisky · Spencer A. Wood Published 2019-01-10 DOI 10.1038/s41893-018-0202-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Urban nature can improve air/water quality, mitigate flooding, and enhance health and well-being, but its value is highly context-dependent. Social, ecological, and technological factors strongly moderate whether and for whom urban ecosystem services are realized. A review of over 1,200 references across ten ecosystem services shows that efficient, equitable nature-based solutions require matching the right intervention to the right context — and that ignoring co-benefits, disservices, and distributional consequences consistently leads to over- or under-valuation.

Core Methods

  • Systematic literature review: teams of disciplinary experts each reviewed one of ten urban ecosystem services using a shared synthesis framework, drawing on over 1,200 unique references. Reviews are included as stand-alone Supplementary documents for practitioners.
  • Framework-guided synthesis: a common conceptual framework (Fig. 1) structures the review around: (1) actions → changes in ecosystem services → value; (2) moderation by social, ecological, and technological factors; (3) co-benefits and disservices; (4) comparison with non-nature substitutes.
  • Cross-service comparative synthesis: findings from all ten service reviews are condensed into Table 2, which includes expert assessments of uncertainty, potential magnitude, and the most influential contextual moderators for each service.
  • Equity analysis: three dimensions of equity are applied across services — exposure disparities, process inequities, and distributional inequities in assets — to systematically identify who benefits from and who is harmed by urban nature investments.
  • Comparator framing: each service is evaluated against its non-nature substitute (e.g., desalination vs. infiltration, air conditioning vs. tree cover, sewage treatment vs. wetland restoration), providing a framework for cost-effectiveness comparison.

① Operationalize the paper's social-ecological-technological moderator framework with spatial ML

Extending this paperThe paper's core contribution is a conceptual framework identifying social, ecological, and technological context as moderators of urban nature value, but it remains qualitative. A direct extension: operationalize each moderator category as spatial features (income/demography from census, vegetation structure from remote sensing, infrastructure type from OSM) and train an explainable gradient boosting model to predict which moderator combination yields the highest ecosystem service value for a given nature-based solution type — turning Table 2's qualitative uncertainty ratings into a quantitative, spatially explicit prediction tool.

② Quantify green gentrification risk at neighborhood scale using multi-source spatial data

Extending this paperThe paper flags green gentrification (new green spaces driving housing price increases and displacement of vulnerable residents) as a growing environmental justice concern, citing Seoul, Barcelona, and Bangalore. However, it offers no spatial prediction of where gentrification risk is highest before investment occurs. Extending it: fuse pre-existing housing price gradients, income distributions, and planned green-space investment locations into a spatiotemporal displacement risk model, providing the paper's equity framework with predictive teeth that planners can act on before displacement occurs.

Added value to my researchThis connects the paper's equity analysis to my urban analytics work on housing prices and public-facility access. The geographically weighted regression (GWR/MGWR) toolkit I use for spatial heterogeneity analysis is directly applicable: a MGWR of green-space proximity on housing prices, stratified by income, would quantify the gentrification gradient the paper describes qualitatively — and identify neighborhoods where nature investment risks displacing the people it should help most.

④ Close the paper's Global South gap with wearable and mobile-phone behavioral data

Extending this paperThe paper identifies the geographic concentration of urban ecosystem services research (mostly Europe, China, USA) as its most critical limitation. Global South cities are under-studied partly because survey and health-records data are sparse. Extending it: deploy passive sensing (GPS trajectories, mobile-phone CDRs) as a proxy for urban nature use — park visitation rates, dwell times, and activity intensity derived from mobility data can stand in for survey-based recreation values in data-scarce contexts, testing whether the paper's social and technological moderator findings hold outside high-income settings.

⑤ Recast the paper's nature-versus-grey comparator framework as an equity-aware optimization

Extending this paperThe paper compares nature-based solutions against grey alternatives (e.g., rain gardens vs. combined sewer upgrades) but only discusses this comparison qualitatively. Extending it: formulate a multi-objective facility-location problem where the decision variables are the type (green vs. grey) and location of infrastructure interventions, the objectives are cost-effectiveness and equity (minimizing disparity in ecosystem service access across income groups), and the constraints are budget and spatial feasibility. The paper's Table 2 moderator ratings become the constraint parameters — grounding the optimization in the paper's own evidence synthesis.

Added value to my researchThis generalizes my EV charging-station efficiency-equity optimization to multi-type urban infrastructure. The equity-aware facility-location framework I use for healthcare access (2SFCA/R2SFCA) maps naturally onto the problem: nature-based solutions are "supply nodes," neighborhood exposure to heat/flood/air pollution is "demand," and the optimization identifies the green-grey mix that closes the access gap for vulnerable populations most cost-effectively.
