Urban agriculture growth in US cities

Lisa Palmer Published 2018-01-08 DOI 10.1038/s41893-017-0014-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Urban agriculture in the United States — spanning community gardens, urban farms, and vertical/indoor facilities — has been growing since the 1980s, driven by increased access to unused and post-industrial land. Case studies from Washington DC, New York City, Baltimore, Chicago, Detroit, and Milwaukee show that these spaces deliver social cohesion, biodiversity, food access, and economic development in low-income communities. Sustainability depends on coordinated local policy, land-use reform, and spatial targeting to place gardens where health and food deficits are greatest.

Why It Matters

  • Highlights the "food desert" dimension. In low-income urban neighborhoods where supermarkets have withdrawn, community gardens offer fresh produce at no cost to participants — a direct public-health intervention that complements clinical access strategies.
  • Points to spatial targeting as the critical gap. New York City's Green Thumb rubric and the multi-city research program in Baltimore, Chicago, and New York represent emerging spatial analytic approaches to site selection for new gardens — a planning priority that lacks standardized methods.
  • Calls out the commercial frontier. Controlled-environment agriculture (vertical farms, shipping-container farms) is emerging as a business model for urban food production, blurring the boundary between community gardening and urban industry.
  • Signals the need for data infrastructure. Counting and mapping urban agriculture is hindered by the distinction between sanctioned and unsanctioned spaces; better geospatial inventories are essential for research and policy.

① Build a national geospatial inventory of urban agriculture sites using remote sensing and POI data

Extending this paperThe article repeatedly flags the absence of a systematic spatial inventory of US urban farms and community gardens — distinguishing sanctioned from unsanctioned spaces, and mapping supply against demand (food deserts, health indicators). A direct extension is to combine satellite imagery classification (detecting vegetated patches in urban parcels), OpenStreetMap POI data (gardens, farms), and municipal permit records to produce a national-scale, annually-updated inventory. The paper's own distinction between sanctioned and unsanctioned spaces, and the typology of farm models, would guide the classification scheme. The NYC Green Thumb spatial-targeting rubric described in the article provides a prototype scoring function.

The resulting inventory would enable a 2SFCA-style supply-demand accessibility analysis: quantifying how many residents are within a walkable catchment of an urban farm, stratified by income and food-security indicators. My spatiotemporal kernel density estimation methods could map the intensity of urban agriculture supply across a city's geography, revealing spatial gaps.

② Operationalize the paper's spatial targeting rubric as a facility-location optimization model

Extending this paperNYC Green Thumb director LoSasso describes a multi-criteria siting rubric for new community gardens: avoid overlap with existing gardens, prioritize areas lacking open space, with high obesity, low fruit/vegetable consumption, and food insecurity — using health department and food-bank data. This is an informal 2-step floating catchment area problem dressed in practitioner language. Extending the paper means formalizing this rubric as a facility-location optimization: minimize the population-weighted food-access deficit subject to constraints on land availability and budget, with equity as an explicit objective. The result would be a generalizable algorithm that planners in any US city could apply.

Added value to my researchThis is my EV-charging efficiency-vs-equity optimization applied to urban agriculture siting. The same mixed-integer programming or genetic algorithm framework I use for EV station placement can optimize garden locations, with health-equity weights replacing EV demand weights. Explainable ML (SHAP values) could attribute which neighborhood characteristics most strongly predict underservice, making the model auditable for planners.

④ Link urban agriculture access to health outcomes using R2SFCA and mobile-phone mobility data

Extending this paperThe article cites evidence that participation in urban agriculture increases fruit and vegetable consumption, but the causal mechanism — proximity, participation, or community networks — is not disentangled. Extending the paper: measure realized access to urban farms (using the R2SFCA spatial accessibility index, accounting for distance decay and competition among users), then link accessibility scores to neighborhood-level diet and obesity indicators from health-department data, controlling for socioeconomic confounders. Mobile-phone GPS trajectories could reveal who actually travels to which garden (actual catchments vs. theoretical ones), distinguishing proximity from use.

Added value to my researchThis is at the core of my health geography and accessibility research: replacing theoretical 2SFCA catchments with revealed-preference catchments from GPS/mobile data, then estimating the health gradient of urban-farm accessibility using MGWR to capture spatial non-stationarity of the diet-health-access relationship across neighborhoods with different demographics and transit access.

Extending this paperThe article highlights AeroFarms (Newark) and Urban Pastoral (Baltimore) as emerging controlled-environment agriculture (CEA) businesses using buildings, shipping containers, and vertical stacks under artificial light.
