Global urbanicity is associated with brain and behaviour in young people

Jiayuan Xu · Xiaoxuan Liu · Qiaojun Li · Ran Goldblatt · Wen Qin · Feng Liu · Congying Chu · Qiang Luo · Alex Ing · Lining Guo · et al. (Gunter Schumann, senior author) Published 2021-10-28 DOI 10.1038/s41562-021-01204-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Urban upbringing — quantified for the first time via a satellite-derived composite measure (UrbanSat) spanning nine remote-sensing indices — is robustly associated with structural and functional differences in the medial prefrontal cortex and cerebellum of young people in both China and Europe (n = 3,867). Childhood exposure is the critical window: higher urbanicity during ages 1–15 predicts smaller mPFC volume and surface area but larger cerebellar volume, and these brain differences mediate enhanced perspective-taking and increased depression symptoms. UrbanSat can be computed for any location on Earth from the 1970s onward, making it a globally scalable tool for tracking urbanicity-related mental health risk.

★

Why It Matters

  • First satellite-based global urbanicity measure for neuroscience. UrbanSat replaces inconsistent, infrequent census classifications with a continuously updatable, geography-agnostic composite of nine satellite indices — enabling cross-country, longitudinal brain–urbanicity comparisons that were previously impossible.
  • Identifies childhood as the critical susceptibility window. Using distributed lag models, the study pinpoints age-specific windows (e.g., cerebellar effects ages 1–10; mPFC effects ages 4–15) when urban exposure most strongly shapes brain development — yielding actionable targets for preventive intervention.
  • Demonstrates cross-cultural replication. Brain–urbanicity associations found in 3,306 Chinese CHIMGEN participants were successfully replicated in 561 European IMAGEN adolescents — establishing that urbanicity effects transcend sociocultural and geographical contexts.
  • Shows bidirectional effects on behavior. Urban upbringing simultaneously enhances social cognition (perspective-taking) and increases depression risk — a nuanced finding challenging narratives that frame city living as purely adverse.
  • Provides a scalable global tool for public health surveillance. Because UrbanSat is computable anywhere on Earth, it can underpin planetary-scale monitoring of urbanization's mental health footprint — directly informing policy on urban planning, child welfare, and resilience.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Study cohorts
CHIMGEN (Chinese Imaging Genetics) — 3,306 healthy Chinese Han adults aged 18–30 years recruited from 31 centres across 21 cities in mainland China; IMAGEN-FU2 (European longitudinal) — 561 adolescents (second follow-up at ~19 years) recruited from 8 sites across Germany, France, Ireland, and the UK. Total neuroimaging sample: n = 3,867 (CHIMGEN n = 2,176 with MRI; IMAGEN-FU2 n = 482 with MRI).
Residential histories
Lifetime residential geopositions collected from birth to recruitment (1986–2018 for CHIMGEN; birth to ~19 years for IMAGEN). Addresses obfuscated to 1 km resolution using Google Earth Engine (GEE) coordinates for privacy (github.com/crickfan/geo-anonymization).
Satellite data sources
Nine remote-sensing indices extracted via Google Earth Engine (GEE) and the European Space Agency (ESA) platform: (1) Night-time light (NL) — NOAA/NGDC DMSP-OLS, 1992–2013; (2) NDVI (vegetation) — GIMMS/MODIS, 1981–2017; (3) NDBI (built-up index); (4) NDWI (water index); (5–9) Land-cover fractions (built-up%, cropland%, grassland%, forest%, water body%) from GLCM / Global Land Cover Mapping (GlobeLand30), 1992–2015.
Validation dataset
Global Human Settlement Layer (GHSL) population grid (GHSL-POP) — provided by the European Commission Joint Research Centre; used for years 1990, 2000, and 2015 to validate UrbanSat against ground-level population density in rural, town, and city categories.
Neuroimaging
3.0-T MRI acquired at 28 CHIMGEN sites and 6 IMAGEN sites; measures include grey matter volume (GMV, n up to 2,176), cortical thickness (CT), surface area (SA), diffusion tensor imaging (FA via TBSS), resting-state fMRI (WNFC and BNFC). Preprocessed with SPM12 and FSL v5.0.
Behavioural data
Social cognition (Interpersonal Reactivity Index, IRI — perspective-taking), depression (Beck Depression Inventory, BDI in CHIMGEN; Ruminating Scale Questionnaire, RSQ in IMAGEN), anxiety (DAWBA-GA, CIDI-AS), working memory, verbal learning, information processing speed, cognitive control.
Time coverage
Satellite records 1981–2018; residential geopositions from 1986; MRI data collected from 2009 (IMAGEN baseline) to ~2018 (CHIMGEN).

Data available from authors upon reasonable request and with CHIMGEN and IMAGEN consortium permissions. Code available from corresponding author upon request. Geo-anonymization code: github.com/crickfan/geo-anonymization.

Core Methods

  • UrbanSat construction: nine satellite indices extracted annually per participant from birth to recruitment; missing values imputed via Multiple Imputation by Chained Equations (MICE) with 10 imputed datasets and tenfold cross-validation. Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA) optimized to identify the best-fitting latent urbanicity composite — final model: NL + built-up% + cropland% + NDVI.
  • Voxel-wise brain-imaging analysis: whole-brain multiple regression of mean UrbanSat (<18 years) against GMV, CT, SA (SPM12), FA-TBSS (FSL randomise), and WNFC/BNFC (ICA-based 30-component decomposition); thresholded at FWE-corrected Pc < 0.05 with corrections for number of imaging modalities (×6) and data types (×2).
  • Distributed Lag Models (DLMs): a cross-basis function simultaneously estimates the linear exposure–response and nonlinear lag–response of annual UrbanSat across the life span (lags 1–30 years in CHIMGEN), identifying susceptibility windows where the 95% CI excludes zero.
  • Multi-site meta-analysis: inverse variance-weighted random-effects meta-analysis across all CHIMGEN (28 sites) and IMAGEN (6 sites) centres to confirm robustness against scanner and site effects.
  • Multiple mediation analysis: formal test of whether brain structural and functional variables (GMV, SA, WNFC, BNFC) mediate the UrbanSat–behaviour (perspective-taking and BDI) association, controlling all confounders simultaneously.
  • Statistical controls: age, sex, education, site, BMI, genetic population stratification (principal components), socioeconomic status (SES), intracranial volume (for GMV analyses), mean CT / total SA (for vertex-wise analyses).

① Build a planetary-scale UrbanSat observatory by fusing modern satellite archives

Extending this paperUrbanSat is validated only in China and Europe, covers up to 2018, and relies on satellite products whose temporal coverage gaps required MICE imputation. A direct extension is to ingest Sentinel-2, Landsat-8/9, and VIIRS night-time light into a continuously updating global UrbanSat pipeline, covering all inhabited landmasses at finer spatial resolution (10–30 m rather than 1 km). Pairing this with the GHSL 2023 release and WorldPop 2020 grids would enable year-by-year mental health risk mapping for any city on Earth — turning UrbanSat from a research instrument into a planetary monitoring infrastructure.

Added value to my researchMy multi-source data fusion agenda (mobile phone, social media, remote sensing) maps directly onto this pipeline. Integrating an always-on UrbanSat layer with my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation framework would allow me to produce high-resolution, near-real-time urbanicity surfaces for Chinese cities — a foundation dataset for urban health accessibility research and a practical input for 2SFCA service-area calculations that need residential exposure profiles, not just facility locations.

② Embed UrbanSat's susceptibility-period logic into a distributed-lag accessibility model

Extending this paperThe paper identifies age-specific susceptibility windows (e.g., cerebellar effects ages 1–10; mPFC effects ages 4–15) using distributed lag models, but applies them only to static residential histories. A natural extension: re-cast the DLM as a time-varying, cohort-aware exposure model that accounts for residential mobility and tracks how cumulative urbanicity dose accumulates or dissipates after migration — testing whether reversible or irreversible neural changes follow different dose-response curves. The paper's own migration-timing analysis (Fig. 3d–e) seeds this design but stops short of a continuous-dose model.

Added value to my researchMy 2SFCA and R2SFCA models compute static accessibility at one point in time, ignoring that individuals age through different accessibility environments. A DLM-inspired longitudinal 2SFCA that weights each year's facility accessibility by an age-specific susceptibility multiplier — derived from papers like this one — would enable me to model how cumulative healthcare access history predicts current health outcomes, a novel framing for spatial accessibility research.

③ Add an explainable AI layer to decode which satellite components drive neural effects

Extending this paperUrbanSat's CFA assigns fixed factor loadings (NL highest, NDVI lowest), but does not reveal why specific satellite indices predict specific brain regions, nor whether these drivers vary across age, sex, or urban typology. An extension: train a Shapley-value-attributed deep neural predictor of mPFC/cerebellar change from the full nine-dimensional satellite time series — obtaining, for each participant and brain region, an age-resolved explanation of which satellite feature contributed most. This would clarify whether NL's outsized weight reflects light pollution, economic activity, or social isolation pathways.

Added value to my researchExplainable ML for spatial prediction is a core method in my toolkit. Applying SHAP attribution to a brain-imaging–urbanicity model would let me demonstrate the method's utility in neuroscience, and the resulting feature-importance maps (which satellite index matters most, when, and where) translate directly into policy-actionable urban design signals — e.g., whether reducing night-time light vs. increasing greenery best protects mPFC development in specific age groups.

④ Link UrbanSat to urban amenity access maps for a unified brain–accessibility model

Extending this paperThe paper treats urbanicity as a general density signal but cannot distinguish whether the observed brain effects are driven by social crowding, pollution, green space deprivation, or limited access to healthcare and recreational amenities. Extending it: cross-link UrbanSat scores at each residential location with OpenStreetMap-derived facility accessibility scores (parks, clinics, schools, cultural venues) to decompose the urbanicity signal into its behaviorally salient components — directly testing which specific amenities moderate mPFC and cerebellar effects in mediation analyses.

Added value to my researchThis is the bridge between the neuroimaging literature and my urban health accessibility work. Demonstrating that mental health facility accessibility specifically mediates the urbanicity–depression path (rather than generic density) would give my R2SFCA mental-health accessibility papers a neurobiological grounding. It also identifies specific facility types worth prioritizing in spatial accessibility policies targeting child mental health.

⑤ Extend UrbanSat's geographic scope to the Global South using transfer learning

Extending this paperBoth CHIMGEN and IMAGEN cohorts are from high-income, data-rich settings — 21 Chinese cities and 8 European sites. The authors acknowledge generalizability to other geographies as future work. A critical extension: train a transfer-learning UrbanSat model on the existing China–Europe validation against GHSL-POP, then adapt it to Global South cities (sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, Latin America) where census data are sparse and urbanization rates are fastest. Fine-tuning geospatial foundation models on the nine-satellite time series would address the authors' own call for global applicability without requiring new neuroimaging datasets.

Added value to my researchFine-tuning geospatial foundation models is one of my declared research directions. Using UrbanSat's validated CFA structure as a pseudo-label generator for remote regions — where I fine-tune a vision-language geospatial model on satellite tiles annotated by UrbanSat scores — would produce a globally transferable urbanicity encoder. This encoder could then serve as a plug-in feature extractor for my 2SFCA models in low-data-availability settings, advancing both reproducible open science and equity-focused health geography.
