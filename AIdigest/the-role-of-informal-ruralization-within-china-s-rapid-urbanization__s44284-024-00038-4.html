The role of informal ruralization within China's rapid urbanization

Hanxi Wang Published 2024-02-27 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00038-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Chengshi kaihuang (CK, 城市开荒) — an informal, individual practice of cultivating vegetables in neglected urban wastelands — is documented through remote sensing as a bottom-up "ruralizing" force operating inside, not at the fringe of, a Chinese megacity. A supervised land-cover classification of Wuhan's central districts (2017–2022) estimates that an average of 6.34% of the area (~12,941 acres) was under active CK each year — a scale far exceeding documented urban agriculture in comparably sized Western cities. Almost all CK sites (97.4%) emerged on land freshly demolished by rapid urbanization, and CK tends to return persistently even after forced removal, showing that ordinary people exercise spatial agency beyond simple top-down vs. bottom-up binaries.

★

Why It Matters

  • Makes an invisible, informal phenomenon measurable. CK is illegal, undocumented, and individually performed; this is the first study to map it at city scale by fusing remote sensing with social-media "gray literature," giving an empirical footprint to a practice previously known only through small ethnographies.
  • Reframes the rural–urban relationship. It provides rare quantitative evidence that ruralization happens within central urban districts, not just at the peri-urban edge, challenging the "planetary urbanization" view of the rural as residual or obsolete.
  • Reveals the spatial inefficiencies of rapid growth. CK colonizes the vacant lots left by demolition; 66% of sites sat idle 2–5 years before cultivation, turning the practice into a sensor for stalled, speculative, and fragmented land left by the urbanization machine.
  • Bottom-up resilience at surprising scale. The ~12,941-acre estimate dwarfs community gardens in New York (~120 acres), Chicago (~65 acres), and Toronto (~1,606 acres), signaling latent capacity for food provisioning, urban metabolism, and self-help in the Global South.

News reports and government notices from Baidu News and practitioner posts/videos from Douyin (Chinese TikTok), searched for "城市开荒种菜"; used for site discovery, national occurrence tallies, and discourse analysis.

Core Methods

  • Gray-literature contextual analysis: manual coding of Baidu News and Douyin posts to extract CK's physical/temporal signatures, geographic locations, and cultural-political discourse (slogans, narratives of escape and self-sufficiency).
  • VHR visual interpretation: Google Earth Pro time-series imagery used to confirm CK's small, patchy, irregular geometry and to reconstruct each site's pre- and post-emergence land-use history.
  • Snowballing supervised LCC: a two-stage random-forest classifier — an initial LCC seeded from 7 sites yields candidate maps, sampled to 544 verified sites, which retrain a far more accurate final LCC. Two classes only: CK vs. non-CK.
  • Feature engineering: 7 spectral bands + 7 indices (NDVI, EVI, Bare Soil Index, NDWI, Normalized Difference Latent Heat Index, multitemporal BSI std-dev) plus object-based image analysis segmentation and 9 GLCM texture features compressed via PCA — 16 bands per year.
  • Accuracy assessment: confusion matrices on a held-out 30% validation set; overall accuracy >98% and kappa >0.80 for most years, with consumer accuracy exceeding producer accuracy for CK (accurate but not fully comprehensive detection).
  • Sampling of dynamics: 544 CK sites scored for pre-emergence type (5 categories), post-emergence trajectory, and return-after-removal behavior.
