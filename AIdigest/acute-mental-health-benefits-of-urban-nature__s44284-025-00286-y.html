Acute mental health benefits of urban nature

Yingjie Li · Yuanyuan Mao · Lisa Mandle · Anders Rydström · Roy P. Remme · Xin Lan · Tong Wu · Chao Song · Yougeng Lu · Kari C. Nadeau · Andreas Meyer-Lindenberg · Gretchen C. Daily · Anne D. Guerry Published 2025-07-30 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00286-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across a systematic review of 449 studies and a meta-analysis of 78 field experiments, short-term exposure to urban nature delivers substantial, broad-spectrum mental health benefits spanning 12 outcomes. Green spaces — urban forests and parks — stand out for reducing negative moods such as depression and anxiety and lifting overall well-being. Benefits are strongest among young adults yet remain consistently positive across all age groups, making protection and expansion of accessible urban nature a concrete public-health lever for cities worldwide.

Why It Matters

  • Quantifies a public-health intuition with pooled evidence. Mental disorders are more prevalent in cities; this work converts scattered field experiments into a single quantitative effect-size synthesis (Hedges' g) across 12 mental-health outcomes.
  • Ranks the "active ingredient" of urban nature. By disaggregating effects across nature types, it elevates urban forests and parks as the strongest contributors to mood improvement — directly informing what to plant and protect.
  • Identifies who benefits most. The age-group subgroup analysis shows the largest gains in young adults, sharpening targeting for campus, workplace, and youth-oriented green-space policy.
  • Hints at dose–response. Subgroup analyses by exposure duration suggest more time in nature yields larger benefits, supporting prescriptive "nature dose" guidance.
  • Fully open and reproducible. Included studies, processed data, and analysis code ship via a public GitHub repository, so the synthesis can be audited and extended.

Core Methods

  • Systematic review (PRISMA): structured screening of the literature to 449 included studies, with the screening cascade documented in a flow diagram (Fig. 5).
  • Random-effects meta-analysis: pooled standardized effect sizes (Hedges' g) across 12 outcomes, with heterogeneity quantified via I² and displayed in forest plots (Fig. 2).
  • Subgroup / moderator analyses: stratified pooling by nature type (Fig. 3), age group (Fig. 4), and Extended-Data strata for exposure duration, gender representation, and region.
  • Distributional comparisons: boxplots of mean effect sizes with Kruskal–Wallis tests and post-hoc Dunn's test (Bonferroni-corrected) for pairwise group differences.
  • Dose–response exploration: effect sizes plotted against exposure duration to probe potential time-in-nature dose–response relationships.

① Geolocate the effect-size corpus and model spatial heterogeneity

Extending this paperThe meta-analysis pools effect sizes globally and maps where studies come from (Fig. 1), but it treats benefit magnitude as aspatial — heterogeneity (high I²) is absorbed into random effects rather than explained by place. A direct extension: attach each primary study to coordinates, join the paper's own GHS density context, and fit a meta-regression with spatially varying coefficients (GWR/MGWR) so that the nature–mental-health effect becomes a mappable surface rather than one pooled number.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my MGWR/spatial non-stationarity line: the residual heterogeneity the paper flags becomes a spatial signal I can model, linking benefit magnitude to local density, greenness, and socio-environmental context.

② Couple "benefit per exposure" with accessibility to convert effects into population dose

Extending this paperThe paper measures the per-person benefit of an exposure but says nothing about who can actually reach urban forests and parks. Extending it: combine its effect sizes with a 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility surface for green space, turning "benefit-if-exposed" into realized population-level mental-health gain — and exposing equity gaps where the highest-benefit nature types are least accessible.

Added value to my researchThis fuses my R2SFCA accessibility framework with an empirically validated benefit weight, giving health-geography accessibility a direct outcome currency instead of mere proximity counts.

③ Formalize the dose–response into a transferable greenness-dose function

Extending this paperThe Extended-Data duration analyses suggest a dose–response but stop at boxplots. Extending it: fit an explicit dose–response curve (benefit vs. exposure duration and greenness intensity), test for saturation/thresholds, and harmonize it with remote-sensing greenness (NDVI) metrics so the curve is computable from satellite data anywhere.

④ Move from acute lab/field effects to longitudinal real-world exposure via wearables & mobility

Extending this paperThe synthesis is built on acute, short-duration field experiments — controlled but artificial. Extending it: validate the pooled effects against passively sensed, longitudinal exposure (GPS trajectories, wearables, mobile-phone records) to see whether the acute benefits persist under everyday, repeated, partly self-selected exposure.
