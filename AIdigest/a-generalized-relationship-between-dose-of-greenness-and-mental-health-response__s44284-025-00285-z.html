A generalized relationship between dose of greenness and mental health response

Bin Jiang · Jiali Li · Peng Gong · Chris Webster · Gunter Schumann · Xueming Liu · Pongsakorn Suppakittpaisarn Published 2025-07-25 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00285-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across four decades of evidence, the relationship between the dose of urban greenness and mental health response is best described not by a straight line but by a generalized quadratic (inverted-U) curve. Pooling 69 refitted curves, the authors show a quadratic pattern fits most published curves and identify "highly beneficial" and "best" doses for both eye-level greenness (street-level visible green) and top-down greenness (overhead/canopy green). The practical upshot: a moderate dose of green exposure — not the maximum — yields the most salubrious mental-health benefit, so green resources should be allocated toward an optimal, not maximal, target.

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a long-standing "more is always better" assumption. Decades of studies assumed a linear dose–response; this synthesis shows the true shape is curvilinear, meaning beyond an optimal dose extra greenness adds little — or can even detract.
  • Gives planners a concrete target, not a slogan. Instead of "add more green," it specifies highly beneficial and best doses for eye-level and top-down greenness — turning green provision into an optimization with a defined sweet spot.
  • Harmonizes a fragmented literature. Prior studies used inconsistent metrics and contexts and reached inconclusive verdicts; the meta-analysis standardizes and refits curves onto a common footing, recovering a generalizable pattern.
  • Directly informs cost-effective, equitable green allocation. If benefits saturate, scarce green resources can be redistributed to under-served areas rather than over-greening already-green districts — an efficiency and equity argument.
  • Fully open and reproducible. All five structured databases (784 → 133 → 69 curves) and the R curve-fitting code are public on GitHub, enabling reuse and re-analysis.

Core Methods

  • Systematic search & screening: six-iteration multi-database search (WoS, Scopus, PubMed), narrowing 784 eligible studies to 133 included to 69 analyzable curves with documented inclusion criteria.
  • Curve extraction & standardization: digitize and standardize the dose–response data points from each primary study so heterogeneous metrics and contexts become comparable on common axes.
  • Curve refitting & model comparison: refit candidate functional forms (linear vs. quadratic, etc.) to each standardized curve and identify which family best fits — concluding a quadratic (inverted-U) pattern dominates.
  • Dose-threshold identification: from the fitted quadratics, derive "highly beneficial" and "best" doses separately for eye-level and top-down greenness.
  • Risk-of-bias assessment: formal quality scoring of each study, with results re-checked on quality-verified subsets to test robustness of the generalized pattern.
  • Open analysis stack: curve fitting implemented in R; all data and code released for reproduction.

① Spatialize the "best dose" into a city-wide greenness-deficit/surplus map

Extending this paperThe paper delivers a generalized quadratic curve and optimal dose, but stops at the curve — it never projects those thresholds back onto real urban space. A direct extension: fuse the fitted "best dose" of eye-level and top-down greenness with city-scale street-view-derived green-view indices and NDVI canopy layers to produce a per-neighborhood deficit/surplus map — where exactly are residents below the beneficial dose, and where is greening past the point of diminishing return?

Added value to my researchThis turns a saturating benefit curve into a 2SFCA-style "green-health accessibility" surface: instead of accumulating raw green supply, the supply term is capped at the optimal dose, so my accessibility models reward reaching the sweet spot rather than maximizing greenness — directly relevant to my healthy-cities and service-accessibility work.

② Replace the global quadratic with explainable, context-conditioned dose curves

Extending this paperThe paper fits one generalized quadratic and notes that context (continent, design, outcome type) varies but is largely pooled out. ③ Move from static dose to dynamic, mobility-weighted greenness exposure

Extending this paperThe paper's "dose" is essentially a residential / static exposure level taken from primary studies. People, though, accumulate green exposure along daily trajectories. Extending it: re-express the dose as a mobility-weighted, spatiotemporal exposure built from GPS trajectories and street-view green along routes, then test whether the inverted-U holds for experienced rather than residential greenness.
