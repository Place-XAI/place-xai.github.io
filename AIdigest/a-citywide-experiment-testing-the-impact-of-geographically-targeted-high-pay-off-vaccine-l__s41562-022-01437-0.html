A citywide experiment testing the impact of geographically targeted, high-pay-off vaccine lotteries

Katherine L. Milkman · Linnea Gandhi · Sean F. Ellis · Heather N. Graci · Dena M. Gromet · Rayyan S. Mobarak · Alison M. Buttenheim · Angela L. Duckworth · Devin Pope · Ala Stanford · Richard Thaler · Kevin G. Volpp Published 2022-09-01 DOI 10.1038/s41562-022-01437-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A pre-registered, citywide experiment in Philadelphia gave residents of three randomly selected zip codes 50–100× higher odds of winning up to US$50,000 in vaccine lottery prizes. Despite strong evidence of awareness in treatment zip codes, no statistically significant vaccination increase was detected overall: the pooled difference-in-differences estimate was nearly zero, and the 95% CI provides a 9% upper bound on net benefits. High-pay-off geographically targeted vaccine lotteries do not appear to be a reliable or cost-effective tool for boosting immunisation in undervaccinated communities.

★

Why It Matters

  • First pre-registered, experimentally controlled vaccine lottery study at city scale. Unlike concurrent studies that compare states post hoc, this design randomises at the zip-code level, enabling clean causal inference without relying on cross-state comparisons.
  • Covers more than 587,000 adults in undervaccinated communities. The scale is more than 1,000 times larger than typical lottery-incentive experiments, making the null result credibly precise.
  • Quantifies an upper bound, not just a null finding. Equivalence tests tightly bound the benefit at ≤9% and at a marginal cost exceeding US$4,000 per additional vaccination — a direct policy signal for programme designers.
  • Highlights the salience gap. Media attention collapsed after the first drawing; subsequent zip codes saw far lower awareness, suggesting that lottery incentives may require sustained, high-visibility outreach — a rarely tested condition in prior literature.
  • Adds to a null-results canon in public health. In a field where publication bias favours positive findings, a well-powered, pre-registered null advances collective understanding and saves public money.

Core Methods

  • Pre-registered randomised experiment: 3 treatment zip codes randomly drawn from the 20 lowest-vaccination zip codes in Philadelphia; remaining 17 serve as controls. Pre-registration on OSF before the sweepstakes launch.
  • Difference-in-differences (DiD) OLS regression: weekly first-dose vaccinations per 100,000 adults predicted by zip-code fixed effects, week fixed effects, and a binary treatment indicator for the relevant two-week treatment period. Run separately for each zip code and pooled across all three.
  • Three clustering approaches: standard errors clustered by week (15 clusters), by zip code (18 clusters), and HC3 robust; the most conservative reported to guard against small-cluster bias (following Dube et al.).
  • Synthetic control method (Abadie et al.): constructs a weighted counterfactual from the 17 control zip codes matching each treatment zip code's pre-treatment vaccination trajectory; significance tested via permutation (RMSPE ratios). Extended to the pooled case following Cavallo et al.
  • Parallel-trends testing: interaction terms between week indicators and treatment-zip-code indicator added to DiD model; F-tests reveal violations for the third zip code, justifying caution on that DiD estimate.
  • Equivalence testing: one-sided t-tests on upper bounds to formally rule out effect sizes corresponding to US$1,000 per additional vaccination (a policy-relevant threshold).
  • Permutation tests: each control zip code used as a placebo treatment unit; distribution of placebo coefficients provides non-parametric inference (Supplementary Section 3).
  • County-level DiD: compares Philadelphia County with surrounding counties and Allegheny County to estimate the overall effect of the broader sweepstakes (secondary, non-experimental analysis).

① Spatially predict where lottery salience would have mattered most, using pre-treatment mobility and socio-demographic features

Extending this paperThe paper's key finding is that media salience decayed sharply after the first drawing, yet it never asks where within Philadelphia targeted communication would have converted awareness into vaccination. A direct extension: build a spatial prediction model — using ACS socio-demographics, prior vaccination rates, mobility patterns, and media-exposure proxies — to identify which zip codes would have been most responsive if a sustained, localised media campaign had accompanied each lottery drawing. The paper's own registration data can serve as a training signal for awareness propensity.

② Replace geography-based targeting with mobility-flow-based targeting to reach the unvaccinated where they actually go

Extending this paperThe paper's intervention unit is the residential zip code, but unvaccinated individuals may work, shop, or attend healthcare in different zip codes. Extending it: overlay the lottery's zip-code boundaries with origin-destination mobility flows (mobile-phone data) to identify the actual activity catchments of low-vaccination zip codes. A lottery targeted to the visited pharmacies, transit stops, or community centres — not just the home zip — could reach more true non-vaccinators. This reframes the geographic targeting question from residential to behavioural geography.

Added value to my researchThis connects directly to my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and mobile-phone trajectory work: use GPS or call-detail records to map where residents of undervaccinated zip codes spend daytime hours, then compute mobility-weighted accessibility to vaccination sites rather than straight-line or network proximity. The same framework applies to my 2SFCA/R2SFCA models for primary care, where the "service area" should be demand-flow-derived rather than administratively bounded.

③ Model the optimal lottery design using spatial equity-efficiency trade-off simulation

Extending this paperThe Philly Vax Sweepstakes allocated half of all prizes to a single zip code, boosting odds by 50–100×. But was this the optimal prize-concentration ratio? The paper never explores the design space: a smaller odds boost spread across more zip codes might produce a better population-level effect. Extending it: simulate the efficiency-equity frontier by varying the prize share allocated to treatment zips and the number of simultaneously targeted zips, using the paper's empirical DiD estimates as calibration points and a spatial SIR model to propagate herd-immunity effects across neighboring zip codes.

Added value to my researchMy work on EV charging-station efficiency-vs-equity optimization uses a similar multi-objective framework: here the objectives are (1) total vaccines administered and (2) spatial equity in vaccination coverage across zip codes. The spatial simulation approach — with Monte Carlo sensitivity over prize allocation scenarios — maps directly to my Monte Carlo and agent-based modelling methods. The equity-efficiency Pareto surface would give policymakers a directly actionable design chart for future geographically targeted incentive programs.

④ Use the spatial experiment's null result to infer demand-side barriers: a 2SFCA-based access decomposition

Extending this paperIf lottery incentives failed despite strong awareness, the residual barrier is not financial incentives — it is something else: trust, physical access to vaccination sites, language barriers, work-schedule inflexibility, or vaccine hesitancy. The paper acknowledges this but does not quantify which barriers dominate. Extending it: combine the zip-code-level treatment non-response with a spatial accessibility decomposition — measuring travel time to vaccination sites, healthcare facility density, provider language capacity, and socio-economic disadvantage — to distinguish financial-incentive-resistant zip codes from access-constrained ones.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my 2SFCA/R2SFCA healthcare accessibility agenda applied to vaccination: compute modified 2SFCA scores for each of the 20 study zip codes using vaccination-site locations and catchment-size calibrated by ACS travel-to-work data, then regress vaccination-rate non-response on 2SFCA residuals. The result is a principled way to prioritise future interventions: zip codes with low access scores need facility deployment or mobile-unit investment; zip codes with high access scores but low uptake may need trust-building outreach.

⑤ Extend the geographic targeting design with a GeoAI-driven zip-code selection algorithm for future public-health lotteries

Extending this paperThe study selected 20 eligible zip codes by pre-sweepstakes vaccination rate, then randomly drew 3 treatment zip codes. This is agnostic to responsiveness heterogeneity: some zip codes would have responded, others would not, regardless of incentive. Extending it: train a GeoAI propensity model — using multi-source data (prior vaccination trajectory, mobility, social media sentiment, healthcare access, community trust indices) — to rank zip codes by predicted treatment responsiveness rather than baseline vaccination rate alone. The experimental framework of the paper then becomes a Bayesian adaptive design: allocate lottery resources to the highest-propensity zip codes first, update the propensity model after each drawing, and re-target dynamically.

Added value to my researchThis integrates my GeoAI and multi-source data fusion methods (mobile phone, social media, POIs, remote sensing) into a real-time public-health decision tool. The adaptive targeting loop mirrors active-learning frameworks in my spatiotemporal modelling research, and the propensity model — predicting health behaviour change as a function of spatial context — is a generalisation of my spatial accessibility and health geography work. It also demonstrates that GeoAI can optimise not just facility placement but intervention targeting at the neighbourhood scale.
