Learning properties of ordered and disordered materials from multi-fidelity data

Chi Chen · Yunxing Zuo · Weike Ye · Xiangguo Li · Shyue Ping Ong Published 2021-01-14 DOI 10.1038/s43588-020-00002-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Multi-fidelity graph networks (MEGNet) provide a universal framework for materials property prediction that bridges cheap low-fidelity computations (e.g., PBE) with scarce high-fidelity data (e.g., HSE, experiment), achieving a 22–45% reduction in mean absolute error on experimental band gap predictions. The same learned elemental embeddings also offer a natural representation for disordered crystals — addressing a long-standing blind spot in computational materials science — by encoding mixed-occupancy sites as linear combinations of element vectors.

★

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the data-scarcity bottleneck. High-accuracy ab initio calculations (HSE, SCAN) and experiments are orders of magnitude fewer than cheap PBE results; multi-fidelity graph networks exploit all fidelity tiers jointly, allowing models to achieve experimental-quality predictions from a fraction of the usual training data.
  • Generalizes across both ordered and disordered crystals. Over 120,000 of the ~200,000 crystals in the Inorganic Crystal Structure Database exhibit site disorder; prior ML models could not handle these. The elemental-embedding interpolation scheme extends graph networks to the vast disordered landscape for the first time.
  • Outperforms transfer learning and model stacking baselines. Direct multi-fidelity joint training captures non-linear cross-fidelity relationships that two-step (freeze-then-retrain) transfer learning misses, especially for experimental targets.
  • Latent space analysis reveals why multi-fidelity works. t-SNE visualization shows the large PBE data set teaches the graph network far richer structural features that separate materials by band gap — providing mechanistic insight, not just a performance claim.
  • Open data and code. The full multi-fidelity band gap dataset (figshare) and MEGNet codebase (GitHub) are publicly released, enabling reproducibility and downstream benchmarking.

Core Methods

  • Multi-fidelity graph network (MEGNet): atoms = nodes, bonds = edges with Gaussian-expanded distances, and the global state encodes fidelity level (PBE=0, GLLB-SC=1, SCAN=2, HSE=3, experiment=4) as a trainable fidelity embedding vector. Sequential graph convolution updates atom, bond, and state features jointly.
  • Elemental embedding matrix: each element's atomic number is mapped to a trainable 16-dimensional embedding; for disordered sites, the node feature is computed as a weighted linear combination of constituent element embeddings according to their occupancy fractions — no retraining needed for previously unseen compositions.
  • Multi-fidelity joint training: all fidelity tiers trained simultaneously on a shared graph architecture; fidelity-level information flows through the global state without requiring all properties labeled for all data points (overcoming multi-task learning's homogeneity requirement).
  • Baseline comparisons: 1-fi (single fidelity), 1-fi-stacked (linear correction on frozen PBE model), and transfer learning (frozen graph-conv layers, retrained output) — all outperformed by multi-fidelity joint training, especially on experimental targets.
  • Data scaling analysis: 2-fi models trained on systematically subsampled high-fi data (varying Nhigh-fi) with varying NPBE — confirms approximately linear MAE decay with log10(NPBE).
  • Latent space visualization: t-SNE (2D projection, complexity=1,000) of learned structural features; pairwise band-gap difference vs. normalized feature distance plotted over 3,651,753 unique crystal pairs to quantify feature resolution.
  • Training setup: TensorFlow; 3 graph-conv layers; Set2Set readout; MLP layers [64, 64, 32]; shifted softplus activation; Adam optimizer (lr=10-3); batch size 128; max 1,500 epochs; 80:10:10 train/val/test split repeated 6 times per fidelity.

① Replace discrete fidelity integers with a learned continuous fidelity manifold

Extending this paperThe paper encodes fidelity as a discrete integer (0–4) fed into a trainable embedding table. This works when fidelity levels are pre-defined and non-overlapping, but collapses when new computational methods (e.g., r2SCAN, meta-GGA hybrids) are added post-training. A direct extension: replace the integer-to-embedding lookup with a continuous fidelity encoder that maps method descriptors (accuracy, cost, systematic bias) to a shared latent fidelity space. Trained jointly with the graph network, this would allow smooth interpolation between known fidelities and zero-shot generalization to new computational methods without retraining. The paper's own data — with its five fidelity tiers spanning 0.27 to 0.62 eV MAE — provides a clean test bed: does a continuous encoder learn a manifold where position correlates with systematic correction magnitude?

Added value to my researchIn GeoAI, data sources occupy an analogous "fidelity spectrum": GPS trajectories are high-resolution but sparse; mobile phone records are dense but coarse; social media check-ins are opportunistic. Casting these as points on a learnable fidelity manifold — rather than domain-specific preprocessing choices — would enable my multi-source data fusion pipelines to jointly train on GPS, cellular, and POI layers with principled cross-source calibration, directly mirroring the paper's cross-fidelity correction logic.

② Active fidelity selection: decide which tier to compute next using the model's own uncertainty

Extending this paperThe paper trains on fixed, pre-existing datasets for each fidelity. But in practice, a computational chemist can choose whether to run a new PBE, HSE, or experimental measurement for a candidate material. An extension: augment the multi-fidelity graph network with a Bayesian uncertainty head and couple it to an active learning loop that selects the next (material, fidelity) pair to compute based on expected information gain. The paper already demonstrates that adding NPBE data decreases MAE log-linearly — this gives an empirical cost–accuracy curve for PBE, which could seed a bandit-style acquisition policy for choosing whether to run cheap PBE or expensive HSE for a given candidate.

Added value to my researchThis is directly transferable to my work on geospatial foundation model fine-tuning: instead of passive fine-tuning on whatever labeled spatial data is available, an active fidelity selector would decide whether to collect high-cost field surveys (high fidelity) or cheap remote sensing labels (low fidelity) for the next spatial unit — a principled data-efficient strategy for building spatial models in data-scarce regions relevant to health geography.

③ Extend the elemental-embedding interpolation to spatial attribute interpolation for heterogeneous regions

Extending this paperThe paper's most elegant idea is that disordered sites are handled by a weighted sum of elemental embeddings: Wdisordered = ∑ xi WZi. This works because the learned embeddings encode chemistry in a way that is naturally interpolatable. The extension: apply this same principle to temporal disorder — materials whose structure fluctuates over time (amorphous phases, thermal disorder) can be represented as time-weighted averages of ordered configurations, allowing the existing 4-fi model to predict properties of dynamically disordered systems without explicit molecular dynamics sampling. The paper's own Supplementary discussion of the virtual crystal approximation points toward exactly this direction.

Added value to my researchThe embedding-interpolation idea maps cleanly onto spatially heterogeneous regions in my work: census tracts or grid cells that are transitional between urban types (neither fully residential nor commercial) could be represented as compositional mixtures of "pure" type embeddings in a spatial graph network. This would allow my GeoAI models for urban analytics — predicting health outcomes, facility accessibility, housing prices — to handle mixed land-use zones without discretizing them into dominant categories, preserving gradient information critical for equity analysis.

④ Use latent structural feature geometry as an interpretability tool for graph network predictions

Extending this paperFig. 3 demonstrates that the 2-fi model's latent space has near-linear correspondence between feature distance and band-gap difference — a remarkably clean interpretability signal. The extension: formalize this into an explanatory attribution framework for graph network predictions. For any prediction, the model could report which reference materials in the training set share the closest latent position, providing a "nearest neighbor in feature space" explanation. Combined with the fidelity embedding, this could further attribute how much of a prediction's confidence derives from the PBE backbone vs. the high-fidelity data — giving computational chemists a principled uncertainty decomposition that goes beyond a single scalar MAE.

⑤ Transfer the multi-fidelity framework to spatial data science: satellite + survey + census as fidelity tiers

Extending this paperThe paper frames multi-fidelity as a hierarchy of computational cost and accuracy. The same structure appears in earth-observation science: free global satellite imagery (Sentinel, Landsat) is "PBE-equivalent" — large volume, systematic bias; national household surveys (DHS, MICS) are "HSE-equivalent" — accurate but scarce and geographically clustered; fine-grained census records are "experimental" — rare, high-resolution, country-specific. Extending the paper: encode each geospatial data source as a fidelity tier in a spatial graph network (nodes = grid cells or census units, edges = adjacency), and jointly train to predict socioeconomic or health indicators. The paper's own log-linear scaling result gives a quantitative prediction of how many cheap satellite samples are needed to achieve a target accuracy — directly applicable to survey design in data-sparse regions.

Added value to my researchThis is the closest connection to my core research agenda. My work on multi-source data fusion (mobile phone, social media, remote sensing) already implicitly treats these as different-quality signals; the multi-fidelity graph network provides a principled architecture to do so explicitly. Applied to spatial accessibility of primary care and mental health services, I could train a fidelity-aware graph network on abundant POI data (PBE tier) and scarce utilization records or patient surveys (experimental tier) to produce fine-grained accessibility predictions in underserved areas — directly advancing both health geography and open science / FAIR data goals.
