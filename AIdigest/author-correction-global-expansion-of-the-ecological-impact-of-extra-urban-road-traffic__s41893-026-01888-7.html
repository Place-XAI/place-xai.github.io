Author Correction: Global expansion of the ecological impact of extra-urban road traffic

Maarten J. van Strien · Adrienne Grêt-Regamey Published 2026-06-12 DOI 10.1038/s41893-026-01888-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

This is an Author Correction to the original article (10.1038/s41893-025-01637-2, published September 2025). A coding error in traffic volume predictions for 1975 and 1990 was identified and corrected; predictions for 2000 and 2015 remain unchanged. The key corrected finding: the extent of global terrestrial land influenced by moderate to very high extra-urban road traffic increased by 61% (revised upward from 53%) since 1975, reaching 239 million hectares in 2015 — roughly 1.8% of global terrestrial land. In 2015, 63% of key biodiversity areas contained road effect zones from moderate to very high traffic, up from 50% in 1975.

Core Methods

  • Traffic volume prediction: global time-series predictions for extra-urban road traffic volumes at 1975, 1990, 2000, and 2015, derived from the companion Scientific Data dataset. A code-level error in the 1975 and 1990 prediction subroutine was identified and corrected (2000 and 2015 predictions validated as correct).
  • Road effect zone (REZ) delineation: high-resolution traffic exposure maps translated into spatial road effect zones capturing ecological and environmental conditions likely influenced by road traffic (noise, pollutants, barrier effects, vehicle mortality).
  • Multi-class traffic impact assessment: REZs stratified by traffic intensity (moderate, high, very high) to allow differentiated land-impact accounting across four time points.
  • Land-use overlay analysis: REZ extents intersected with key biodiversity areas (KBAs), cropland, grassland, and shrubland to quantify sectoral impact at global scale.
  • Regional growth-rate decomposition: annual rate of REZ expansion calculated by region (southern Asia: +4.8%/yr moderate, +11.2%/yr high traffic; southern/western Europe showing slight declines), enabling geographic prioritization of interventions.

③ Disentangle road-traffic impact from urban sprawl impact on biodiversity areas

Extending this paperThe corrected paper isolates extra-urban road effect zones — explicitly excluding urban land. But extra-urban roads and urban expansion are jointly driven by the same development forces. An extension: compute a joint decomposition of total terrestrial ecological impact attributable to (a) direct urban footprint and (b) extra-urban REZs, using counterfactual scenarios in which road networks are held constant as cities expand. This would clarify whether road-traffic or urban-core expansion is the dominant driver of KBA encroachment, guiding which interventions (speed limits, wildlife crossings, urban containment) yield the largest conservation return.
