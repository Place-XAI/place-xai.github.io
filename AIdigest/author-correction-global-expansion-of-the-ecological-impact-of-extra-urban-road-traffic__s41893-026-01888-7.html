Global expansion of the ecological impact of extra-urban road traffic

Maarten J. van Strien · Adrienne Grêt-Regamey Original published 2025-09-18 · Author Correction 2026-06-12 Correction DOI 10.1038/s41893-026-01888-7 Original DOI 10.1038/s41893-025-01637-2 Source nature.com ↗
Author Correction (Erratum)

A code error affecting traffic volume predictions for 1975 and 1990 was identified and corrected. Predictions for 2000 and 2015 remain unaffected. Key corrected values: the global REZ increase since 1975 is now 61% (was 53%); baseline in 1975 was 148 million ha (was 156 million ha). This digest covers the corrected paper in full.

Core Conclusion

The global extent of terrestrial land influenced by moderate to very high extra-urban road traffic increased by 61% between 1975 and 2015, reaching 239 million hectares (1.8% of all terrestrial land). By 2015, 63% of key biodiversity areas were impacted by road traffic, with REZ growth in biodiversity hotspots outpacing surrounding regions. Agricultural land — especially cropland — bears the largest absolute footprint, and Asia is the fastest-growing region. Extra-urban REZs are more than three times the size of all urban land globally.

Why It Matters

  • First global, traffic-volume-aware assessment of road effect zones (REZs). Prior studies used fixed 1-km buffers around all roads, which overestimate quiet-road impacts and underestimate busy-road impacts. This study generates high-resolution (20 m × 20 m) traffic exposure maps reactive to actual Annual Average Daily Traffic (AADT) volumes — a methodological paradigm shift for road ecology.
  • Extra-urban REZs dwarf urban land areas. In 2015, the 239 million ha of REZs was more than three times the global urban land area (65 million ha) — repositioning extra-urban road traffic as a first-order, not secondary, global ecological stressor that demands its own policy framework.
  • Biodiversity conservation areas are disproportionately at risk. REZ growth rates inside key biodiversity areas exceeded regional growth rates, signalling that protected-area management strategies have not kept pace with road-traffic expansion, particularly from high and very high traffic in Asia.
  • Agricultural land is the dominant exposure class. Cropland and grassland/shrubland host the largest absolute REZ extents (87–110 million ha for cropland alone in 1990–2015), linking extra-urban traffic to food safety, soil contamination, insect decline, and agro-ecosystem resilience concerns that compound existing agricultural pressures.
  • Delivers open, queryable spatial data. REZ maps aggregated at 5 km × 5 km are publicly released at the ETH Zurich Research Collection, enabling direct uptake in conservation planning, environmental impact assessment, and sustainability policy.

Core Methods

  • Random-forest AADT prediction model: trained on empirical traffic counts from 46 countries using network betweenness centrality, gross domestic product per capita, Human Development Index, and population density as predictors. Generates median AADT estimates for all extra-urban main roads globally across four time steps.
  • Kernel density traffic exposure mapping: ArcGIS Pro quadratic kernel density function applied to predicted AADT values with a 300-m search radius, producing global 20 m × 20 m traffic exposure rasters. Kernel density values linearly transform to AADT units (R² = 1.0 on single-lane roads), enabling biologically meaningful threshold application.
  • Road Effect Zone (REZ) delineation: three AADT thresholds applied simultaneously — moderate (≥ 500 vehicles/day), high (≥ 5,000), very high (≥ 10,000). REZ width varies with actual traffic volume (max 600 m). REZs derived for 1975, 1990, 2000, and 2015.
  • Land-cover and biodiversity area overlay: REZ rasters overlaid with ESA CCI 300 m land-cover maps (rescaled to match 20 m REZ extent) and the World Database of KBAs. Extents computed per land-use class, per KBA, per global region.
  • Temporal change analysis: compound annual growth rate (CAGR) calculated for each region and traffic impact class between 1975 and 2015. REZ growth rates inside KBAs compared against regional rates to test disproportionate biodiversity exposure.
  • Post-processing stack: REZ maps aggregated from 20 m to 5 km × 5 km for global visualization. Python v.3.7.11 (arcpy, numpy, pandas, geopandas, rasterio) for REZ computation; R v.4.4.2 (tmap, terra, tidyverse) for post-processing and visualization. Code publicly available on GitHub.

① Replace the fixed-radius REZ kernel with a learned, ecology-informed distance-decay function

Extending this paperThe paper caps all REZ widths at 300 m from roads regardless of ecosystem type, land-cover context, or species sensitivity — a conservative but ecologically undifferentiated choice. Meta-analyses cited in the paper show that the distance over which roads affect mammal abundances (~105 m), amphibians (~48 m), and birds (~655 m) varies strongly by taxa. A direct extension: train a context-adaptive kernel bandwidth model that conditions REZ width jointly on traffic volume, land-cover class (forest vs. cropland vs. grassland), and species-group vulnerability. The paper's richly stratified output (three AADT thresholds × multiple land-cover classes × global coverage) already provides the training signal for such a model, enabling species-group-specific REZ footprints rather than a single omnibus 300-m cap.

Added value to my researchCalibrating a spatially adaptive distance-decay from observed ecological responses is structurally identical to the challenge of calibrating the distance-decay function in my 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility models. The "context-conditioned kernel that contracts or expands based on local covariates" principle maps directly onto my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation work: instead of a fixed bandwidth, I apply the same logic to delineate healthcare service-area catchments that adapt to travel-time variability, population density, and service-type. Learning the decay function from data — here ecological damage data, there mobility/usage data — is a shared methodological frontier.

② Couple extra-urban traffic REZ projections with urban expansion scenarios to forecast future ecological risk

Extending this paperThe paper's REZ maps end at 2015 and are retrospective; their Discussion notes that GDP growth and urbanisation drive AADT increases, but the paper does not produce forward-looking scenarios. A direct extension: couple the paper's random-forest AADT prediction model with SSP-based urban expansion and GDP growth projections to generate REZ footprint scenarios for 2030 and 2050. This would convert the retrospective global atlas into a prospective decision-support tool, identifying where traffic-calming policies or modal-shift investments are most urgently needed before biodiversity damage becomes irreversible — particularly in rapidly growing Asian cities where REZ growth rates already exceed 471% (southern Asia, 1975–2015, corrected).

③ Add a transport-policy intervention layer to estimate the ecological value of mitigation measures

Extending this paperThe paper maps where REZs are large and growing but stops short of counterfactual analysis. If a country had adopted fuel taxes, vehicle electrification mandates, or speed-limit enforcement 20 years ago, how much smaller would its REZ footprint be today? Extending it: overlay the paper's AADT time-series with country-level transport-policy archives and run difference-in-differences or synthetic-control analyses to estimate policy-attributable REZ reduction. This converts a descriptive global atlas into an intervention-effectiveness study and directly supports the paper's call for prioritised mitigation in regions currently under moderate traffic (to prevent transition to high traffic).
