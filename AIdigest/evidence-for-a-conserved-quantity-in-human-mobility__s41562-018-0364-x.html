Evidence for a conserved quantity in human mobility

Laura Alessandretti · Piotr Sapiezynski · Vedran Sekara · Sune Lehmann · Andrea Baronchelli Published 2018-06-18 DOI 10.1038/s41562-018-0364-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across ~40,000 individuals tracked for 10–24 months in four independent datasets, the number of familiar locations each person regularly visits is a conserved quantity — stable at approximately 25 places — even as individual routines continuously evolve and new locations are explored. This "location capacity" is conserved at both the population and individual level, cannot be explained by time constraints alone, and is positively correlated with the size of an individual's social circle, suggesting a link to the cognitive limit known as the Dunbar number.

★

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a long-standing contradiction in mobility science. Prior work showed simultaneously that individuals repeatedly exploit few locations and continuously explore new ones. This paper shows both are true: the activity set constantly turns over while its size remains fixed.
  • Identifies the first mobility conservation law at the individual scale. Location capacity (~25) is conserved across all seven duration classes examined — short stops (10–30 min/wk) through home locations (48–168 h/wk) — pointing to a deep cognitive constraint independent of scheduling.
  • Bridges spatial and social behaviour. A significant positive correlation between location capacity and social-network size (Pearson r up to 0.54, p < 10⁻¹³) links mobility science to social cognition and the Dunbar number — opening a new cross-disciplinary research front.
  • Improves state-of-the-art mobility models. Adding a finite memory parameter M to the exploration-and-preferential-return model reproduces the long-term evolution of activity sets that current models miss — a concrete, immediately usable modelling advance.

Core Methods

  • Activity set construction: for each individual, the activity set ASi(t) is the set of locations visited at least twice, averaging more than 10 min/week during a rolling 20-week window — operationalizing the geographical concept of "activity space."
  • Location capacity & net gain: the capacity Ci counts familiar locations at any moment; the net gain Gi = added – removed tracks turnover. Linear and power-law fits test whether Ci drifts in time; individual-level conservation tested by comparing |⟨Gi⟩| against its standard deviation.
  • Heaps' law fit: total discovered locations grow as Li ∝ tα with α ≈ 0.63–0.76 across datasets, confirming sub-linear exploration consistent with prior short-term findings.
  • Jaccard similarity analysis: turnover of the weekly activity set is measured by Ji(t, γ) between sets separated by lag γ; power-law decay J̄ ∝ γλ (λ = −0.15 to −3.00) confirms gradual, continuous evolution.
  • Randomization controls: local (within-day module shuffle) and global (cross-individual day-module assembly) randomizations break temporal correlations; real-data capacity is significantly smaller than randomized under Kolmogorov–Smirnov tests, ruling out a pure time-constraint explanation.
  • Social–spatial correlation: Pearson correlation between normalized location capacity and normalized social network size (phone calls, SMS, Facebook); controlled for extraversion using CNS personality survey data.
  • Model extension: the exploration-and-preferential-return model augmented with finite memory M (past M days govern return probability) qualitatively reproduces all empirical findings including long-term activity-set evolution.

① Encode the conserved capacity as a hard constraint in mobility-flow generation models

Extending this paperThis paper demonstrates that location capacity (~25) is conserved at the individual level, yet its mobility model extension (finite-memory EPR) only enforces this implicitly through a tuned parameter M. A direct extension: incorporate the conserved capacity as a hard architectural constraint — a fixed-size "slot" memory — in a deep generative model of mobility flows (e.g. a graph-based neural ODE), so that the conservation law is never violated by construction. The paper's own finding that the conservation cannot be purely time-derived would then serve as a physics-informed prior in the loss function, and the resulting model's capacity to reproduce Jaccard-decay curves could be tested against the paper's Fig. 3 benchmarks.

② Decompose the global mobility-variation map by local activity-set capacity

Extending this paperThis paper analyses four datasets drawn from Europe and the US; its conserved quantity (~25 locations) may well vary across cultural, economic, and climatic contexts. A natural extension: apply the paper's activity-set framework to the 300-million-user global smartphone dataset used in the "Mapping global variation in human mobility" study (Pullano et al., NHB 2020) and test whether location capacity varies systematically with GDP, urbanization rate, or cultural mobility norms. If the capacity is truly universal, that would be the strongest global confirmation yet; if it varies, the deviations would reveal which external factors modulate the cognitive constraint the paper hypothesizes.

⑤ Model activity-set turnover as a spatiotemporal point process and learn its decay kernel from multi-city data

Extending this paperThe paper characterizes turnover via the Jaccard-similarity power-law decay J̄ ∝ γλ — a single-parameter summary fitted separately per dataset. This could be substantially enriched: model the addition and removal of locations as a marked spatiotemporal Hawkes process where the self-exciting kernel encodes the "habit formation" and "forgetting" dynamics that underlie the paper's conserved capacity. Fitting this model across multiple datasets simultaneously would test whether the exponent λ is universal or driven by dataset-specific collection artifacts (a confound the paper acknowledges), and the learned kernel could replace the paper's finite-memory heuristic M with a principled probabilistic analogue.

Added value to my researchThe Hawkes-process formulation is directly compatible with my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation toolkit and agent-based modelling workflow. Treating location acquisition and abandonment as self-exciting events would let me simulate realistic long-term mobility trajectories — feeding synthetic but statistically consistent individual traces into R2SFCA demand layers without requiring privacy-sensitive GPS data, a clear reproducibility and open-science contribution.
