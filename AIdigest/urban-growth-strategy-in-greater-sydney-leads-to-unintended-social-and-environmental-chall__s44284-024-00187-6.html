Urban growth strategy in Greater Sydney leads to unintended social and environmental challenges

Juan Pablo Ríos-Ocampo · Michael Shayne Gary Published 2025-01-03 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00187-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Coding 500 pages of Greater Sydney urban plans (1968, 2005, 2018) into causal maps reveals that policymakers held a dominant growth strategy — three reinforcing feedback loops driving economic, population and infrastructure growth. That same strategy generated ten balancing loops of social and environmental challenges (congestion, sprawl, unaffordable housing, pollution, heat islands, water stress). Although policymakers progressively recognized these problems, they never identified them as unintended consequences of growth nor changed the strategy — they treated symptoms piecemeal, perpetuating a growth mental model.

Core Methods

  • Archival text analysis: written governmental urban plans used as the data source for eliciting policymakers' perceptions of causal relationships in the urban system.
  • Systematic causal coding: an iterative procedure adapted from Kim & Andersen and Eker & Zimmermann — discover themes, aggregate variables, identify causal links, and link every relationship back to a supporting quotation.
  • Computer-aided qualitative analysis (NVivo): each causal relationship grounded by ≥1 verbatim quote; polarity keywords (boost, improve, decrease, rise, increase) used to assign +/− link signs.
  • Causal-map / system-dynamics representation: variables and signed links assembled into causal loop diagrams with stocks/flows; loops classified as reinforcing (R) or balancing (B).
  • Longitudinal comparison: same agency across 1968/2005/2018 enables tracking how perceptions of social and environmental challenges evolved; later maps add only newly emergent loops for clarity.

① Automate causal-map extraction with an LLM coder over many cities

Extending this paperThe study is explicitly limited to three plans, single-coder, because manual NVivo coding of 500 pages is labor-intensive. A direct extension: fine-tune/prompt an LLM to extract variables, polarity and supporting quotes at scale, validating against the authors' hand-coded gold standard, then run it across the six omitted Sydney plans and dozens of other cities to test the claimed generalizability of the growth-then-symptoms pattern.

Added value to my researchThis is a reusable multi-source text-to-graph pipeline for my open-science/FAIR work — converting unstructured policy corpora (here urban plans, elsewhere health-system or telehealth policy documents) into auditable causal graphs that downstream spatial models can consume.

② Turn the qualitative loops into a quantified, simulatable system-dynamics model

Extending this paperThe paper produces causal loop diagrams but no simulation — loop dominance over time is inferred, not computed. Extending it: parameterize the 3 reinforcing + 10 balancing loops with Greater Sydney time series (population, housing prices, travel time, green-space loss, water storage) and build a stock-and-flow model to quantify when balancing loops overtake growth loops and to stress-test intervention scenarios.

Added value to my researchDirectly feeds my agent-based / SIR-style spatiotemporal modeling: the same calibrated feedback structure can be coupled to a spatial demand model so that, e.g., healthcare-access erosion from sprawl is simulated rather than asserted.

③ Ground each loop in spatial data and test it as a falsifiable hypothesis

Extending this paperEvery causal link rests on a quotation, not a measurement. Extending it: operationalize loops like "urban sprawl → green-space loss → reduced attractiveness" using remote sensing, POIs and mobile-phone data, then test the implied directional relationships with explainable spatial ML / MGWR, converting policymaker beliefs into empirically validated (or refuted) spatial mechanisms.

④ Map the equity blind spot: who bears the unintended consequences

Extending this paperThe paper aggregates impacts to a single proxy — "attractiveness of the city to residents" — and never disaggregates which neighborhoods or groups absorb congestion, unaffordability, heat and pollution. Extending it: attach a distributional/equity layer to the balancing loops, revealing that piecemeal symptom-fixing may itself be spatially regressive.
