Assessing global urban CO2 removal

Quirina Rodriguez Mendez · Sabine Fuss · Sarah Lück · Felix Creutzig Published 2024-05-02 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00069-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Cities can be carbon sinks, not only carbon sources. Synthesizing the literature, the authors quantify the global potential of four urban carbon-dioxide-removal (CDR) options — storing carbon in urban vegetation, soils and buildings, plus capturing CO2 from indoor air via decentralized direct air capture (DAC) — and conclude that deploying them at the urban scale could contribute substantially to global climate mitigation while delivering human and environmental co-benefits. Realizing this potential, however, is constrained by deep uncertainties, economic barriers and governance gaps, motivating a research agenda built around field deployments, context-specific urban geographies, and robust cross-sectoral carbon accounting.

Why It Matters

  • Reframes the city as a sink, not just a source. Urban climate research is dominated by emission inventories; this work flips the lens to the negative-emissions potential of the built environment, opening a complementary path to net-zero.
  • Unifies four scattered CDR options into one comparable assessment. Vegetation, soils, building-stored carbon and indoor DAC are usually studied in separate literatures — here they are placed on a common 2050-potential, co-benefit and barrier grid.
  • Surfaces co-benefits beyond carbon. The same options buy shade, cooler streets, better air quality and well-being, strengthening the political and public-health case for urban climate action.
  • Names the blockers honestly. By cataloguing uncertainties, costs and governance/accounting gaps for each option, it gives planners and funders a realistic map of where deployment actually stalls.

Core Methods

  • Structured literature review: a defined data-collection and screening pipeline (Extended Data Fig. 3) to assemble the evidence base across the four CDR options.
  • Potential quantification: synthesize reported per-option carbon storage/capture rates into global urban potentials by 2050, reported as ranges to reflect uncertainty (Fig. 3).
  • Typology construction: classify removal options by source and final destination of the CO2 (Fig. 1), unifying vegetation, soil, building and DAC pathways plus albedo.
  • Co-benefit and barrier mapping: systematically catalogue principal co-benefits (Figs. 2–3) and implementation barriers — technical, economic and governance/accounting (Fig. 4) — per option.
  • Gap analysis & agenda-setting: distill recommendations centered on field deployments, urban-geography/socioeconomic specificity, and cross-sectoral carbon accounting.

① Replace literature-synthesized potentials with a spatially explicit, satellite-driven CDR map

Extending this paperThe paper's global potentials are aggregated from the literature rather than mapped grid by grid, so they cannot tell a city where vegetation, soil or building carbon actually accrues. A direct extension: bind each of its four CDR options to spatially explicit layers — urban tree-cover and soil-organic-carbon products — and produce a downscaled, per-city CDR potential surface, converting the review's ranges into actionable maps.
