Cities incorporate equity in their climate policies but overlook procedural justice in decision-making

Mahir Yazar · Håvard Haarstad · Johan Elfving Published 2024-12-09 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00167-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across C40 cities with climate action plans, fewer than half lean toward embedding procedural justice (fairness, equality and inclusion) in how decisions are actually made — even as distributive "equity" language is common. Cities that signed the C40 equity pledge, joined C40 later, or sit in the Global South are positively associated with procedural-justice integration. Cities that engage substantively show visible collaboration and clear plans; those that do not focus on outcome fairness while leaving marginalized groups out — a pattern the authors call tokenistic participation. The remedy is concrete organizational tools, not normative prescription.

Why It Matters

  • Names a blind spot in urban climate governance. Cities are good at promising fair outcomes but largely silent on fair processes; the paper makes that gap measurable rather than rhetorical.
  • Diagnoses "tokenistic participation." It distinguishes substantive engagement (collaboration, transparent planning) from box-ticking, giving advocates and auditors a vocabulary to call out symbolic inclusion.
  • Identifies who does better — and why. Equity-pledge adoption, later C40 membership, and Global-South location correlate with stronger procedural justice, challenging the assumption that wealthier early-mover cities lead on inclusion.
  • Points to actionable levers. The conclusion is operational: cities need organizational tools to overcome historical legacies of injustice, not more aspirational policy language.

Core Methods

  • Topic modeling of policy text: latent-topic extraction (LDA-style) over the C40 climate action plans to surface the dominant policy choices and how they are framed.
  • Procedural-justice categorization: cities are coded as engaged with vs. lacking procedural justice based on whether plans show fairness in process (collaboration, transparent planning) or only outcome fairness.
  • Correlational / regression analysis: testing associations between procedural-justice integration and city covariates — equity-pledge status, C40 membership years, Global North/South location, GDP, population, green space (Python + Stata).
  • Comparative framing: contrasting the most frequent policy choices in justice-engaged vs. justice-lacking cities (Figs. 2–3) to characterize substantive vs. tokenistic participation.
  • Mixed-methods reading: quantitative text/statistics combined with qualitative interpretation of decision-making processes and historical injustice legacies.

① Geolocate procedural justice — couple the text labels to where marginalized groups actually live

Extending this paperThe paper codes each city's procedural-justice engagement from plan text alone; it never asks whether "engaged" cities actually reach the neighborhoods that bear climate burdens. A direct extension: overlay the per-city justice labels onto intra-city maps of exposure and marginalization, testing whether procedural engagement in the document corresponds to real spatial inclusion of disadvantaged areas — turning a document-level binary into a spatially validated one.

Added value to my researchThis is a natural bridge to my health-geography equity work: the same exposure-and-marginalization layers I build for primary-care and mental-health accessibility (2SFCA) become the ground truth that tells substantive participation from tokenistic, letting me audit whether process fairness maps onto access fairness.

② From plans to implementation — does coded procedural justice survive contact with delivery?

Extending this paperThe study measures stated procedural justice in CAPs, not what cities do. Extending it means pairing each city's text label with an implementation trace — budgets, ordinance enforcement, who shows up at hearings — to test whether plan-level inclusion predicts on-the-ground inclusion, or whether the gap is precisely where tokenism hides.

Added value to my researchFor my open-science / reproducibility agenda this motivates a multi-source fusion pipeline (plan text + administrative records + social-media participation signals) and an explainable model that attributes the plan-to-practice gap to specific governance features.

③ Upgrade the topic model — from LDA to embedding-based, justice-aware document modeling

Extending this paperThe paper's LDA-style topics capture surface co-occurrence and can blur outcome-fairness language with process-fairness language. Extending it with transformer embeddings and a fine-tuned classifier for procedural vs. distributive justice would sharpen the very distinction the paper rests on, and let the labels transfer to non-C40 cities and other languages.

Added value to my researchThis feeds my geospatial foundation-model fine-tuning line: a justice-aware text encoder becomes one modality fused with remote-sensing and POI features, so policy framing and physical accessibility are modeled in one representation.
