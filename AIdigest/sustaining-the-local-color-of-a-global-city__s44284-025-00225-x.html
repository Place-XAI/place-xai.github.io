Sustaining the local color of a global city

Xuan Xue · Zihui Tian · Yaotian Yang · Junqi Wang · Shi-Jie Cao Published 2025-04-10 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00225-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Through a chromatic reconstruction of 3,103 heritage buildings in Singapore spanning 40+ years before and after conservation, the study shows that the "local color" of inhabited heritage sites is not constant but ongoing and culturally re-inscribed over time. Three trends — historicization, ethnicization and commercialization — reshape color across five measurable dimensions (dominant colors, color complexity, color harmony, average saturation, average value). The authors argue color should be treated as a living part of a city's sociocultural ecology, not a fixed material attribute to be frozen in conservation.

★

Why It Matters

  • Challenges a core assumption of heritage policy. Conservation guidelines often presume each heritage site has a "true" historical palette to be restored and locked in; this work shows the palette is itself a moving, negotiated cultural product.
  • Quantifies culture through color. It operationalizes intangible cultural change into five measurable chromatic dimensions, turning a humanities question about identity into a comparable, reproducible measurement.
  • Bridges computation and the archive. Machine-learning color extraction is fused with archival photographs, paintings, postcards and fieldwork — a hybrid method that gives the numbers historical meaning.
  • Reframes conservation as ecology, not preservation. By linking color to migration, ethnicity and commerce, it offers planners a way to manage living heritage in cosmopolitan, demographically shifting cities rather than embalming it.

Core Methods

  • Chromatic reconstruction: machine-learning color extraction from building/street imagery to recover dominant colors and quantify each façade's palette.
  • Five-dimension color metrics: dominant colors, color complexity, color harmony, average saturation and average value, computed per building, per street and per district.
  • Before/after natural experiment: compare pre- vs. post-conservation streetscapes (Extended Data shophouse pairs) to detect chromatic change attributable to conservation.
  • Cross-scale aggregation: roll measurements up from individual shophouses to three styles, three districts and 80 streets, with diagrammatic façade matrices (Fig. 6).
  • Mixed-methods triangulation: fuse computational outputs with archival sources (photos, paintings, postcards) and fieldwork to interpret historicization, ethnicization and commercialization.

② Couple chromatic change to demographic and migration dynamics

Extending this paperThe paper attributes color change to ethnicization and migration but never models that coupling statistically. Extending it: join the per-street color series to fine-grained census/ethnic-composition and migration data and fit a spatiotemporal model linking demographic turnover to chromatic drift, converting a qualitative narrative into a measured space-human coupling.

③ Make the color metrics explainable and attribution-aware

Extending this paperThe five color dimensions are reported as outcomes; the paper does not quantify which drivers (tourism intensity, retail mix, ownership, conservation-rule changes) explain each dimension. Extending it with an explainable-ML attribution layer (e.g., SHAP over street-level predictors) would tell planners how much commercialization vs. ethnicization moves saturation or harmony at each site.

⑤ Audit the archive: bias and provenance in cultural color data

Extending this paperThe pre-conservation palette leans on paintings, postcards and photographers' archives, each with its own medium bias (monochrome lithography, pastel postcards). Extending the paper: explicitly model archival/source bias and propagate that uncertainty into the color-change estimates, so claims of chromatic drift are robust to the medium that recorded them.
