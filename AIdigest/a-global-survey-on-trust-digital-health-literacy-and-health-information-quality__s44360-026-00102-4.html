A global survey on trust, digital health literacy and health information quality

Rachael Piltch-Loeb · Katarzyna Wyka · Trenton M. White · Scott C. Ratzan · Ken Rabin · Rebecca K. Ivic · Carolina Batista · Ayman El-Mohandes Published 2026-04-28 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00102-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 30 countries (N = 31,000 adults), the qualities people most value in health information are consistent worldwide — clarity, accessibility and source identification — while the perceived credibility of who delivers the message varies. Trust in healthcare providers was consistently higher than in government or community sources: identical messages attributed to providers elicited stronger concern, appeal and behavioural intent. Most respondents preferred mixed words-and-images formats, but acceptance of AI-generated health content varied widely. Effective global health communication must therefore align message design (transparency, simplicity) with professional endorsement.

Why It Matters

  • Separates message design from messenger trust. By holding message content fixed and varying only the attributed source, the study isolates a clean causal-flavoured signal: who endorses a message changes agreement, concern and behavioural intent independent of what it says.
  • Identifies universal "quality cues." Clarity, accessibility and source identification rank highest across very different national contexts — giving communicators a transferable checklist rather than country-by-country guesswork.
  • Documents the AI-content trust gap early. As generative AI floods health information channels, the study provides a 30-country baseline on how acceptance of AI-generated content varies — a benchmark for the next decade of digital health communication.
  • Maps the digital divide in literacy, not just access. High digital health literacy in upper-middle- and low-middle-income countries challenges the assumption that DHL tracks income linearly, reframing where communication interventions are most needed.

Core Methods

  • Cross-sectional survey design: a standardized online questionnaire fielded across 30 countries to capture perceptions of quality health information, DHL, source trust, and format/platform preferences.
  • Source-attribution manipulation: identical message statements presented as coming from healthcare providers vs. government vs. community organizations, comparing agreement with statements of concern, appeal and behavioural intent — isolating messenger credibility from message content.
  • Multi-level stratified analysis: responses analysed by country, economic standing (World Bank income groups), governmental characteristics (EIU Democracy Index), and individual demographics.
  • Quality-cue ranking: elicitation and ranking of valued information features (clarity, accessibility, source identification, format) to surface cross-country regularities.
  • Format/AI preference assessment: measurement of preferred presentation formats (words, images, mixed) and acceptance of AI-generated health content.

① Geolocate the survey to map trust as a spatial surface

Extending this paperThe paper stratifies trust and DHL by country, income group and regime type, but treats each nation as a single point. A direct extension: attach sub-national location to respondents and model trust-in-providers, DHL and AI-acceptance as continuous spatial surfaces, exposing within-country urban–rural and core–periphery gradients that the country-level averages hide — turning a 30-country table into a global trust geography.

Added value to my researchThis is a natural input layer for my health-geography work: a spatially-varying "trust/DHL" covariate can be fused with 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility so that effective access weights not just travel time but whether a population is likely to trust and act on a provider's message — a behaviourally-aware accessibility metric.

② Explain the messenger-trust gap with interpretable ML

Extending this paperThe paper reports that providers out-trust governments and communities, and that AI-acceptance "varies widely," but the drivers stay descriptive. Extending it: fit an explainable model (e.g., gradient boosting + SHAP, or MGWR for the spatial version) on the pooled responses to quantify which features — income group, Democracy Index, age, education, prior platform mix — most move provider-vs-government trust and AI-content acceptance, and where those effects flip sign.

Added value to my researchDirectly feeds my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction agenda: a worked example of attributing a behavioural outcome to socio-spatial covariates, with MGWR capturing non-stationary effects of governance/economy on trust across regions.

③ Close the loop from stated AI-acceptance to real LLM health-query behaviour

Extending this paperThe survey measures stated acceptance of AI-generated health content. A high-value extension links this self-report to observed behaviour — pairing the 30-country acceptance map with large-scale logs of how people actually query LLM chatbots for health, to test whether stated trust predicts real reliance, and where the say–do gap is largest.

Added value to my researchConnects to my multi-source data-fusion and foundation-model lines: combining survey priors with behavioural logs is exactly the fusion pattern I use (mobile/social/RS), here applied to validating fine-tuned geospatial/health foundation models against revealed user trust.

④ Turn quality cues into a deployable, equity-tested message generator

Extending this paperThe paper hands communicators a list of valued cues (clarity, accessibility, source identification, mixed words-and-images, provider endorsement) but stops at description. Extending it: encode those cues as constraints on an LLM-based health-message generator and run a 30-country-style randomized test of whether cue-compliant, provider-attributed messages raise comprehension and intent — and audit for the socio-demographic gaps such generation can introduce.

⑤ Couple trust/credibility with misinformation diffusion on networks

Extending this paperThe paper measures static source credibility but not how messages spread. Extending it: use the empirically-measured provider/government/community trust weights as node-level susceptibility parameters in a network diffusion / SIR-style model, simulating how the same content propagates differently depending on attributed source and local trust — bridging credibility cues to population-scale information dynamics.
