Characterization and forecast of global influenza subtype dynamics

Francesco Bonacina · Pierre-Yves Boëlle · Vittoria Colizza · Olivier Lopez · Maud Thomas · Chiara Poletto Published 2026-03-11 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00069-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Treating each country's yearly mix of influenza A/H1N1pdm, A/H3N2 and B as a compositional trajectory (CoDA, isometric log-ratio) reveals a hidden spatial structure in global subtype circulation. International air mobility, epidemic synchrony and climate shape this structure, splitting the world into two macroregions — one of synchronous subtype alternation (Europe, North Africa, West Asia) and one of persistent mixing. Encoding this global history in a Bayesian hierarchical vector-autoregressive (HVAR) model improves one-year-ahead forecasts of next season's subtype composition over naive baselines (global dominance-state accuracy 34% vs 27%, AUROC up to 0.82–0.91 for some subtype/observable pairs).

★

Why It Matters

  • Subtype mix drives epidemic burden. A/H3N2-dominated seasons hit the elderly harder and are more severe, while A/H1N1pdm and B fall more on the young — so anticipating which subtype dominates next year informs hospital capacity, vaccination and age-targeted messaging, well beyond forecasting case counts.
  • Imports a rigorous method into epidemiology. Compositional data analysis (sum-to-one percentages handled via log-ratios) is standard in ecology/geology but rare in infectious disease; here it unlocks state-of-the-art time-series tools on a kind of data previously hard to model.
  • Turns geography into a predictor. It shows air-travel connectivity, not just climate, organizes which countries co-circulate the same subtypes — quantifying mobility's role in phase-locking subtype alternation across continents.
  • Defines a new, reproducible forecasting task. Five algorithms (from naive to HVAR), explicit probabilistic scoring (energy score, AUROC), and open code/data give the field a baseline benchmark for one-year-ahead subtype-composition prediction.

Core Methods

  • Compositional data analysis (CoDA): each country/year is a (B%, H1%, H3%) composition; the isometric log-ratio (i.l.r.) transform maps it to two unbounded Euclidean coordinates (sensitivity-checked against additive log-ratio), enabling standard statistics on sum-to-one data. Zeros imputed via geometric Bayesian-multiplicative treatment.
  • Mixing score: signed distance from a composition to the codominance boundary in the log-ratio plane — a concise metric of subtype dominance vs co-dominance, used to flag anomalous years (2003, 2009, 2020, 2021).
  • Geographical drivers: country-pair trajectory distances regressed on air-traffic, temperature, humidity and epidemic-synchrony class via univariable Mantel tests (Spearman) and multivariable linear regression.
  • Clustering: Ward hierarchical clustering of trajectories with Silhouette-selected k = 2 (and a finer 6-group split), broadly recovering WHO Influenza Transmission Zones.
  • Forecasting (5 models): M1 past-frequencies, M2 H1–H3 alternation, M3 average composition, M4 VAR (lag 1), and M5 Bayesian hierarchical VAR fitted per cluster via Gibbs sampling (lag 1–2), borrowing strength across similar countries.
  • Evaluation: dominance-state accuracy, multivariate energy score (proper score), and AUROC for binary dominance/negligibility, with bootstrap confidence intervals (200 resamples) and extensive sensitivity scenarios.

① Replace the cluster prior in HVAR with a continuous mobility-weighted graph

Extending this paperThe paper's HVAR borrows strength only within a discrete two-group clustering, and the authors themselves note that "incorporating other countries' trajectories at different degrees according to similarity could increase quality." A direct extension: drop the hard clusters and let each country's VAR coefficients shrink toward neighbours weighted by the IATA air-travel network, i.e. a graph-regularized / graph-neural VAR where the very mobility matrix shown to predict trajectory distance becomes the model's coupling kernel.

Added value to my researchThis is exactly my physics-informed graph-learning agenda: encode a mobility/distance Laplacian as a soft constraint on a spatiotemporal predictor. The same construction transfers to my telehealth and primary-care work — predicting service-demand trajectories where regions borrow strength through realized commuting/referral flows rather than arbitrary administrative groupings.

② Fuse the subtype-composition forecast with incidence-level epidemic forecasting

Extending this paperThe paper forecasts which subtype mix arrives but explicitly leaves how big/when to other consortia (Flu Scenario Modeling Hub, FluSight). Extending it: use the predicted next-year composition as a conditioning prior for short-term incidence models — training intensity/peak-timing forecasters only on past seasons of similar subtype frequency, as the authors suggest, to sharpen burden and age-distribution projections.

Added value to my researchIt hands me a two-stage GeoAI pipeline (composition → magnitude) that I can wire into spatiotemporal kernel-density and ML-ensemble forecasting, and into agent-based / SIR simulations where the dominant subtype sets transmissibility and age-mixing parameters.

③ Downscale from country-year to within-country space-time compositions

Extending this paperThe analysis is national and annual; the authors flag that within-country spatial coupling and weekly resolution are unexplored. Extending it: apply the CoDA + mixing-score machinery to sub-national, weekly surveillance, mapping how subtype dominance diffuses across regions within a season — turning the global "hidden structure" into an actionable intra-country diffusion map.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my spatiotemporal-analysis lane: a mixing-score raster over time is a natural input for MGWR/GWR (does local density, urbanization or connectivity modulate dominance?) and for spatiotemporal KDE of dominance fronts, linking pathogen ecology to the fine-scale population-density heterogeneity I study.

④ Make the geographical-driver model explainable and counterfactual

Extending this paperThe paper establishes air-travel > synchrony > temperature > humidity via linear/Mantel association, but stops at correlation. Extending it: refit trajectory-distance prediction with explainable ML (e.g., gradient-boosting + SHAP) to capture non-linearities and interactions, then run mobility counterfactuals ("what if connectivity dropped 50%?") akin to the COVID-19 segregation the data already revealed in 2020–2021.

⑤ Generalize the compositional-forecast template to other co-circulating pathogens

Extending this paperThe authors note their CoDA pipeline transfers to "SARS-CoV-2 variants, RSV types, HPV genotypes, Dengue serotypes" and antibiotic-resistance strain mixes. Extending it: build a shared multi-pathogen compositional-forecasting benchmark (same mixing-score, energy-score, HVAR baselines) so any strain/genotype percentage series gets a standard, comparable one-step-ahead forecast.
