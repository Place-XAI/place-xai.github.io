Advancing a slum–circular economy model for sustainability transition in cities of the Global South

Matthew Abunyewah · Michael Odei Erdiaw-Kwasie · Seth Asare Okyere · Festival Godwin Boateng Published 2023-07-27 DOI 10.1038/s41893-023-01176-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Slum communities across the Global South already practice subaltern circularity — everyday life-making strategies in livelihoods, housing, and space that directly embody circular economy (CE) principles — yet remain invisible in mainstream CE policy and finance. This Perspective develops a theoretical slum–CE model mapping how waste picking, adaptive housing construction, and community-managed green spaces align with CE's core principles of designing out waste, keeping products in use, and regenerating natural systems. Recognizing and integrating these grassroot circular practices into formal CE frameworks offers a pathway toward inclusive, just sustainability transitions in cities of the Global South.

Why It Matters

  • Repositions slums from problem to asset in CE discourse. The dominant framing casts informal settlements as barriers to sustainability; this Perspective inverts that, showing they are existing circularity laboratories with globally significant scale — Dharavi alone accounts for 60–80% of Mumbai's waste recycling.
  • Closes a major gap in subaltern CE scholarship. Prior CE research focused on Global North countries or narrowly on waste picking; this is the first systematic theoretical model covering livelihoods, housing, and public space as interlocking CE elements in Global South slums.
  • Generates empirically testable propositions. Three literature-grounded propositions — on design knowledge, coalition-building, and waste-picker livelihoods — translate qualitative insight into a rigorous research agenda, moving the field from narrative to testable theory.
  • Connects environmental justice to CE implementation. By surfacing the health and labour risks embedded in informal recycling (e.g., e-waste exposure in Ghana and India), the model insists that circularity gains must not reproduce inequality, putting social sustainability at the core of CE transition planning.

① Map and quantify the slum CE spatial footprint using remote sensing and GeoAI

Extending this paperThe paper's slum–CE model is richly theoretical but relies on qualitative city-level examples; it cannot tell us where circular practices are concentrated, at what density, or how they vary spatially within and across cities. A direct extension: use high-resolution satellite imagery and geospatial deep learning to delineate informal settlement extents and infer the spatial density of CE-relevant activities (waste sorting sites, community gardens, informal repair hubs) across the Global South. The corpus contains a companion paper that already maps sub-Saharan African slums and their socioeconomic inequality using geospatial data integration with household surveys — this provides the spatial foundation the model currently lacks.

Added value to my researchMapping slum CE activities is a direct GeoAI application: fine-tuning a geospatial foundation model on informal settlement imagery to produce multi-class activity maps that can then be used as explanatory variables in spatially weighted regression (GWR/MGWR) models — linking place characteristics to circular practice intensity. This bridges my remote sensing + explainable ML agenda to an equity-sensitive urban sustainability domain.

② Quantify flood-driven disruption to slum CE cycles and model intervention tipping points

Extending this paperThe paper documents how slum dwellers in Dhaka use in situ materials and flood-adaptive construction — acknowledging environmental stress as a CE design driver — but does not model how flooding disrupts or accelerates CE cycles. A natural extension: combine the slum–CE model with flood exposure data to identify which CE practices (waste-picker livelihoods, community gardens, informal repair) are most vulnerable to disruption, and at what flood return-period threshold the informal CE economy collapses, creating a CE resilience metric for global slum populations. The corpus contains a quantitative study of disproportionate flood exposure for slum populations of the Global South that provides exactly this risk layer.

Added value to my researchThis translates into a Monte Carlo simulation of CE resilience under climate stressors — a methodology I use for uncertainty quantification in spatial models. Coupling flood risk probability distributions with CE activity spatial maps creates a spatiotemporal risk surface that can inform both public health and sustainability policy, directly linking my health geography and spatiotemporal kernel density estimation methods to informal urban economies.

Cross-ref (corpus): Disproportionate flood exposure for slum populations of the Global South — 10.1038/s44284-025-00273-3

③ Test whether urban-scale waste flows obey scaling laws that validate the CE model's claims

Extending this paperThe paper makes a sweeping theoretical claim — that slum circularity is embedded across all CE dimensions — but has no quantitative scaling anchor. If informal CE practices obey predictable urban scaling relationships (waste recycled ∝ populationα), that would provide the model with a quantitative universality it currently lacks and could help identify which city sizes benefit most from formalizing slum CE. The corpus contains a worldwide scaling analysis of waste generation in urban systems that provides exactly this cross-city quantitative framework against which to test the informal CE hypothesis.

The answer would inform whether policy should concentrate CE formalization in megacities or invest more in secondary cities — a spatial policy optimization problem I can model with my MGWR toolkit.

④ From theoretical propositions to peer-to-peer resource-sharing networks: a graph-learning approach

The paper develops this verbally; a computational extension would construct a multi-layer network of informal CE flows (material, social capital, economic) and apply community detection and flow analytics to identify the structural positions of formal actors (cooperatives, NGOs) that most efficiently amplify circularity. The corpus's study of peer-to-peer resource sharing for community resilience provides a complementary network-science framework for modeling exactly these informal exchange topologies.

⑤ Connect CE health risks to psychosis and public health geography: a spatial health equity lens

Extending this paperThe paper explicitly flags health risks of informal CE (e-waste exposure causing cancer and skeletal deformities; toxic working environments in Ghana and India) as a tension with CE social sustainability principles — but does not integrate a health geography framework. A direct extension: build on the paper's environmental-justice critique to map the spatial co-occurrence of informal CE activity intensity and adverse health outcomes (respiratory disease, psychosis incidence, cancer registries) in Global South cities, testing whether CE formalization interventions reduce health burdens or merely relocate them to newly designated recycling zones. The corpus includes a study finding that slum living predicts psychosis severity in first-episode patients — exactly the kind of health endpoint to include.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely in my health geography agenda: spatial accessibility of mental and primary health care is a core research theme, and the question of whether informal CE workers have equitable access to health services — particularly for occupational disease — is a 2SFCA/R2SFCA measurement problem. Overlaying CE activity density with health-facility catchment areas would reveal whether environmental justice failures in informal CE are compounded by healthcare access inequity, producing a compound vulnerability index directly informative for public health policy.
