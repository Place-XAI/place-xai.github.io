Enhancing computational fluid dynamics with machine learning

Ricardo Vinuesa · Steven L. Brunton Published 2022-06-27 DOI 10.1038/s43588-022-00264-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Machine learning is becoming a core technology for computational fluid dynamics (CFD), with high-impact opportunities in three areas: accelerating direct numerical simulations (DNS) via learned discretization corrections, improving turbulence closure models (RANS and LES) with physics-aware neural networks and reinforcement learning, and developing enhanced reduced-order models through deep autoencoders and sparse dynamical-systems identification. Embedding physical constraints, enabling interpretability, and adopting open benchmarks are identified as critical steps to realize this potential at scale.

Core Methods

  • ML for DNS acceleration: learned spatial-derivative estimation, coarse-to-fine correction (super-resolution), CNN-based Poisson solvers, and ML-generated inflow boundary conditions — all aimed at cutting the quadratic-to-cubic cost scaling of DNS with Reynolds number.
  • RANS turbulence closure with ML: tensor-basis neural networks (embedding Galilean invariance), physics-informed random forests, gene-expression programming (GEP), SINDy-based closed-form models, and interpretable residual networks (PiResNet) — all targeting the Reynolds-stress closure problem for complex geometries and separated flows.
  • LES subgrid-scale modeling: supervised CNN/U-net approaches trained on filtered DNS, and unsupervised multi-agent reinforcement learning (RL) to tune the Smagorinski dissipation coefficient at each grid point — generalizing across grid sizes.
  • Reduced-order models (ROMs): deep (nonlinear) autoencoders for manifold-coordinate learning, combined with LSTM networks, echo-state/reservoir networks, SINDy sparse regression, and operator inference for dynamics identification — extending classical POD/DMD into nonlinear regimes.
  • Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs): automatic-differentiation-based PDE residual enforcement used for turbulence modeling, ROM generation, noisy-data denoising, and Poisson-equation acceleration.
  • Emerging methods discussed: non-intrusive sensing (CNN wall-to-flow prediction), super-resolution via GANs, physical symmetry/invariance embedding, transfer learning, and quantum-computing prospects.

① Transfer the paper's learned PDE-correction paradigm to spatiotemporal urban prediction

Extending this paperThe paper's most concrete deliverable is the coarse-to-fine ML correction for DNS: a learned operator maps a low-resolution flow field to the missing fine-scale detail, eliminating the need to solve the full high-resolution system. The paper acknowledges this principle generalizes to "weather, climate, and engineering" flows but stops short of formalizing the operator. Extending it via the corpus's deep transfer operator framework — which learns conditional mappings between PDE solution families across distribution shifts — would allow the paper's correction to generalize across Reynolds numbers and geometry classes without retraining from scratch.

③ Extend the paper's multi-agent RL turbulence framework to distributed urban-network control

Extending this paperThe paper highlights Novati et al.'s multi-agent RL as the most sophisticated LES subgrid-scale approach: each agent at a grid point observes local and global flow invariants and receives a reward based on simulation accuracy. The paper notes good generalization across grid sizes but limited testing beyond isotropic turbulence. The corpus paper on ML for PDEs identifies multi-scale operator learning as the logical complement — embedding the agents' reward structure directly into the operator's physics loss would unify the paper's RL approach with the broader PDE-solver literature and enable transfer to complex geometries.

Added value to my researchThe multi-agent RL grid-point architecture is structurally analogous to a distributed facility-location optimizer: each healthcare or EV-charging facility "agent" observes local demand and global network state to adjust its effective service catchment — generalizing my 2SFCA/R2SFCA from static zones to adaptive, RL-tuned service areas that respond to changing urban conditions.
