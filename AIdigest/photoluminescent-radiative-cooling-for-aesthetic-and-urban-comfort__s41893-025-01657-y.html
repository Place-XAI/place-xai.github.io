Photoluminescent radiative cooling for aesthetic and urban comfort

Fu, Yang · Ma, Xue · Zhang, Xiao-Wen · Li, Ze · Wang, Chuyao · Lin, Kaixin · Zhou, Yiying · Pan, Aiqiang · Chen, Xu · Li, Xin · Wang, Wenqi · Kwok, Chui Ting · Zhu, Yi-Hao · Xue, Xiao · Zhao, Xin · Rogach, Andrey L. · Li, Longnan · Li, Wei · Tso, Chi Yan Published 2025-10-07 DOI 10.1038/s41893-025-01657-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Inspired by coral polyp photoadaptation, photoluminescence-based aesthetic composites (PLACs) using rare-earth-doped phosphors achieve vivid coloured radiative cooling with effective spectral reflectance exceeding 100% and peak reflectance up to 141%, while maintaining a moderate overall solar reflectance of 90.2–93.2%. In outdoor field tests, PLACs kept surfaces 2.2–3.9 °C below ambient and up to 7.9 °C cooler than non-photoluminescent counterparts. Beyond material performance, the moderate whiteness of PLACs reduces sunlight trapping in urban canyons, cutting mean radiant temperature by up to 9.3 °C, thermal discomfort periods by 8.6%, and visual discomfort periods by 5.8% — simultaneously solving the colour-cooling trade-off and the urban microclimate challenge posed by super-white passive radiative cooling surfaces.

Why It Matters

  • Resolves the long-standing colour-cooling dilemma. Conventional passive radiative cooling materials are constrained to near-white appearances; PLACs use light down-conversion to decouple colour from scattering-based whiteness, enabling vivid green, yellow and red surfaces that still achieve subambient temperatures — expanding the real-world applicability of radiative cooling to architecture, textiles and consumer products.
  • Exposes and quantifies the urban microclimate penalty of super-white coatings. The paper shows through simulations of 19 global climate zones that super-white passive radiative cooling walls intensify sunlight trapping in urban canyons, increasing rather than reducing pedestrian heat and glare stress — a previously underexamined side effect at city scale.
  • Introduces a standardized measurement methodology (SFP). The spectro-fluorescence-photometry method fills a critical analytical gap: existing spectrophotometers misattribute re-emitted photons as reflected light in PL materials, causing systematic measurement errors. The SFP protocol enables accurate characterization and reproducible cross-material comparison.
  • Quantifies energy-saving potential globally. EnergyPlus simulations across 19 representative cities from tropical to subarctic climates show annual cooling energy reductions of 13–63 GJ yr−1 for a typical mid-rise apartment — equivalent to 4–20 months of household electricity consumption in hot regions.
  • Demonstrates scalability and broad substrate compatibility. PLACs can be spray-, spin-, or blade-coated onto metal, wood, ceramic, polymer and textile substrates, with super-hydrophobicity engineered on demand and performance confirmed stable through 5-month UV ageing and 300-hour accelerated weathering tests.

Core Methods

  • Photoluminescence composite fabrication: PDMS matrix with rare-earth-doped phosphors and 50 nm SiO2 nanoparticles; volume fraction of PL pigments optimized at ~8% to balance solar reflectance and light conversion efficiency (plateau at ~54–63% conversion efficiency).
  • Spectro-fluorescence-photometry (SFP): novel two-step methodology using short-pass filters to decouple PL re-emission from true spectral reflectance — corrects systematic overestimation of reflectance in conventional spectrophotometry of PL materials.
  • Mie / Rayleigh scattering engineering: 50 nm SiO2 nanoparticles chosen to exploit Rayleigh scattering for UV-range path elongation (enhancing phosphor excitation) while suppressing Mie scattering in visible range (preventing re-absorption of PL emission). COMSOL Multiphysics used for electromagnetic field simulation.
  • Outdoor cooling power measurement: feedback-controlled resistance heater under each sample; DC power adjusted so sample temperature tracks ambient; steady-state heater output = net cooling power (averaged 11:00–15:00).
  • Building energy simulation: EnergyPlus v22.1.0 with PNNL mid-rise apartment prototype, standard HVAC/occupancy schedules, across 19 ASHRAE climate zones. Cooling energy savings reported relative to default building surface and relative to same-colour non-PL baseline.
  • Urban canyon microclimate simulation (PUCM): 2D canyon model solving shortwave and longwave radiative energy balance; outputs wall/ground surface temperatures and mean radiant temperature (MRT). UTCI calculated as thermal comfort index; DGP calculated from daylight intensity distributions as visual comfort index.
  • Durability tests: UV ageing (40 W UVA-340 lamp, ASTM-154, 5 months), natural outdoor exposure (1 month rooftop), and accelerated weathering (Ci5000 xenon lamp, 300 h, GB/T 9755-2014), with combined UV, humidity and rainfall cycling.

Extending this paperThe paper's urban comfort gains depend on the canyon aspect ratio and the existing albedo of surrounding buildings — yet the authors evaluate only a handful of fixed canyon configurations. Cities with the densest ultra-white building stock would score highest, converting the paper's simulation output from a design diagram into an actionable spatial decision layer.

② Build an explainable ML surrogate for the PUCM to enable city-wide PLAC scenario planning

⑤ Extend the SFP characterization standard into a remote-sensing validation pipeline for urban albedo products

Extending this paperThe paper introduces the SFP method as a laboratory standard but limits it to individual sample characterization. An important extension would validate whether photoluminescent building materials cause analogous misinterpretation artefacts in satellite-derived urban surface albedo products (e.g. MODIS MCD43, Sentinel-2 surface reflectance) that also rely on spectral radiance measurements without PL decoupling. If fluorescent urban surfaces confound satellite albedo retrievals, the paper's SFP insight demands a correction algorithm for remote-sensing urban albedo maps — resolving a measurement bias that would otherwise systematically distort UHI studies globally.
