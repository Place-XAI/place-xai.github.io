Empirical evidence of air pollution reduction from electric vehicle usage across Chinese cities

Yuanhong Ma · Minghao Qiu · Yuan Wang · Jiaofeng Pan · Jianfeng Guo · Fu Gu · Xitong Li Published 2026-03-10 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00395-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using real EV charging records from 2016–2023 across 292 Chinese cities, the study empirically shows that a 10% increase in EV charging is associated with −0.08% surface NO2 and −0.04% PM2.5, with no significant effect on ambient O3. The benefits are heterogeneous: they are stronger at extremely low temperatures (below −7 °C) despite reduced battery performance, and larger in southern cities than northern ones — likely because of higher EV utilization rates. The takeaway is that EV air-quality gains depend on actual usage, not just adoption, giving operators and policy-makers concrete levers to allocate EV resources.

Why It Matters

  • Measures usage, not just adoption. Most prior EV air-quality work relies on fleet-size scenarios or emission-inventory models; this paper uses observed charging behavior as a direct proxy for real on-road EV activity, closing the gap between projected and realized benefits.
  • Quantifies a heterogeneous, conditional benefit. The effect is not a flat constant — it depends on ambient temperature and region, revealing that the same EV adoption buys different air-quality returns in different places and seasons.
  • Counter-intuitive cold-weather finding. Benefits intensify at extreme cold (below −7 °C) even though battery efficiency drops, pointing to displacement of heavily polluting cold-start internal-combustion trips.
  • Directly policy-actionable. Findings tell operators and city planners where and when EV deployment delivers the most air-quality value, helping target subsidies and charging-infrastructure rollout toward high-return cities.

Core Methods

  • Panel econometric identification: a city–time fixed-effects regression linking percent change in EV charging to percent change in pollutant concentrations, controlling for meteorology and socio-economics to isolate the EV signal.
  • Elasticity estimation: reports the air-quality response per 10% increase in charging separately for NO2, PM2.5 and O3.
  • Temperature-dependent heterogeneity: interaction/binned-temperature models reveal the effect varies with ambient temperature, peaking below −7 °C (Fig. 2).
  • Regional stratification: separate estimates for southern vs. northern cities, attributing the gap to differing EV utilization rates (Fig. 3).
  • Counterfactual projection: reconstructs "with vs. without EV deployment" NO2 / PM2.5 trajectories over 2016–2023 to quantify cumulative avoided pollution (Fig. 4).

① From city-mean elasticities to explainable, spatially-varying effects

Extending this paperThe paper reports a single national elasticity (−0.08% NO2 per 10% charging) plus coarse temperature/region splits, but it never maps a continuous, place-specific effect surface. A direct extension: replace the pooled fixed-effects model with MGWR or an explainable gradient-boosting + SHAP pipeline so each city gets its own EV-to-air-quality coefficient, exposing why southern cities outperform northern ones beyond the "utilization rate" hypothesis (fleet age, grid mix, road density).

② Couple charging-usage benefits with a grid-mix / marginal-emissions layer

Extending this paperThe paper treats charging as uniformly "clean," but the air-quality return of a charged kWh depends on when and where the electricity is generated. Extending it: merge the charging records with marginal generation-emission signals so the estimated benefit is conditioned on the local grid carbon/pollution intensity at charging time — likely sharpening the north–south gap and the cold-weather anomaly.
