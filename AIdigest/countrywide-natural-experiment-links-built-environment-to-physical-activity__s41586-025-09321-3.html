Countrywide natural experiment links built environment to physical activity

Tim Althoff · Boris Ivanovic · Abby C. King · Jennifer L. Hicks · Scott L. Delp · Jure Leskovec Published 2025-08-13 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09321-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using a countrywide natural experiment of 2.1 million smartphone users and 7,447 US city-to-city relocations, this study provides robust causal evidence that more walkable built environments directly cause more physical activity. Moving from a 25th- to a 75th-percentile walkable city raises daily steps by roughly 1,100 steps, the extra activity is predominantly moderate-to-vigorous, effects are sustained over 3 months, and residential self-selection is ruled out — implying that making all US cities as walkable as Chicago would bring 36 million more Americans into compliance with aerobic exercise guidelines.

★

Why It Matters

  • First large-scale causal evidence from a natural experiment. Previous built-environment and physical-activity research relied on small cohorts, self-reported data, or cross-sectional designs. This study's within-person longitudinal design across 1,609 US cities resolves decades of mixed findings and establishes a credible causal pathway from walkability to activity.
  • Clinically meaningful effect sizes. A 1,100-step increase is equivalent to ~11 extra minutes of walking per day. For a 49–80-point walkability gain, the fraction of participants meeting aerobic guidelines nearly doubles (21.5% → 42.5%), directly addressing a major chronic-disease risk factor.
  • Population-scale policy simulation. The paper translates individual-level findings into a national estimate: bringing all US cities to Chicago/Philadelphia's walkability level (score 78) could move 36 million more Americans into guideline compliance — a number comparable in scale to major pharmaceutical interventions.
  • Generalizes across demographics. Walkability benefits hold across age groups, genders, BMI categories, and baseline activity levels, with the lone exception of women over 50 — which itself has actionable implications for targeted complementary interventions.
  • Open data and replicable design. Code and anonymized data are available at GitHub (behavioral-data/movers-public), enabling replication and extension to other countries and health outcomes.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Primary cohort
Azumio Argus smartphone app — anonymized, passively collected step data from 2,112,288 US smartphone users over 3 years (March 2013 – February 2016). From these, 5,424 participants were identified as having relocated at least once (7,447 total relocations across 1,609 US cities, covering >137 million Americans / >42% of the US population).
Activity measure
Minute-by-minute step counts derived from smartphone accelerometers; 248,266 days of valid physical activity recordings surrounding relocations (595,803 days in 180-day window). Average participant recorded 5,574 steps/day over 14.2-hour spans.
Walkability scores
Walk Score (walkscore.com) — publicly available, city-level scores (0–100) based on proximity to amenities within 0.25–1.5-mile radius and pedestrian-friendly street features. City-level score is population-density-weighted block-level average.
Geographic context
US Census Bureau 2016 cartographic boundary files for city-level geographic context; Köppen climate classifications used as a confounder control across paired cities.
Socioeconomic controls
Median household income at city level (for confounding analysis); US Census Bureau 2013 survey for relocation-motivation distributions.
Time window
Physical activity data: March 2013 – February 2016 (3-year observation). Walk Scores: accessed at time of study. Activity windows: ±30 days around each relocation (±90 days for long-term persistence analyses).
Availability
Data and code openly available at github.com/behavioral-data/movers-public. Original app data obtained via Data Use Agreement with Azumio; study deemed exempt by Stanford University IRB.

Core Methods

  • Quasi-experimental natural experiment design: relocations are treated as natural assignments of individuals to new built-environment conditions; within-person changes before/after relocation substitute for a randomized controlled trial.
  • Relocation identification: participants flagged as relocated if they spent ≥14 days in one location (within 100 km radius), then moved ≥100 km and remained in the new location ≥14 days; results robust to 21- and 30-day thresholds (Extended Data Fig. 6).
  • Aggregating quasi-experiments: relocations binned by walkability difference (Δ Walk Score: −16 to +16 / 16–48 / 49–80 point categories); a linear model m·Δ + b fit to city-pair data (slope m = 16.6 steps/Walk Score point, P < 10⁻¹⁰).
  • Within-person longitudinal analysis: each relocation episode provides a pre/post comparison for the same individual, controlling for all time-invariant personal characteristics; 5-day buffer around move date excluded to avoid confounding by relocation process itself.
  • MVPA estimation: minutes at ≥100 steps/min classified as moderate-to-vigorous physical activity (MVPA); Δ T_MVPA summed across intensity levels; compared against US/international 150-min/week guideline.
  • Selection-effect tests: (1) no activity increase in same-walkability relocations (bootstrapped 95% CI: [−76, 122] steps); (2) symmetric increases/decreases for opposite-direction moves; (3) movers and non-movers similar in age, gender, weight status; (4) US Census evidence that ≥77% of movers cite housing/family/employment rather than walkability reasons.
  • Nationwide simulation: ratio-based post-stratification weights align smartphone user age distribution to US Census 2016; bootstrap (1,000 replications) estimates fraction meeting aerobic guidelines if all cities reach target walkability score.
  • Seasonality adjustment: calendar-month weights applied to equalize seasonal relocation distributions; activity estimates pooled across months before averaging.

① From city-level Walk Score to neighbourhood-level causal maps of walkability components

Extending this paperThe paper explicitly flags its core limitation: Walk Score is a city-level aggregate that cannot identify which elements of walkability drive activity gains — whether intersection density, grocery proximity, park access, or transit stops. A direct extension would disaggregate the relocation design to census-tract or block-group level using a richer, multi-component walkability index. Coupled with satellite building-function mapping at city scale (which now classifies every building's use in urban China and is applicable globally), the extended study could attribute steps to specific amenity types and identify the highest-return built-environment investments.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly onto my spatial accessibility methods: 2SFCA/R2SFCA already decomposes supply by facility type and applies distance-decay weighting. Treating step counts as the outcome variable in an R2SFCA-style demand-weighted framework would let me estimate an activity-return function per amenity category — transforming walkability analysis from a composite score into a spatially explicit, multi-source accessibility measurement problem aligned with my facility-access and healthy-cities research.

② Use the relocation design to test GeoAI-predicted walkability against behavioural outcomes

Extending this paperThe paper uses Walk Score as its exposure, which is pre-computed and opaque. An extension would replace Walk Score with an explainable GeoAI-derived walkability surface — trained on street-network topology, land-use mix, POI density, and street-view imagery — and then test whether GeoAI walkability predictions better explain within-person step changes at the relocation level. The natural experiment design provides a clean outcome label (observed step change) to supervise and validate the GeoAI predictions, turning the paper's quasi-experimental scaffold into a benchmark for walkability model comparison.

Added value to my researchExplainable ML for spatial prediction is a core methodological theme in my work. Using the paper's dataset as an evaluation harness for GeoAI walkability models gives me a rare objective ground-truth (observed behavioral response rather than self-reported walkability perception), directly advancing my agenda of building and validating interpretable geospatial models. The SHAP/MGWR attribution of which spatial features predict step change would produce policy-relevant feature importance at the neighbourhood level.

③ Integrate the step-change findings into a spatiotemporal kernel density model of activity gaps

Extending this paperThe paper reports population-averaged step changes by walkability bin, but does not spatially map the distribution of activity deficits across the 1,609 cities. An extension would apply spatiotemporal kernel density estimation (STKDE) to the relocation dataset: estimating activity-deficit surfaces across the US city system, identifying hotspot cities where low walkability generates the largest health burden, and tracking how those surfaces shift over the 2013–2016 observation window. This moves the paper's policy finding (36 million more Americans) from a national headline to a spatially targeted priority map for urban intervention.

Added value to my researchSpatiotemporal kernel density estimation is a named method in my toolkit. Applying STKDE to step-change residuals across US cities would produce the activity-deficit heat maps I could overlay with healthcare access surfaces (from my 2SFCA work) to identify double-disadvantaged areas — low walkability AND poor healthcare access — which represent the highest-priority zones for integrated urban health policy. This directly advances my health geography and urban analytics agendas simultaneously.

④ Extend the natural experiment to health-service utilization and chronic-disease outcomes

Extending this paperThe paper establishes the walkability → steps → MVPA chain but stops short of linking it to downstream health outcomes. The relocation design is ideally suited to a downstream extension: linking the same mover cohort (or a subsequent cohort using electronic health records or insurance claims) to rates of primary-care visits, hypertension diagnoses, or diabetes incidence stratified by post-relocation walkability. The paper's symmetric, placebo-controlled design structure could be transplanted directly into a health-utilization natural experiment, testing whether the 1,100-step gain translates into measurable reductions in chronic-disease burden after 1–3 years.

Added value to my researchHealth geography and healthcare accessibility are central to my research. Coupling this paper's walkability-induced activity gains to healthcare utilization would let me quantify the downstream demand reduction on primary-care and chronic-disease management services — directly informing my 2SFCA accessibility models by estimating how built-environment improvements shift the demand surface. This creates a closed-loop model: walkability up → steps up → disease burden down → primary-care demand changes → spatial accessibility implications shift.

⑤ Quantify the equity dimension: who benefits least from walkability and why

Extending this paperThe paper briefly notes that women over 50 show non-significant walkability benefits (P = 0.14) and attributes this to cultural, physiological, and transit-related barriers — but leaves the equity dimension largely qualitative. An extension would systematically model heterogeneous treatment effects by race, income, disability status, and transit access, using the same relocation design with additional administrative data linkage. This would directly test whether built-environment walkability improvements are equitable by design or whether they primarily benefit already-advantaged groups, transforming the paper's universal headline into a spatially differentiated equity analysis.

Added value to my researchSpatial equity is threaded through my urban analytics work (EV charging efficiency-vs-equity, 15-minute neighborhood access, healthcare facility spatial equity). This paper's relocation quasi-experiment provides a natural counterfactual engine for estimating who benefits from walkability improvements. Coupling heterogeneous treatment effect estimation with my MGWR/GWR framework would let me map spatially varying walkability returns across sociodemographic gradients — producing the equity-aware spatial model of built-environment effects that my research agenda requires. It would also inform whether telehealth and digital-health access (another theme of mine) can partially substitute for the activity deficits of low-walkability areas for disadvantaged groups.
