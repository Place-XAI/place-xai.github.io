Core Conclusion

MIRA (Medical Intelligence for Reasoning and Action), an autonomous AI agent operating inside a sandboxed EHR, achieved diagnostic accuracy of 87.8% across 8 emergency-department conditions — statistically outperforming board-certified physicians (78.1%, P<0.001) and a mixed-seniority cohort (71.1%, P<0.001). It navigated more than 85,000 clinical action options using 11 FHIR-compliant tools, showed higher guideline adherence (+35 percentage points vs. physicians) and procedure recall (53.5% vs. 38.3%), and maintained strong medication safety with zero high-severity drug interactions, renal-dosing errors or allergy mismatches in a blinded safety audit. These results suggest that an EHR-integrated AI agent can convert clinical intent into structured, actionable operations at physician-level performance, though prospective real-world validation remains essential.

Why It Matters

  • First full end-to-end EHR agent benchmark. Prior LLM healthcare studies addressed isolated subtasks (Q&A, coding, note extraction); MIRA is the first to demonstrate complete emergency-department workflows — from history-taking through labs, imaging, medications, and admission — inside a FHIR-compliant sandboxed environment.
  • Outperforms physicians in a controlled comparison. On 311 matched cases evaluated under identical information conditions, MIRA's diagnostic accuracy significantly exceeded two independent physician cohorts, establishing a credible performance ceiling for future clinical AI systems.
  • Quantifies safety, not just accuracy. A blinded 6-domain drug-safety audit (drug–drug interactions, renal dosing, allergies, QT risk, opioid risk, therapeutic duplication) found zero high-severity safety events — a rare systematic safety evaluation for an autonomous clinical AI agent.
  • Provides an open, reproducible benchmark. The MIRA codebase (github.com/Dyke-F/MIRA) and MIMIC-IV-derived benchmark enable community replication, iterative model improvement, and cross-system comparison.
  • Flags bias and governance gaps for prospective work. Perturbation experiments detect a marginal sex-bias effect before correction, signaling the need for intersectional fairness audits and governance frameworks before any real-world deployment.

Two cohorts evaluated 311 matched cases each: (1) 4 board-certified physicians with 7–11 years of experience; (2) 6 physicians of mixed seniority (4 residents + 2 board-certified specialists). All conditions identical to MIRA.

Core Methods

  • Two-agent architecture: a patient agent (GPT-4o; grounded in MIMIC-IV HPI; no external tools) engages in multi-turn conversation with a physician agent MIRA (GPT-4o, temperature 0.01 + o1-preview for planning tool); parallel tool calls within each agent turn; maximum 20 conversation turns enforced.
  • FHIR-compliant tool suite (11 tools): PatientHistory, PhysicalExaminationRequest, LabRequest (246 LOINC codes), UrineRequest (28 codes), MicrobiologyRequest (176 LOINC codes), RadiologyRequest (SNOMED-CT + LOINC modality/anatomy), MedicationRequest (FHIR Timing), ProcedureSearch (RAG), ProcedureRequest (ICD-9/10), Plan (o1-preview reasoning), Admission/CloseCase. Token masking prevents invalid code hallucination.
  • Retrieval-augmented procedure search: jina-embeddings-v3 embeddings over all ICD-9/10 procedure descriptions stored in a local Qdrant vector index; natural-language-to-code mapping enables zero-shot procedure requests without pre-enumeration.
  • Sandboxed EHR server: HAPI-FHIR instance in Docker; FHIR resources (Patient, Practitioner, Organization) created from MIMIC-IV data at simulation runtime; all tool calls routed through standard FHIR operations.
  • Evaluation vs. MIMIC-IV ground truth: diagnostic accuracy (LLM-as-judge + blinded physician concordance 96.5%), test-selection recall via McNemar and Wilcoxon tests, procedure recall and Tversky distance, medication reconciliation precision/recall, guideline adherence per disease (7 diseases × multiple medication categories).
  • Patient-agent robustness: inter-answer consistency testing (n=622 question-answer pairs, 99.4% consistent), diagnostic information leak auditing (n=933, zero leaks), adversarial prompt-injection resistance (11 attack patterns, 880 responses, zero leaks).
  • Perturbation (bias) experiments: 6 prompt-level biases (sex, health belief, anxiety, language) × 8 diagnoses × 10 cases = 480 paired evaluations; McNemar's exact test with Holm correction on diagnostic accuracy risk differences.
  • Statistical controls: Holm and Benjamini–Hochberg FDR corrections, Wilson and Clopper–Pearson 95% CIs, 10,000-resample patient-cluster bootstrap for precision/recall/F1.

① Extend MIRA's disposition tool with a spatial care-access sub-agent

Extending this paperMIRA's CloseCase/Admission tool issues binary admit-or-discharge decisions without any awareness of where patients live or whether an appropriate alternative care site is geographically reachable. A direct extension of the paper: equip the Admission tool with a spatial sub-agent that queries the patient's residential location, computes network travel times to the nearest suitable primary-care or specialist facility, and incorporates care-level availability into the disposition recommendation. This would let MIRA suggest "discharge with same-day follow-up at clinic X (12-minute travel)" rather than defaulting to admission — closing the loop between clinical reasoning and geographic access that the paper currently leaves open.

Added value to my researchMIRA's FHIR-structured outputs would feed directly into a real-time 2SFCA/R2SFCA computation layer: each AI-generated disposition becomes an accessibility-weighted care pathway that can be audited for geographic equity. Mobile phone GPS trajectories could validate whether the recommended facilities are actually reachable by the population served, connecting my spatiotemporal health-geography methods to the clinical AI pipeline.

② Fine-tune MIRA's medical-embedding layer on domain-specific annotation pairs

Extending this paperMIRA's procedure search relies on static jina-embeddings-v3 over ICD descriptions, while lab and imaging codes are hard-enumerated. Both approaches require manual re-engineering when code releases update or when institutions use non-standard vocabularies. A direct extension: treat the 574-case benchmark as a contrastive fine-tuning corpus — each correct tool call (e.g., LOINC code matched to a blood parameter) is a positive pair, and near-miss mismatches are hard negatives — and fine-tune a biomedical embedding model so that the retrieval layer generalizes zero-shot to new code systems and institutional extensions without re-enumeration.

Added value to my researchFine-tuning geospatial foundation models on domain annotation pairs is a core theme of my work. The same contrastive supervision pattern applies to geospatial code retrieval: mapping free-text POI descriptions or address strings to administrative boundary codes or place-taxonomy entries — a bottleneck in multi-source data fusion that mirrors the medical-coding problem at domain scale.

③ Instrument MIRA's action traces as a labeled dataset for explainable clinical AI

Extending this paperThe paper logs full action traces for all 574 cases (Fig. 3b, Extended Data Fig. 2), capturing how MIRA transitions from history-taking through labs, imaging, and medication to admission. These traces are currently used only for descriptive workflow analysis. Extending the paper: treat each trace as a labeled explanation sequence — with each correct diagnosis anchored by an ordered set of tool calls and intermediate reasoning steps — and train a post-hoc attribution model that, given any clinical state, identifies which evidence most influenced the final decision. This would let physicians audit MIRA's logic mid-encounter, directly addressing the governance and transparency gap flagged in the paper's Discussion.

Added value to my researchMIRA's multi-step tool-call sequences are structurally analogous to multi-step spatial prediction traces (e.g., which spatial predictors entered a GWR model at each location and why). Adapting SHAP-based or attention attribution from clinical action sequences to spatiotemporal reasoning chains would yield a unified explainability paradigm for both clinical and geospatial AI agents — a direct extension of my explainable ML for spatial prediction agenda.

④ Connect a patient-facing self-triage agent to MIRA's EHR backend

Extending this paperMIRA currently assumes the patient is already inside the emergency department. A natural upstream extension: connect a patient-facing self-triage agent (guided by structured clinical flowcharts as safety guardrails) to MIRA's FHIR backend, so that symptom reports collected remotely pre-populate the EHR sandbox before arrival, reducing the history-taking burden in the ED. The paper's own patient-agent architecture — grounded in HPI, zero information leaks under adversarial prompting — provides the robustness model for this front-end; adding flowchart-structured dialogue constraints would provide systematic coverage guarantees that a free-form conversational agent alone cannot offer.

Added value to my researchA self-triage + EHR-agent pipeline creates a longitudinal, geolocated health-seeking behavior dataset: timestamps of symptom onset, self-triage completion, and ED arrival, linked to the patient's home census tract. Fusing these event logs with spatial accessibility metrics (travel time to nearest ED, telehealth penetration by neighborhood) would quantify the digital and geographic barriers between symptom onset and care initiation — a novel spatiotemporal dataset for health-geography research on digital health divides.

⑤ Embed an intersectional fairness audit into MIRA's perturbation framework

Extending this paperThe paper tests 6 prompt-level bias conditions, one at a time, but does not examine interactions between demographic strata (e.g., non-native language speaker + female + low-income profile). Extending the perturbation framework: construct synthetic intersectional patient profiles crossing age, sex, language, and socioeconomic proxies, evaluate MIRA across all combinations using the existing blinded-physician pipeline, and report both diagnostic accuracy disparities and prescription-safety inequities across groups. The paper's own marginal sex-bias finding (nominally P < 0.05) makes this extension not just scientifically warranted but an ethical prerequisite before deployment.

Added value to my researchSocio-demographic bias in LLM clinical recommendations is structurally parallel to spatial health inequity: both are systematic divergences in outcomes across population strata. My MGWR and spatiotemporal kernel density methods could test whether MIRA's bias magnitude is spatially autocorrelated — i.e., whether patients from low-accessibility neighborhoods also receive lower-quality AI-generated recommendations — linking clinical AI fairness to the geographic health-disparities literature in a unified analytical framework.
