Genetics and the geography of health, behaviour and attainment

Daniel W. Belsky · Avshalom Caspi · Louise Arseneault · David L. Corcoran · Benjamin W. Domingue · Kathleen Mullan Harris · Renate M. Houts · Jonathan S. Mill · Terrie E. Moffitt · Joseph Prinz · Karen Sugden · Jasmin Wertz · Benjamin Williams · Candice L. Odgers Published 2019-04-08 DOI 10.1038/s41562-019-0562-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using polygenic scores from over 7,000 UK and US young people, this study finds that genetic selection into disadvantaged neighbourhoods is not a plausible explanation for neighbourhood gradients in obesity or mental health. However, modest genetic selection is evident for teen pregnancy and poor educational outcomes, with both genetic and neighbourhood risks inherited together from parents — meaning neighbourhood interventions remain valid but must be paired with family-directed approaches for these outcomes.

★

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a long-standing causal puzzle in neighbourhood research. By using DNA — which cannot be caused by neighbourhood conditions — the study rules out reverse causation when testing whether high-risk individuals cluster in disadvantaged places.
  • Clears the path for place-based health interventions. Finding no genetic selection for obesity and mental health gradients strengthens the case that these neighbourhood effects are genuinely causal, justifying neighbourhood-level policy investment.
  • Provides a nuanced warning for education and teen-pregnancy programmes. Modest gene–neighbourhood correlations for these outcomes mean estimated neighbourhood effects are partly confounded by selection, and programmes must also target individuals and families.
  • Demonstrates that heritability does not imply social confounding. A trait being highly heritable does not mean its neighbourhood gradient is genetically explained — a conceptual advance for both genetics and social science.
  • Extends across two large, independent national cohorts. Consistency of findings between the UK E-Risk study and the US Add Health study increases generalizability across very different institutional contexts.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

E-Risk cohort (UK)
Environmental Risk Longitudinal Study — 2,232 British twins born in England and Wales in 1994–1995, drawn from a population birth registry. Genetic data available for n = 1,999 children in 1,011 families; mothers' genetic data for n = 860. Follow-up home visits at ages 5, 7, 10, 12, and 18 (2012–2014). Data not publicly available; access on request at Duke University or King's College London.
Add Health cohort (US)
National Longitudinal Study of Adolescent to Adult Health — nationally representative probability sample of US adolescents in grades 7–12 in 1994–1995 (144 schools). Followed through 4 waves to 2007–2008. Genotype data (Illumina Omni1/Omni2.5) available for 9,975; n = 5,325 European-descent participants with both genetic and neighbourhood data used here. Access via Carolina Population Center / dbGaP.
Neighbourhood data (UK)
ACORN consumer geodemographic classification (CACI) — assigned at output-area level (~100–125 households) at ages 5, 7, 10, 12. Plus an ecological-risk index combining: (1) government geodemographic data; (2) official UK police crime records (open data); (3) Google Street View virtual systematic social observation; (4) neighbourhood resident surveys collected when children were 13–14 years old. Index covers deprivation, dilapidation, social disconnection, and danger.
Neighbourhood data (US)
US Census tract-level data: 1990 Decennial Census linked to participants' 1994–1995 addresses; 2005–2009 American Community Survey panels linked to 2007–2008 addresses. Variables: female-headed households, poverty, public assistance, low education, unemployment — combined into a deprivation decile score.
Genetic data
Illumina HumanOmniExpress 12 BeadChip (E-Risk); imputed with IMPUTE2 / 1000 Genomes Phase 3. Polygenic scores for four GWAS-derived phenotypes: body mass index, schizophrenia, age at first birth, and educational attainment. Polygenic scoring via PRSice software following Dudbridge (2013).

Core Methods

  • Polygenic score construction: SNPs from published GWAS results matched to cohort genotype databases; each SNP weighted by its GWAS effect size; scores averaged across the genome using PRSice software (Dudbridge 2013 method). Scores residualized on 10 principal components to control for population stratification; standardized to mean = 0, s.d. = 1.
  • Poisson / linear regression models: Dichotomous outcomes (obesity, teen pregnancy, poor qualifications, NEET) analysed with Poisson regression to estimate relative risks (RRs). Neighbourhood disadvantage and polygenic scores alternated as predictors and outcomes. Standard errors clustered at the family level to account for twin non-independence.
  • Neighbourhood disadvantage measurement: Two parallel indices — (1) commercial ACORN geodemographic classification, averaged across ages 5–12; (2) composite ecological-risk index (deprivation, dilapidation, disconnection, danger) built from four independent data sources including Google Street View virtual walk-throughs.
  • Maternal polygenic score analysis: Mothers' polygenic scores (n = 858) used to test whether child–neighbourhood gene–environment correlations reflect passive inheritance from parents; child scores re-analysed with mother's score as covariate.
  • Neighbourhood mobility analysis (Add Health): Adult neighbourhood disadvantage (2007–2008) regressed on polygenic score + childhood neighbourhood disadvantage + covariates to isolate upward/downward mobility component attributed to genetic risk.
  • SNP heritability (GCTA): GCTA software used in Add Health (n = 4,655 unrelated individuals; GRM < 0.05) to estimate the fraction of variance in neighbourhood disadvantage and neighbourhood mobility explained by genome-wide SNPs; power insufficient for E-Risk.
  • Power analysis: Post-hoc power analysis (Stata 'power' command) confirmed >80% power to detect associations with r = 0.1 in both cohorts.

① Map where gene–neighbourhood correlations are strongest using fine-grained spatial analysis

Extending this paperThe paper establishes that polygenic risk for educational attainment is correlated with neighbourhood disadvantage (r ≈ 0.17–0.18), but it treats this association as spatially uniform — a single national coefficient for the UK and US. A direct extension: use the paper's own data to test whether this gene–neighbourhood correlation is spatially heterogeneous, varying by city, region, or urban-rural gradient. Regions with higher residential sorting, weaker social mobility, or stronger intergenerational poverty transmission may show stronger gene–neighbourhood clustering. The corpus paper on intergenerational social mobility in Britain provides precisely the geographical variation in social mobility needed to stratify this test — areas of high deprivation persistence could show the strongest genetic concentration effects.

Added value to my researchThis motivates a geographically weighted regression (GWR/MGWR) extension of sociogenomic neighbourhood analysis — estimating spatially varying gene–neighbourhood effect sizes. My MGWR/GWR toolkit could quantify where the genetic gradient is strongest, pointing to where place-based interventions are most versus least confounded by selection — a direct input to spatial health policy prioritization.

② Integrate mobility-flow data to disentangle residential sorting from neighbourhood exposure

Extending this paperThe paper's neighbourhood mobility analysis (Fig. 4) uses residential address at two time-points — a coarse proxy for where people actually spend time and what environments they are exposed to. A key extension: replace or supplement this with GPS-derived activity spaces or mobility trajectories that capture experienced neighbourhood disadvantage rather than residential neighbourhood only. People with high polygenic risk for poor educational outcomes who live in mixed neighbourhoods but systematically travel to and interact within high-deprivation activity zones would be misclassified by the current design. The corpus provides methodology for linking income segregation to experienced mobility patterns.

Added value to my researchThis is directly relevant to my work on spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and mobile phone trajectory analysis. Replacing residential-address neighbourhood measures with experienced neighbourhood exposure derived from GPS/mobile-phone data would produce more honest gene–environment correlation estimates — and could be applied to health outcome modelling where activity-space disadvantage matters more than home location.

Cross-ref (corpus): Mobility patterns are associated with experienced income segregation in large US cities — 10.1038/s41467-021-24899-8

③ Test whether housing policy can break the intergenerational gene–neighbourhood correlation

Extending this paperThe paper ends with the recommendation that, because genetic and neighbourhood risks are co-inherited from parents, breaking this intergenerational correlation requires multi-level interventions. A powerful extension using a natural-experiment design: test whether large-scale affordable housing programmes (which randomly assign families to different neighbourhoods) reduce the gene–neighbourhood correlation in the next generation. If parents with high educational-attainment polygenic risk are no longer spatially sorted into high-disadvantage neighbourhoods due to subsidized housing assignment, the parent-to-child transmission of correlated genetic and neighbourhood risk should attenuate. The Singapore public housing study provides exactly such a quasi-experimental template — and the finding that affordable housing enhances intergenerational mobility for lower-SES families is mechanistically consistent with this paper.

Added value to my researchThis connects to my public-facility accessibility and housing-price research. If housing policy can decouple genetic risk from neighbourhood risk, the spatial planning question becomes: where should affordable housing be placed to maximally disrupt this correlation? Proximity to education and health services — measurable via R2SFCA — would be a key siting criterion, linking facility accessibility optimization directly to gene–environment de-correlation as a policy objective.

Cross-ref (corpus): Affordable public housing and intergenerational mobility — 10.1038/s44284-025-00375-y

④ Use explainable ML to identify which neighbourhood features drive the gene–environment correlation

Extending this paperThe paper aggregates neighbourhood characteristics into two composite indices (ACORN and the ecological-risk index), losing information about which specific neighbourhood features — physical, social, economic — drive gene–neighbourhood correlations. A direct extension: replace the composite index with a feature-rich neighbourhood representation including POI density, green space, transit access, crime sub-types, housing tenure, and school quality, then apply an explainable gradient-boosting model to predict gene–neighbourhood correlations across census tracts. SHAP values would reveal the neighbourhood features most responsible for concentrating high-risk individuals — transforming the paper's binary selection/non-selection finding into a feature-level spatial policy map.

Added value to my researchThis is precisely my explainable machine learning for spatial prediction agenda. Training an XGBoost or LightGBM model with SHAP decomposition on this task would identify which urban amenity or deprivation features most drive residential sorting by genetic risk — turning a sociogenomic finding into an actionable, interpretable spatial governance tool directly applicable to health geography interventions.

Cross-ref (corpus): US cities are defined by rings and pockets with limited socioeconomic mixing — 10.1038/s44284-025-00350-7

⑤ Extend the genetic neighbourhood-mobility model to assess digital and healthcare access as mediators

Extending this paperThe paper shows that individuals with high polygenic risk for poor educational outcomes exhibit downward neighbourhood mobility — moving to more disadvantaged census tracts in young adulthood. But it does not ask what opportunities or barriers in the destination neighbourhood mediate this mobility trap. A natural extension: link the neighbourhood mobility outcome with measures of healthcare access, school quality, employment proximity, and digital infrastructure in destination tracts. This would show whether high-risk individuals who land in high-access-to-services neighbourhoods escape the disadvantage spiral — isolating which specific neighbourhood resources break the mobility trap.

Added value to my researchThis directly integrates with my spatial accessibility of primary care and mental health services work using 2SFCA/R2SFCA. If individuals with high polygenic risk for poor education or teen pregnancy who move to neighbourhoods with high mental health and primary care accessibility show attenuated disadvantage trajectories, this would constitute the strongest evidence yet that improving neighbourhood service access mitigates genetically channelled neighbourhood disadvantage — a high-impact finding for my health geography research programme.
