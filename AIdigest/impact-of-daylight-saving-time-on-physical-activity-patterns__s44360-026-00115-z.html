Impact of daylight saving time on physical activity patterns

Hayoung Jeong · Srikar Katta · Will Ke Wang · Alexander Volfovsky · Jessilyn Dunn Published 2026-04-23 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00115-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Contrary to common belief, daylight saving time (DST) transitions produce no net change in total daily steps; instead, physical activity is reallocated across times of day — fall transitions shift steps from evening to morning, spring transitions do the reverse. Crucially, these effects vary substantially by demographics and chronotype-based phenotype (morning, neutral, and evening walkers), implying that structural constraints — rigid work schedules, perceived neighbourhood safety — limit how freely some populations can adapt. Resting heart rate shows subtle intraday mirroring of these behavioural shifts but remains clinically insignificant.

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a longstanding policy debate with large-scale causal evidence. The question of whether DST promotes physical activity has been debated for decades; this is among the first causal analyses using continuous, longitudinal wearables data at population scale.
  • Reveals that reallocation, not net gain, is the true mechanism. Policymakers cannot claim DST increases total activity; it merely shifts when people move — a fundamentally different policy framing.
  • Highlights structural health inequities. The heterogeneity across income, age, sex, and chronotype shows that the same time-shift policy has unequal effects — those with rigid schedules or safety-constrained neighbourhoods cannot adapt flexibly.
  • Demonstrates observational real-world evidence (RWE) methodology. The natural difference-in-differences design with Arizona as control sets a replicable template for wearable-data causal policy evaluation, relevant well beyond DST.

Core Methods

  • Natural difference-in-differences (DiD) design: Arizona (no DST) serves as the control against neighbouring Mountain Time states (Colorado, New Mexico, Utah) that observe DST — a geographic quasi-experiment avoiding common pre–post confounders.
  • Longitudinal Fitbit data processing: continuous passive sensing of step counts and resting heart rate; quality control applied following established All of Us Fitbit data protocols (Lederer et al.).
  • Time-of-day decomposition: full-day, morning, and evening step-count windows analysed separately to reveal within-day activity reallocation rather than just daily totals.
  • Data-driven phenotyping: unsupervised clustering of participants by habitual activity timing to derive three chronotype-based phenotypes (morning, neutral, evening walker); DiD estimates then stratified by phenotype.
  • Heterogeneous treatment effects: DiD regressions stratified by age, income, sex, and walkability-related built environment features; two-sided t-tests with 95% confidence intervals; effects with p ≥ 0.05 flagged.
  • Physiological outcome analysis: intraday resting heart rate DiD across equivalent morning/evening/full-day windows to assess cardiovascular mirroring of behavioural shifts.
  • No strict parametric assumptions: the DiD design is non-parametric in its identification; uncertainty expressed as 95% CIs rather than distributional assumptions.

① Spatialise the DiD: heterogeneous treatment effects across the urban–rural gradient

Extending this paperThe paper stratifies effects by demographic subgroups and walkability features but treats geography as a binary (Arizona vs. Mountain Time states). A direct extension is to embed the DiD in a geographically weighted regression (GWR/MGWR) framework: estimate a local DiD coefficient for each participant's residential location, then map how the magnitude of DST's step-reallocation effect varies continuously across urban density, land use, and transit access. This would transform the paper's aggregate heterogeneity findings into a spatially explicit surface — revealing which neighbourhoods are most vulnerable to DST's inequitable disruptions and directly extending the paper's walkability subgroup analysis with continuous spatial variation.

Added value to my researchThis is a natural application of my MGWR/GWR toolkit to a wearables-based public health outcome. A location-specific DST-effect surface estimated from All of Us data could be overlaid with my existing spatial accessibility layers (2SFCA service-area maps, park proximity, healthcare facility catchments), yielding a joint exposure-activity-access index for health policy. It also opens a path toward a spatiotemporal kernel density estimator of activity reallocation that I can apply to GPS trajectory datasets.

② Replace the binary control group with a counterfactual built-environment benchmark

Extending this paperThe paper's natural experiment elegantly uses Arizona's no-DST status, but this single geographic control cannot account for the many ways Arizona differs from Colorado/New Mexico/Utah (climate, urbanisation, demographics). Extending the paper: integrate walkability scores and built environment features — sidewalk density, transit access, green space proximity — as a synthetic control or propensity-score matched comparison, constructing a more credible counterfactual. The paper already hints at walkability heterogeneity (Extended Data Fig. 2); a full counterfactual design would turn that hint into a primary causal claim about whether built environment mediates DST's step-reallocation effect.

Added value to my researchThis directly links to my work on walkability and urban health. Treating built-environment features as mediators in a causal wearables analysis — rather than just stratifiers — would let me test whether my spatial accessibility metrics (2SFCA scores for parks, transit, care) explain why some populations realloc their steps differently than others, bridging the causal inference tradition with GeoAI-based accessibility modelling.

③ Extend the phenotype framework with geospatial clustering of activity-timing signatures

Extending this paperThe paper's three chronotype phenotypes (morning, neutral, evening walker) are derived from the temporal distribution of individual step counts, but treat phenotype membership as a fixed label. Extending it: apply a spatiotemporal clustering approach that simultaneously captures when and where participants walk — incorporating GPS or home-location data — to yield geospatially grounded phenotypes. A participant's chronotype may not be stable across all seasons or residence types; a longitudinal phenotype trajectory model would reveal whether DST shifts phenotype membership for borderline walkers or merely displaces within-phenotype timing.

Mapping where different chronotype clusters concentrate in a city — and how DST transitions perturb those spatial clusters — would generate new GeoAI features for urban health prediction models and give my agent-based models of daily mobility a circadian-rhythm layer calibrated from real wearables data.

④ Link activity reallocation to healthcare utilisation and telehealth access inequities

Extending this paperThe paper shows that DST's step-reallocation is unequal across income groups and ages, but stops at the behavioural outcome. A downstream extension: link the same All of Us cohort's wearables records to their electronic health records (EHR) and healthcare visits — also available on the Workbench — to test whether DST-induced activity reallocation in low-income or elderly participants predicts downstream health service use (e.g., cardiovascular events, primary care visits). This converts the paper's behavioural finding into a health-system impact estimate, strengthening its policy case.

⑤ Use the DiD template to evaluate physical-activity co-benefits of other environmental time policies

Extending this paperThe paper's natural DiD design — exploiting a policy that changes only the clock, leaving behaviour otherwise unconstrained — is a reusable template. An immediate extension applies the same Fitbit-DiD architecture to other environmental time-policy experiments: for example, cities that adopted extended evening park lighting, altered school start times, or changed public transit schedules. Each creates a comparable natural experiment with a geographic control group. Pooling multiple such experiments in a meta-DiD framework would yield a generalizable dose-response relationship between ambient-light/schedule changes and activity reallocation — with the paper's DST estimates as the anchor point.

Added value to my researchFrom a public health and urban analytics standpoint, a meta-DiD library of time-policy experiments would be a valuable empirical foundation for my healthy-cities work. Understanding how scheduling and lighting interventions shift activity distributions — stratified by walkability and transit access — would allow me to model the physical-activity co-benefits of urban infrastructure investments alongside my EV-charging and green-space accessibility analyses, creating a multi-outcome urban health optimisation framework.
