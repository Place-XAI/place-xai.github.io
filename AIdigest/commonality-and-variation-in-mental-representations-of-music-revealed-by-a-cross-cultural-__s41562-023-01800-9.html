Core Conclusion

Across 39 participant groups in 15 countries — spanning industrialized societies and Indigenous populations — every group showed discrete rhythm "categories" clustered at small-integer-ratio rhythms, suggesting a universal feature of human music cognition. However, the relative prominence of specific integer ratios varied substantially across groups and tracked local musical traditions, demonstrating that universal biological constraints interact with culture-specific exposure to produce the global diversity in how people represent musical rhythm.

★

Why It Matters

  • First large-scale cross-cultural test of music universals. Previous cross-cultural music cognition studies were limited to small numbers of societies; this study tests 39 groups across five continents, providing the statistical power needed to reliably distinguish universal from culture-specific features.
  • Resolves a long-standing debate about discrete representations. The paper provides direct behavioral evidence — not just corpus analysis — that discrete, integer-ratio rhythm categories are a widespread feature of human minds, not merely a Western scholarly invention or artifact of Western music training.
  • Exposes the bias of WEIRD sampling. University students and online participants from non-Western countries showed rhythm priors far more similar to US participants than to local non-student groups, demonstrating that convenience samples systematically underrepresent global cognitive diversity.
  • Links cognitive representations to cultural transmission. Discrete rhythm categories stabilize musical systems by reducing transmission errors; the finding helps explain both why music traditions are stable and why they nonetheless vary culturally.
  • Demonstrates that musical experience shapes perception more than language or ethnicity. Participants speaking the same language but embedded in different musical environments showed distinct rhythm priors, while those with similar musical exposure showed similar priors despite different languages.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Participant groups
39 groups across 15 countries spanning five continents: Bolivia, Uruguay, Mali, Botswana, Namibia, Bulgaria, Turkey, India, China, Japan, South Korea, Sweden, Brazil, United States, United Kingdom. Groups ranged from industrialized urban populations to small-scale Indigenous societies (e.g., Tsimane' of Bolivia, San of Botswana).
Participant types
Non-musicians, local traditional musicians, Western-trained musicians, dancers, university students, and online participants — enabling systematic comparisons of musical experience and cultural exposure.
Experimental paradigm
Iterated rhythm reproduction ("telephone") paradigm: participants tapped back random seed rhythms (three-interval cycles, 2,000 ms total duration); reproductions were fed back as the next stimulus for five iterations. A faster-tempo variant (1,000 ms) was also run in 13 of the 39 groups.
Data repository
Raw data for all groups, experiment instructions, analysis code, and all figure data are deposited at OSF: osf.io/6zd4v (CC-BY 4.0). Repository includes tapping sequences, demographic questionnaires, and Gaussian mixture model results.
Musical corpus
For the Sagele (Mali) group, the authors recorded a representative corpus of local musical repertoire to verify that 46% of excerpts contained a prominent 3:3:2 rhythmic pattern — validating the link between measured priors and local music. No large pre-existing database was used; all data are primary field collection.
Statistical analysis
Kernel density estimation of prior distributions; Gaussian mixture models (22 components constrained to small-integer ratios); multidimensional scaling (Jensen–Shannon divergence); bootstrapping (1,000–10,000 samples); Bonferroni correction; Wilcoxon rank-sum tests; permutation tests.

Core Methods

  • Iterated reproduction paradigm (Bayesian prior estimation): seed rhythms converge to stationary distributions after five tapping iterations; the final distribution is treated as an estimate of the listener's Bayesian prior over rhythms. Independent of any prior hypothesis about rhythm structure.
  • Kernel density estimation (KDE) in triangular rhythm space: two-dimensional KDE in the simplex of three-interval rhythms (each axis = one interval, total = 2,000 ms), visualized as a heatmap over the triangle.
  • Gaussian mixture model (GMM) fitting: 22 components constrained to small-integer-ratio points; component weights quantify the strength of individual rhythm categories (e.g., 1:1:1, 1:1:2, 3:3:2, 2:2:3, 7:2:3) for each group.
  • Multidimensional scaling (MDS): Jensen–Shannon divergence between all pairs of estimated priors; two-dimensional MDS reveals dominant axes of cross-cultural variation; the first axis (85.9% variance) is strongly correlated with 3:3:2 weight.
  • Bayesian categorization simulation: GMM-based category boundaries compared to published Western musician perceptual judgements (Desain & Honing), testing whether culture-specific priors predict culture-specific categorical perception.
  • Bootstrap inference and permutation tests: 1,000–10,000 bootstrap samples for standard errors; Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons; one-sided Wilcoxon rank-sum tests for group contrasts.
  • Cross-group comparisons (student/online vs. non-student): Jensen–Shannon divergence between pairs of student groups compared to matched non-student pairs; Cohen's d effect sizes reported throughout.

① Map the geography of rhythm cognition using spatial analysis methods

Extending this paperThe paper positions 39 groups in two-dimensional multidimensional scaling space but does not embed these positions in geographic space. A direct extension: treat each group's GMM weight vector as a multivariate spatial observation, apply geographically weighted regression (GWR/MGWR) to model the spatial non-stationarity of rhythm priors as a function of covariates (globalization index, population density, urbanicity, language family, trade routes), and produce continuous global maps of predicted rhythm category prevalence. The paper already notes that the US and UK groups cluster near the centre — possibly because of Western music's global influence — but never formalizes this as a spatially explicit model.

Added value to my researchThe corpus of 39 group observations is a perfect test case for MGWR, one of my core methods. Cognitive geography (spatial variation in mental representations) is an emerging frontier; applying MGWR to cross-cultural cognition data would demonstrate that spatial methods from health geography transfer directly to cognitive science. It would also allow me to derive spatially varying coefficients for cultural exposure predictors, analogous to how I model healthcare accessibility across density gradients.

② Use music streaming data to operationalize "musical exposure" at scale

Extending this paperThe paper infers musical exposure indirectly from participant self-reports and from ethnomusicological knowledge of local traditions. A critical extension: link the paper's per-group GMM weights to objective, large-scale music consumption data (e.g., streaming platform listening histories, as used in the corpus paper on cultural diversity in urban populations). For example, the 3:3:2 weight of a group could be regressed on the fraction of Afro-diasporic or sub-Saharan African listening in that country's Spotify/Apple Music charts. This would replace anecdotal exposure characterization with a quantitative, reproducible measure, and could be run at the city or sub-national level to test whether rhythm priors track fine-grained spatial variation in music consumption.

③ Cross-cultural urban visual perception vs. auditory rhythm perception — a comparative framework

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that rhythm perception is both universal and culturally variable — but restricts itself to auditory stimuli. A comparative study using the same iterated-reproduction logic on visual stimuli (streetscape aesthetics, geometric patterns, urban form) would test whether the universality-plus-variation architecture is modality-general. Are there "integer-ratio analogues" in visual perception of urban scenes? The corpus paper on global urban visual perception shows that preferences for urban streetscapes already vary across demographics and cultures in a structured way. Cross-referencing its factor structure with the rhythm prior's MDS space would reveal whether visual and auditory cultural cognition co-vary geographically.

④ Treat the iterated reproduction paradigm as a cultural transmission model for geospatial signals

Extending this paperThe paper's "telephone" iterated reproduction paradigm is explicitly a model of cultural transmission: each reproduction passes through a Bayesian prior, stabilizing signals at discrete attractors. This is formally identical to how spatial knowledge is transmitted — mental maps, neighborhood reputation, place-based stereotypes. Extending the paradigm to spatial stimuli (e.g., participants reproduce a drawn route or a neighborhood boundary sketch) would test whether mental representations of space also converge to discrete categories, and whether these categories correspond to topological or metric regularities. The paper's finding that musicianship (exposure via active practice) matters less than passive listening has an analogue: place familiarity may shape spatial representations more than formal cartographic training.

Added value to my researchMy agent-based modeling work studies how individuals make spatiotemporal decisions (job-housing, healthcare access). The iterated reproduction framework offers a computationally tractable Bayesian model of how spatial priors form and persist in a population — a complement to agent-based simulation that could parametrize prior distributions over travel behavior or service-area mental models. It also opens a route to studying how health information diffuses through social networks in a way that reflects systematic cognitive biases.

⑤ Quantify WEIRD sampling bias in spatial data science using the paper's divergence framework

Extending this paperOne of the paper's most impactful findings is that student and online participants systematically underrepresent cultural diversity — quantified as significantly smaller Jensen–Shannon divergence between student-group pairs than between matched non-student pairs. This exact framework (inter-group JSD as a measure of diversity representation) can be applied to any crowdsourced spatial dataset. For example, OpenStreetMap contributors, Google Places reviewers, or ride-hailing users are convenience samples analogous to student/online participants; their spatial coverage, POI tagging patterns, or mobility traces likely underrepresent Indigenous, rural, and low-income communities in the same way the paper shows rhythm data underrepresent cognitive diversity. The paper's JSD-based diversity audit is immediately transferable as a data-quality and representativeness metric for geospatial datasets.

Added value to my researchMy open science and FAIR data work requires tools to audit the representativeness of multi-source datasets (mobile phone, social media, OSM). Adopting the paper's JSD diversity framework to measure how well my data sources cover the spatial distribution of populations — across urban, peri-urban, and rural strata — would give me a principled, peer-reviewed metric for reporting data quality. This is also directly relevant to my research on digital divides in telehealth: a group-divergence analysis of telehealth user demographics versus population demographics would expose the same WEIRD-sampling problem in healthcare data.
