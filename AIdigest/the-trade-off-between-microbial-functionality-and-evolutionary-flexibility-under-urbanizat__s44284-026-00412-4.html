The trade-off between microbial functionality and evolutionary flexibility under urbanization

Shu-Yi-Dan Zhou · Chaotang Lei · Xu Li · Han Sheng · Qi Zhang · David T. Tissue · Josep Peñuelas · Juxiu Liu Published 2026-03-11 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00412-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 54 urban-park and forest sites in the rapidly urbanizing Pearl River Delta, urban parks host higher microbial alpha diversity, biomass, and nutrient-cycling functional genes than natural forests — differences driven mainly by nutrient enrichment and soil pH. But genomic analysis exposes a trade-off: urban parks boost short-term functionality while shrinking genome size and reducing evolutionary flexibility, pushing communities toward functional specialization. Forest soils instead retain higher genomic diversity and long-term evolvability/resilience. Predicting ecosystem-service sustainability under urbanization therefore requires integrating ecological and genomic evidence.

  • Links city design to deep-time ecosystem resilience. By showing urbanization favors functional specialists over evolvable generalists, the work connects everyday green-space management to the long-run capacity of soils to absorb future environmental change.

Core Methods

  • Paired field survey along an urbanization gradient: compare urban park forests (UPs) vs natural forests (NFs) across 54 sites, measuring soil physicochemistry, microbial biomass, and alpha diversity (bacteria + archaea).
  • Functional-gene profiling: quantify nutrient-cycling functional genes from metagenomes and aggregate into a microbial multifunctionality (MMF) metric (Fig. 3).
  • Driver attribution: relate functional/diversity differences to environmental factors, isolating nutrient enrichment and soil pH as primary drivers.
  • Genome-resolved metagenomics: assemble and bin 208 MAGs, then compare genome size and gene-content specialization between UPs and NFs (Fig. 4).
  • Population-genomic / evolvability analysis: quantify genomic genetic variation (nucleotide diversity, selection signatures) to contrast short-term functionality against long-term evolutionary flexibility (Fig. 5).

① Map the functionality–evolvability trade-off as a continuous urban surface

Extending this paperThe paper contrasts two discrete categories (urban parks vs natural forests) at 54 points. A direct extension: treat urbanization as a continuous gradient and regress MMF and genome-size/evolvability against fine-grained urban-intensity covariates (impervious surface, population density, NDVI, distance-to-core), then interpolate the trade-off as a city-wide raster. This converts a binary contrast into a spatially explicit map of where short-term function is being bought at the cost of long-term adaptive capacity.
