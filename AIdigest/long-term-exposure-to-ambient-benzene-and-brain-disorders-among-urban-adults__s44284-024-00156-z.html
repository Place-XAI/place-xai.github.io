Long-term exposure to ambient benzene and brain disorders among urban adults

Yongxuan Li · Yujia Bao · Ne Qiang · Min Zhong · Zheshen Han · Yuanyuan Li · Yanqiu Zhou · Jingqi Zhou · Xiaobei Deng · Chen Shen · Lefei Han · Jinjun Ran Published 2024-11-15 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00156-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

In a large UK cohort of urban adults, long-term exposure to ambient benzene — even at low levels (<5 µg m⁻³) — is associated with elevated risk of brain disorders. Each interquartile-range increment of 0.30 µg m⁻³ in benzene was linked to higher risk of dementia (HR 1.18), major depression (HR 1.09) and anxiety disorder (HR 1.16). Neuroimaging analysis tied exposure to specific brain structures — the thalamus and the superior temporal gyrus — providing population-level evidence that a common urban carcinogen also harms the brain.

★

Why It Matters

  • Extends a known carcinogen's harm to the brain. Benzene is regulated chiefly as a cancer risk; this is population-scale evidence that ambient benzene is also associated with neurodegenerative and psychiatric outcomes, broadening the public-health stakes of a ubiquitous urban pollutant.
  • Effects appear below current "safe" levels. Associations are detected even under 5 µg m⁻³, suggesting that air-quality and health guidelines for benzene may need revisiting in light of brain-health endpoints.
  • Connects exposure to mechanism. Brain-imaging phenotypes (thalamus, superior temporal gyrus) plus stratification by peripheral inflammation and metabolic syndrome offer a plausible biological pathway, not just a statistical association.
  • Squarely an urbanization problem. Cities concentrate the combustion (transit, industry, heating) that generates benzene, so the burden grows precisely where populations are densest — relevant to sustainable-development and urban-planning decisions worldwide.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Study population
UK Biobank urban residents enrolled 2006–2010 and followed up to 2022. 261,909 participants for the brain-disorder (incidence) analysis; 23,911 participants with brain-imaging phenotypes. Conducted under UK Biobank application no. 99001.

Ambient benzene concentration before 2010, provided by the UK Department for Environment, Food & Rural Affairs (DEFRA) via the Pollution Climate Mapping (PCM) modeled air-quality product (UK-AIR). Exposure expressed per interquartile-range increment of 0.30 µg m⁻³; analyses focus on the low-level range (<5 µg m⁻³).
Outcomes
Incident brain disorders — dementia, major depression, anxiety disorder (and related conditions) — ascertained from UK Biobank health-record linkage; brain structural/imaging phenotypes (e.g., thalamus, superior temporal gyrus volumes).

Core Methods

  • Exposure assignment: link DEFRA/PCM modeled ambient benzene concentrations to each participant's residential location, summarized per IQR increment (0.30 µg m⁻³).
  • Survival modeling: prospective cohort analysis with Cox proportional-hazards-style models to estimate hazard ratios for incident dementia, major depression and anxiety disorder.
  • Exposure–response analysis: characterize the shape of the benzene–risk relationship (Fig. 4), including the low-concentration range.
  • Effect-modification / stratification: stratify associations by peripheral inflammation and metabolic syndrome (metS) status (Fig. 3) to probe susceptibility.
  • Neuroimaging analysis: relate benzene exposure to brain structural phenotypes (thalamus, superior temporal gyrus), then link those phenotypes to disorders.
  • Structural equation modeling (SEM): integrate exposure → brain structure → disorder pathways into a mediation-style model (Fig. 6).

① Replace the national PCM surface with a GeoAI fine-scale benzene exposure model

Extending this paperThe exposure side rests on a single DEFRA/PCM national modeled surface assigned to residence, which smooths over street-canyon and traffic micro-gradients that matter most in dense cities. A direct extension: build an explainable machine-learning land-use-regression model fusing OpenStreetMap road/traffic features, remote-sensing NO₂/aerosol proxies and mobile-phone activity to produce high-resolution benzene fields, then re-estimate the hazard ratios — testing whether finer exposure sharpens or attenuates the dementia/depression signals.

② Convert residential exposure into mobility-weighted personal exposure

Extending this paperThe paper assigns benzene at the home address, ignoring where people actually spend their day — commuting through high-traffic corridors can dominate real intake. Extending it with GPS-trajectory / mobile-phone activity spaces would yield mobility-weighted personal exposure, letting the authors test whether dynamic exposure strengthens the brain-disorder associations relative to static home estimates.

Added value to my researchThis connects directly to my spatiotemporal analysis with GPS and mobile-phone records: activity-space exposure modeling and spatiotemporal kernel density estimation become the engine for a more honest "where harm accrues" map, useful across my urban-health work.
