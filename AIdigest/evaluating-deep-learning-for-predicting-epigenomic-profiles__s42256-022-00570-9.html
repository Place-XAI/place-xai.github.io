Evaluating deep learning for predicting epigenomic profiles

Shushan Toneyan · Ziqi Tang · Peter K. Koo Published 2022-12-05 DOI 10.1038/s42256-022-00570-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A unified evaluation framework called GOPHER is introduced to fairly compare binary and quantitative deep learning models trained on chromatin accessibility (ATAC-seq) data across 15 human cell lines. The study finds that quantitative (regression) models consistently outperform binary (classification) models in generalization and out-of-distribution variant effect prediction. A novel robustness metric based on translational shifts improves model selection and can rescue underperforming models, and quantitative models yield more interpretable and biologically consistent representations of transcription factor motifs and their cooperative interactions.

★

Why It Matters

  • Closes a critical evaluation gap. As deep learning models for regulatory genomics multiply rapidly, the lack of a unified evaluation standard makes it impossible to determine what actually drives performance improvements — GOPHER provides this missing infrastructure.
  • Settles a key modelling debate. The classification vs. regression framing of epigenomic prediction has persisted without direct comparison; this paper builds a conversion bridge and demonstrates quantitative models are broadly superior across the whole chromosome, not just peak-centred regions.
  • Robustness as a new evaluation axis. The translational-shift robustness metric reveals that prediction performance and model trustworthiness are not the same thing, offering a practical selection criterion that improves variant effect predictions in 50 out of 56 tested models without any retraining.
  • Biological interpretability is measurable. Quantitative models learn cooperative transcription factor binding interactions (AP-1 cooperativity) that binary models fail to capture, directly linking modelling choices to biological discovery capacity.
  • Downstream clinical relevance. Better variant effect prediction on CAGI5 saturation mutagenesis data directly impacts the ability to prioritize disease-associated non-coding variants, connecting model evaluation to human health.

Core Methods

  • GOPHER evaluation framework: Python/TensorFlow-Keras package supporting data processing, augmentation strategies (random reverse complement, random shift), and comprehensive evaluation for binary and quantitative models of epigenomic profiles.
  • Binary vs. quantitative prediction task framing and conversion: binary classification uses IDR peak caller labels; quantitative regression predicts log-fold-normalized bigWig coverage values. A prediction format conversion bridge (logits as pseudo-coverage; coverage vs. binary labels via AUROC/AUPR) enables cross-task comparison without retraining.
  • Multi-resolution target evaluation: systematic down-sampling of higher-resolution predictions to lower-resolution bin sizes isolates resolution-induced Pearson's r inflation, enabling fair comparison across models trained at different target resolutions (base to 1 kb bins).
  • Loss function comparison: five loss functions (Poisson NLL, MSE, Pearson's r, and combinations) evaluated across eight resolution levels; Poisson NLL consistently superior.
  • Translational-shift robustness metric: N=20 randomly shifted 2,048 bp subsequences generated from each 3,072 bp sequence; predictions aligned in overlapping region; per-position standard deviation normalized by mean coverage = variation score (lower = more robust).
  • Variant effect prediction with robust averaging: 20 randomly shifted sequences generated separately for reference and alternative alleles; log ratio of averaged summed coverage values as effect size; evaluated against CAGI5 saturation mutagenesis measurements.
  • Model interpretability: first-layer filter visualization via activation-based alignments and Tomtom motif matching; global importance analysis (GIA) via dinucleotide-shuffled background sequences with embedded patterns; occlusion-based interventional experiments on AP-1 motif instances.
  • Architecture benchmarking: Basenji (dilated residual CNN), BPNet (dilated CNN with task-specific heads), baseline CNN, ResidualBind-base, and ResidualBind-32, with both ReLU and exponential first-layer activations; hyperparameter search via WandB tracking.

① Port GOPHER's robustness metric to spatiotemporal prediction models

Extending this paperGOPHER's translational-shift robustness test is a domain-agnostic concept: perturb the input slightly, measure prediction variance, and use this as a model selection criterion independent of accuracy. The paper demonstrates this for DNA sequences; a direct extension would formalise how different augmentation choices (reverse complement alone vs. combined with random shifts) interact with model architecture, and develop an automated robustness-guided training curriculum so models are explicitly trained to be robust rather than relying on post-hoc selection. The paper's finding that augmentations sufficient for one training set may be redundant for another (coverage-threshold data provides natural diversity) motivates adaptive augmentation strategies.

Added value to my researchMy explainable ML models for spatial prediction (disease-risk mapping, EV charging demand) currently use only accuracy metrics for model selection. Applying GOPHER's robustness logic — perturbing input spatial windows and measuring prediction variance across neighbouring regions — would yield a spatial robustness score identifying models that overfit local spatial autocorrelation. Models sensitive to small geographic perturbations may be learning spatial noise rather than true structure; this metric, borrowed from genomics, would identify them before deployment in health geography or urban analytics contexts.

② Extend GOPHER's task-conversion bridge to multi-threshold spatial accessibility models

Extending this paperThe paper's most technically elegant contribution is the prediction format conversion strategy: a way to compare binary and quantitative models using a common metric space without retraining. The conversion is currently ad hoc (logits used as coverage proxies without theoretical grounding). A rigorous extension would formalise the conversion with calibration correction — ensuring that binary logit distributions are properly scaled to match quantitative coverage distributions — and extend the framework to probabilistic models and multi-label outputs. This would transform GOPHER's current pragmatic bridge into a principled task-interoperability standard for any field that debates classification vs. regression framing.

Added value to my researchMy 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility models face an analogous binary-vs-continuous debate: healthcare accessibility is either classified (accessible/inaccessible given a threshold travel time) or quantified (a continuous score). The paper's conversion bridge inspires a unified spatial accessibility comparison framework where binary-threshold access models (within/outside a 15-minute isochrone) and continuous-score models (R2SFCA) are evaluated in the same metric space. This would clarify when spatial access thresholds discard actionable heterogeneity — a direct policy implication for health equity research and facility siting decisions.

③ Apply GIA-style global importance analysis to spatial feature interactions in GeoAI models

Extending this paperGlobal Importance Analysis (GIA) is the paper's primary interpretability tool: embed hypothesis patterns in randomised background sequences, measure prediction change, and reveal model-learned feature interactions independently of specific input context. This is a marginalization-based explanation method that is more powerful than gradient-based attribution because it directly tests causal hypotheses. A direct extension of this paper would scale GIA to multi-task settings: currently applied per cell line, it could be run across all 15 simultaneously to identify which motif cooperativities are universal vs. cell-type-specific, directly expanding the AP-1 cooperativity finding into a cell-type atlas of regulatory grammar.

Added value to my researchMy spatial ML models use SHAP/LIME for local feature attribution, but these are sensitive to correlated spatial features (POI density and income are spatially co-located). GIA's marginalization logic translates directly to a spatial GIA: replace a specific spatial feature type with background noise across many units, measure mean prediction change across the dataset. This would reveal whether co-presence of a primary care clinic and a transit stop produces a cooperativity bonus for health accessibility that additive SHAP decomposition misses — directly improving explainable GeoAI for health policy and facility-location optimization.

④ Leverage the paper's training-set-selection findings for spatial training data curation

Extending this paperOne of the paper's most practically important findings is that training set composition dominates generalization: models trained only on high-activity genomic peaks generalize poorly genome-wide, while models including low-activity coverage-threshold regions generalize far better. The paper notes a trade-off: peak-centred models scale peak heights better but produce more false positives genome-wide. An extension would resolve this trade-off through curriculum learning — beginning with coverage-threshold data to learn global structure, then fine-tuning on peak-centred data to sharpen peak-region predictions — combining the best of both regimes rather than choosing one.

Added value to my researchIn urban/health GeoAI, training data are almost always dominated by data-rich urban cores — the spatial analogue of peak-centred data. My spatial prediction models (disease burden, EV demand, housing prices) trained on dense-city data notoriously generalize poorly to peri-urban or rural areas, exactly paralleling the paper's peak-centred generalization failure. The paper's finding motivates a coverage-threshold sampling strategy for spatial training data: deliberately including low-signal, low-density spatial units to improve whole-geography generalization. This is a principled, experimentally validated solution to spatial extrapolation failure in GeoAI that I can implement directly by analogy.

⑤ Use GOPHER's multi-resolution framework to design scale-adaptive geospatial foundation models

Extending this paperThe paper's resolution analysis reveals a subtle and important artefact: Pearson's r increases mechanically as bin size grows because binning smooths high-frequency noise, making coarser predictions appear better than they are. The paper's remedy — evaluate higher-resolution models at lower resolution by down-sampling — is a rigorous resolution-calibration principle. An extension would pair this evaluation framework with transformer architectures (explicitly noted in Discussion as future work), testing whether transformers genuinely capture long-range regulatory interactions or merely benefit from implicit smoothing effects that the paper's down-sampling evaluation would reveal.

Added value to my researchGeospatial foundation models face an identical multi-resolution evaluation problem: models trained at 100 m resolution appear to outperform those trained at 1 km resolution because spatial aggregation reduces variance, not because of genuine signal learning. The paper's down-sampling calibration strategy gives me a principled framework for scale-adaptive evaluation of geospatial foundation models: evaluate higher-resolution spatial outputs at the lower-resolution target to isolate resolution artefacts from genuine information gain. This is directly applicable when comparing models trained at different spatial resolutions for urban accessibility, health geography, or land use prediction, enabling honest comparison across spatial scales.
