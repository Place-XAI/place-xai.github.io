Impact of socioeconomic factors on soil-borne animal pathogenic fungi in urban greenspaces

Shuhong Luo · Jigang Han · Ruirui Chen · Manuel Delgado-Baquerizo · Weiwei Zhang · Youzhi Feng Published 2024-05-13 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00073-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across a global survey of 56 cities and a high-resolution within-Shanghai survey, socioeconomic factors — not just climate and soil — are crucial predictors of the community composition and diversity of soil-borne animal (and human) pathogenic fungi in urban greenspaces. Trichosporon emerges as a universal indicator of population density at both scales, while GDP per capita and hospital-bed density help constrain its abundance locally. The implication: poorer and more densely populated cities are expected to harbor larger proportions of soil-borne pathogens, a previously hidden dimension of urban health inequity.

Why It Matters

  • Names a human-development driver of the urban pathogen microbiome. Animal pathogenic fungi (including human pathogens) cause millions of deaths annually; this is among the first studies to show that socioeconomic development — not only climate/soil — shapes their distribution from local to global scales.
  • Reveals a hidden health-equity gradient. The finding that poorer, more crowded cities harbor more soil-borne pathogens turns greenspace soil into an environmental-justice variable, linking economic status to invisible biological exposure.
  • Delivers a transferable indicator. Trichosporon tracks population density at both the global and local (Shanghai) scale, offering a single, monitorable taxon as a proxy for human-pressure on the soil pathobiome.
  • Connects two scales in one design. Pairing a 56-city global comparison with a fine-grained single-city survey lets the same socioeconomic signal be validated across nested resolutions — a template for cross-scale urban-ecology evidence.

Core Methods

  • Nested two-scale design: a global 56-city comparison paired with a high-resolution single-city (Shanghai) survey, so the same socioeconomic hypothesis is tested across resolutions.
  • Amplicon community profiling: soil fungal communities characterized by ITS sequencing, denoised with DADA2, and assigned to animal/human pathogen guilds with FUNGuild.
  • Predictor attribution: relate community composition and diversity of pathogens to socioeconomic predictors (population density, GDP per capita, hospital beds) while statistically controlling for climate and soil properties.
  • Indicator-species analysis: identify taxa (notably Trichosporon) that consistently track population density across scales.
  • Local-scale modeling: quantify how GDP per capita and hospital-bed density constrain Trichosporon abundance within Shanghai's greenspaces.
