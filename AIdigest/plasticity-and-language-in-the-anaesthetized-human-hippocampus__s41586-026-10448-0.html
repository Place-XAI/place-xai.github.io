Plasticity and language in the anaesthetized human hippocampus

Kalman A. Katlowitz · Eric R. Cole · Elizabeth A. Mickiewicz · Shraddha Shah · Melissa Franch · Joshua A. Adkinson · James L. Belanger · Raissa K. Mathura · Domokos Meszéna · Matthew McGinley · William Muñoz · Garrett P. Banks · Sydney S. Cash · Chih-Wei Hsu · Angelique C. Paulk · Nicole R. Provenza · Andrew J. Watrous · Ziv Williams · Alica M. Core Conclusion

The human hippocampus continues to perform complex sensory processing under general anaesthesia: unconscious patients showed oddball tone discrimination, representational plasticity growing over ~10 minutes, and robust encoding of the semantic and grammatical features of natural speech — including predictive coding of upcoming words — at the level of single neurons and local field potentials. These results demonstrate that high-order cognition in the hippocampus does not require conscious awareness, and that learning-like plasticity and language comprehension are properties that can emerge from local hippocampal circuitry alone.

★

Why It Matters

  • Overturns a foundational assumption. Leading theories of consciousness hold that semantic processing and predictive coding require conscious access; this work directly contradicts that by showing both in deeply anaesthetized patients.
  • First intraoperative Neuropixels language study. High-density Neuropixels recordings (384 channels, 651 isolated units across 7 patients) during open-brain surgery are technically unprecedented, enabling single-unit resolution during an otherwise inaccessible state.
  • Mechanistic account via RNN. A biologically constrained excitatory–inhibitory recurrent neural network reproduces the plasticity trajectory without any explicit oddball supervision — demonstrating that local computation, not global conscious broadcasting, suffices.
  • Clinical implications for implicit memory. Explains post-anaesthesia implicit recall and opens a window on monitoring cognitive load and possibly delivering information therapeutically during surgery.
  • Benchmarks anaesthetized vs. awake processing. Parallel awake-patient data (10 patients, EMU) shows anaesthetized hippocampal language encoding is comparable to or stronger than waking, a striking quantitative equivalence.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Participants
7 patients undergoing anteromesial temporal lobectomy for drug-resistant temporal lobe epilepsy (age 25–54, mean 39.6 ± 11.8 years; 3 female, 4 male; 4 left-sided, 3 right-sided resections). All under propofol total intravenous anaesthesia with BIS monitor values 45–60.
Neural recordings
Neuropixels 1.0-S probes (IMEC) — 384 recording channels (960 total contacts), 70 µm width, 10 mm length. AP band: 0.3–10 kHz at 30 kHz; LFP band: 0.5–500 Hz at 2,500 Hz. Total: 651 isolated single/multi-units across 7 patients (average firing rate 1.8 ± 1.1 Hz).
Auditory oddball task
3 patients (p4, p5, p6) — pure tones (200 Hz / 5 kHz or 1 kHz / 3 kHz), 100 ms duration, random SOA 1–3 s, 80/20 probability distribution with midpoint swap; 300 trials (p4), counterbalanced 150+washout+150 for p5 and p6. 172 units (oddball task).
Language task
4 patients (p6, p8, p9, p11) — 10–20 min podcast episodes (The Moth Radio Hour; Kurzgesagt). 3,024 words (p6), 1,565 words each (p8, p9), 962 words (p11). 375 single units analysed for language. Transcribed with AssemblyAI, corrected in Praat.
Awake comparison cohort
10 awake patients from the epilepsy monitoring unit (EMU), 356 single units — microwire electrode recordings; same podcast stimuli. Used for direct benchmark of semantic encoding strength.
Neural data repository
Core data deposited in DABI (Data Archive and Biology Interface; dabi.loni.usc.edu/dsi/U01NS108923) — accessible to anyone with a university affiliation.
Code

NLP / embedding tools

Core Methods

  • Intraoperative Neuropixels recording: ROSA robotic arm insertion, AlphaOmega microdrive, real-time motion correction via DREDge; automated spike sorting with Kilosort 2.0/4.0, manual curation in Phy; unit quality metrics from SpikeInterface (d′ > 1, < 3% ISI violations).
  • Auditory oddball analysis: GLME and LME models of tone- and oddball-evoked firing rates; tenfold cross-validated SVM decoding of tone and oddball identity across single units and LFP frequency bands (delta through high-gamma); phase-amplitude coupling (theta–gamma) analysis.
  • Plasticity tracking: sliding-window SVM decoding (50-trial windows) over ~10 min; Euclidean distance and cosine angle between standard and oddball population vectors across oddball index as a measure of representational divergence.
  • E-I recurrent neural network model: 200-unit continuous-rate RNN (80% excitatory / 20% inhibitory), trained via BPTT on a 3-stage oddball paradigm; systematic lesioning of each recurrent connection subtype to isolate the role of inhibition.
  • Language encoding: word-frequency correlation (Spearman); linear model from semantic embeddings (fastText Word2Vec, PCA to 30% variance) predicting unit firing rates — tenfold cross-validated; SVM decoding of 12 semantic and 15 POS categories (radial-basis kernel, weighted for class imbalance).
  • Predictive coding analysis: lagged linear regression over ±5 words to recover encoding of past and upcoming words; piecewise exponential fit to characterize the temporal decay of forward/backward prediction; GPT-2-based surprisal correlated with firing rates to assess contextual prediction.
  • Cell-type classification: CellExplorer for pyramidal vs. narrow/wide interneuron classification based on waveform shape and autocorrelogram features.

① Replace the paper's E-I RNN with a foundation model of neural activity to generalize across patients and brain states

Extending this paperThe paper's biologically plausible E-I RNN is bespoke — trained per recording session, not transferable across patients or brain states. A direct extension: replace or augment it with a foundation model of neural activity pre-trained across many individuals and brain regions, then fine-tuned on the paper's 7-patient hippocampal corpus. The paper's oddball-learning trajectory and semantic encoding vectors would serve as a uniquely constrained benchmark: the foundation model should reproduce the unconscious plasticity gradient without being explicitly told the patient is anaesthetized. This tests whether latent brain-state embeddings generalize across consciousness levels — a hypothesis the paper raises but cannot answer with a bespoke model.

Added value to my researchThe same paradigm maps onto my interest in fine-tuning geospatial foundation models: just as a neural foundation model must adapt to novel brain states, a geospatial foundation model must adapt to novel spatial contexts (new cities, healthcare settings). The cross-domain analogy informs transfer-learning strategies — particularly how much fine-tuning data is needed when the target domain is structurally different from pre-training (unconscious vs. awake; new city vs. training cities).

② Leverage the paper's aperiodic slope findings to build a consciousness-state classifier from LFP

Extending this paperThe paper notes that the aperiodic (1/f) slope of the LFP outperforms individual spectral bands in predicting semantic embeddings, echoing a broader literature on aperiodic exponents as markers of excitation–inhibition balance. A direct extension of this paper: train a patient-level state classifier that maps LFP aperiodic slope and spectral features to a consciousness depth score, validated against BIS monitor readings across the 7 patients. The paper's uniquely staged anaesthesia protocol — with explicit BIS 45–60 control — provides a rare ground-truth label for such a classifier. Extending to the awake EMU cohort (10 patients) could yield a continuous consciousness continuum rather than a binary awake/under.

Added value to my researchThe classifier architecture (LFP features → state score) parallels my use of spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and feature-importance explainability for health-state inference. Specifically, it maps onto how I derive spatial accessibility deprivation indices from multi-source signals: the key insight shared across domains is that a single robust scalar (aperiodic exponent; spatial accessibility score) can proxy complex system state. Explainability methods (SHAP, LIME) I apply to spatial prediction could be applied to identify which LFP frequency bands most reliably track the anaesthesia continuum.

③ Use the paper's cognitive-strategy-like oddball learning as a test bed for discovering hippocampal learning rules with tiny RNNs

Extending this paperThe paper shows that the hippocampus, even unconscious, progressively reshapes its representational manifold in a manner that resembles reinforcement-based statistical learning. Yet the E-I RNN explanation is a forward-engineering model — it was designed to discriminate tones, and oddball learning emerged accidentally. An extension: apply a reverse-engineering approach with minimal, interpretable RNNs to infer the simplest circuit that reproduces the full plasticity trajectory (Euclidean distance curve, cosine angle divergence, tone-vs.-oddball trade-off) observed across the paper's two-patient oddball corpus. This would move from "our model can do this" to "what is the minimal circuit that must underlie this?"

Added value to my researchThe reverse-engineering paradigm aligns with my agent-based modelling approach to spatial systems: just as I ask what minimal agent rules reproduce observed urban mobility patterns, the question here is what minimal neural rules reproduce anaesthetic-state plasticity. Both problems share the structure of minimizing model complexity while fitting emergent macro-patterns — a common inverse problem across GeoAI and neuroscience.

Cross-ref (corpus): Discovering cognitive strategies with tiny recurrent neural networks — 10.1038/s41586-025-09142-4

④ Extend the paper's word-by-word decoding to a spatiotemporal graph of inter-regional language flow

Extending this paperThe paper establishes that the hippocampus in isolation encodes semantic, syntactic, and predictive speech features. But the hippocampus does not process language in a vacuum — it receives preprocessed input from temporal and frontal cortices. Extending the paper to a multi-region simultaneously recorded setting (combining these intraoperative hippocampal recordings with simultaneous cortical ECoG from the lateral temporal cortex resected just before hippocampal recordings) would allow construction of a directed inter-areal language processing graph, tracking how semantic features propagate from cortex to hippocampus. This would test whether the hippocampus's semantic encoding under anaesthesia is driven by cortical input or generated locally.

Added value to my researchThis inter-region graph approach maps directly onto my physics-informed graph learning work: characterizing directed information flow between brain regions is structurally identical to characterizing directed accessibility or mobility flow between spatial zones. The methodological toolkit — graph neural networks with physics-informed constraints on flow conservation — could be applied to both settings, enriching both research threads with a cross-domain validation framework.

Cross-ref (corpus): Inter-brain neural dynamics in biological and artificial intelligence systems — 10.1038/s41586-025-09196-4

⑤ Map the paper's neural plasticity–loss-of-tone trade-off onto continual learning dynamics in artificial networks

Extending this paperThe paper documents a striking trade-off: as oddball encoding improves over ~10 minutes, tone identity encoding decreases. This is a biological instance of a forgetting–adaptation balance in a non-stationary environment — exactly the problem studied as "loss of plasticity" in deep continual learning. Extending the paper: treat the hippocampal E-I RNN as a continual learner and measure whether the weight-update rule during the three-stage training (80/20 → 50/50 → 20/80) exhibits the same plasticity-loss signatures as deep networks trained on sequential tasks. The paper's RNN code and data are publicly available, making this extension immediately feasible.

Added value to my researchContinual learning is directly relevant to my data science and reproducibility agenda: geospatial models deployed across time and cities encounter distribution shift (new POIs, demographic change, pandemic effects). The biological solution here — actively sacrificing fine-grained tone identity to sharpen oddball detection — is a principled strategy for adaptive forgetting that could inspire regularization methods in spatiotemporal geospatial models that must adapt to distributional drift without full retraining.

Cross-ref (corpus): Loss of plasticity in deep continual learning — 10.1038/s41586-024-07711-7
