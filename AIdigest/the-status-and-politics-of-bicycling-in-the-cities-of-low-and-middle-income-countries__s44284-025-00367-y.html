The status and politics of bicycling in the cities of low- and middle-income countries

Smruthi Bala Kannan · Rahul Goel · Ernest Agyemang · Zahidul Quayyum · Srishti Agrawal · Anisur Rahman Bayazid · Jonathan Anjaria · Kavi Bhalla Published 2026-01-20 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00367-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

In four LMIC cities (New Delhi and Chennai in India, Dhaka in Bangladesh, Accra in Ghana), everyday bicycling is persistent but politically invisible — undertaken mainly by low-income adult men, while women and children cycle within neighborhood enclaves. The real barriers extend far beyond physical bike lanes: financial systems, repair and maintenance networks, manufacturing/design capacity, and social support all shape who can ride. Through a mobility-justice (kinopolitics) lens, the authors argue that imported high-income-country "build-a-lane" logics fail these riders, and call for an ethics-of-care, repair-oriented approach led by health and environmental agencies and grounded in context-sensitive research.

Why It Matters

  • Reframes who cycling policy is for. Most LMIC cycling investment chases future elite/car-converting riders; this work centers the existing, marginalized bicyclists who already keep these systems alive, exposing a deep equity blind spot.
  • Shows infrastructure is more than asphalt. By documenting financial, maintenance, manufacturing/design, and human-social infrastructures, it explains why isolated, short, elite-adjacent bike lanes — and unmaintained bike-share schemes — routinely fail.
  • Connects mobility to public health and climate. Air pollution and physical inactivity are leading noncommunicable-disease risks in LMICs (a large share of global physical-inactivity deaths occur there); supporting current cyclists is a direct health and emissions lever.
  • Names the governance gap. Responsibility for cycling is scattered across education, transport, and sport agencies; the paper proposes health/environmental agencies as stewards — an actionable institutional pivot.
  • Surfaces gendered and intergenerational exclusion. Women trip-chain short distances and dismount on arterials; kin actively dissuade children and elders from riding — barriers no bike-lane count captures.

Core Methods

  • Mixed-methods, multi-sited design: interdependent intercept surveys, semi-structured interviews, and ethnographic observation, with locally trained teams maintaining cross-site consistency (explicitly not a strict comparative design — methods vary by site funding/timeline).
  • Intercept sampling on arterials: recruiting bicyclists where the activity occurs; attire and bicycle features recorded as proxies for income, class, and employment.
  • Thematic qualitative coding: open coding in Dedoose, an a-priori code list refined via literature review, organized through an infrastructures lens (built, financial, maintenance, design/manufacturing, human-social).
  • Theoretical framing — mobility justice / kinopolitics: Mimi Sheller's mobility-justice framework and the "new mobilities paradigm" used to read urban fragmentation ("splintering") and uneven mobility power relations.
  • Triangulation & analytic memos: survey findings, observations, and interviews triangulated; analytical depth prioritized over theme-frequency/saturation counts; intersectional attention to gender, class, age, and migration.
  • Iterative implementation framework: synthesis distilled into a practitioner-facing framework (ethics of care / repair-oriented policy; Supplementary Information 4).

① Map the "invisible" cyclists the paper describes onto a quantitative exposure-and-access surface

Extending this paperThe paper's central finding — that low-income cyclists are politically invisible and concentrated on a few arterial corridors at certain times — is established ethnographically but never georeferenced. A direct extension: fuse the intercept-survey sites with street-network and foot/cycle-traffic models to estimate where and when these riders are most exposed to high-speed traffic, turning narrative invisibility into a mappable risk surface that planners cannot ignore.

Added value to my researchThis is a textbook input for my spatiotemporal kernel density and explainable-ML exposure work: cyclist counts plus time-of-day plus weather (heatwaves, monsoon flooding noted in the paper) become a multi-source GeoAI hotspot model for vulnerable active-travel populations.

Cross-ref (corpus): Spatial distribution of foot traffic in New York City and applications for urban planning — 10.1038/s44284-025-00383-y

② Treat repair/parking/retail shops as an accessibility supply layer (a "care infrastructure" 2SFCA)

Extending this paperThe paper's most novel claim is that maintenance and human-social infrastructures (repair stalls, second-hand markets, paid parking near transit) sustain cycling more than bike lanes do — yet their spatial coverage is undocumented. Extension: inventory these precarious footpath repair/parking providers and model whether current bicyclists actually live and ride within reach of them, identifying "repair deserts."

Added value to my researchThis is a perfect non-clinical test bed for 2SFCA/R2SFCA: substitute "primary-care facilities" with "bicycle-repair providers," learn the distance-decay from observed riding distances (~2 km for women, long single rides for men), and delineate equity-weighted service areas — extending my accessibility methods to mobility-support services.

Cross-ref (corpus): Inequities in spatial access to childbirth care in the Grand Conakry conurbation, Guinea — 10.1038/s44284-025-00220-2

③ Quantify the health and emissions co-benefits of protecting (not just converting) current cyclists

Extending this paperThe paper argues policy wrongly chases car-converting elites while neglecting existing low-income riders, and motivates this on health/climate grounds without modeling it. Extension: build a scenario model comparing "protect current cyclists" vs. "convert car users," propagating physical-activity gains, injury reductions, and avoided emissions through a health-impact pathway for each city.

Added value to my researchFeeds my urban-health and healthy-cities line: a Monte Carlo / SIR-adjacent health-impact pipeline with explainable attribution of which infrastructure investments yield the largest equity-weighted health return — and which neighborhoods benefit.

Cross-ref (corpus): Benefit of physical activity initiatives for climate change mitigation and adaptation — 10.1038/s44360-026-00057-6

④ Test the paper's "short, elite-adjacent, fragmented lanes fail" claim with a causal facility-design study

Extending this paperThe paper observes that disconnected lanes near high-income areas are flooded by motorcycles and rarely used, but the evidence is observational. Extension: pair its qualitative typology with a longitudinal, facility-type comparison (low-stress connected networks vs. painted stretches) to test whether connectivity and stress level, not lane presence per se, drive ridership in LMIC contexts.

Added value to my researchThis is an explainable-ML / network-community-detection opportunity: model cycling network connectivity and "low-stress" subgraphs, then use feature attribution to rank which design attributes actually raise ridership equitably — extending my efficiency-vs-equity facility-location optimization to bike networks.

Cross-ref (corpus): The link between low-stress bicycle facilities and bicycle commuting — 10.1038/s44284-025-00255-5

⑤ Build a 15-minute / proximity accessibility metric that is honest about who can actually move

Extending this paperThe paper shows women trip-chain short distances and dismount on arterials, while men ride long single commutes — a vivid demonstration that a single uniform "X-minute" radius misrepresents real reach. Extension: derive gender- and age-specific catchment functions from the survey trip data and recompute neighborhood proximity scores, exposing how standard proximity metrics overstate access for women and children.

Added value to my researchDirectly upgrades my 15-minute-neighborhood / R2SFCA work: replace assumed isotropic catchments with population-segmented, behaviorally learned distance-decay, and integrate mobility-survey fusion — a more just proximity metric for the Global South.
