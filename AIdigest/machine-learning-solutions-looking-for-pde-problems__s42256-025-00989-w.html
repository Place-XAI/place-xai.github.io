Machine learning solutions looking for PDE problems

Nature Machine Intelligence Editorial Published 2025-01-27 DOI 10.1038/s42256-025-00989-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Machine learning models for solving partial differential equations are overhyped relative to validated results: a survey of 82 papers in fluid dynamics finds systematic comparisons against weak numerical baselines and underreporting of negative results. The community lacks rigorous, CASP-like benchmark challenges that combine speed, accuracy, uncertainty quantification, and domain relevance — and until those exist, ML-based PDE solvers risk remaining "solutions looking for a problem." Progress will require deep collaboration with domain experts and a shift toward problems that existing numerical methods genuinely cannot handle.

Core Methods

  • Systematic literature survey: McGreivy & Hakim surveyed 82 papers on ML-based fluid-dynamics PDE solvers, categorising the quality and fairness of comparisons against numerical baselines.
  • Qualitative expert synthesis: the editorial team conducted structured interviews with three leading researchers (Koumoutsakos, McGreivy, Brandstetter) to distil consensus and divergence on benchmark design, evaluation fairness, and the genuine advantages of ML over numerics.
  • Benchmark desiderata framework: McGreivy formalised three necessary properties for a useful PDE benchmark — (1) unsolvability by current methods, (2) verifiability, (3) usefulness — and noted the inherent tension between (1) and (2) when ground-truth requires high-fidelity direct numerical simulation.
  • Analogy mapping: CASP (Critical Assessment of protein Structure Prediction) is used as a structural analogy to argue that externally-judged community challenges, rather than author-reported comparisons, are necessary to advance the field credibly.
  • Conceptual positioning: Brandstetter's "complexity-threshold hypothesis" — that DL benefits only materialise at scales an order of magnitude larger than current benchmarks — is presented as a falsifiable research direction.

③ Apply transfer operator learning to cross-city spatiotemporal forecasting

Extending this paperThe editorial identifies "generalization" as a key user demand currently unmet by ML-PDE solvers — models trained on one PDE regime fail in another. Deep transfer operator learning (conditional shift adaptation) addresses exactly this: train a base neural operator on well-simulated cities or domains with rich data, then transfer to data-sparse targets under distributional shift. Extending the editorial's framing: compile a comparative study of transfer-operator architectures on a standardised set of spatiotemporal PDEs (heat, diffusion, shallow water), using "the Well" as a source domain, measuring cross-domain accuracy under the benchmark trilemma criteria.

Added value to my researchMy fine-tuning of geospatial foundation models and multi-source data fusion work maps directly: transfer operator learning provides a principled route to adapting a model trained on dense-data cities to sparse-data or low-income cities — addressing the spatial equity dimension of my accessibility research without requiring fresh large-scale data collection in every new city.

⑤ Neural operators for free-boundary urban phenomena: a testbed for the editorial's agenda

Extending this paperThe editorial calls for ML-PDE problems that are genuinely unsolvable by numerics alone. Free-boundary PDE problems — where the domain shape is unknown and must be solved jointly with the field — are a strong candidate: they arise in urban flooding (water-land interfaces evolving with rainfall), wildfire spread, and slum-growth as an expanding spatial domain. Neural operators that handle free boundaries without re-meshing could plausibly outperform numerical methods on realistic large-scale city geometries, making them an ideal CASP-style challenge domain. The editorial's claims could be tested head-to-head in this setting.

Added value to my researchUrban flooding and environmental health access are directly within my public/urban health GeoAI scope. A neural operator that solves a free-boundary flood-propagation PDE over a city road network could be composed with my 2SFCA accessibility model to compute flood-disrupted healthcare access in near-real-time — a concrete GeoAI pipeline combining scientific ML with spatial health policy, exactly the coupling I seek between space-human systems and public health outcomes.
