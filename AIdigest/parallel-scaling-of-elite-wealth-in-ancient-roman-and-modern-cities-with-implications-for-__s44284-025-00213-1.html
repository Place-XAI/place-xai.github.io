Parallel scaling of elite wealth in ancient Roman and modern cities with implications for understanding urban inequality

W. Christopher Carleton · Hugh Elton · Will Miranda · Isaac Work · Daniel Safarik · Ricarda Winkelmann · Manfred Laubichler · Jürgen Renn · Patrick Roberts Published 2025-03-04 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00213-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across societies separated by more than 1,000 years, physical expressions of elite wealth scale with urban population size in a strikingly similar, sublinear power law (scaling exponent means clustered ~0.3–0.5). Roman monuments and honorific inscriptions and modern skyscrapers and billionaire counts all track population in the same way, suggesting an ancient, enduring mechanism of urban inequality. An agent-based network simulation shows that preferential attachment in social networks — unequal access to interaction potential — is a simple but powerful driver of this concentration, pointing to an extension of settlement scaling theory (SST).

★

Why It Matters

  • Inequality may be structural to urbanism, not just to capitalism. The same scaling signature appears in Imperial Rome and the 21st-century global economy, implying that wealth concentration is partly a by-product of how cities work rather than a quirk of any single economic system.
  • Bridges archaeology and modern urban science. By placing Roman monumentality and inscriptions on the same axis as skyscrapers and billionaires, it turns 2,000 years of urban history into one comparable scaling dataset.
  • Offers a mechanism, not just a correlation. The network-Boltzmann ABM links macro-scale scaling to micro-scale interaction inequality (preferential attachment), giving SST a candidate generative process for inequality.
  • Reframes the sustainability agenda. If wealth-population scaling is intrinsically coupled to inequality, then SDG-11 ("inclusive, safe, resilient and sustainable cities") must grapple with a deep structural tendency rather than a purely policy-fixable distortion.

Core Methods

  • Power-law scaling regression: fit y = a·x^b (log y = log a + b·log x) where b is the scaling exponent of interest, comparing elite-wealth proxies against population size.
  • Bayesian negative-binomial models: count-aware models (built in R with nimble, fit by MCMC) handle overdispersion and archaeological false-zeros; a hierarchical structure allows variation among Roman provinces.
  • Uncertainty propagation: a first submodel imputes missing Roman city populations from an urban-area→population scaling relationship; that uncertainty flows into the scaling submodel within one joint MCMC.
  • Six Roman subsets + robustness checks: with/without zero counts, walls-only area definitions, and exclusion of debatable "monuments" (fora/agorai/grids/walls) to test sensitivity.
  • Model comparison & outliers: power-law vs linear-log compared via PSIS-LOO / l.p.p.d.; influential points flagged by Pareto-k and re-run to confirm exponent stability; pseudo-R² (~0.15–0.57) from MCMC output.
  • Agent-based network model: a Boltzmann wealth model on a network (Python mesa + networkx); a preferential-attachment strength parameter s (levels 0–3.5, 20 agents, 10 steps, 100 runs each) shows Gini rises with connection inequality.

① From elite-wealth proxies to a within-city inequality field

Extending this paperThe paper measures inequality only through aggregate, between-city proxies (counts of monuments, inscriptions, billionaires, tall buildings) and a single exponent per dataset. A natural extension is to spatialize it: estimate intra-urban distributions of elite-wealth markers (e.g. monument and skyscraper locations) and ask whether the same ~0.3–0.5 exponent emerges within cities, connecting this work directly to the literature showing that urban scaling laws are themselves generated by within-city inequalities.

Added value to my researchThis maps onto my spatial-entropy and intra-city accessibility work: a geocoded "elite-wealth surface" is exactly the kind of layer I fuse with healthcare and amenity access to test whether monumental/wealth concentration co-locates with service deserts.

② Replace the toy 20-agent ABM with a calibrated network-generative model

Extending this paperThe paper's ABM is deliberately minimal — 20 agents, 10 steps, a single preferential-attachment knob — chosen for legible visualizations rather than calibration. Extending it: scale the network-Boltzmann model to realistic populations and fit the preferential-attachment strength s to the empirically observed scaling exponent and Gini values, turning a heuristic illustration into an inference engine that estimates network inequality from observable wealth proxies.

Added value to my researchDirectly feeds my agent-based modeling and network community-detection lines: a calibrated preferential-attachment generator becomes a synthetic-population engine for testing how interaction-network structure shapes accessibility and service uptake.

③ Extend SST from mean-field to full-distribution with explainable ML

Extending this paperThe authors explicitly note SST is a mean-field theory and call for extending it to whole distributions and parameters beyond the mean. A concrete route: model the conditional distribution of elite-wealth counts given population with distributional/quantile learners, then use explainable ML (SHAP, GWR-style local attribution) to surface which contextual factors (province, civic status, era, economy) bend the exponent — the very "outlier cities" the paper flags as informative.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction agenda: combine MGWR/GWR local coefficients with ML ensembles to map where the wealth-scaling relationship is non-stationary, exactly as I do for primary-care and mental-health accessibility.

④ Couple wealth scaling to knowledge/innovation network evolution

Extending this paperThe paper proposes preferential attachment as the engine but tests it only abstractly. A strong extension is to confront it with real evolving urban networks — e.g. technology-transfer or knowledge-flow networks across cities — and check whether the growth of inter-city connectivity reproduces the same sublinear elite-wealth exponent, tying the mechanism to observable network dynamics rather than simulation alone.

⑤ Add an independent archaeological-genomic axis to the inequality test

Extending this paperThe paper's Roman inequality signal rests entirely on monumentality and epigraphy, which it acknowledges are noisy elite-expenditure proxies. A powerful, falsifiable extension is to triangulate with independent inequality estimators — house-floor-area Gini coefficients and bioarchaeological / ancient-DNA evidence of social stratification — to test the paper's explicit prediction that similar scaling exponents should correspond to similar archaeological Gini values.
