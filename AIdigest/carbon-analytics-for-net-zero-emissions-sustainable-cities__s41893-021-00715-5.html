Carbon analytics for net-zero emissions sustainable cities

Core Conclusion

There is no single best carbon accounting approach for cities — each of the four major GHG accounting frameworks (territorial Scope 1, community-wide infrastructure Scope 1+2+3, consumption-based, and total supply-chain) maps to a distinct decarbonization purpose. Cities should mainstream community-wide Scope 1+2+3 accounting across seven physical provisioning systems (energy, water, buildings, mobility, waste, food, green space) as the most actionable path to net-zero, since decarbonizing these systems simultaneously advances multiple SDGs and automatically reduces embodied carbon in global trade.

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a long-standing scientific dispute. After 15 years of debate over which accounting scope is "more comprehensive," this framework clarifies that each approach is complete within its own stated purpose — ending the false hierarchy and redirecting effort toward aligned use.
  • Defines what "net-zero city" actually means. The paper articulates a net-zero city as one with net-zero community-wide physical provisioning systems — an ambitious yet realistic target that avoids perverse incentives (e.g., offshoring industry) inherent in purely territorial definitions.
  • Links carbon with multiple SDGs. The seven provisioning systems collectively account for >90% of GHGs, >96% of water withdrawals, and ~20 million premature deaths globally — making decarbonization inseparable from SDG goals on health, equity, water, land, and food.
  • Charts a data revolution roadmap. The framework identifies concrete innovation gaps: better usage-activity data (mobile phone mobility, satellite electricity monitoring, 3D city imaging, wastewater nutrition analytics), supply chain mapping via input-output and blockchain, and atmospheric inverse modelling — each with named methods and references.

Core Methods

  • Conceptual framework development: systematic review and synthesis of four GHG accounting approaches (Scope 1 territorial; Scope 1+2+3 community-wide infrastructure; consumption-based; total supply-chain input-output), mapping each to a distinct urban decarbonization policy purpose.
  • Mathematical equivalence proof: cites prior work (Chavez & Ramaswami, Energy Policy 2013) showing no single approach is simultaneously more comprehensive and locally representative — used as the theoretical anchor for the framework.
  • Seven-sector provisioning systems lens: uses aggregate statistics across published studies to demonstrate that seven physical sectors cover >90% GHGs and >96% water withdrawals, establishing their centrality to SDG linkage.
  • Net-zero city definition: logical/conceptual derivation — a net-zero city defined through community-wide provisioning systems avoids perverse incentives of territorial and household definitions, and renders total supply-chain accounting redundant by covering embodied carbon in global trade.
  • Innovation roadmap (Fig. 1): identifies three analytical advances needed — (1) usage-activity data via remote sensing and mobile-phone sensing, (2) supply chain mapping via physically based I-O and blockchain, and (3) integration of atmospheric inverse modelling with urban metabolism studies.
  • SDG-nexus analytics: conceptual mapping of provisioning systems to SDGs 2 (food), 3 (health), 6 (water), 10 (equity), 13 (climate), 15 (land), and 17 (global partnerships).

① Operationalize the framework's usage-activity data gap with mobile-phone and remote sensing fusion

Extending this paperThe paper identifies usage-activity data as the foundational bottleneck for all four accounting approaches, and names mobile phone and satellite data as candidate solutions — but leaves the fusion architecture unspecified. A direct extension is to build a spatiotemporal data pipeline that fuses mobile-phone mobility trajectories (for transport energy estimation), nighttime light satellite imagery (for electricity use), and 3D LiDAR city models (for construction material stocks) into a unified urban metabolism ledger. This would instantiate the paper's Fig. 1 left column in a working system, enabling real-time Scope 1+2+3 estimation without relying on self-reported fuel use. The inter-city SDG sustainability tracking paper from the corpus demonstrates that such emission-linked SDG monitoring across cities is feasible at scale when remotely sensed and administrative data are combined.

Added value to my researchThis aligns with my multi-source data fusion agenda (mobile phone, remote sensing, social media). Specifically, the mobile-phone trajectory layer for transport GHG estimation maps directly onto my spatiotemporal kernel density methods: I can estimate carbon-intensity hotspots from GPS/mobile data with the same framework I use for health geography — repurposing my spatiotemporal KDE and agent-based modeling stack for urban carbon metabolism monitoring.

② Build an explainable ML model of city-level GHG drivers across the seven provisioning systems

Extending this paperThe paper asserts that the seven provisioning systems cover >90% of GHGs and charts how each sector links to distinct SDGs, but does not empirically quantify which systems are the dominant drivers in different city archetypes. An extension is to assemble a multi-city panel of Scope 1+2+3 inventories (using the GPC database) and train an explainable gradient boosting model with SHAP to decompose variance in total city carbon footprints across the seven sectors, city form (density, compactness), income, and climate zone. This would test the paper's implicit claim that community-wide infrastructure systems are the most policy-leverageable drivers — and reveal heterogeneity across city types. The health co-benefits paper in the corpus shows that city-level GHG and health co-benefit modeling with subnational resolution is achievable.

Added value to my researchThis is a core application of my explainable ML for spatial prediction agenda. My existing MGWR/GWR toolkit for spatial non-stationarity can be extended to ask: do the sectoral drivers of urban carbon follow a geographically weighted pattern? SHAP-driven spatial attribution maps would help planners identify which provisioning system to target in their specific urban context — exactly the policy-translation function I aim for in my GeoAI work.

③ Extend the SDG-nexus analytics to a spatial equity audit of provisioning system access

Extending this paperThe paper links decarbonizing the seven provisioning systems to SDG 10 (reduced inequalities) and SDG 3 (health), but does so only at the aggregate city level. A critical extension: operationalize the equity dimension spatially by computing intra-city distributional access to each provisioning system (transport, food, green space, healthcare infrastructure) using fine-grained spatial data, then test whether neighborhoods with the worst GHG-per-capita profiles are also those with the worst access scores. This spatial coupling of carbon exposure and service access would reveal whether the community-wide Scope 1+2+3 accounting approach, when disaggregated, actually captures environmental justice. The corpus paper on city footprints and SDGs provides a conceptual anchor for such integrated city-scale sustainability assessments.

④ Physics-informed graph learning for transboundary supply chain carbon flow estimation

Extending this paperThe paper calls for "advances in physically based input-output modelling and blockchain technologies" to map urban supply chains — but these approaches are data-intensive and city-specific. An extension is to model city supply chains as a directed bipartite graph (cities as demand nodes, production regions as supply nodes, physical flows as edges) and train a physics-informed graph neural network with mass-balance conservation constraints as soft losses. The model would learn to infer transboundary Scope 3 flows for cities lacking high-quality input-output tables, using only basic economic and population metadata as inputs — effectively making the paper's most technically demanding component transferable to data-poor cities. The building operating carbon dynamics corpus paper shows that multi-city carbon modeling can achieve spatial granularity when physical constraints are embedded.

Added value to my researchPhysics-informed graph learning is a core methodological focus of my lab. The supply chain flow problem is an ideal domain to develop and validate conservation-constrained GNN architectures: mass-balance of carbon (emissions from imports must equal production-site emissions along the chain) is a hard physical prior that regularizes training exactly as I aim to do with my spatiotemporal physics-informed models. This would yield a transferable GeoAI carbon accounting engine for cities without local inventory capacity.

⑤ Scale the framework to food-system SDG nexus via multi-indicator spatial modeling

Extending this paperThe paper singles out food as one of the seven provisioning systems critical for SDG 2 (zero hunger), SDG 3 (health), and SDG 13 (climate), but the food-carbon-SDG nexus is the least developed in the framework. An extension is to build a spatially explicit model that traces transboundary agricultural carbon flows (from production regions to urban consumers), disaggregates them by food type and dietary pattern, and links them to multi-indicator SDG outcomes (water use, biodiversity, health). The multi-indicator sustainability assessment of global food systems corpus paper provides exactly the multi-SDG quantification scaffold that the paper's conceptual food nexus idea needs to become operational.
