The cost of anti-Asian racism during the COVID-19 pandemic

Justin T. Huang · Masha Krupenkin · David Rothschild · Julia Lee Cunningham Published 2023-01-19 DOI 10.1038/s41562-022-01493-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Asian restaurants in the United States experienced an 18.4% decrease in foot traffic relative to comparable non-Asian restaurants following the onset of COVID-19, representing an estimated US$7.42 billion in lost revenue in 2020. The avoidance was larger in areas with higher Trump vote share and extended to non-Chinese Asian restaurants — driven by collective blame, out-group homogeneity bias, and ethnic misidentification rather than food-safety concerns, demonstrating that anti-China political rhetoric imposed a measurable economic cost on the entire Asian American community.

★

Why It Matters

  • First large-scale quantification of pandemic-era consumer discrimination. Covering hundreds of billions of device-location trips at 381,692 restaurant locations, the study converts anecdotal reports of anti-Asian sentiment into a precise economic estimate — US$7.42 billion in foregone revenue — where prior work relied on self-reported hate incidents or police statistics.
  • Reveals blame-spillover beyond the targeted ethnic group. Non-Chinese Asian restaurants lost more traffic (25.0%) than Chinese restaurants (10.9%), establishing ethnic misidentification and out-group homogeneity as economically consequential mechanisms, not just social-psychology curiosities.
  • Documents geographic polarization of discrimination. ZIP-code-level analysis links Trump 2016 vote share to significantly greater restaurant avoidance, connecting macroscale political rhetoric to hyperlocal consumer behaviour — a methodological template for studying partisan-driven market distortions.
  • Underlines communication responsibility in public health crises. Findings show that ethnically targeted disease-naming by political leaders creates lasting economic harm to minority communities, with direct implications for WHO guidance and crisis communication policy worldwide.

Core Methods

  • Difference-in-differences (DiD) panel regression: log-linear model estimating the differential impact of COVID-19 onset (13 March 2020) on log restaurant visits for Asian vs. non-Asian restaurants, with restaurant-level fixed effects (ηi), week fixed effects (δt), and ZIP-code COVID-19 case rate as a time-varying covariate — isolating demand shifts from common temporal shocks.
  • ZIP-code-level heterogeneity analysis: DiD model repeated for each ZIP code separately; estimated impact coefficients regressed on Trump 2016 vote share (controlling for median household income, college education and racial demographics), visualized with LOESS smoothing (span = 0.5) and 95% CI bands.
  • Counterfactual revenue simulation: interaction term set to zero to simulate "no discrimination" baseline; additional predicted SafeGraph visits extrapolated to full-year 2020 and converted to US dollars using per-visit revenue from Census Monthly Retail Trade totals.
  • Multi-wave survey design (Pollfish): four nationally raked waves (n ≈ 1,000 each) measuring pandemic blame attribution, food-safety fear, and out-group homogeneity perceptions; between-participants answer-set manipulation in Wave 4 to test Chinese vs. generic Asian blame framing.
  • Ethnic misidentification experiment (MTurk): stratified random sample of 2,000 mono-ethnic restaurant names; each participant classified 20 restaurants by ethnicity; misidentification rates compared across partisan-vote categories using two-sided Z-tests for difference in proportions.
  • Google Trends discontinuity analysis: weekly search indices for anti-China, bat/dog-consumption and food-safety queries compared across five calendar years (2016–2020) to isolate pandemic-specific spikes from seasonal patterns.

① Map the spatial geometry of discrimination with geographically weighted regression

Extending this paperThe paper's heterogeneity analysis stops at ZIP-code regression with a single LOESS-smoothed predictor. A direct extension: treat the ZIP-code impact coefficients as the dependent variable in a Multiscale Geographically Weighted Regression (MGWR) framework, replacing the fixed Trump-vote-share coefficient with a spatially varying surface. This would reveal whether the link between partisanship and discrimination is itself spatially non-stationary — for instance, stronger in suburban rather than rural high-Trump areas — and identify the neighbourhood scale at which political cuing translates into consumer avoidance. SafeGraph already provides the spatial support; the extension is purely the spatial statistical wrapper around the paper's own DiD residuals.

Added value to my researchMGWR/GWR is a core method in my repertoire. Applying it to discrimination coefficients rather than health outcomes opens a new application domain — spatial econometrics of racial bias — for my toolkit. The resulting discrimination surface can be fused with 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility layers to measure whether minority-owned businesses are doubly disadvantaged: lower physical accessibility and higher discrimination intensity co-located, producing a compound equity metric directly relevant to urban health policy research.

② Build a real-time early-warning system linking search-trend signals to anticipated mobility-based discrimination

Extending this paperThe paper uses Google Trends retrospectively to confirm sentiment spikes and SafeGraph data to measure resulting consumer avoidance with a temporal lag. A prospective extension: train a spatiotemporal predictive model where search-trend signals serve as leading indicators to nowcast business-traffic impacts at the DMA or metropolitan area level, days before economic harm materialises. The DiD model can be reformulated as a VAR / Granger-causality framework with search indices as inputs, producing an actionable early-warning score for minority business communities and city health agencies.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly onto my multi-source data fusion agenda (social media + GPS mobility data). Extending the fusion pipeline from physical health outcomes to economic health of minority communities broadens the scope of my spatiotemporal kernel density and time-series methods. It also aligns with my open science / FAIR principles: a real-time dashboard built entirely on public Trends data with a trained model would be fully replicable and deployable without licence restrictions.

③ Decompose the discrimination signal with explainable ML to separate political rhetoric from endemic structural bias

Extending this paperThe paper's stated limitation is that it cannot disentangle political-leader rhetoric from pre-existing latent anti-Asian sentiment. A direct extension: apply a gradient-boosted model with SHAP explanations trained on ZIP-code impact coefficients, with features encompassing Trump vote share, pre-pandemic hate-crime rates, local media-market partisan lean, distance to Asian cultural enclaves, economic precarity indices, and pre-pandemic Asian restaurant density. SHAP interaction values decompose how much avoidance is attributable to partisanship vs. structural demographic factors vs. geographic isolation — answering the paper's own limitation directly and quantitatively.

Added value to my researchExplainable ML for spatial prediction is central to my GeoAI agenda. Using the discrimination coefficient as the target creates a novel test case for spatial explainability: can SHAP recover the partisan signal that DiD identifies, or does it expose confounders the regression misses? This cross-validation between causal inference (DiD) and explainable ML would strengthen both methodologies and is publishable as a standalone methods contribution at the intersection of spatial analysis and social science.

④ Extend the discrimination-adjusted accessibility framework to minority-serving healthcare providers

Extending this paperThe authors note that restaurants are a "bellwether" and the same mechanisms apply to ethnic-minority-owned dental offices, physicians, and legal practices. A direct extension: apply the same DiD framework to mobile-device visits to clinics coded by physician ethnicity (using the National Plan and Provider Enumeration System). If pandemic rhetoric similarly depressed visits to Asian-owned medical practices, the public-health cost is compounded: not only economic loss but reduced access to care for patients who selected those providers for linguistic or cultural concordance.

Added value to my researchThis connects directly to my 2SFCA / R2SFCA healthcare accessibility work: effective supply-side capacity is not just facility count but also utilization under discrimination shocks. Incorporating a "discrimination-adjusted visit rate" into the supply term of 2SFCA would make my accessibility models behaviourally realistic — and this paper's DiD coefficient provides an empirically grounded adjustment factor for any minority-serving facility during analogous crisis events, bridging health geography and racial-equity research.

⑤ Test a segregation–discrimination feedback hypothesis using network-based ethnic segregation metrics

Extending this paperThe paper documents avoidance but does not ask whether cities with higher baseline ethnic residential segregation — where cross-race exposure is structurally lower — experienced stronger pandemic discrimination shocks. An extension: merge the ZIP-code DiD coefficients with a city-level random-walk ethnic segregation index (from the corpus) and test whether more segregated cities had significantly deeper restaurant avoidance. This would close the loop from the paper's psychological mechanism (cross-race effect → misidentification) back to a structural urban driver (segregation geometry → reduced cross-race contact → amplified out-group homogeneity), forming a complete causal pathway.

Added value to my researchI can operationalise the segregation metric using network community detection and spatial entropy tools from my urban-analytics stack, then test mediation: segregation → cross-race contact → misidentification rate → DiD avoidance coefficient. This integrates my urban agglomeration methods with a public-health equity outcome, enriching both my urban analytics and health geography research lines with a discrimination-geography angle that is novel and publishable across disciplines.
