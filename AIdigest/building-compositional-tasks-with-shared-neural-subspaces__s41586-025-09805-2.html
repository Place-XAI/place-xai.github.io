Building compositional tasks with shared neural subspaces

Sina Tafazoli · Flora M. Bouchacourt · Adel Ardalan · Nikola T. Markov · Motoaki Uchimura · Marcelo G. Mattar · Nathaniel D. Daw · Timothy J. Buschman Published 2025-11-26 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09805-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The brain achieves cognitive flexibility by composing tasks from shared neural subspaces: the same low-dimensional subspaces in prefrontal cortex encode task-relevant sensory and motor information across multiple tasks, with each task selectively engaging and transforming these shared representations. Monkeys performing three compositionally related tasks updated an internal task belief that dynamically gated which shared subspaces were recruited — amplifying relevant and suppressing irrelevant sensory and motor dimensions — providing a neural mechanism for rapid adaptation to changing task demands without re-learning representations from scratch.

Why It Matters

  • Neural basis of compositional intelligence identified. The paper provides the first direct experimental evidence that the mammalian brain reuses representational subspaces across tasks — confirming in neural recordings a computational principle long assumed in artificial neural networks but not verified empirically.
  • Bridges AI and neuroscience. By showing that the brain implements the same multi-task representational reuse found in trained deep networks, the work offers a biological grounding for compositional AI architectures and points toward more brain-plausible designs.
  • Explains how rapid task adaptation works without catastrophic forgetting. The task-belief gating mechanism — amplifying relevant and suppressing irrelevant neural dimensions — gives a mechanistic account of continual learning without overwriting prior representations, addressing a central challenge for AI systems.
  • Generalizable to a broad behavioral repertoire. Although tested with three tasks in two monkeys, the subspace-composition mechanism is theoretically combinatorial: sequencing N shared subcomponents can implement exponentially many behaviors, suggesting a scalable basis for general intelligence.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Animal subjects
Two adult rhesus macaques (monkeys "Si" and "Ch") trained to perform three compositionally related categorization tasks (S1, C1, C2).
Neural recordings
Simultaneous multi-electrode recordings from five cortical and subcortical regions: lateral prefrontal cortex (LPFC, 480 neurons), frontal eye fields (FEF, 149 neurons), parietal cortex (PAR, 64 neurons), anterior inferior temporal cortex (aIT, 239 neurons), and striatum/caudate nucleus (149 neurons). Total: 1,081 neurons across sessions combined into pseudopopulations.
Task structure
Three tasks (S1, C1, C2) crossing two categorization subtasks (shape vs. colour) with two response axes (axis 1 vs. axis 2). Recordings included 94 blocks of S1, 97 of C1, and 189 of C2. Task switched when performance ≥ 70%.
Stimulus design
Parametric morph stimuli in a 2-D feature space varying independently in shape (bunny–tee continuum) and colour (red–green continuum); four discrete saccade targets.
Analysis method
Linear classifiers (250 iterations, n = 40 randomly sampled trials each) for cross-task decoding; targeted dimensionality reduction (TDR); cross-temporal correlation; compression index (CPI) quantifying relative subspace separability; permutation tests for significance.

Core Methods

  • Cross-task linear classification: trained classifiers on one task's neural activity to decode colour category, shape category, or motor response direction; tested on withheld trials from a different task to quantify subspace sharing (generalization accuracy above chance = shared subspace).
  • Cross-temporal decoding: classifiers trained at one timepoint were tested across all timepoints to map the temporal dynamics of subspace engagement (cross-temporal generalization matrices, Figs. 2e,h).
  • Targeted dimensionality reduction (TDR): projected population activity onto task-variable-encoding dimensions to visualize sensory-to-motor trajectory dynamics in a low-dimensional neural state space (Fig. 3f).
  • Task-belief decoding: trained a classifier on late-block (last 75 trials) neural activity during fixation to track the monkey's internal task representation over time as it discovered the current task; correlated belief strength with shared subspace engagement trial-by-trial (Figs. 4b–h).
  • Compression index (CPI): log-ratio of colour vs. shape subspace separability, quantifying dynamic gain modulation of task-relevant vs. task-irrelevant sensory dimensions (Fig. 5c–f).
  • Coefficient of partial determination (CPD): estimated the proportion of variance in each neuron's activity uniquely explained by each task variable (colour, shape, task identity, reward, response direction), confirming mixed selectivity across all five regions.
  • Bayesian task-belief model: iterative belief-updating framework (conceptually analogous to hidden Markov model inference) to formalize how monkeys inferred the current task from response feedback.

① Map the paper's task-belief gating to an attention mechanism for spatial context switching in GeoAI

Extending this paperThe paper shows that the LPFC maintains a distributed "task belief" that selectively gates which shared subspaces are engaged — functionally equivalent to a soft attention over a fixed dictionary of task components. The paper currently characterises this mechanism purely in terms of neural decoding accuracy. A direct extension would formalise it as a dynamic attention-weighted combination of shared basis subspaces, train a recurrent network with the same architecture on the same three tasks, and compare the learned gating weights with the neural compression-index trajectories — turning a neural description into a testable computational model grounded in the observed data, drawing on the corpus paper on tiny recurrent networks for cognitive strategies.

Added value to my researchIn spatiotemporal GeoAI, spatial context changes continuously (urban vs. rural, weekday vs. weekend, healthy vs. underserved areas). This paper's gating principle directly motivates a context-belief attention layer in spatial prediction models: rather than training separate models per region type or relying on fixed geographically weighted regression bandwidths, a belief-state vector encoding local spatial context could selectively activate shared geographic subspaces (e.g., population density, land-use patterns, network topology) — a learnable analogue of my MGWR that infers the relevant spatial process from data rather than assuming it.

② Use the shared-subspace geometry to quantify representational overlap in geospatial foundation models

Extending this paperThe paper's key finding — that LPFC uses shared low-dimensional subspaces across tasks, while striatum and parietal cortex use more task-specific representations — implies a geometry-of-generalization principle: regions that generalise best have the most aligned subspaces. Extending this: apply the cross-task linear decoding protocol to embeddings extracted from a multi-task geospatial foundation model trained simultaneously on, for example, population density prediction, health outcome mapping, and mobility flow estimation, to measure how much representational geometry is actually shared versus task-specific at each layer — a direct application of the paper's classifier-based geometry analysis.

Added value to my researchI fine-tune geospatial foundation models for specific downstream tasks (spatial accessibility prediction, health geography). This paper's method gives me a quantitative probe: I can measure the cross-task decodability of spatial embeddings before and after fine-tuning to determine whether fine-tuning collapses the shared subspace (like task-specific representations in striatum) or preserves it (like LPFC) — providing a geometry-based diagnostic for transfer learning in GeoAI that goes beyond loss-curve comparisons.

③ Apply the paper's continual-learning gating to prevent catastrophic forgetting in multi-city spatial models

Extending this paperThe paper shows that suppressing task-irrelevant representations during task discovery prevents interference — a biologically implemented solution to catastrophic forgetting. The paper's task-belief CPI (compression index) directly measures which subspace dimensions are actively suppressed. Extending this: implement a neural-inspired selective plasticity rule where only the dimensions with high current-task CPI are allowed to update during continual training on a sequence of cities or time periods, directly testing whether the brain's gating strategy translates into a practical continual-learning algorithm — benchmarked against the corpus paper on loss of plasticity in deep learning.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal models are retrained as new city data arrives (or as urban contexts shift), and they suffer from catastrophic forgetting of earlier patterns. The paper's mechanism suggests a CPI-guided selective fine-tuning: freeze the dimensions encoding task-invariant geographic priors (shared subspace analogue) and only update the city-specific projection heads — a biologically motivated continual-learning strategy for multi-city GeoAI models that maintains transferability while adapting locally.

④ Model task-belief updating as a spatial decision process for healthcare access inference

Extending this paperThe paper's monkeys inferred the current task from reward feedback via an iterative belief-updating process that the authors liken to apparent learning / task inference. The belief converged faster when the previous task context was similar (C1 after C1 vs. C1 after S1). Extending this: formalise the belief-updating as a partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP) and compare the inferred belief-state trajectories with the empirical neural decoding curves — testing whether a POMDP normative model can predict not just final task performance but the within-block neural dynamics, linking the paper's neural findings to normative decision theory.

Added value to my researchIn healthcare accessibility modelling, patients update their beliefs about service availability based on experienced access barriers (wait times, travel costs, digital barriers). The paper's belief-updating circuit — iteratively integrating evidence to infer which "task" (care pathway) is currently optimal — directly maps onto a POMDP model of patient care-seeking behaviour: I can adapt the belief-state framework to model dynamic service-area delineation in my R2SFCA, where catchment boundaries are not fixed but updated as patients learn which facilities are realistically accessible to them.

⑤ Translate the subspace-composition principle into a modular graph neural network for multi-domain spatial prediction

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that sequential composition of shared subspaces — first engaging the shared colour subspace, then transforming it into the task-specific motor subspace — implements arbitrarily complex behaviours from a small dictionary of components. Extending this: build a modular graph neural network where each module encodes one domain-specific subspace (e.g., transport network, land-use patterns, demographic structure) and a task-belief controller selects and sequences which modules to engage for each prediction target, testing whether subspace composition that works in primate prefrontal cortex also improves sample efficiency in multi-domain spatial learning — leveraging the orthogonal state-machine structure documented in the hippocampus corpus paper as a design principle for the module architecture.

Added value to my researchMy physics-informed graph learning work trains separate models for each spatial prediction task (e.g., accessibility, health outcomes, mobility flows). This paper's subspace-composition principle suggests a shared-subspace GNN where geographic basis modules are shared across tasks and a lightweight task-belief controller routes computation — directly unifying my separate prediction pipelines into a single transferable GeoAI architecture that generalises across cities and spatial prediction targets, yielding better data efficiency and interpretable modularity (the task-belief gating weights become spatially explainable).
