Explicit dynamic cross-strand interactions for DNA sequence language modelling

Cheng Yang · Yuansheng Liu · Lei Ling · Yang Liu · Fengxin Li · Changjian Chen · Long Wang · Feng Yu · Liang Qiao · Xiangxiang Zeng · Kenli Li · Alexander Schönhuth · Xiao Luo Published 2026-06-04 DOI 10.1038/s42256-026-01249-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

CrossDNA is a parameter-efficient DNA language model that explicitly models dynamic cross-strand interactions by processing forward and reverse-complement sequences in dual branches with a lightweight interstrand communication module. With only million-scale parameters, CrossDNA achieves performance comparable or superior to DNA foundation models with hundreds of millions of parameters across diverse genomics benchmarks — including enhancer prediction, chromatin profiling, and long-range sequence tasks — demonstrating that biologically-inspired double-strand modelling yields major gains in representation quality and parameter efficiency. These advances directly enable interpretation of regulatory logic, discovery of novel regulatory elements, and prioritization of disease-associated non-coding variants.

Why It Matters

  • First explicit, dynamic double-strand DNA language model. All previous models either treated DNA as one-directional text or enforced reverse-complement symmetry via data augmentation or equivariance — both rely on implicit, static approximations. CrossDNA is the first to model strand interactions explicitly and dynamically during representation learning.
  • Extreme parameter efficiency. CrossDNA's million-scale parameter count matches or beats models with hundreds of millions of parameters, proving that architectural inductive biases grounded in biology can substitute for raw model scale.
  • Broad benchmark coverage and orientation robustness. Consistent gains across Genomic Benchmarks (8 tasks), NTv2 tasks, chromatin profiling (DeepSEA), enhancer activity (DeepSTARR), DNA long-range benchmarks, and zero-shot embedding quality — demonstrating generality, not task-specific tuning.
  • Biological utility beyond classification. The model enables functional interpretability (regulatory logic), discovery of novel regulatory elements across species, and prioritization of non-coding disease variants — bridging model performance to translational biology.
  • Fully open and reproducible. Code on GitHub, weights on Zenodo, and all pretraining and fine-tuning data from publicly accessible sources — lowering barriers to downstream adoption.

Core Methods

  • Dual-branch duplex-inspired training: CrossDNA maintains two branches that receive alternating segments of the forward and reverse-complement (RC) strand, rotating inputs so each branch is exposed to both polarities — simulating the information exchange between DNA strands during biological processes.
  • Cross-strand communication module: a lightweight module that passes representations between branches at each layer, enabling dynamic and explicit interstrand information transfer rather than the static, implicit symmetry enforcement used by prior models.
  • Self-distillation: a teacher model provides supervision to reduce semantic discontinuity introduced by the rotating dual-strand input, stabilizing pre-training.
  • Recurrent long-context backbone (Comba-SWA): a Mamba-inspired state-space backbone (Comba) is coupled with sliding-window attention (SWA), enabling efficient modelling of sequences up to 100 kb without the quadratic cost of full attention.
  • Masked language model (MLM) pre-training: CrossDNA is pre-trained on hg38 with masked token prediction; ablations test the contribution of CrossView, Self-Distillation, and TokenBridge components at 2 kb, 4 kb, and 100 kb lengths.
  • Evaluation metrics: Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC) for classification tasks, Pearson correlation for regression tasks (enhancer activity), normalized mutual information (NMI) for UMAP-based embedding clustering, and Hodges-Lehmann paired effect sizes for statistical comparison across model scaling variants.
  • Parameter scaling analysis: three CrossDNA variants — 8.1M (128-d), 71.6M (512-d), 519M (1,024-d) — evaluated across 42 datasets to characterize performance scaling.

① Transfer CrossDNA's dual-branch architecture to geospatial sequence modelling

Extending this paper CrossDNA's core insight is that sequences have two complementary representations (forward and reverse-complement strands) whose dynamic interaction improves learning — but the paper limits this to DNA. A direct extension: apply the dual-branch cross-view communication design to other bidirectional or multi-view sequence domains (e.g., trajectories observed at different spatial resolutions, or time series measured by different sensors). Pairing CrossDNA's alternating-exposure training with the sequence-level masked pre-training of a foundation model of transcription would test whether biologically-inspired dual-branch pretraining generalizes beyond genomics.

Added value to my research My spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and GPS trajectory work similarly requires fusing complementary views of movement (forward in time vs. reverse, or coarse vs. fine resolution). CrossDNA's lightweight cross-view communication module offers a concrete architectural template for building dual-view spatiotemporal encoders that I could apply to mobile-phone trajectory data for urban mobility modelling — yielding richer representations without increasing model size.

② Add explicit spatial regulatory grammar to CrossDNA's long-range backbone

Extending this paper CrossDNA handles sequences up to 100 kb via Comba-SWA, but its long-range modelling treats position largely implicitly. The paper demonstrates gains on DNALONGBENCH, yet does not exploit known regulatory syntax — the grammar of transcription-factor binding sites, spacing constraints, and co-operative binding. Extending CrossDNA with the regulatory syntax rules discovered in large-scale multiomics studies (e.g., TF binding-site co-occurrence and chromatin accessibility co-variation) as soft physics constraints during fine-tuning would ground the model's long-range attention in biologically interpretable structure, making variant prioritization more trustworthy.

Added value to my research This mirrors my physics-informed graph learning agenda: injecting domain priors as soft constraints in the loss function rather than hard-coding them in architecture. Adapting this principle to my GNN models for spatial accessibility means I can encode, for instance, travel-time triangle inequalities or facility catchment constraints as physics terms — a direct methodological parallel to regulatory syntax constraints in CrossDNA.

③ Use CrossDNA-style parameter efficiency principles for fine-tuning geospatial foundation models

Extending this paper The paper's most striking result is that explicit biological inductive bias (double-strand architecture) achieves parity with models 100× larger. This raises a hypothesis worth testing within this paper's scope: does the dual-branch module remain effective as a plug-in adapter that can be grafted onto already-trained large DNA foundation models (e.g., Nucleotide Transformer, Evo), making them strand-aware via a lightweight fine-tuning stage without full re-pretraining? Testing CrossDNA's communication module as a parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) add-on to existing large models would be a natural extension that maximizes the paper's practical impact.
