Social capital of urban infrastructure with Bogotá's cable car

Andrés F. Useche · María Alejandra Rubio · Diana Higuera-Mendieta · Paula Guevara-Aladino · Eliana Martínez-Herrera · Luis A. Guzman · Víctor Cantillo-Garcia · Joan Benach · Olga L. Sarmiento Published 2026-01-12 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00366-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A new aerial cable-car line (TransMiCable, opened 2018) serving a low-income, hilly informal-settlement periphery of Bogotá did more than move people — it reshaped social capital. Across six SC dimensions the authors identify three latent classes (predominantly bonding, bridging, linking). Residents of the intervention area were 3.34× more likely (95% CI 2.43–4.62) to shift from bonding (inward family trust) to bridging (trust in friends and neighbors) than a comparable control area, evidence that the transport intervention strengthened cross-community ties and that community participation is integral to urban transformation.

Why It Matters

  • Measures the social return on transport, not just the travel-time return. Most appraisal of transit infrastructure stops at mobility and access; this study quantifies how a cable car changes trust, networks and civic engagement — outcomes usually treated as unmeasurable side-effects.
  • Targets the populations inequality hits hardest. The intervention sits in Ciudad Bolívar, a steep, peripheral informal settlement — exactly the Latin-American urban context where deep inequities concentrate and where conventional bus/metro access is poor.
  • Provides a causal-leaning estimate from a real intervention. Using an intervention-vs-control natural-experiment design (the TrUST study), it reports a concrete effect size — a 3.34× odds of moving toward bridging social capital — rather than a cross-sectional correlation.
  • Reframes a famous policy model. Medellín's Metrocable inspired worldwide imitation on equity grounds; this paper supplies the missing social-cohesion evidence for Bogotá's successor, informing whether cable cars deliver community benefits beyond commute savings.

Core Methods

  • Latent class analysis (LCA): from six measured SC dimensions, infer unobserved social-capital classes — yielding predominantly bonding, bridging and linking profiles rather than imposing them a priori.
  • Latent transition modeling: estimate movement between SC classes across baseline and follow-up, the basis for the bonding→bridging transition odds.
  • Natural-experiment / intervention–control contrast: compare transition probabilities for residents in the TransMiCable intervention area against a matched control area, isolating the cable-car's association.
  • Effect estimation: report the odds ratio of transitioning (3.34, 95% CI 2.43–4.62), quantifying the intervention's pull toward bridging social capital.
  • Conceptual framing: operationalizes Putnam/Woolcock-style bonding–bridging–linking theory into measurable urban-transport outcomes for an informal-settlement setting.

① Spatialize the social-capital effect with a built-environment accessibility model

Extending this paperThe paper treats the cable car as a single binary "intervention vs control" exposure, but TransMiCable changes the whole accessibility surface — travel time to jobs, services and the rest of Bogotá's TransMilenio network varies sharply by station distance, terrain slope and walk-shed. Extending it: replace the binary exposure with a continuous, network-based accessibility gradient per respondent and test a dose–response relationship between accessibility gain and the bonding→bridging transition, the way a countrywide natural experiment links the built environment to behavior.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my 2SFCA/R2SFCA machinery: compute station-based catchments and supply-weighted access scores, then use them as the spatial exposure variable for a health/social outcome — bridging transport accessibility and social-cohesion epidemiology.

② Add a network-segregation lens to the bonding–bridging transition

Extending this paperThe paper measures social capital as individual class membership, not as actual network structure. Bonding→bridging implies new cross-group ties, yet no graph is observed. Extending it: pair the survey with mobility/contact data to reconstruct the egocentric social network, and test whether the cable car reduces network segregation (homophily, triadic closure constrained by urban topology) — turning an asserted bridging effect into a measured drop in fragmentation.

Added value to my researchThis feeds my network community-detection and spatial-entropy work: quantify segregation before/after an infrastructure shock and attribute it with explainable spatial features, linking urban topology to inequality dynamics.

Cross-ref (corpus): Inequality is rising where social network segregation interacts with urban topology — 10.1038/s41467-021-21465-0

④ Generalize across transit modes and peripheries with explainable ML

Extending this paperThe result is one cable car in one city; whether the social-capital dividend is specific to aerial cable cars (which physically open up steep, severed peripheries) or generic to any transit upgrade is untested. Extending it: pool multi-city, multi-mode transit natural experiments and use explainable ML to learn which infrastructure and neighborhood features (slope, prior isolation, mode type, station density) predict the bonding→bridging transition — separating the cable-car-specific effect from a general transit effect.

Added value to my researchThis matches my explainable machine learning for spatial prediction: SHAP-style attribution over transit and built-environment features to identify the mechanisms of social benefit, generalizable to my urban-analytics and healthy-cities work.

⑤ Fuse survey social capital with multi-source urban data to scale measurement

Extending this paperThe SC measure is survey-only — costly, sparse and a snapshot. Extending it: train a model that predicts neighborhood social capital from multi-source proxies (mobile-phone co-presence, social-media interaction graphs, POIs, remote-sensing morphology of informal settlements) calibrated on the TrUST survey, so social capital can be mapped continuously and updated across Bogotá's peripheries rather than only where a survey ran.
