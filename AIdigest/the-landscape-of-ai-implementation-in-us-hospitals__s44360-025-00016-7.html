The landscape of AI implementation in US hospitals

Yeon-Mi Hwang · Madelena Y. Ng · Malvika Pillai · Michelle P. Sahai · Tina Hernandez-Boussard Published 2026-01-15 DOI 10.1038/s44360-025-00016-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 3,560 US hospitals, predictive AI adoption in 2023–2024 is geographically clustered, with pronounced hotspots and coldspots. Hospitals serving areas with greater healthcare access needs (provider shortages, medically underserved designations) were less likely to have AI-based predictive models — implementation ratios as low as 0.40 in the highest-need areas. Geographically weighted regression (GWR) revealed that the factors driving adoption vary substantially across regions, with interoperability (the "core index") being the single most spatially consistent predictor. These findings provide a baseline map of early AI deployment and caution against uniform national rollout strategies.

Core Methods

  • AI implementation scoring: hospitals classified into three tiers (no predictive model / non-AI predictive model / AI-based predictive model) with exploratory composite scores for breadth, development, evaluation, and LLM readiness (0–1 scale, standardized).
  • Spatial autocorrelation — Global Moran's I: computed on hospital coordinates and AI implementation scores using k=6 nearest neighbors to assess whether adoption is spatially clustered rather than randomly distributed.
  • DBSCAN density-based clustering: adaptive DBSCAN (ε=0.35, density-upweighted AI scores) identified 434 clusters across 3,328 hospitals; used to find geographically proximate hospitals with similar adoption levels for targeted intervention design.
  • Getis–Ord Gi* hotspot analysis: identified statistically significant hotspots (high adoption) and coldspots (low adoption) at both hospital level and HSA level; corrected for multiple testing using Benjamini–Hochberg FDR and Storey's q-value.
  • Random forest + SHAP feature importance: random forest regression (100 trees, 5-fold CV, R²=0.58) on hospital-level and geospatial features; SHAP values used to rank and directionally interpret predictors of predictive AI adoption.
  • Geographically weighted regression (GWR): Gaussian kernel, bandwidth ≈222 km radius at mid-US latitudes; spatially varying coefficients mapped to reveal regional heterogeneity in the predictors of AI implementation.
  • Cross-tabulation with implementation ratios (IRs): IR <1.0 indicates lower AI adoption in high-need areas vs. low-need areas; used to quantify the equity gap across HPSA, MUA, ADI, and SVI dimensions.
  • Exploratory longitudinal analysis: mixed-effects models on 15 quarterly CMS quality time points (2022–2025) to explore associations between AI adoption status and quality metric trajectories; treated as hypothesis-generating only.

① Extend the paper's GWR to a spatiotemporal accessibility model of AI-enabled care

Extending this paperThe paper's GWR is cross-sectional (2023–2024) and focused on predicting AI adoption rather than its downstream care effects. A direct extension: replace the static kernel with a spatiotemporal GWR that tracks how AI adoption and its quality correlates evolve together across the 15 quarterly CMS time points already assembled. This would convert the paper's "baseline snapshot" into a trajectory model — showing whether coldspot regions are catching up or falling further behind, and whether interoperability's coefficient strengthens as early-adopter advantages compound. The paper already notes that its longitudinal analysis is "exploratory"; formalizing it with a dynamic spatial panel model is a natural published next step.

② Map the paper's IR gap onto a 2SFCA-style healthcare-access surface

Extending this paperThe paper's implementation ratio (IR) captures the probability of AI adoption conditional on community need, but it treats all hospitals within a need-tier as substitutable. A more spatially precise extension: apply a two-step floating catchment area (2SFCA) framework to compute each community's effective exposure to AI-capable hospitals, weighted by travel time and supply capacity. This would convert the paper's county/HSA binary designations into a continuous AI-accessibility surface — revealing which communities physically cannot reach an AI-adopting hospital within clinically meaningful travel times, not just which counties have lower proportions. The paper's own Dartmouth Atlas HSA crosswalk is already the structural input needed for this extension.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my 2SFCA/R2SFCA toolkit to a new supply type — AI-capable hospitals as the "facility" and provider shortage areas as the demand layer. It would generate the first AI-accessibility surface for the USA, extending my prior work on primary-care and mental-health service areas into the technological readiness dimension and producing a novel GeoAI health-equity metric.

③ Reframe the paper's interoperability predictor as a data-quality mediator for clinical AI fairness

Extending this paperThe paper finds that the core index (interoperability) is the most consistent GWR predictor of AI adoption but explicitly notes that this may partly reflect EHR vendor dominance rather than genuine institutional readiness. A deeper extension: treat low interoperability as a proxy for EHR data sparsity and bias and test whether hospitals in coldspot regions — which also tend to serve minority and low-income populations — produce more biased AI predictions when their data are used to train multi-site models. This bridges the paper's adoption geography to the clinical AI fairness literature, asking not just "where is AI deployed?" but "how does the digital divide corrupt the models that are deployed?"

It also informs the responsible use of mobile-phone and social-media proxies in health geography — sources that similarly skew toward high-connectivity populations.

④ Use mobile-phone mobility data to validate the paper's hospital-service-area demand estimates

Extending this paperThe paper relies on the Dartmouth Atlas HSA boundaries — administrative regions last substantially revised in 1999 — as its unit for spatial aggregation and hotspot visualization. A methodological extension: replace or validate these boundaries using observed patient-flow data from mobile-phone GPS traces or insurance claims, producing dynamically delineated service areas that reflect current travel behavior. Given the paper's finding that rural areas and high-need communities show the sharpest AI coldspots, administrative HSA boundaries may systematically underestimate the true catchment area that patients in those regions traverse to reach any hospital at all — biasing the IR calculation downward for the neediest areas.

Added value to my researchThis is directly relevant to my work on mobile-phone data for health geography and multi-source data fusion. Deriving hospital catchment areas from GPS trajectories rather than administrative boundaries — using spatiotemporal kernel density estimation over observed hospital-visit flows — would make the paper's equity metrics more accurate and provide a transferable methodology for any facility-access study using administrative geographic units.

⑤ Apply fairness-aware ML to the paper's random forest to design equitable AI deployment policies

Extending this paperThe paper's random forest (R²=0.58) predicts which hospitals will adopt AI but is agnostic to equity: its optimal policy recommendations would simply reinforce deployment toward already-advantaged (high interoperability, large bed count, system-affiliated) hospitals. Extending it: reframe the classification task as a constrained optimization with fairness constraints — specifically, requiring that predicted AI deployment probabilities for high-HPSA and high-ADI hospitals meet a minimum parity threshold relative to low-need hospitals. The paper's own IR framework provides the parity metric; deep reinforcement learning or fairness-constrained gradient boosting can find the policy that maximizes coverage while satisfying the constraint.

Added value to my researchThis generalizes my EV-charging efficiency-vs-equity optimization to a healthcare AI deployment setting — with the paper's IR targets as the equity constraint and the SHAP-identified predictors as policy levers. The approach also aligns with my GeoAI agenda: training a spatially aware deployment model that explicitly encodes the geographic cost of inequity, making the "where to invest next" decision transparent and auditable.
