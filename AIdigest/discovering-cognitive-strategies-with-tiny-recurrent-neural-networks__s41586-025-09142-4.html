Discovering cognitive strategies with tiny recurrent neural networks

Li, Ji-An · Benna, Marcus K. · Mattar, Marcelo G. Published 2025-07-02 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09142-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Recurrent neural networks with as few as 1–4 units consistently outperform classical cognitive models (Bayesian inference, model-free and model-based reinforcement learning) in predicting individual animal and human decisions across six reward-learning tasks — while remaining interpretable via dynamical systems analysis. A knowledge-distillation approach further enables accurate individual modelling even with as few as 350 trials per participant, opening the framework to human cognitive neuroscience and computational psychiatry. This "tiny RNN" strategy offers a principled, data-driven route to discovering new cognitive strategies that handcrafted models systematically miss.

Why It Matters

  • Bridges flexibility and interpretability. Prior work forced a choice between flexible large networks (hard to interpret) and sparse cognitive models (easy to interpret but often wrong). Tiny RNNs achieve both at once — they outperform classical models yet are fully analysable as discrete dynamical systems.
  • Discovers genuinely new cognitive strategies. Phase-portrait analysis reveals mechanisms absent from decades of cognitive-model literature: state-dependent learning rates, reward-induced indifference, and novel forms of value updating — validated by targeted hypothesis testing.
  • Solves the data-scarcity problem for human studies. Knowledge distillation from a multi-subject teacher network allows tiny student RNNs to surpass cognitive models with only a few hundred trials per person, matching the scale of typical human experiments.
  • Applicable to both biological and artificial agents. The same framework dissects the decision strategy of meta-RL agents, showing they behave more like Bayesian inference than model-based RL — a finding that corrects a prevailing interpretation in the meta-RL literature.
  • Open science and reproducibility. All six datasets and the complete codebase (github.com/jil095/tinyRNN) are publicly available, making the pipeline immediately adoptable.

Core Methods

  • Gated recurrent units (GRU) with 1–4 hidden units fitted to individual-subject choice sequences by maximum-likelihood (cross-entropy minimization).
  • Nested 10-fold cross-validation: separates training, validation (hyperparameter tuning), and held-out test folds on consecutive trial blocks to prevent temporal leakage and avoid AIC/BIC bias.
  • Knowledge distillation: a large multi-subject teacher network (with subject-ID embeddings) is first trained on all subjects; tiny student RNNs are then distilled per individual, enabling accurate fitting from as few as ~350 trials per participant.
  • Phase-portrait analysis (dynamical systems): visualizes how action-preference logits evolve as a function of inputs and current state, identifying fixed points, attractors, and novel behavioural signatures in 1-D models.
  • Vector field analysis: for d = 2 models, two-dimensional vector fields reveal trial-by-trial state updates and attractor geometry (e.g., drift-to-the-other patterns) not captured by classical models.
  • Dynamical regression: approximates one-step state dynamics as a linear function of the current state to summarize higher-dimensional (d > 2) model behaviour at the participant level.
  • Preference setpoint analysis: computes normalized asymptotic preferences after repeated exposure to each input type — summarizing the directional effect of rewards and actions in a single interpretable quantity.

① Transfer tiny-RNN cognitive discovery to interpretable spatial decision-making agents

Extending this paperThe paper fits tiny GRU networks to individual subjects in closed-loop reward-learning tasks. A direct extension: apply the same framework to sequential spatial decision problems — e.g., evacuation routing, neighborhood exploration, or facility choice — where human agents cycle through location-observation-reward loops analogous to the reversal tasks here. The phase-portrait interpretive toolkit would then expose spatial cognitive strategies (anchor bias, recency weighting of perceived safety) that handcrafted agent-based models routinely miss.

Added value to my researchMy agent-based models of urban health behavior (e.g., telehealth adoption, facility-seeking) currently impose fixed utility functions. The tinyRNN framework would let me discover the actual update rules individuals use from observed mobility or facility-choice sequences — providing data-driven, interpretable ABM parameters rather than assumed ones, directly improving model validity for public-health policy.

② Replace the paper's nested cross-validation with spatial cross-validation for geo-behavioral data

Extending this paperThe paper's nested 10-fold cross-validation splits on consecutive trials — appropriate for stationary laboratory tasks. Extending it to naturalistic geo-behavioral sequences (GPS traces, POI visit logs) requires spatiotemporal cross-validation that respects both temporal autocorrelation and spatial clustering, so that the distilled student RNN does not exploit local spatial regularities as shortcuts. This extension would also benchmark tiny RNNs against established spatiotemporal sequence models.

Added value to my researchSpatial cross-validation is a persistent methodological gap in my explainable-ML pipelines for GWR and MGWR. Formalizing it within the tinyRNN framework creates a unified evaluation protocol I can apply across healthcare-accessibility prediction, housing-price modeling, and spatiotemporal KDE of disease incidence — directly improving reproducibility and generalizability.

③ Apply knowledge distillation to heterogeneous multi-source geographic data fusion

Extending this paperThe paper's knowledge-distillation solution to data scarcity — a large teacher trained on pooled multi-subject data, then small students per individual — maps cleanly onto a problem in geodata fusion: training a global teacher on dense, high-quality data (e.g., multi-source mobile phone + social media + remote sensing) and distilling city-specific or region-specific student models where any single source is sparse. The tinyRNN's teacher-student architecture would provide a principled, interpretable alternative to ad-hoc imputation.

Added value to my researchFor my multi-source data fusion work (mobile phone records, social media, remote sensing), a teacher-student distillation scheme offers a theoretically grounded way to handle missing modalities in different cities or time periods — superior to standard imputation and directly applicable to fine-tuning geospatial foundation models on sparse local datasets.

An extension: apply the same attractor/fixed-point analysis to recurrent spatial prediction networks (e.g., spatiotemporal GNNs predicting health outcomes over time) to detect whether the network has learned spurious spatial attractors (e.g., persistent over-prediction in deprived areas regardless of true covariates), enabling systematic bias auditing beyond feature-importance explanations.
