Artificial light at night outweighs temperature in lengthening urban growing seasons

Lvlv Wang · Lin Meng · Andrew D. Richardson · Franz Hölker · Huidong Li · Jiafu Mao · Travis Longcore · Jun Xia · Dunxian She Published 2025-06-16 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00258-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 428 Northern Hemisphere cities (2014–2020), artificial light at night (ALAN) increases exponentially toward urban centers and exerts a stronger influence than air temperature in lengthening the urban growing season — especially by delaying its end (EOS). The growing season is on average ~6.4 days earlier in spring and ~7.1 days later in autumn in urban versus rural areas; ALAN's effect on EOS (R≈0.49) significantly outweighs temperature's (R≈0.003) in all seven climate zones, though the balance of effects varies with climate and latitude. ALAN is therefore a critical, previously underestimated driver of urban vegetation dynamics.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reassigns blame for longer urban growing seasons. Decades of work attributed earlier spring and later autumn in cities to the urban heat island; this paper shows light, not just heat, can be the dominant driver — particularly for the end of season.
  • Disentangles two confounded urban stressors. ALAN and warming both rise from rural to urban, so they are hard to separate; a buffer-gradient design plus partial correlation isolates each effect across hundreds of cities.
  • Quantifies the spatial structure of light pollution. ALAN rises exponentially (logistic in impervious fraction) while temperature rises concave-quadratically, implying suburb-to-core phenology is light-driven while rural-to-suburb is heat-driven.
  • Direct policy hook for lighting design. Findings argue for ecology-aware urban lighting (spectrum, intensity, timing) to limit frost-damage risk, pollen-allergy onset and disrupted plant–pollinator timing while keeping streets functional.
  • Climate-change relevance. Longer growing seasons feed back on carbon, water and energy budgets, so mis-attributing the cause biases projections of urban vegetation under continued urbanization and warming.

Core Methods

  • Rural–urban buffer gradient: each city is split into ten buffers by impervious-surface index β (Δβ=0.1, kernel-smoothed from GAIA), buffer 1 = outermost rural, buffer 10 = urban core; rural areas are expanded outward (Dex formula) to balance rural/urban sample size.
  • Nonlinear curve fitting: SOS/EOS and Ta fit as quadratic polynomials in β; ALAN fit as a logistic growth curve — chosen from five candidates by highest R², lowest bias/RMSE.
  • Urban–rural difference: ΔI = mean(buffers 6–10) − mean(buffers 1–5) for SOS, EOS, ALAN and Ta, with alternative buffer cut-offs for robustness.
  • Partial correlation analysis: isolates ALAN vs Ta effects on SOS/EOS while controlling the other; an ALAN×Ta interaction term and Tmax/Tmin tests confirm robustness.
  • Dominance & significance: the larger absolute partial coefficient picks the dominant driver per city; Fisher Z-transformation tests whether ALAN and Ta coefficients differ.
  • Climate & latitude stratification: partial coefficients grouped by Köppen zones (≥10 cities) and 3° latitude bins; two-sided t-tests compare RALAN vs RTa.
  • Independent validation: 30-m urban phenology over 62 USA cities, with developed-open-space (lawn/grass) excluded to rule out a land-cover artifact.

① Replace partial correlation with explainable ML to capture ALAN×Ta nonlinearity

Extending this paperThe attribution rests on partial correlation plus a single interaction term — a linear-in-effects view of two confounded, collinear drivers. Recasting it as an explainable gradient-boosting / SHAP framework over the per-buffer panel (β, ALAN, Ta, latitude, Köppen zone, land cover) would let ALAN and temperature interact nonlinearly and reveal threshold and saturation behavior the logistic/quadratic fits cannot, sharpening the claim that "ALAN outweighs temperature" with feature-attribution evidence rather than coefficient comparison.

② Add the spectral dimension the paper flags but cannot measure

Extending this paperThe authors explicitly note that VIIRS DNB sees only 0.5–0.9 µm panchromatic light and cannot resolve the blue-rich spectrum of LEDs that plant photoreceptors (cryptochrome, phytochrome) respond to. Extending it: fuse VIIRS with finer multispectral nighttime imagery (e.g., ISS/JL1-style RGB night lights) to build a wavelength-resolved ALAN exposure and test whether blue-shifted lighting amplifies EOS delay — turning a stated limitation into a hypothesis test.

Added value to my researchWavelength-specific night-light fusion is a clean multi-source data-fusion problem and connects to my interest in how nocturnal light affects human outcomes (sleep, mental health) along the same urban gradients — the same exposure surface, two response systems.

③ Turn the buffer gradient into a physics-informed graph over the city network

Extending this paperThe ten-buffer scheme collapses each city to a 1-D radial profile, discarding the polycentric, network structure of light and impervious surfaces (the paper itself flags multi-subcenter cities). Extending it: represent each city as a spatial graph of cells and learn a physics-informed graph operator that maps (impervious field, ALAN, Ta) → SOS/EOS, with the paper's logistic ALAN–β and quadratic Ta–β relations encoded as soft constraints so it generalizes to unseen city morphologies.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my physics-informed graph learning line: baking known spatial response laws into a GNN yields a transferable urban-environment operator, the same machinery I want for accessibility and exposure surfaces that must extrapolate across cities.
