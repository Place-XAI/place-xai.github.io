Learning to be uncertain before learning from data

Takuya Isomura Published 2026-04-09 DOI 10.1038/s42256-026-01205-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Neural networks are structurally overconfident at random initialization — before any data are observed, weight symmetry alone produces spuriously high predictive confidence. A brief "noise warm-up" phase — training on random inputs paired with random labels — collapses this initial overconfidence to chance level, yielding models that are better calibrated, more reliable at flagging out-of-distribution inputs, and thus more trustworthy in deployment. The insight reframes miscalibration as a property of initial conditions, not just training data.

Why It Matters

  • Identifies a previously overlooked root cause of miscalibration. Overconfidence has been attributed to overfitting or distribution shift; this paper shows it begins at random initialization — before a single real data point is seen — demanding a new intervention point.
  • Offers a simple, cost-free fix. Noise warm-up requires no architectural changes, no extra real data, and only a brief additional training pass — its cost is negligible relative to standard pre-training or fine-tuning pipelines.
  • Directly improves out-of-distribution (OOD) detection. Miscalibrated confidence on OOD inputs is a safety-critical failure mode; by anchoring the initial epistemic state toward maximum uncertainty, the intervention makes anomaly detection structurally more reliable.
  • Bridges neuroscience and machine learning. The noise warm-up mechanism has an analogue in biological neural development: spontaneous activity in the developing brain before sensory experience may serve a similar epistemic-prior-setting role.

Core Methods

  • Noise warm-up pre-conditioning: before exposure to any real training data, briefly train the network on pairs of random inputs and random labels. This enforces maximum-entropy output distributions as the network's starting epistemic state.
  • Confidence analysis before and after warm-up: inspect the predictive confidence distribution at three stages — (a) after random initialization, (b) after noise warm-up, and (c) after real-data training — for both in-distribution and out-of-distribution inputs.
  • Calibration measurement: compare predicted confidence against accuracy for the 4 vs. 9 classification task to demonstrate overconfidence collapse and improved Expected Calibration Error (ECE).
  • OOD detection evaluation: feed inputs outside the training distribution to the conventionally initialized vs. noise-warmed networks and compare the confidence scores — demonstrating that noise warm-up suppresses spuriously high OOD confidence.
  • Conceptual framing via information theory: the paper interprets warm-up as encoding a maximum-entropy prior into the network's weights, connecting to free-energy / Bayesian brain frameworks and the neuroscience of spontaneous pre-sensory activity.

② Use the noise warm-up prior as an uncertainty-aware active learning trigger for spatial data collection

Extending this paperThe paper establishes that noise warm-up shapes the network's initial epistemic state toward maximum entropy. A natural extension is to combine this calibrated prior with an active learning loop: at each training iteration, query the noise-warmed model's predictive entropy to select which spatial locations to label next, rather than using a fixed random sample. Because the warm-up guarantees the initial uncertainty is meaningfully distributed rather than artificially compressed, the active learning signal starts reliable from the very first query — bypassing the cold-start calibration failure that plagues standard active learning on neural networks.
