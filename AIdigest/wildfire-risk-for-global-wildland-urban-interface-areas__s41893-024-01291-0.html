Wildfire risk for global wildland–urban interface areas

Bin Chen · Shengbiao Wu · Yufang Jin · Yimeng Song · Chao Wu · Sergey Venevsky · Bing Xu · Chris Webster · Peng Gong Published 2024-02-26 DOI 10.1038/s41893-024-01291-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The authors produced the first global wildland–urban interface (WUI) dataset at fine resolution for both a 2020 baseline and a 1985–2020 time series, estimating total global WUI area at 6.62 million km². Between 1985 and 2020, global WUI areas expanded by 12.56%. Overlaying burned-area and population data revealed that 7.07% of WUI areas (housing ~4.47 million people) lie within 2,400 m of wildfire threat — with African countries facing disproportionately higher risk than wealthier nations with the largest WUI footprints.

★

Why It Matters

  • First globally consistent, fine-resolution WUI dataset. Prior WUI maps were confined to individual countries or regions; this work, integrating 30 m building footprints and 10 m land cover, enables reliable cross-national comparison for the first time.
  • Quantifies the human cost of WUI expansion. At least 4.47 million people live within 2,400 m of wildfire threat zones in WUI areas globally — and 10.11 million within 4,800 m — transforming an abstract hazard into a concrete population-risk metric.
  • Reveals an equity gap in wildfire risk. Although the United States, Brazil, China, India and Australia have the largest WUI extents, African countries experience far higher wildfire risk rates within their WUI areas — a global inequality story with direct policy implications.
  • Enables historical trend analysis. The 35-year time series (1985–2020 at 5-year intervals) enables study of both the biophysical and socio-economic drivers of WUI change and future fire-risk trajectory modeling.
  • Directly actionable for insurance, land-use planning, and community resilience. Spatially explicit WUI risk maps can guide fuel-treatment priority, evacuation planning, and the emerging challenge of wildfire insurance regulation.

Core Methods

  • WUI definition (US Federal Register): two types — intermix WUI (housing density >1 house/160,000 m² and >50% wildland vegetation) and interface WUI (housing >1 house/40 acres, <50% vegetation, but within 2.4 km of a dense vegetation patch ≥5 km²). The 400 m grid is the minimum mapping unit.
  • Multi-source building footprint fusion: Meta HRPD 30 m (primary) aggregated to 400 m housing density grids; WSF 10 m used for 15 countries without HRPD coverage; both aggregated consistently to 400 m.
  • Wildland vegetation extraction: forest, shrub, and grassland from ESA WorldCover; urban green space and oil palm plantations removed using separate thematic masks.
  • Time-series WUI mapping: same methodology applied to GLC-FCS (30 m) + GHSL (100 m) at five-year intervals from 1985 to 2020; 2020 baseline used as benchmark (R² = 0.78 vs. time-series product).
  • Wildfire risk estimation: MODIS MCD64A1 annual burned area maps; 2,400 m and 4,800 m outside buffer zones generated to represent ember-spread risk; intersected with WUI footprints.
  • Population exposure: WorldPop 100 m grid intersected with WUI and wildfire buffer layers to estimate exposed population under three scenarios: direct burn, 2,400 m buffer, and 4,800 m buffer.
  • Validation: state-level linear regression against SILVIS Lab US WUI (R² = 0.72) and global 10 m WUI (R² = 0.90); continental and GFED-region aggregations used for trend analysis.

① Replace static buffer-zone risk with a structure-level spatiotemporal fire-spread model

Extending this paperThe paper estimates wildfire risk to WUI areas using fixed 2,400 m and 4,800 m buffer zones around historical burned areas — a practical but coarse approximation that ignores wind direction, slope, fuel continuity, and ember transport physics. A direct extension would integrate a spatially explicit fire-spread simulation (e.g., FlamMap or cellular-automaton fire models) calibrated to local vegetation density and topography, replacing the isotropic buffers with directional, probabilistic risk footprints at the structure level. Combined with the paper's fine-resolution housing footprint, this would produce per-parcel risk scores that vary by season and weather scenario — far more useful for evacuation planning and insurance pricing than a continent-level average.

Added value to my researchThis is directly applicable to my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and agent-based modeling work: treating ember ignition as a spatial point process, I can model structure-to-structure fire spread as a contagion network — the same SIR-style framework I apply to disease transmission — and estimate marginal risk reduction from fuel treatments in urban fringe areas.

② Fuse the global WUI map with socioeconomic vulnerability layers to produce a compounded-risk index

Extending this paperThe paper exposes a critical equity gap — African WUI areas burn at far higher rates than wealthy-nation WUI areas — but the analysis stops at physical exposure (burned area + population count). A compelling extension would overlay the WUI wildfire-risk map with socioeconomic vulnerability indicators (income, housing quality, healthcare access, informal-settlement density) to produce a compound vulnerability-exposure index. This would reveal which WUI populations are both physically exposed and least able to evacuate, recover, or access medical care after wildfire smoke events — directly linking the paper's global hazard layer to development equity questions.

Added value to my researchThis matches my health geography and accessibility research: wildfires displace populations and cause respiratory hospitalization; mapping WUI risk against healthcare facility access (2SFCA) would identify communities where wildfire smoke burden is highest and emergency care is simultaneously farthest away — a compound vulnerability index I can operationalize using my existing spatial accessibility methods.

③ Develop an explainable ML model to predict future WUI expansion trajectories under SSP scenarios

Extending this paperThe paper documents 35 years of WUI growth but does not project forward. The logical next step is to train a spatially explicit prediction model using the paper's 1985–2020 time series together with socioeconomic (SSP population pathways), climate (CMIP6 land-use scenarios), and infrastructure (road network expansion) drivers. An explainable ML approach — using SHAP values applied to a gradient-boosted spatial model — would identify which drivers (population growth, temperature anomalies, road access) are most responsible for WUI expansion in each world region, and project 2050 WUI maps at the 400 m scale under SSP1–SSP5.

Added value to my researchThis sits squarely in my explainable machine learning for spatial prediction agenda. The WUI expansion problem is structurally identical to urban sprawl prediction: land-use change driven by population, accessibility, and biophysical gradients. Applying my MGWR/GWR + SHAP pipeline to WUI change detection would reveal spatially varying driver importance — for example, whether road expansion or population growth dominates WUI growth in the African context vs. North America — and produce locally calibrated forecasts.

④ Build a governance typology for WUI management using the Pyro-Socio-Ecological Zone framework

Extending this paperThe paper uses a single universal WUI definition (US Federal Register) applied globally — an acknowledged limitation because fire suppression capacity, land tenure, and governance regimes vary enormously across countries. A direct extension is to overlay the paper's WUI hazard maps with governance and socio-ecological zone classifications, producing a typology of WUI management contexts (e.g., "high-risk, low-governance capacity" vs. "high-risk, well-funded suppression") that can inform differentiated international policy recommendations. The recently proposed Pyro-Socio-Ecological Zone (PSEZ) framework provides exactly this governance layer.

Added value to my researchThis connects to my interest in multi-source data fusion and urban policy: integrating the paper's biophysical WUI map with governance-quality indicators and fire-management expenditure data would enable me to apply spatial network community detection to identify clusters of countries sharing similar risk profiles and institutional capacity — guiding which policy packages are transferable across borders.

⑤ Harmonize competing global urban land datasets to reduce WUI mapping uncertainty

Extending this paperThe paper uses Meta HRPD and WSF as its building-footprint inputs — two of many competing global human settlement products that show substantial disagreement at the urban fringe, precisely where WUI is delineated. A sensitivity analysis extending this paper would quantify how much WUI area estimates change across different settlement products (GHSL, GUF, WorldCover, GlobeLand30, WSF) — essentially a product-ensemble approach analogous to multi-model climate ensembles. By computing the inter-product spread on WUI area at the country level, the extension would produce calibrated uncertainty bounds on the paper's 6.62 million km² headline figure and identify regions where investment in better settlement mapping is most urgently needed for fire-risk assessment.

Added value to my researchThis feeds directly into my open science / FAIR data and multi-source data fusion agenda. I frequently need to choose among global gridded datasets (population, land cover, building footprint) for accessibility and health analyses; a principled uncertainty quantification pipeline — applied here to WUI but generalizable — would serve as a reusable methodological contribution for any study that combines heterogeneous global rasters to delineate urban–rural gradients.
