AI-generated visuals of car-free US cities help improve support for sustainable policies

Rachit Dubey · Mathew D. Hardy · Thomas L. Griffiths · Rahul Bhui Published 2024-02-23 DOI 10.1038/s41893-024-01299-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Showing Americans photorealistic AI-generated images of car-free, pedestrian-friendly versions of familiar US streets — created using DALL-E 2 and Stable Diffusion — significantly increases support for a hypothetical sustainable transport bill across two large-scale survey studies (N = 3,129). The effect is especially pronounced among Republican participants, with 32.5% willing to sign a petition in the AI-imagination condition versus only 17.6% in the baseline — suggesting that evoking the imagination of positive outcomes, rather than changing climate beliefs, is a viable cross-partisan strategy for building support for green policies.

★

Why It Matters

  • Crosses the partisan divide without fighting over climate beliefs. Instead of attempting the near-impossible task of shifting deeply held views on climate change, this approach works by helping people imagine improved daily life — a mechanism that succeeds across party lines.
  • Demonstrates a new role for generative AI in civic communication. DALL-E 2 and Stable Diffusion are used not for creative entertainment but as policy communication tools, producing personalized, photorealistic depictions of hypothetical futures at scale.
  • Provides causal evidence, not just correlation. The randomized controlled design (three-arm RCT across two pre-registered studies) establishes that the AI-imagination treatment — not mere visual saliency — drives the increase in support.
  • Open data and replication. Anonymized participant data and analysis code are fully public at github.com/rachit-dubey/car-free-america, enabling immediate replication and extension to other policy domains.

Core Methods

  • Randomized controlled experiment (three-arm RCT): participants assigned to (1) baseline (text only), (2) visual control (simple cartoonized illustrations), or (3) AI imagination (photorealistic DALL-E 2 / Stable Diffusion inpainting). The three-arm design isolates the imagination-evoking component from generic visual salience.
  • Pre-registered replication: Study 2 replicates Study 1 with a distinct image set (Stable Diffusion instead of DALL-E 2) and adds two mechanistic measures — self-rated imagination vividness and perceived life improvement — to test the mediation pathway.
  • Outcome measures: 10-point Likert policy support scale; binary petition willingness (yes/no). Effect sizes estimated with two-sample t-tests and proportion z-tests.
  • Subgroup analysis by party affiliation: self-reported Democrat / Independent / Republican breakdown to assess partisan heterogeneity of treatment effects.
  • Image quality check: post-hoc comparison of colourfulness scores between visual-control and AI-imagination images to rule out confounds from color/salience differences.
  • Statistical environment: Python 3.12.1; all code and data publicly released on GitHub.

① Geo-personalize the AI-generated visuals using real neighborhood context

Extending this paperThe paper uses a single set of US street scenes for all participants, regardless of where they actually live. A direct extension: use each participant's reported ZIP code or city to select the closest real street from Google Street View and inpaint it with generative AI, making the imagined future literally their own neighborhood. The paper's own mechanism — imagination of improved personal life — predicts that geographic self-relevance would amplify the effect further, and this could be tested in a 2×3 design crossing local vs. generic imagery with the three conditions. Linking to corpus work on predicting urban mobility flows from satellite imagery, the street selection could also be guided by a spatial model of pedestrian potential, prioritizing streets where car-free redesigns are most spatially plausible.

Added value to my researchThis is directly relevant to my work on spatial accessibility and healthy-city metrics: GeoAI-generated neighborhood visualizations could serve as a participatory communication layer for accessibility planning — showing residents not just scores but rendered futures of walkable streets near their home. My spatiotemporal kernel density pipeline could identify the highest-density pedestrian-demand corridors in a city to prioritize which streets are visualized, grounding the generative imagery in quantified spatial need.

② Decompose the imagination mechanism with a spatiotemporal panel design

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that AI-imagination treatment raises support at a single time point, and Study 2 adds one-shot mechanism measures (imagination vividness, perceived life improvement). What is missing is a longitudinal tracking arm: does the support increase persist after one week, one month? Does it translate into actual civic behavior (contacting representatives, voting)? An extension would re-contact the same Prolific panel at 30 and 90 days, adding a behavioral outcome — whether participants signed any real transport petitions or attended public hearings. The paper's open dataset (GitHub) makes within-subject extensions directly feasible.

Added value to my researchMy research on telehealth and digital divide faces the same gap: survey-measured willingness to adopt digital health services does not always predict actual uptake. This paper's framework — evoking imagination of a positive future use-case as a demand lever — could be adapted as a communication design principle for digital-health outreach, especially in underserved communities where physical travel to healthcare is the dominant alternative framing.

Cross-ref (corpus): Understanding and combatting misinformation across 16 countries on six continents — 10.1038/s41562-023-01641-6

③ Quantify the emission and health co-benefits of the AI-imagined car-free scenarios

Extending this paperThe paper changes attitudes toward a transport bill but never quantifies what the bill's actual implementation would yield in terms of CO₂ reductions, energy savings, or health outcomes. A natural extension: use the paper's three bill proposals (more public transit investment, better sidewalks, dedicated bus/bike lanes) as the policy inputs to a transport-emission model, estimating cumulative city-level emission savings. These concrete numbers — "this bill would reduce Austin's transport emissions by X% over 20 years" — could then be combined with the AI visuals in a richer treatment arm to test whether quantified co-benefits amplify the imagination effect beyond visuals alone.

Added value to my researchMy interest in urban health policy and accessibility equity directly maps here: the emission-reduction quantification could be linked to a spatial health-impact layer using my MGWR or 2SFCA toolkit, estimating which neighborhoods — by income, race, or current transit access — gain the most health benefit from the policy. This turns the paper's persuasion study into a platform for equity-weighted cost-benefit analysis of sustainable transport bills.

④ Extend the cross-partisan framing to multi-city transport policy portfolios

Extending this paperThe paper tests a single hypothetical federal bill framed as ambitious and polarizing. An extension would adapt the paradigm to city-level policy portfolios — showing participants AI-generated visuals of their own city's streets under each of several documented policy mixes (congestion pricing, bus rapid transit, protected bike lanes, car-free zones). Rather than a single binary vote, participants would allocate a fixed budget across the portfolio, revealing preference ordering under visual imagination treatment. This connects the paper's mechanism to the real menu of policy options that cities actually choose from, making the persuasion design immediately deployable in public consultation processes.

Added value to my researchMy EV charging station efficiency-vs-equity optimization work faces a directly analogous public-acceptance challenge: communities near proposed EV infrastructure often resist siting decisions. Adapting the AI-imagination paradigm — showing residents photorealistic images of the proposed site with and without the charging hub — could be embedded in my facility-location framework as a stated-preference demand elicitation component, ensuring the optimizer accounts for community acceptance, not just spatial coverage.

⑤ Build an explainable spatial model of where AI-imagination persuasion works best

Extending this paperThe paper shows that treatment effects differ by party affiliation but does not investigate whether effects vary by geography: do participants from auto-dependent suburban areas respond differently from those in dense urban cores? Using participants' ZIP-code data (collectible via Prolific) linked to urban-form indicators (walkability index, transit access score, car-ownership rate), a geographically weighted regression of treatment-effect size on neighborhood context would reveal whether the imagination intervention is most potent precisely where car-free futures feel most distant — or conversely, where people can already visualize partial transitions. This would produce a spatial targeting map for policy communicators.

Added value to my researchThis directly applies my MGWR / GWR and explainable machine learning toolkit to a behavioral-science question. More broadly, it demonstrates a general method: using spatial non-stationarity analysis to explain heterogeneous treatment effects in survey experiments — a methodological bridge between my GeoAI methods and the large causal-inference literature that typically ignores space. The resulting spatial targeting output could feed into my urban-analytics work, identifying which neighborhoods need not just better transit but better vision communication of what transit can look like.
