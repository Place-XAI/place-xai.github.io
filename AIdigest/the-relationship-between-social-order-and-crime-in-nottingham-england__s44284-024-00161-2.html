The relationship between social order and crime in Nottingham, England

Federico Varese · Fanqi Zeng Published 2024-11-01 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00161-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

An entrenched, governance-type organized crime group in Nottingham's Bestwood ward appears to suppress ordinary crime: across all eight police-labeled crime types, Bestwood's crime counts per population density are significantly lower than in its most-similar comparison ward, Bulwell (Welch's t-tests, P<0.05–0.01). Crucially, this criminal governance emerges in a white, British-born, non-immigrant, less affluent community within a state of high policing capacity — overturning the assumption that such governance is confined to the global south, mafia territories, or ethnic enclaves.

Core Methods

  • Mixed-methods triangulation: qualitative interviews with local officials are cross-validated against the quantitative phone-call dataset to identify Bestwood as a governance-type organized-crime site.
  • Correspondence analysis (CA): a biplot relates the 20 wards (blue dots) to the 8 crime types (red squares) in 2D; the first two dimensions explain 82.9% of inertia, revealing ward–crime associations (Fig. 2).
  • "Most-similar wards" matching: Bestwood is paired with Bulwell, its closest analogue on socio-demographic and deprivation covariates, to isolate the governance effect.
  • Crime-rate normalization: crime counts per crime type are normalized by local population density before comparison.
  • Statistical testing & bootstrap: two-sided Welch's t-tests compare the matched wards across all 8 CTs (*** P<0.01, ** P<0.05), with bootstrap resampling for robustness (Supplementary).
  • Stack: Python 3.8.8 with geopandas, pandas, numpy, scipy, scikit-learn, Prince (CA), matplotlib, seaborn.

① Move from ward-level matching to event-level spatiotemporal modeling of governed territory

Extending this paperThe paper aggregates eight years of calls to ward-level counts and contrasts two wards with Welch's t-tests. But the call data are geo-referenced and time-stamped, so the same dataset could support an event-level spatiotemporal model (e.g., point-process / spatiotemporal KDE) that maps the boundary and intensity gradient of Bestwood's governed territory continuously, rather than collapsing it to a ward polygon. This would test whether crime suppression decays smoothly from a gang's core and whether enforcement/reporting bias contaminates the labeled crime types.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation line: the gang-territory boundary becomes a learnable surface, and the same event-level lens lets me separate true incidence from reporting/enforcement artifacts — a problem identical to bias correction in health-event surveillance.

Cross-ref (corpus): Event-level prediction of urban crime reveals a signature of enforcement bias in US cities — 10.1038/s41562-022-01372-0

② Replace single-pair matching with a principled spatial similarity / segregation metric

Extending this paperThe identification rests on Bestwood's single "most-similar" ward, Bulwell. Extending it: embed all 20 wards in a covariate-and-network similarity space and use a scale-free segregation/random-walk metric to build many synthetic controls (synthetic-control or k-nearest matched donors), turning a one-pair comparison into a robust counterfactual with confidence bands. This also quantifies how socially "isolated" the governed ward is — a structural correlate of criminal governance.

Added value to my researchThe same scale-invariant similarity machinery feeds my 2SFCA/R2SFCA catchment delineation and healthy-cities work: defining service areas and matched neighborhoods both reduce to "which units are genuinely comparable in space," and random-walk segregation indices give a defensible answer.

Cross-ref (corpus): Quantifying ethnic segregation in cities through random walks — 10.1038/s41467-022-33344-3

③ Make the causal claim explicit with an exogenous-shock natural experiment

Extending this paperThe paper shows a robust association (governed ward has lower crime) but cannot fully rule out reverse causality or confounding. Its 2012–2019 panel could be combined with an exogenous shock — e.g., a leadership arrest or a sudden mobility/policing change — to run a difference-in-differences design that tests whether removing the governing group raises ordinary crime, sharpening the causal story.

Added value to my researchThis quasi-experimental template is exactly what I use to evaluate policy interventions in health geography (telehealth roll-outs, facility openings): shock-based DiD on spatiotemporal panels lets me attribute changes in accessibility/outcomes to the intervention rather than to trend.

Cross-ref (corpus): A global analysis of the impact of COVID-19 stay-at-home restrictions on crime — 10.1038/s41562-021-01139-z

④ Reframe "criminal governance vs. state policing" as substitutable providers of social order

Extending this paperThe paper's striking premise is that a gang supplies order where a high-capacity state already polices. Extending it: model the governed ward and the state as competing/substituting providers of safety, and ask under what local conditions (deprivation, isolation, state legitimacy) one displaces the other — borrowing causal-inference designs that evaluate state policing's actual effect on crime and security.

Added value to my researchThis "competing-provider" logic maps onto my service-accessibility framing: residents draw "order" (or care) from whichever provider is most accessible/legitimate, exactly the supply-competition that R2SFCA encodes — here applied to safety provision instead of clinics.

⑤ Build an explainable spatial classifier that flags candidate "governed" neighborhoods

Extending this paperThe Bestwood/Bulwell contrast is hand-curated. Extending it to a screening tool: train an explainable spatial model on each ward's crime-type signature (the CA structure of Fig. 2), deprivation, and call patterns to predict a "criminal-governance likelihood," using SHAP/feature attribution to surface which crime-type anomalies (e.g., suppressed acquisitive crime alongside retained weapon possession) drive the flag — converting a case study into a transferable detector.
