Cost-effectiveness analysis of human–AI collaboration in disease screening

Yueye Wang · Xiaotong Han · Wenyi Hu · Keyao Zhou · Chi Liu · Jian Zhang · Lisa Zhuoting Zhu · Sanil Joseph · Qiuxia Yin · Lixia Luo · Mingguang He · Lei Zhang Published 2026-06-05 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00142-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 8 AI-based screening strategies in 270 human–AI collaboration scenarios modelled on a real-world diabetic retinopathy (DR) programme in China, a "copilot human–AI" strategy — where AI triages and human graders review the AI-filtered cases (AI·M + M2) — is the most cost-effective. An annual copilot strategy across all ages would save US$1.32 million while gaining 14.73 quality-adjusted life-years (QALYs) over a lifetime, a net monetary benefit of US$1.88 million per 100,000 population versus the status quo. The headline message: human involvement remains indispensable for maximizing both clinical benefit and cost-effectiveness in AI-driven medicine — fully autonomous AI is not the economic optimum.

Why It Matters

  • Reframes the AI deployment debate from "replace" to "collaborate." Most economic evaluations pit autonomous AI against human graders; this work systematically scores the combinations in between and shows the human-in-the-loop "copilot" wins on cost and health.
  • Puts a price on human judgement. By modelling 270 scenarios it quantifies exactly when AI alone, human alone, or a blend is optimal — and finds AI-only is dominated (higher cost, lower QALYs) in the reference setting.
  • Grounded in a real screening programme. Parameters come from an operational DR screening effort (Lifeline Express), not a hypothetical accuracy benchmark — the conclusions are deployment-relevant.
  • Actionable thresholds for policy. Sensitivity analysis pinpoints the tipping points (e.g., if AI specificity exceeds 96.9% AI-only becomes best; if secondary-grader specificity falls below 88.7% manual becomes best), giving health systems concrete decision rules.

Core Methods

  • Markov state-transition microsimulation: a DR-progression Markov model (no DR → non-VTDR → VTDR/DME → blindness/death, Extended Data Fig. 5) simulates lifetime costs and QALYs for a 100,000-person diabetic cohort.
  • Strategy space: 8 AI-based screening strategies (AI-only, AI as primary/secondary grader, "copilot" AI·M + M2, etc.) compared against manual annual screening as the status quo, spanning 270 human–AI collaboration scenarios.
  • AI threshold-filtering rule: a confidence-based filter with two tuned parameters (n1, n2) decides which AI-negative images go to human review (Extended Data Fig. 6); grid search over n1 ∈ 10–50%, n2 ∈ 0.1–0.3 selected n1 = 40%, n2 = 0.1.
  • Health-economic outputs: incremental cost-effectiveness ratios (ICERs), net monetary benefit, cost-effectiveness frontiers, and cost-effectiveness acceptability curves against WTP thresholds (1× / 3× per-capita GDP).
  • Uncertainty analysis: one-way tornado (top-5 influential parameters) and probabilistic sensitivity analysis (Monte Carlo scatter) to test robustness of the copilot conclusion across age groups and timeframes (5–30 years).

① Add a spatial-accessibility layer to the copilot economics

Extending this paperThe Markov model treats the screening cohort as spatially homogeneous: every patient faces the same access, the same grader pool, the same cost. But the copilot's value depends on where screening happens — rural sites with thin grader supply may benefit far more from AI triage than dense urban clinics. A natural extension: stratify the 270 scenarios by spatial accessibility tiers (travel time to graded review, local ophthalmologist density), turning one national ICER into a geography-conditioned cost-effectiveness surface.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct bridge to my 2SFCA/R2SFCA work: the "secondary grader" supply becomes a constrained resource with a catchment, and the copilot threshold (n1, n2) becomes a spatially varying dial that trades human-review capacity against access — a health-economics-aware accessibility model.

② Stress-test the copilot for algorithmic bias across subpopulations

Extending this paperThe paper's sensitivity analysis varies aggregate AI specificity/sensitivity but assumes one performance profile for everyone. If AI accuracy degrades for under-represented groups, the copilot's QALY gains and cost savings could be unevenly distributed — possibly negative for some. Extending it: re-run the cost-effectiveness frontier with subgroup-specific AI error rates and report an equity-adjusted net monetary benefit, not just a population mean.

Added value to my researchThis connects health-economics to my explainable-ML agenda: attribute which patient and image features drive the human-review trigger, and use causal fairness diagnostics to ensure a copilot deployed across heterogeneous regions does not amplify spatial health disparities.

③ Replace literature-fixed transition rates with digital-twin simulation

Extending this paperThe Markov transition probabilities, mortality, and utilities are fixed literature point estimates (Extended Data Table 3). A richer extension: drive the DR-progression model with patient-level digital twins that learn heterogeneous progression trajectories, so the copilot's value is evaluated against realistic disease-velocity distributions rather than a single average path.

Added value to my researchDigital-twin / agent-based simulation is already in my methods stack; coupling it with a spatiotemporal progression model lets me explore where and when to deploy screening capacity — an agent-based, spatially explicit complement to the aggregate Markov frame.

④ Generalize the "indispensable human" finding to other AI-assisted screening

Extending this paperThe copilot conclusion is shown for DR; its transferability is asserted, not tested. A high-value extension: replicate the 8-strategy × scenario sweep for other imaging-based screens (breast/lung cancer, blood-test-based eye screening) to find whether the copilot optimum is a general law or DR-specific, and what accuracy/cost regime flips it toward autonomous AI.

Added value to my researchThis gives me a reusable health-economic evaluation template for any spatial-deployment study I run on AI-driven public-health services — a standard way to ask "is the human still worth it here?" before recommending an allocation.

⑤ Co-optimize copilot thresholds for efficiency and equity of access

Extending this paperThe paper optimizes a single objective — cost-effectiveness — and finds one best threshold pair. But a system that maximizes mean net benefit may concentrate human review where it is cheapest, starving harder-to-reach populations. Extending it: cast threshold and frequency selection as a multi-objective (efficiency × equity) optimization, tracing the Pareto frontier of population NMB versus access-equity rather than picking a single point.
