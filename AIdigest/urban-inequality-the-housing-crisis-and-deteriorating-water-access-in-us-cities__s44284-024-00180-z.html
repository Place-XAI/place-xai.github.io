Urban inequality, the housing crisis and deteriorating water access in US cities

Katie Meehan · Jason R. Jurjevich · Lucy Everitt · Nicholas M. J. W. Chun · Justin Sherrill Published 2024-12-19 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00180-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Household running-water access in the United States has become a decidedly urban problem and is deteriorating since the 2008 financial crash. In 2021, >500,000 households (~1.1 million people) still lacked running water, and >71% of them lived in cities. "Plumbing poverty" rose in 12 of the 15 largest cities between 2013–2017 and 2017–2021 and falls disproportionately on households of color (e.g., 82% in Los Angeles, 79% in Miami). The authors argue a "reproductive squeeze" — unaffordable housing, stagnant wages and a cost-of-living crisis — is pushing low-income, racialized households into homes without running water, exemplified by Portland, Oregon (a ~56% rise since 2000).

Why It Matters

  • Reframes water poverty as an urban, high-income-country problem. The dominant narrative treats lack of running water as a rural or Global South issue; this paper shows the United States is experiencing the urbanization of water poverty, with an "urban pivot" past the 50% mark around 1990.
  • Connects two crises usually studied apart. It links the post-2008 housing/political-economy shock directly to water access, naming the "reproductive squeeze" as the mechanism — a bridge between urban economic geography and household water insecurity.
  • Quantifies a racialized burden. People of color are the majority of those without running water in 12 of the 15 largest cities, with measurable overrepresentation (racial disparity rate up to 26% in Philadelphia), even in growing, affluent cities.
  • Speaks to global development goals. Worsening access casts doubt on US progress toward SDG 6 (clean water and sanitation) and SDG 11 (sustainable cities), and exposes blind spots in how the Census measures plumbing and water shut-offs.

Core Methods

  • Longitudinal microdata analysis: harmonize 2000 Census (SF 3) and ACS five-year microdata to track households without running water from 1970–2021, decomposing rural vs urban shares to identify the 1990 "urban pivot."
  • Descriptive + comparative statistics: rank the top 15 cities by absolute counts (Table 1), compute pre/post-crisis percentage change (Fig. 3), and test statistical significance of city-level changes (all significant except Phoenix, San Francisco, Seattle).
  • Two-predictor comparative framework: position the 50 cities each year against the two strongest known predictors of insecure water access — a modified Palma income-inequality ratio (x) and racialized access (% non-White without water, y) — using a transformed location quotient for cross-city comparability (Fig. 4 bubble plots + animated video).
  • Income-decile profiling: map the distribution of waterless households across income deciles for the Portland metro (Fig. 5), justifying the 20% bottom threshold of the modified Palma ratio.
  • Spatial visualization: hexagonal tessellation with areal-weighted interpolation from county counts to reduce spatial bias in the national map (Fig. 1).
  • Conceptual framing: critical urban political economy + feminist social-reproduction theory, operationalized as the "reproductive squeeze," with Portland as an interpretive bellwether case study.

① Turn the "reproductive squeeze" narrative into a causal, explainable model

Extending this paperThe paper convincingly describes the housing→water mechanism but stops at descriptive statistics and a two-predictor bubble plot (Palma ratio × racialized access). A direct extension: build a panel / quasi-experimental model across the 50 MSAs treating the 2008 crash and COVID-era shut-off moratoriums as shocks, and add an explainable-ML attribution layer (e.g., SHAP over rents, eviction rates, corporate-landlord share, wages) to test which structural drivers actually move plumbing poverty — converting "the squeeze" from concept to quantified, ranked causes.

② Recast plumbing poverty as a spatial accessibility (2SFCA) problem

Extending this paperThe paper measures water access as a binary household attribute ("has running water?") drawn from Census microdata, ignoring the supply side — utility service territories, affordability, and distance to functioning infrastructure. Extending it: reframe water security as a two-step floating catchment (2SFCA/R2SFCA) problem where "supply" is reliable, affordable municipal provision and "demand" is the racialized low-income population, exposing service-area gaps that a yes/no metric hides.

Added value to my researchIt transplants my healthcare R2SFCA service-area delineation onto a new essential service (water), validating the method's portability and giving me a second domain to test catchment-size sensitivity and the digital/utility "divide" framing I already use for telehealth.

③ Fuse multi-source data to recover the households the Census misses

Extending this paperThe authors candidly flag that Census microdata undercount unhoused and "group-quarters" residents and cannot detect water shut-offs — so plumbing poverty is underestimated. Extending it: fuse the Census signal with utility disconnection records, eviction filings, 311 complaints, mobile-phone presence and remote-sensing of informal/vacant housing to build a nowcast that fills the Census blind spots between five-year windows.

Added value to my researchThis is a textbook multi-source data-fusion problem on my agenda (mobile-phone records + social media + remote sensing); the same fusion that recovers hidden waterless households recovers hidden unmet-care demand, and the open/FAIR figshare release here is a model for my reproducibility practice.

④ Couple the water findings to the housing-market system that produces them

Extending this paperThe paper's Portland case shows median home prices +140% and rents +78% alongside rising waterlessness, but the housing market is treated as exogenous context. Extending it: build a coupled housing–water model (or agent-based simulation) where corporate-landlord entry, affordability, and displacement endogenously generate downgrading into substandard housing — turning the qualitative "squeeze" into a simulatable space–human coupling.
