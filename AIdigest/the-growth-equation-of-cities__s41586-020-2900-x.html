The growth equation of cities

Vincent Verbavatz · Marc Barthelemy Published 2020-11-18 DOI 10.1038/s41586-020-2900-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

City population growth is driven not by Gaussian random shocks (Gibrat's law) but by rare, large interurban migratory events that follow a Lévy-stable distribution. A stochastic differential equation built from this insight reproduces the full, non-power-law shape of the city-size distribution across Canada, France, the UK and the USA, explains why Zipf's law fails as a universal principle, and correctly predicts the observed frequency of dramatic rises and falls in city rank — demonstrating that rare events, not steady drift, govern urban hierarchy.

★

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a century-old puzzle. Zipf's law (rank × size = constant) has been the default model of city-size distributions since 1913; this paper demonstrates it is a finite-time artefact, not a universal law, and provides the underlying stochastic mechanism that generates the observed complexity.
  • Identifies the hidden driver of urban change. By showing that Lévy-distributed migration shocks — not ordinary population flows — dominate city growth, the paper redirects attention from average trends to tail events (sudden economic booms, industrial collapse, disaster), with direct implications for long-range urban planning.
  • Explains both structure and dynamics simultaneously. The growth equation reproduces the static city-size distribution and the temporal volatility of city ranks (rank clocks), something no previous model achieved in a unified framework.
  • Shifts urban policy perspective. Because rare migratory shocks are unpredictable, the model implies that planning for resilience against large, sudden population swings is as important as managing steady growth — a qualitative shift in how planners should think about uncertainty.

Core Methods

  • Empirical distribution fitting: inter-city migration flows are normalized and fit to Lévy-stable vs. Gaussian distributions using empirical left-cumulative distribution functions; maximum-likelihood estimation (MLE) used for Pareto tail exponents.
  • Stochastic differential equation (SDE) construction: a Langevin-type growth equation with Lévy-distributed noise is derived from the migration data, parameterized by α (Lévy exponent), β (spatial coupling), r (baseline growth rate), and D (diffusion coefficient).
  • Fokker-Planck / fractional Fokker-Planck analysis: the Fokker-Planck equation corresponding to the SDE is solved analytically and numerically to derive the stationary city-size distribution — shown to deviate systematically from a power law (Zipf/Pareto) due to finite-time effects.
  • Rank-clock simulation: the SDE is integrated numerically (10 runs) for t = 500 time steps to generate synthetic rank clocks that reproduce the frequency of large rank fluctuations observed in historical data — compared against Gabaix's Gaussian model and Gibrat's law.
  • Scaling-law validation: Pareto exponents measured as a function of the lower size threshold Smin for eight countries, demonstrating that the apparent exponent is an artefact of the threshold choice and that no universal Zipf exponent exists.

① Extend the growth equation from national to global urban systems

Extending this paperThe paper calibrates its stochastic equation on four high-income, data-rich countries with well-maintained national migration registers. A direct extension is to apply the same Lévy-SDE framework to the Global South — especially Africa, which accounts for a large and accelerating share of future urbanization. The key challenge is that migration-flow matrices at fine spatial resolution are unavailable or unreliable for most countries, so the model would need to be adapted to use proxy data (mobile-phone CDRs, satellite night-light growth rates) as surrogates for the migration noise term. Testing whether the Lévy exponent α and finite-time breakdown of Zipf's law are also observed outside the four study countries would either confirm the paper's universality claim or identify cultural/institutional conditions under which the growth equation changes character.

Added value to my researchThis extension directly enriches my multi-source data fusion agenda: mobile-phone records and remote-sensing proxies for migration would need the same spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and data harmonization pipeline I apply to GPS/CDR data in Chinese and developing-country contexts. The resulting cross-country α estimates would also be a novel spatial covariate for modeling health-service demand growth in resource-limited settings — regions where population shocks from conflict or climate displacement are most acute.

② Couple the growth equation with intra-urban spatial structure

Extending this paperThe paper treats each city as a dimensionless point — population is a single scalar evolving in time. It cannot say anything about where within a city growth is occurring (center vs. suburbs, dense vs. sparse tracts) or how spatial heterogeneity feeds back into the migration shocks. Extending it: incorporate a spatial field — mapping the SDE from city-level to tract-level — so that the Lévy noise term acts differentially on sub-city zones. This would merge the paper's growth equation with the empirical finding from the "Spatial structure of city population growth" paper that county-level growth is spatially heterogeneous within metropolitan areas.

Added value to my researchA spatially resolved growth equation would directly inform my work on urban agglomeration and 15-minute neighborhoods: it would show which sub-city zones are structurally exposed to sudden population shocks, allowing my accessibility models (2SFCA) to preemptively identify areas where service catchments will be overloaded or emptied by the next rare migration event. This turns a retrospective accessibility audit into a prospective planning tool.

③ Use the Lévy-shock framework to model digital-service and telehealth demand spikes

Extending this paperThe paper establishes that rare, large migratory shocks — not steady growth — govern the city hierarchy. The same mathematical structure (Lévy-stable noise in a Langevin equation) applies whenever a demand process is driven by rare large events rather than many small increments. An extension of the paper's framework outside demography: model the temporal demand for telehealth or digital-primary-care services as a Lévy-driven process, where rare events (pandemics, sudden rural-broadband rollouts, regulatory shifts) create demand spikes that dwarf ordinary trend growth. The paper's Fokker-Planck solution would give the stationary demand distribution and the expected frequency of extreme service loads.

Added value to my researchThis is highly relevant to my work on telehealth adoption and digital divides in healthcare access. My 2SFCA-based accessibility models assume quasi-static demand; incorporating Lévy-distributed demand shocks would allow me to quantify the tail risk of service overload — the probability that a rare demand event leaves a geographic area without functional telehealth capacity. This reframes digital-divide research from a mean-access question to a distributional resilience question.

④ Integrate the growth equation into a predictive GeoAI model for urban hierarchy futures

Extending this paperThe paper derives its stochastic equation analytically from empirical migration data, but it does not produce city-specific forecasts — only the statistical distribution of outcomes. An extension: treat the SDE's Lévy exponent α and coupling parameter β as spatially varying, learnable functions of city-level features (economic diversity, connectivity, amenity mix), and train a graph neural network to predict α and β from observable urban indicators. The resulting model would output city-specific growth distributions rather than a single universal distribution, creating a GeoAI wrapper around the paper's physics.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my physics-informed graph learning agenda: the paper's SDE provides the physics prior (conservation of total urban population, Lévy noise structure), and the GNN learns the city-specific parameters. The trained model could be used in my urban analytics work to forecast which cities are at elevated risk of sudden rank-change — a key input for equity-weighted infrastructure planning, including EV charging stations, primary-care facilities, and public transit investments.

⑤ Combine the growth equation with global demographic divergence data to benchmark future megacity emergence

Extending this paperThe paper's model predicts that megacities arise from the accumulated effect of rare large migration shocks, not from a deterministic growth advantage. However, it was calibrated on data from before 2020 and does not incorporate demographic divergence — the global trend in which some city systems are growing rapidly while others shrink. Extending it: re-parameterize the growth equation with updated data stratified by demographic regime (growing vs. shrinking urban systems), and use the resulting regime-specific Lévy exponents to forecast the probability of future megacity emergence across different world regions under different climate-migration scenarios.

Added value to my researchThis extension creates a macroscopic context for my micro-level spatiotemporal analyses. Understanding which cities are structurally likely to experience large population shocks in the next 20 years directly shapes the priority regions for my GeoAI accessibility work: health-service planning, housing access, and EV-infrastructure deployment in cities that are likely to undergo rapid population restructuring. It also connects my work on urban agglomeration to global health geography at a scale relevant to WHO and UN-Habitat policy.
