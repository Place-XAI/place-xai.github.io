A machine learning route between band mapping and band structure

R. Patrick Xian · Vincent Stimper · Marios Zacharias · Maciej Dendzik · Shuo Dong · Samuel Beaulieu · Bernhard Schölkopf · Martin Wolf · Laurenz Rettig · Christian Carbogno · Stefan Bauer · Ralph Ernstorfer Published 2022-12-30 DOI 10.1038/s43588-022-00382-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A probabilistic machine learning pipeline based on Markov random fields bridges photoemission band mapping and electronic band structure reconstruction, successfully recovering all 14 valence bands of the semiconductor WSe2 at full Brillouin-zone coverage. The approach outperforms conventional pointwise line fitting in accuracy, stability, and computational efficiency by orders of magnitude, while geometric featurization via Zernike polynomials enables data reduction by over 5,000× and direct integration into materials science databases.

★

Why It Matters

  • Closes a critical gap in materials characterization. Photoemission band mapping produces enormous, high-dimensional data (hundreds of MB per experiment), yet extracting quantitative band structure has been limited to sparse, hand-picked momentum points — this pipeline automates global reconstruction at scale.
  • Combines domain knowledge with probabilistic inference. By encoding band continuity as a Markov random field prior and using DFT calculations as a warm start, the method achieves higher accuracy than supervised deep learning without requiring ground-truth labels from experiment.
  • Enables database-scale band structure informatics. The geometric fingerprinting via Zernike polynomials compresses each energy band to a feature vector of ~24 kB (from ~235 MB raw data), making systematic, resolution-independent comparison and archiving viable for the first time.
  • Reveals previously inaccessible physics. The full-zone reconstruction of WSe2 uncovers a spin-orbit gap at the saddle point M' of 338 meV — substantially larger than DFT predictions — demonstrating that the method produces scientifically novel findings beyond mere automation.
  • Generalizes across material classes. Validated on a semiconductor (WSe2), a topological insulator (Bi2Te2Se), and a metal (Au(111)), confirming broad applicability to the growing corpus of publicly accessible photoemission datasets.

Core Methods

  • Markov Random Field (MRF) probabilistic model: energy values at each momentum grid point are random variables; a nearest-neighbor Gaussian prior enforces band continuity, and photoemission intensity at each EDC provides the likelihood. The joint posterior is maximized via MAP estimation.
  • Data pre-processing pipeline: (1) intensity symmetrization along crystallographic symmetry axes; (2) multidimensional contrast-limited adaptive histogram equalization (MCLAHE) for contrast enhancement; (3) Gaussian smoothing along kx, ky, and E axes.
  • DFT warm-start initialization: semi-local DFT calculations (LDA, GGA-PBE, GGA-PBEsol, HSE06 via FHI-aims) provide initial band positions; hyperparameter tuning aligns them to experimental data via rigid energy shifts and momentum scaling.
  • Parallelized MAP optimization: iterated conditional mode (ICM) with checkerboard coloring on the MRF graph; implemented in TensorFlow on multicore CPUs and GPUs (single band reconstructed in ~7 s on NVIDIA GTX980).
  • Zernike polynomial featurization: each 3D dispersion surface is decomposed into hexagonal Zernike polynomials, producing geometry fingerprints for multiscale band comparison; cosine similarity and Euclidean distance metrics quantify theory–experiment agreement at any resolution.
  • Benchmarking framework: comparison against Levenberg–Marquardt pointwise line fitting (the state-of-the-art alternative); metrics include per-band per-spectrum computing time, r.m.s. error, hyperparameter tuning time, and reconstruction instability (s.d. of residuals).

① Replace the band-by-band sequential MRF with a joint multi-band deep learning Hamiltonian

Extending this paperThe paper reconstructs energy bands sequentially, one band at a time, which requires knowing the number of bands Nb in advance and cannot learn inter-band interactions (crossings, spin-orbit coupling patterns) in a unified pass. A natural extension: replace the MRF's factored posterior with a deep-learning DFT Hamiltonian network that predicts all bands jointly from the raw 3D photoemission volume, leveraging the same DFT warm-start idea but as a trainable prior that encodes crystal symmetry and inter-band coupling directly into the network weights. This addresses the paper's first stated limitation (needing known Nb) while maintaining its domain-knowledge-guided philosophy.

Added value to my researchThe core lesson — encoding physical structure (periodicity, symmetry, continuity) as inductive bias into a learnable model rather than as a hard constraint — directly maps onto my physics-informed graph learning agenda. In my context, replacing band periodicity with spatial autocorrelation structure (Moran's I, variogram) as a graph-neural-network prior would yield a spatially self-consistent GeoAI model for health-outcome prediction, where the "warm start" is a spatial lag model rather than DFT.

② Extend the Zernike fingerprint idea to a universal materials-database similarity engine

Extending this paperThe paper's Zernike polynomial featurization compresses each 3D dispersion surface to ~24 kB while preserving geometry at <0.8% relative error. This is demonstrated on three materials but never scaled to a database. Extending the paper: build a geometry-fingerprint index over the full NOMAD/Materials Project database of DFT band structures, enabling nearest-neighbor retrieval of materials with similar band topologies — turning the paper's data reduction step into a searchable phonon/band-structure analogue of crystal structure databases. The paper anticipates this but does not implement it.

Added value to my researchZernike-like geometric decomposition is directly applicable to spatiotemporal kernel density fields in my work: decompose a city's POI density surface or disease-incidence surface into orthonormal basis coefficients, enabling similarity search across cities or time periods without recomputing spatial statistics from scratch — a data reduction for urban geospatial fingerprinting analogous to the paper's band fingerprinting.

③ Quantify epistemic uncertainty in the reconstruction and propagate it to downstream applications

Extending this paperThe paper's probabilistic MRF formulation naturally supports uncertainty quantification: the posterior width encodes confidence in each reconstructed band locus. However, the paper reports only point estimates (MAP) and benchmarks r.m.s. error against synthetic ground truth — it never outputs per-pixel uncertainty maps or propagates uncertainty into the derived local band parameters (spin-orbit gap, trigonal warping). Extending it with posterior sampling (MCMC or variational inference) would produce credible intervals on the 338 meV M' gap and other physically significant values, making the reconstruction outputs usable in quantitative theory–experiment discrepancy analysis.

Added value to my researchMy explainable ML for spatial prediction also suffers from point-estimate overconfidence. The same extension — from MAP to a Laplace approximation or variational posterior — would give spatially resolved confidence intervals on, e.g., R2SFCA accessibility scores or MGWR coefficient surfaces, directly improving how I communicate uncertainty to health-policy stakeholders.

④ Adapt the pre-processing + reconstruction pipeline to time-resolved photoemission for non-equilibrium dynamics

Extending this paperThe paper explicitly anticipates use case (1): online monitoring of band structure during phase transitions, yielding 4D data I(k, E, t). The current MRF operates on a single snapshot. Extending it to time-resolved band mapping requires a temporal smoothness prior in addition to the spatial NN prior — a 3D MRF on (kx, ky, t) slices — effectively tracking how band energies evolve after an ultrafast laser pulse. This would make the pipeline a real-time diagnostic tool for pump-probe spectroscopy, where speed (seconds per band per snapshot) is critical.

Added value to my researchTemporal extension of a Markov-structured spatial model maps directly onto my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation work: treating time as a third MRF dimension, with energy-band loci replaced by event-density surfaces, would allow tracking of, e.g., social-media event clusters or mobile-phone activity surfaces through time — a probabilistic spatiotemporal signal extraction framework analogous to this band-tracking pipeline.

⑤ Transfer the warm-start + scalable MAP paradigm to open materials databases for pre-train/fine-tune workflows

Extending this paperThe paper's "warm start" from DFT is a principled form of transfer learning: a cheaper, approximate model provides initialization for a more expensive, accurate reconstruction. The framework does not exploit the growing availability of large-scale DFT databases (Materials Project, AFLOW, NOMAD) as pre-training corpora. Extending it: pre-train a neural-network posterior approximator on thousands of DFT band structures to learn a universal band-shape prior, then fine-tune on new experimental photoemission data with minimal hyperparameter tuning — reducing the reconstruction from tens of minutes of tuning to seconds. This is the natural next step given the paper's data-reduction and database-integration ambitions.

Added value to my researchThe warm-start/fine-tune paradigm directly informs my interest in fine-tuning geospatial foundation models: just as DFT provides an approximate prior that the MRF refines with experimental data, a pretrained geospatial foundation model (e.g., on satellite imagery) provides spatial priors that can be fine-tuned with scarce local health-outcome labels — the methodological lesson is identical across domains.
