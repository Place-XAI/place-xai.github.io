Urban sprawl is associated with reduced access and increased costs of water and sanitation

Rafael Prieto-Curiel · Pavel Luengas-Sierra · Christian Borja-Vega Published 2025-12-02 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00338-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 105 cities in Asia, Africa and Latin America, urban form shapes who gets water and sanitation. Using two comparable metrics — remoteness (distance of a location to the city center) and sparseness (its population-weighted average) — the authors show that less-remote areas have higher income, closer critical infrastructure and better access to piped water and sewage, while sparser, more sprawled cities face higher water tariffs and lower access. Modeling expansion under three scenarios, they find that if cities grew compactly rather than horizontally, an extra 220 million people could gain piped water and 190 million sewage services.

★

Why It Matters

  • Connects urban geometry to a basic human right. Water and sanitation access is usually framed as an investment or governance problem; this work shows that the shape of a city itself — how remote and how sparse it is — is a measurable driver of who is served.
  • Builds genuinely comparable cities. By crafting comparable radial urban forms for over 100 cities across three continents, it lets sprawl be compared apples-to-apples rather than city-by-city anecdote.
  • Quantifies the cost of sprawl in people, not just dollars. The compact-versus-horizontal scenarios translate urban-form choices into hundreds of millions of people gaining or losing piped water and sewage — a concrete planning lever for the Global South.
  • Targets the cities that are growing fastest. The sample is exactly the rapidly urbanizing regions of Asia, Africa and Latin America, where infrastructure decisions made now lock in decades of service inequity.

Core Methods

  • Comparable urban-form construction: craft radial, comparable urban forms for 105 cities from building footprints / OSM, so distance-to-center is measured consistently across continents (Figs. 2–4).
  • Two new metrics: remoteness = distance of any location to the city center; sparseness = population-weighted average of all locations' remoteness, summarizing how spread out a city is.
  • Multi-scale association analysis: link remoteness/sparseness to income, infrastructure proximity, water tariffs, and access to piped water and sewage across city-, pixel- and survey-level data (Figs. 5–6).
  • Scenario modeling of urban expansion: simulate growth under three regimes — compact, persistent, and horizontal — and estimate the population gaining access under each.
  • Population-weighted accounting: translate form differences into headline impacts (≈220 million more with piped water, ≈190 million with sewage under compact vs. horizontal growth).

① From radial remoteness to a network-based 2SFCA of water/sanitation supply

Extending this paperThe paper's remoteness and sparseness metrics are essentially monocentric, straight-line distances to a single center. Real water and sewage networks are tree-like and polycentric. A direct extension: replace radial distance with a two-step floating catchment (2SFCA) defined on the actual pipe/road network, where "supply" is treatment-plant and trunk-main capacity and "demand" is the survey-weighted population — turning the paper's correlational story into a capacity-constrained accessibility map.

Added value to my researchThis is a clean new application domain for my R2SFCA service-area delineation: water and sanitation infrastructure as the "supply," with sparseness used as a built-in distance-decay prior — extending my health-access toolkit to environmental-services equity.

② Turn sparseness into a predictive scaling law for service cost

Extending this paperThe paper reports that sparser cities have higher water tariffs and lower access, but stops at association. Extending it: fit a scaling relation between sparseness and per-capita network cost / tariff, testing whether a single exponent holds across Africa, Asia and Latin America or splits by region — converting the paper's radial profiles into a closed-form cost-of-sprawl law usable for any unsampled city.

Added value to my researchDirectly feeds my urban-analytics work on agglomeration and public-facility accessibility vs. cost: a sparseness exponent becomes a transferable covariate in MGWR models linking urban form to infrastructure cost and housing-price gradients.

③ Explain — not just measure — who is left without water

Extending this paperThe paper links remoteness to income and access but treats the relationship largely descriptively. Extending it: train an explainable ML ensemble (gradient boosting + SHAP) on the pixel- and survey-level tables to attribute access gaps to remoteness, sparseness, income, and infrastructure proximity jointly, surfacing nonlinear thresholds and interaction effects the radial profiles cannot show.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction line: the 105-city / 20,000-pixel / 125,000-respondent stack is an ideal multi-scale testbed for SHAP-based attribution of an equity outcome, transferable to my telehealth and primary-care access work.

④ Make the three growth scenarios agent-based and dynamic

Extending this paperThe compact / persistent / horizontal scenarios are static accounting exercises. Extending them with an agent-based / cellular model of household location, developer behavior and incremental network extension would let the 220-million-people figure emerge from dynamics — and would expose path dependence, where early sprawl forecloses later compact retrofits.

⑤ A transferable physics-informed graph operator for service-equity at the urban edge

Extending this paperThe paper optimizes each city's radial profile in isolation yet claims cross-continental regularity. Extending it: treat the 105 cities as one meta-graph and learn a physics-informed graph operator mapping (building footprints, density field, distance-to-center) → water/sewage access, with the sparseness–access relationship encoded as a soft constraint so the model extrapolates to fast-growing peri-urban edges not in the sample.
