Global divergence in urban demographic change and migration patterns

Andrew Zimmer · Nina Brooks · Andrea E. Gaughan · Cascade Tuholske Published 2026-05-26 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00447-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using annual age- and sex-structured population estimates for more than 10,000 cities worldwide (2000–2020), the study shows urban demographic change is highly uneven and obscured by national averages. The global urban dependency ratio fell from 0.87 to 0.59, yet smaller cities stayed consistently younger (especially in Africa); sex ratios diverge sharply (strong male surpluses across the Middle East and North Africa, consistent with labor migration); and overall, 45% of urban growth came from net migration and 55% from natural increase, with large between-city variation.

★

Why It Matters

  • First globally consistent city-level demographic picture. Previous urban demographic estimates were aggregated to the national level; this work resolves age–sex structure for >10,000 cities under one definition, exposing variation that national means hide (e.g., Kano's young dependency ratio 0.83 vs. Lagos's 0.47 within the same country).
  • Connects demography to vulnerability. Dependency and sex ratios shape exposure to extreme heat, flooding, labor-market and political stress — the dataset lets planners pinpoint which specific cities carry which risk profile.
  • Repositions smaller cities. Cities below 50,000–300,000 are consistently younger and under-invested, yet collectively hold a large share of the urban population — flagged here as critical, under-examined centers of demographic change.
  • Quantifies the migration vs. natural-increase split. A globally consistent demographic-accounting framework decomposes growth city by city (e.g., Ouagadougou 76% migration-driven vs. its secondary city Koudougou 17%).
  • Open, reusable infrastructure. The Global Urban Demographic Dataset is published on Harvard Dataverse with a public web viewer and open code — research as a shared data product.

Core Methods

  • Grid-to-boundary aggregation: overlay WorldPop 1-km age–sex grids onto GHS-UCDB urban polygons; population counted where grid-cell centroids fall inside each polygon, for every year 2000–2020.
  • Demographic indicators: compute total, young (0–14), working-age (15–64) and old (65+) dependency ratios and sex ratios per city per year.
  • Demographic-accounting (population balance): births estimated from the surviving <1-year cohort; deaths from gridded (2000–2019) then national (2020) death rates; natural change = births − deaths; net migration = total change − natural change (residual method), with an infant-migration adjustment.
  • Urban-continuum analysis: stratify dependency ratios across city-size bands and by continent to expose small- vs. large-city gradients.
  • Robustness checks: static vs. dynamic GHS-UCDB boundaries; gridded vs. national mortality inputs; comparison against UN-DESA national dependency ratios to validate aggregate patterns.

Expanded Ideas — this paper's extensibility & added value to my research

Each idea stays anchored to this paper: first how the paper itself could be extended (drawing on our corpus), then the added value for my research lines (migis.net — GeoAI, 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility, health geography, explainable & physics-informed spatial learning).

① Close the residual-migration loop with observed flows

Extending this paperNet migration here is a residual (total change − natural change), so it absorbs all model error and reveals magnitude but not origin–destination structure. A direct extension: fuse the paper's city-level migration totals with high-resolution global migration flow estimates to constrain the residual into directed flows, turning each city's net number into an explicit origin–destination network and validating the male-dominated MENA inflows the paper infers indirectly.

Added value to my researchFlow-constrained demographic accounting is exactly the multi-source fusion my work needs: pairing residual demographic balance with mobility data gives a reproducible recipe for reconstructing migration where censuses are sparse — directly usable in my GeoAI / FAIR data-fusion pipelines.

Cross-ref (corpus): World's human migration patterns in 2000–2019 unveiled by high-resolution data — 10.1038/s41562-023-01689-4

② Add explainable attribution to the small-city "youth gap"

Extending this paperThe paper documents that smaller cities are consistently younger and offers narrative mechanisms (rural integration, selective working-age out-migration) but never quantifies their relative weight. Extending it with an explainable ML model (gradient boosting + SHAP, or MGWR for spatial non-stationarity) predicting young dependency ratio from city size, economic, climate and connectivity covariates would turn the described mechanisms into measured, spatially varying effect sizes.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my explainable-spatial-prediction agenda: a transferable template for attributing demographic outcomes with MGWR/ML ensembles, reusable for my healthy-cities and accessibility studies where "why is this place different" is the core question.

Cross-ref (corpus): Demography and the emergence of universal patterns in urban systems — 10.1038/s41467-020-18205-1

③ Couple the demographic dataset with service-accessibility demand

Extending this paperThe paper computes dependency and sex ratios but stops at population structure; it never asks whether the young, old or migrant-heavy cities it flags actually have the schools, clinics or eldercare they imply. A natural extension: overlay the age–sex grids with facility locations to derive age-weighted service-need surfaces, converting demographic imbalance into concrete healthcare/education demand gaps city by city.

Added value to my researchThe 1-km age–sex grids are an ideal demand layer for 2SFCA/R2SFCA: replacing crude total-population demand with age-structured demand (children → primary care/vaccination, 65+ → mental-health/eldercare) sharpens every accessibility study I run, especially telehealth and primary-care service-area delineation.

Cross-ref (corpus): Visitation patterns reveal service access disparities for ageing populations in the USA — 10.1038/s41562-025-02285-4

④ From snapshots to a city-rank dynamics model

Extending this paperThe 21-year annual panel is treated largely cross-sectionally (2000 vs. 2020). Extending it into an explicit dynamical model of rank/size and age-structure co-evolution — where migration shocks reshuffle the urban hierarchy — would let the paper forecast which cities cross demographic-vulnerability thresholds, not just describe the past.

Added value to my researchThis connects to my urban-agglomeration and job–housing network work: modeling demographic flows as a temporal network supports community-detection and regional-collaboration analyses, and agent-based / SIR-style simulation of how migration propagates structural change across a city system.

Cross-ref (corpus): Domestic migration and city rank dynamics — 10.1038/s44284-024-00170-1

⑤ Cross the demographic surface with climate-hazard exposure

Extending this paperThe paper repeatedly invokes heat and flood vulnerability (Lagos, Matsue, migrant construction labor) but never builds a combined exposure metric. Extending it: intersect the age–sex grids with gridded heat/flood-zone layers to produce a globally consistent demographically weighted hazard-exposure index, ranking cities by population most at risk rather than by hazard alone.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct multi-source remote-sensing + demography fusion for public/urban-health policy — a spatiotemporal exposure surface that feeds my health-geography and healthy-cities lines, and a clean target for fine-tuning geospatial foundation models on age-structured vulnerability.
