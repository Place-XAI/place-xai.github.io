HESpotEx: a dual-stream deep learning framework for spot-level gene expression prediction from histological images

Yin Wang · Peng Qin · Fanyi Meng · You Wan · Weilong Zhang · Yuan Zhou Published 2026-05-15 DOI 10.1038/s43588-026-00992-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

HESpotEx is a dual-stream deep learning framework that predicts spatial gene expression profiles — up to 5,457 genes per sampling spot — directly from H&E-stained whole-slide histopathological images, eliminating the need for costly spatial transcriptomics assays. By aligning a pretrained image encoder (Quilt-Net) with a graph attention autoencoder via Pearson correlation loss, and decoding predictions through a ResNet-LSTM graph convolution network, HESpotEx consistently outperforms seven competing methods across cancer, noncancer, and large-scale cohort benchmarks (mean PCC 0.498 on 1,042 TCGA-BRCA samples) and demonstrates clinically meaningful prognostic stratification from histology alone.

★

Why It Matters

  • Democratizes spatial molecular profiling. Spatial transcriptomics costs thousands of US dollars per sample; HESpotEx recovers much of its molecular information from routine H&E slides already archived at scale in hospitals worldwide.
  • Strong and reproducible performance gains. On the external breast cancer cohort, HESpotEx achieved 140% higher PCC than the next-best method (IGI-DL, 0.24 vs. 0.10), and on TCGA-BRCA its mean PCC of 0.498 far exceeded the second-ranked method at 0.088.
  • Prognostic signal from histology alone. Predicted expression profiles — without any bulk RNA-seq or clinical data — stratified 169 HER2+ breast cancer patients into subgroups with significantly different overall survival (log-rank P = 0.037; HR 2.399).
  • Generalizes beyond cancer. Validated on noncommunicable inflammatory skin diseases (atopic dermatitis, lichen planus, psoriasis) and high-resolution Xenium / Visium HD platforms, showing broad tissue-type and technology robustness.
  • Open and reproducible. Code released on GitHub under MIT license with processed datasets on Zenodo, enabling community benchmarking and extension.

Core Methods

  • Dual-stream embedding alignment: a graph attention autoencoder (GATE) encodes ST expression profiles into spot embeddings; a pretrained Quilt-Net image encoder + MLP produces patch embeddings from H&E tiles; Pearson correlation loss aligns the two embedding spaces in a shared low-dimensional representation (size 64).
  • GCN decoder with residual structure: a graph convolution network aggregates patch embeddings from spatial neighbors (k-nearest by Euclidean distance); LSTM layer-aggregation mitigates oversmoothing; residual addition of MLP output from original patch embeddings prevents neighbor bias.
  • Cell-nuclei postprocessing: Cellpose nuclear segmentation quantifies the number of cell nuclei per image patch; predicted expression is scaled proportionally, reflecting the intuition that higher cell density implies greater total expression.
  • Two-stage training: Stage 1 trains GATE on ST expression graphs to produce latent spot embeddings; Stage 2 trains the GCN decoder to map image patch embeddings to gene expression, supervised jointly by PCC loss and MSE loss.
  • Leave-one-out cross-validation: for within-dataset benchmarking (HER2+, cSCC), all baseline models were retrained under the same split; external validation used the held-out breast cancer cohort and TCGA-BRCA.
  • Benchmarking against 7+ methods: compared with OmiCLIP, IGI-DL, TCGN, mclSTExp, BLEEP, THItoGene, Hist2ST (and iStar, scstGCN for high-resolution tasks); evaluation metrics: Pearson Correlation Coefficient (PCC), KL divergence, MSE.
  • Ablation studies: systematically evaluated dual-stream vs. image-only; Quilt-Net vs. 5 other pretrained encoders; batch effect correction (ComBat/Harmony); MLP+GCN vs. alternatives (Transformer, directed GATE decoder).

① Extend HESpotEx's spatial graph to encode true tissue topology via a pathology foundation model

Extending this paper HESpotEx builds its spot graph from simple Euclidean distance between sampling coordinates. Yet tissue architecture is hierarchical: nuclei cluster into cells, cells into functional regions (tumor vs. stroma vs. lymphoid aggregates), regions into spatial domains. A direct extension: replace or augment HESpotEx's GCN graph with a multi-scale hierarchical graph derived from a whole-slide foundation model (e.g., UNI or CONCH at patch level, Prov-GigaPath at slide level), so that the structural priors baked into a billion-parameter pretrained encoder — rather than raw Euclidean proximity — guide which spots exchange neighborhood information. This would address HESpotEx's acknowledged limitation that its accuracy depends on spatially coherent gene expression and that "housekeeping genes" with weak spatial patterns remain difficult to predict.

Added value to my research Hierarchical spatial graph construction mirrors how I build multi-scale accessibility graphs in urban contexts (neighborhood → district → region). Adapting HESpotEx's GATE + GCN pipeline to encode functional spatial hierarchy rather than raw Euclidean adjacency would directly inform my physics-informed graph learning work: the same idea of encoding domain-specific topology (tissue region vs. street network catchment) as a graph prior can replace ad hoc k-nearest-neighbor graphs in both biomedical and urban GeoAI settings.

② Replace PCC alignment with contrastive spatial-context loss to capture tissue-region specificity

Extending this paper HESpotEx aligns image patch embeddings and spot expression embeddings using a global Pearson correlation loss across the entire tissue section. This treats every spot equally, ignoring the fact that spots in tumor cores, tumor margins, or stromal regions have systematically different image–expression coupling strengths. A contrastive extension — drawing on the multimodal generative AI copilot paradigm — would apply spatially-stratified contrastive learning: positive pairs come from the same tissue sub-region (defined by unsupervised spatial clustering), and negatives are drawn from distinct regions. This teaches the alignment to respect the spatial hierarchy of tissue organization, potentially sharpening prediction in heterogeneous tumors where HESpotEx currently under-predicts low-expression and spatially diffuse genes.

Added value to my research Spatially-stratified contrastive learning is a direct analogy to the neighborhood-stratified loss functions I use in spatiotemporal kernel density estimation. The idea of defining "positive pairs" by spatial context rather than global statistics can be ported to my geospatial foundation model fine-tuning work: region-aware contrastive pretraining on remote sensing + POI data would create spatially coherent embeddings for urban health accessibility prediction.

③ Transfer HESpotEx's prognostic stratification pipeline to spatial health geography at the population level

Extending this paper Fig. 4 demonstrates that predicted gene expression from WSIs — without any clinical data — separates HER2+ breast cancer patients into significantly different survival groups. The paper treats this as a byproduct, but a deliberate extension would build a survival-aware fine-tuning stage: after the GCN decoder produces expression predictions, add a differentiable Cox proportional hazards head that back-propagates a partial-likelihood survival loss, guiding the image encoder to weight the image features most predictive of patient outcomes. This directly addresses HESpotEx's limitation that it optimizes for expression accuracy (MSE/PCC), not clinical utility. Evaluation on TCGA-BRCA is ready-made.

④ Extend HESpotEx from single-gene prediction to genotype–phenotype coupling maps via multimodal learning

Extending this paper HESpotEx predicts gene expression one spot at a time, treating each gene independently in the GCN decoder's output layer. It does not model the co-expression structure among genes or the feedback between gene expression patterns and morphological phenotype at the tissue level. A natural extension: incorporate a gene-level graph (co-expression network derived from scRNA-seq reference data) into the decoder so that predictions respect known transcriptional regulatory circuits, and pair this with a phenotype reconstruction head that maps the predicted expression back to interpretable tissue morphological features — turning HESpotEx from a "predictor" into a bidirectional genotype–phenotype mapping engine.

⑤ Port HESpotEx's dual-stream graph-alignment principle to spatial accessibility modeling with multi-source urban data

Extending this paper HESpotEx's core technical contribution — aligning two heterogeneous embedding spaces (image and expression) via a graph-structured low-dimensional latent space — is a generalizable alignment framework. A direct extension to spatial biology would apply the same principle to multi-modal ST data fusion: align Visium (transcriptomics), MERFISH/Xenium (in-situ sequencing), and H&E image modalities in a shared spot-level embedding, enabling cross-platform transfer learning. The current paper validates cross-platform generalization only implicitly (Xenium and Visium HD); a multimodal fusion variant would make this explicit and enable leveraging larger, cheaper ST datasets as weak supervision for high-resolution platforms.
