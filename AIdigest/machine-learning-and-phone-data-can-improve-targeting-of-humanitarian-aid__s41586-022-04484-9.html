Machine learning and phone data can improve targeting of humanitarian aid

Emily Aiken · Suzanne Bellue · Dean Karlan · Chris Udry · Joshua E. Blumenstock Published 2022-03-16 DOI 10.1038/s41586-022-04484-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Training gradient-boosting models on call detail records (CDRs) from Togo's two mobile operators — matched to survey-measured poverty — and then scoring all ~5 million subscribers reduced errors of exclusion by 4–21% relative to geographic targeting available to the government. Relative to a comprehensive social registry (which did not exist in Togo), exclusion errors rose by 9–35%. The Novissi cash-transfer programme used these algorithms in 2020 to disburse millions of US dollars to rural COVID-19 relief recipients, demonstrating that real-time phone metadata can complement — not replace — traditional targeting methods, especially in data-scarce crisis settings.

Why It Matters

  • First randomized-equivalent evaluation of ML-based aid targeting in a live government programme. Results are not simulations alone — the algorithms directly determined which of 154,238 Novissi registrants received COVID-19 cash relief, making this a genuine policy experiment at national scale.
  • Honest quantification of trade-offs. The paper shows both where ML targeting wins (over geography-only methods) and where it loses (against comprehensive proxy-means tests or social registries), setting a benchmark that neither oversells nor undersells algorithmic aid.
  • Fairness audit across sensitive subgroups. Gender, ethnicity, religion, age, disability, and marital status are each tested; no systematic bias is found, a critical result for policy adoption in demographically diverse LMICs.
  • Six-factor exclusion decomposition. The paper dissects programme exclusion into phone ownership (~65% of individuals in Togo), past SIM activity, voter registration, programme awareness, registration success, and algorithmic targeting error — giving practitioners a practical checklist beyond model accuracy.
  • Open-source replication. Full analysis code is available at github.com/emilylaiken/togo-targeting-replication, enabling adaptation for other LMICs or hazard types.

Core Methods

  • Two-step targeting pipeline: (1) rank cantons by satellite-derived relative wealth index to select the 100 poorest; (2) score individual mobile subscribers within those cantons using a CDR-trained gradient-boosting model, then rank by predicted poverty.
  • CDR featurization: 857–1,042 features per subscriber per period via bandicoot, covering call/SMS statistics, social network graph metrics, location mobility (radius of gyration, unique antennas), mobile money transaction patterns, and mobile data usage (2020 only).
  • LightGBM gradient boosting: trained on matched survey-CDR datasets with 5-fold cross-validation and independent hyperparameter search on each fold; predictions produced out-of-sample to simulate real-world deployment.
  • Proxy means test (PMT): Ridge regression on 12 asset/demographic variables, selected by stepwise forward selection, explains 48% of log-consumption variance; used as ground truth in the phone survey where consumption data were not collected.
  • Targeting evaluation: ROC curves and AUC across counterfactual targeting methods (geographic blanketing at prefecture/canton level, occupation-based, phone expenditures simple heuristic, and comprehensive PMT/PPI as upper bounds); also 1,000-bootstrap confidence intervals on recall and precision.
  • Social welfare analysis: CRRA utility function (ρ = 3) with fixed budget of US$4 million across 154,238 programme registrants, simulating welfare curves at all targeting thresholds.
  • Fairness audit: demographic parity and normalized rank residuals computed for gender, ethnicity, religion, age group, disability status, marital status, and household size.
  • Temporal stability test: performance compared across three regimes — current model/current CDR (best case), outdated model/current CDR, outdated model/outdated CDR — using data ~18 months apart.

① Fuse satellite imagery with CDR to build a spatially explicit poverty surface beyond canton boundaries

Extending this paperThe paper uses satellite micro-estimates of wealth only for geographic targeting (selecting the 100 poorest cantons) and CDR only for individual scoring; the two signals are never jointly trained. A direct extension: jointly embed the 2.4 km satellite wealth rasters with subscriber CDR features through a spatial cross-attention mechanism, so the model knows not just who uses their phone how, but also precisely where. This would collapse the paper's two-step pipeline into a unified geospatial poverty estimator and reduce positional uncertainty from admin-3 to sub-kilometre resolution. The satellite data needed (RWI from HDX, and population density) are already identified in the paper and are open-access.

Added value to my researchThis joint embedding architecture is directly transferable to my work on multi-source data fusion for GeoAI: combining remote sensing rasters with fine-grained human activity signals (mobile phone, social media, GPS) is a central challenge in my spatiotemporal analysis pipeline. Using the Togo dataset as a benchmark — since ground truth consumption is available for 4,171 matched households — I could validate whether the fused model outperforms either signal alone, then apply the same pattern to Chinese or US contexts where health-geography data and POI mobility records coexist.

② Replace the PMT ground truth with a real-time dynamic poverty score using CDR change-point detection

Extending this paperThe paper trains the LightGBM model on a snapshot of poverty (a survey collected weeks before the programme) and identifies temporal decay: accuracy drops 4–6% after 18 months. An extension suggested but not implemented by the authors: train the model to detect changes in poverty rather than levels. Using the longitudinal structure of Togo's three CDR windows (2018, 2019, 2020), one could fit a difference-in-differences CDR model — subscribers whose calling behaviour shifted (reduced mobile money in-flows, fewer unique contacts, spatial contraction) after the COVID-19 shock are flagged as newly vulnerable. This approach does not require a new survey for each crisis event and is precisely what the authors call "dynamically prioritize people with the greatest need."

Added value to my researchThis aligns with my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and event-detection work: a CDR change-point model is a temporal generalization of my point-process approach, where instead of detecting disease hotspot emergence I detect livelihood shock propagation across the mobile network graph. The methodology is applicable to any scenario where mobile data are available before and after a crisis — pandemic, flood, earthquake — and has direct relevance to digital-divide analysis in my telehealth and digital-divide research, identifying populations whose digital footprint contracts after a health shock.

③ Recast the paper's exclusion cascade as a spatial accessibility problem and audit it with 2SFCA

Extending this paperTable 2 of the paper lists six sequential barriers to receiving Novissi benefits (SIM ownership, recent SIM activity, voter registration, programme awareness, registration success, algorithmic targeting), but treats them as independent rates. In practice these barriers have strong spatial patterning: phone coverage, voter registration density, and digital literacy correlate with location relative to cell towers, urban centres, and literacy zones. Extending the paper: overlay each barrier rate on Togo's road network and apply a two-step floating catchment area (2SFCA) approach — treating the Novissi USSD platform as a "facility" and each household as a "demand point" — to compute an effective programme accessibility index that combines all six barriers into one spatially resolved score, identifying which cantons are structurally excluded beyond what the ML algorithm can fix.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my R2SFCA/2SFCA service-area delineation framework: substituting a USSD registration platform for a healthcare facility and the six-barrier cascade for a distance-decay function. The analogy is tight — both contexts involve a population competing for a fixed-capacity service, with differential access by socioeconomic status. Running the Togo analysis with R2SFCA would benchmark whether spatial accessibility metrics catch exclusion patterns that the paper's aggregate rates miss, strengthening the methodological case for 2SFCA in non-health domains.

④ Extend the fairness audit to spatial fairness and build an explainability map of algorithmic bias

Extending this paperThe paper's fairness analysis is tabular: it compares demographic parity and rank residuals across subgroups defined by gender, ethnicity, religion. It does not ask where in space the largest fairness violations occur, nor which CDR features drive those violations. An extension using SHAP values geographically aggregated by canton could produce an explainability map: for each canton, which features most inflate or deflate wealth predictions, and do cantons with high SHAP contributions for location features (dominant in Extended Data Table 3) correspond to ethnic or linguistic minority areas? This would transform the fairness audit from a summary statistic into a spatially actionable tool that planners can use to identify where the model needs local recalibration.

Added value to my researchProducing spatially explicit SHAP maps is a core output of my explainable machine learning for spatial prediction research agenda. Applying SHAP to the Togo CDR model over Togo's 397 cantons would serve as a transfer-learning benchmark: does the geographic heterogeneity in feature importance mirror spatial non-stationarity captured by MGWR, or does the ML model learn something that linear spatial regression misses? This directly connects my GWR/MGWR and XAI work, and gives a concrete humanitarian-policy application to an otherwise methodological line of inquiry.

⑤ Transfer the Togo pipeline to urban healthcare facility accessibility in LMICs using mobile phone mobility

Extending this paperThe paper's core insight — that CDR can substitute for missing administrative data during a crisis — generalizes beyond cash targeting to any public-goods allocation problem in LMICs. Specifically, the same trained poverty score (or a similarly derived health vulnerability index) could inform healthcare facility siting and capacity allocation: in countries without census-linked health insurance records, a CDR-based vulnerability score could replace income data in a facility-location optimization model. The paper's own exclusion analysis (65% phone ownership in Togo, dropping to 50% in rural areas) defines the demographic floor for this approach, and the LightGBM feature importances (Extended Data Table 3) already reveal that mobile data usage and mobile money patterns are top predictors — signals plausibly correlated with healthcare-seeking behaviour.

Added value to my researchThis is precisely the bridge between my health geography and accessibility research and multi-source data fusion. In settings where I currently use census data or survey data to define demand in 2SFCA models, a CDR-derived vulnerability index could serve as a demand weight — dynamically updateable after crises, sensitive to seasonal variation in mobility, and disaggregated to the individual rather than the census tract. Combining the Togo methodology with my spatial accessibility modelling would produce a prototype crisis-adaptive healthcare accessibility framework, applicable to Sub-Saharan Africa or South/Southeast Asia where CDR data are increasingly accessible through research partnerships with MNOs.
