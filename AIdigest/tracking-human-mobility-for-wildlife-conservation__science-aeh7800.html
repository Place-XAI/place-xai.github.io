Tracking human mobility for wildlife conservation

Author list not provided in the available metadata (Science Perspective) Published 2026-05-21 DOI 10.1126/science.aeh7800 Source science.org ↗
Core Conclusion

Large-scale human mobility data — the same anonymized device, GPS, and movement records used to study cities and epidemics — can expand ecological analyses of how people affect wild animals. By measuring where and when people actually go (not just where they live), the piece argues mobility data turn the diffuse "human footprint" into a dynamic, behaviorally explicit driver of wildlife disturbance, providing a new evidence base for conservation.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reframes the human side of human–wildlife conflict. Conservation has long quantified animal movement (telemetry, GPS collars) but treated human presence as static land-use. Bringing human mobility data in makes the people as dynamic and measurable as the animals.
  • Upgrades the "human footprint" from a map to a clock. Static disturbance layers (roads, settlements, night lights) miss daily and seasonal pulses of human activity; mobility traces capture when and how intensely humans actually occupy a landscape.
  • Connects two data revolutions. It bridges the mobility-data community (phones, social media, traffic) with movement ecology, suggesting a shared analytical toolkit for the coupled human–natural system.
  • Policy and management relevance. Dynamic exposure estimates can inform protected-area zoning, seasonal access rules, recreation management, and rapid response to disturbance hotspots.
