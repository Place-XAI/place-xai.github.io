Impact of colour vision deficiency on bladder and colorectal cancer survival

Mustafa Fattah · Amer F. Alsoudi · Prithvi Mruthyunjaya · Ehsan Rahimy Published 2026-01-15 DOI 10.1038/s44360-025-00032-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Patients with colour vision deficiency (CVD) and bladder cancer show significantly shorter overall survival and a 52% higher 20-year mortality rate (RR = 1.52, 95% CI = 1.05–2.19, P = 0.025) compared to propensity-score-matched controls without CVD. No significant survival difference was found for colorectal cancer, likely because colorectal cancer presents with additional symptoms and benefits from routine screening — whereas bladder cancer relies almost exclusively on the patient noticing blood in urine. This hypothesis-generating study calls on clinicians to heighten diagnostic suspicion for bladder cancer in CVD patients and consider targeted bladder cancer screening for this high-risk group.

Why It Matters

  • Identifies an overlooked high-risk population. CVD affects 1 in 12 males and 1 in 200 females — a large inherited condition systematically excluded from standard cancer-risk frameworks because visual status is rarely coded or queried in oncology workups.
  • Establishes the first real-world survival evidence. Prior literature was limited to case reports and a single small study on histological staging; this is the first large-scale analysis linking CVD to quantified mortality outcomes in bladder cancer.
  • Explains why painless haematuria is uniquely dangerous for CVD patients. The asymmetry between bladder cancer (80–90% presenting with only painless blood in urine) and colorectal cancer (multiple other warning signs + routine screening) elegantly explains the divergent mortality findings.
  • Points toward a concrete clinical action. The US Preventive Services Task Force currently recommends no asymptomatic bladder cancer screening; this study provides evidence to revisit that recommendation for CVD patients specifically.
  • Highlights misclassification bias from under-diagnosis of CVD, suggesting that the true effect size may be even larger, given that only 11 US states mandate colour-vision testing for schoolchildren.

Core Methods

  • Retrospective cohort design: ICD-10/ICD-O codes used to define CVD and cancer cohorts in the TriNetX platform, comparing outcomes between CVD and non-CVD groups.
  • Propensity score matching (PSM): 1:1 nearest-neighbour greedy matching with a caliper of 0.25 SD, balancing age, age at cancer diagnosis, sex, race, ethnicity, and systemic comorbidities (hypertension, diabetes, hyperlipidaemia, nicotine dependence).
  • Kaplan–Meier survival analysis: survival probability curves compared between CVD and non-CVD cohorts for each cancer type, with log-rank testing.
  • Log-rank test: χ² statistic used to assess significance of survival differences (bladder cancer: χ² = 4.85, P = 0.028; colorectal: χ² = 0.285, P = 0.593).
  • 20-year mortality risk analysis: conducted as a secondary outcome for bladder cancer only (following a pre-specified fixed-sequence method), yielding a risk ratio with 95% CI.
  • Fixed-sequence analysis strategy: 20-year mortality analysis performed only if the primary survival analysis showed a significant difference — minimizing multiple comparisons concerns.

① Integrate spatial accessibility of urology services into the CVD-bladder-cancer risk model

Extending this paperThis paper identifies CVD patients as a high-risk group for bladder cancer mortality, but treats all CVD patients as a single stratum ignoring where they live. A direct extension: link the TriNetX cohort's geographic indicators to spatial accessibility measures for urology clinics. Patients with CVD living in areas with poor urological care access face a compounded hazard — both sensory inability to detect haematuria and structural barriers to timely evaluation. Incorporating spatial access into the propensity score matching or as a post-hoc moderator would reveal whether geographic healthcare deserts amplify CVD-related mortality risk, and would sharpen the paper's call for targeted screening.

② Use explainable AI on EHR data to detect CVD-cancer comorbidity patterns missed by current ICD coding

Extending this paperA major acknowledged limitation is misclassification bias: many CVD cases go uncoded in EHRs (only 11 US states mandate school colour-vision screening), diluting the estimated effect. A powerful extension: train an explainable ML classifier on all available EHR features (medication history, ophthalmology visit patterns, visual acuity codes, occupational codes, self-reported difficulty) to probabilistically identify undiagnosed CVD and use that posterior probability as a continuous exposure variable in the survival model. This would reduce misclassification and likely recover a larger, better-estimated effect, while the explainability layer would reveal which EHR signals most reliably indicate undiagnosed CVD.

③ Extend to urological cancer risk from environmental exposures for a population-level prevention framework

Extending this paperThis paper focuses on a single sensory mechanism (haematuria recognition failure) driving bladder cancer mortality in CVD patients. ④ Build an AI-powered multi-cancer early-warning system explicitly incorporating sensory accessibility

Extending this paperThis paper's core insight — that a sensory limitation (inability to see red blood) prevents patients from self-screening for a life-threatening disease — can be generalized. An extension: design a sensory-adjusted foundation model for cancer screening that explicitly encodes patient sensory profiles (colour-blindness type, visual acuity, hearing impairment) as conditioning variables. Such a model would recommend alternative screening pathways (e.g., dipstick urine tests mailed to CVD patients rather than self-observation) calibrated to each patient's sensory limitations. The paper's corpus neighbour on multi-cancer CT screening offers a computational base for the screening model itself.

⑤ Quantify the economic burden of under-screened CVD-bladder cancer cases to motivate policy change

Extending this paperThe paper argues for bladder cancer screening among CVD patients but does not quantify the cost-effectiveness or economic burden of the status quo. A direct extension: combine the paper's 52% elevated 20-year mortality risk with disease-stage-specific treatment cost data and life-years-lost estimates. Because bladder cancer detected at stage Ta/Tis costs substantially less to treat than at T1+ (the stage CVD patients disproportionately present at, per prior work cited in this paper), screening could be economically dominant — earlier detection would reduce both mortality and treatment expenditure. Health-augmented macroeconomic modelling, as demonstrated for digestive tract diseases in the corpus, could project the national-scale economic burden attributable to CVD-related delayed bladder cancer detection.
