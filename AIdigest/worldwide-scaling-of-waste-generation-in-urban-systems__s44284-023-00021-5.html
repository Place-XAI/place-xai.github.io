Worldwide scaling of waste generation in urban systems

Mingzhen Lu · Chuanbin Zhou · Chenghao Wang · Robert B. Jackson · Christopher P. Kempes Published 2024-01-17 DOI 10.1038/s44284-023-00021-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across more than 1,000 cities in 171 countries, the three major waste streams obey distinct universal scaling laws with city size: wastewater scales superlinearly (β ≈ 1.15), municipal solid waste (MSW) scales linearly (β ≈ 1.04), and greenhouse-gas (GHG) emissions scale sublinearly (β ≈ 0.85). Deviations from these laws are explained by per-capita GDP and local rainfall — richer (and wetter) cities are more wasteful for their size. Crucially, the relationships evolve over time toward higher waste: in China's cities MSW is drifting from sublinear toward linear, eroding the historical economy of scale and signalling a "vicious circle" of rising per-capita waste under urbanization.

★

Why It Matters

  • First worldwide scaling theory of waste. The connection between societal urbanization shifts and waste production had never been described; this paper supplies universal, predictive laws spanning cities of 50,000 to 24 million people.
  • Different waste, different physics. Showing that wastewater (super-), MSW (linear) and GHG (sub-) scale differently means each stream needs a different policy logic — economies of scale exist for emissions but actively work against us for wastewater.
  • A diagnostic for city-level efficiency. Scale-adjusted residuals isolate which cities are "wasteful" vs. "efficient" after removing the size effect, turning abstract data points (Dongguan vs. Tianshui, Seattle vs. Lilongwe) into actionable benchmarks.
  • Forecasting the waste future. With urban population projected to grow ~60% over 30 years, doubling a 1-million city would raise wastewater by 122% — quantifying the stakes of superlinear waste under continued urbanization.
  • Exposes a data desert. By spanning nine waste types at country level (construction, agricultural, industrial, hazardous, e-waste, medical) it reveals how little city-level data exist for most waste streams — an agenda for future monitoring.

Core Methods

  • Urban scaling theory: fit the power law Y = Y₀·N^β relating waste production Y to urban population N; the exponent β (sub-/linear/super-) diagnoses economy or diseconomy of scale and points to underlying mechanisms.
  • Log-log regression: both waste rate and population log₁₀-transformed; scaling estimated per dataset (never pooled across waste types). Robustness checked via binned-city regression to remove over-representation of small cities.
  • Scale-adjusted residuals: deviations from the scaling line define city-specific over/under-performance; residuals regressed on per-capita GDP and precipitation via planar / nonlinear functions (Fig. 2).
  • Temporal scaling: re-estimate β year by year (2006–2020 China; 2012–2019 C40) to track whether economies of scale strengthen or erode through time (Fig. 3).
  • Wasteful-vs-efficient classification: cities split at the 25%/75% residual quantiles; differences tested with Welch's / Student's t-tests after F-test for equal variance.
  • Country-level accounting: linear models + pairwise contrasts (emmeans) across nine waste types to estimate waste ratios relative to MSW (Fig. 5). All analyses in R v4.2.0.

① Disentangle population from density and area in the waste exponents

Extending this paperThe paper scales waste against a single size variable (urban population N) and reads β as mechanism. But population, density and built area are correlated, and an exponent fitted to N alone can absorb density effects — exactly the confound shown for urban CO₂. A direct extension: refit each waste stream as a multivariate scaling surface in (population, density, area), testing whether wastewater's superlinearity survives once density is separated, and whether GHG's economy of scale is really a density economy.

Added value to my researchThis sharpens my explainable-ML spatial prediction work: it is a clean template for decomposing a coupled space–human signal into orthogonal drivers, and for using MGWR/GWR to let the "scaling exponent" itself vary across space rather than be a single global constant.

Cross-ref (corpus): Effects of changing population or density on urban carbon dioxide emissions — 10.1038/s41467-019-11184-y

② Push the scale-adjusted residual from aggregate to within-city inequality

Extending this paperThe paper's residual is one number per city — it cannot see that waste, like income, is heavy-tailed inside a city. Extending it with intra-urban microdata (neighbourhood wastewater, waste-collection routes) would test whether the global exponents emerge from within-city inequalities, and whether "wasteful" cities are wasteful everywhere or only in affluent sub-areas.

Added value to my researchThis maps onto my spatial-entropy and inequality agenda: the same decomposition (between-city scaling vs. within-city dispersion) is exactly how I want to characterize accessibility inequity, letting a single residual become a spatially-resolved equity surface.

Cross-ref (corpus): Urban scaling laws arise from within-city inequalities — 10.1038/s41562-022-01509-1 · Explaining the prevalence, scaling and variance of urban phenomena — 10.1038/s41562-016-0012

③ Couple the waste streams instead of scaling them separately

Extending this paperThe paper deliberately keeps wastewater, MSW and GHG in separate datasets and separate scaling fits. Yet they are physically coupled — food waste can be diverted into sewage, and treatment emits GHG. Extending it into a coupled urban-metabolism model would let one stream's scaling residual predict another's, turning three parallel laws into a single material-flow system.

Added value to my researchThis is a concrete multi-source data fusion case for me: jointly modelling coupled spatial outcomes (analogous to fusing mobile-phone, remote-sensing and POI signals) and validating with physics-informed constraints (mass balance across streams).

Cross-ref (corpus): Redefining separate or integrated food waste and wastewater streams for 29 large cities — 10.1038/s44284-025-00341-8 · Physical and virtual carbon metabolism of global cities — 10.1038/s41467-019-13757-3

④ Stress-test the GHG scaling against inventory under-reporting

Extending this paperThe GHG result (β≈0.85, r²≈0.47, scope-3 excluded) leans on self-reported city inventories (Nangini2019, C40) that are known to be systematically biased. Extending it: re-estimate the GHG exponent after bias-correcting against independent bottom-up estimates (e.g., Vulcan-style), and test whether the apparent economy of scale partly reflects larger cities under-reporting more.

Added value to my researchThis is an uncertainty-aware reproducibility exercise central to my open-science/FAIR work: propagating measurement error into a scaling estimate, and using explainable ML to flag which cities' residuals are "real" vs. reporting artefacts.

Cross-ref (corpus): Under-reporting of greenhouse gas emissions in U.S. cities — 10.1038/s41467-020-20871-0

⑤ Turn the temporal exponent drift into a forecasting / early-warning model

Extending this paperThe paper observes MSW's exponent drifting from sublinear toward linear in China but stops at description. Extending it: model β(t) as a dynamical state driven by GDP growth and urbanization, forecast where each waste stream's exponent is heading, and flag cities approaching a "vicious-circle" tipping point — converting a retrospective trend into a forward early-warning indicator for net-zero/zero-waste policy.
