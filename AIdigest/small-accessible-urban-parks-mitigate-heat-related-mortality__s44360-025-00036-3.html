Small accessible urban parks mitigate heat-related mortality

He, Cheng · Yin, Peng · Chen, Renjie · Wang, Li · Wang, Yuan · Schneider, Alexandra · Kan, Haidong · Zhou, Maigeng Published 2026-01-15 DOI 10.1038/s44360-025-00036-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 265 Chinese cities, green space configuration — not merely quantity — is the key determinant of heatwave-related mortality risk. Cities with higher green coverage (PLAND), more dispersed layouts (SPLIT) and more regular patch boundaries (low LSI) show substantially lower mortality risk during heatwaves. Crucially, simply increasing the number of green patches or enlarging a single large park does not significantly reduce heat mortality. Urban planning should prioritize multiple small, widely accessible community parks over a few large concentrated ones to maximize public health resilience against intensifying heatwave threats.

Why It Matters

  • Shifts the policy lever from coverage to configuration. Prior research focused on how much green space a city has; this study is among the first to show how it is arranged matters equally or more for heat-mortality outcomes, changing the calculus for urban planners worldwide.
  • Quantifies the health benefit with epidemiological precision. Using over 11.9 million non-accidental deaths (2013–2019) from China's national Death Surveillance Points System, the study estimates relative risks (RR 1.04–1.13) that are attributable to specific landscape metrics — actionable numbers, not just correlations.
  • Challenges the "bigger park is better" orthodoxy. The dominant patch index (LPI) and patch density (PD) show no significant mortality-modifying effect, directly contradicting cooling-focused studies that favor larger, contiguous green areas.
  • Opens a new pathway for climate-adaptive urban design. As heatwaves intensify with climate change and urban heat islands expand, distributing small cooling refuges equitably across neighborhoods is a low-cost, high-impact intervention the findings now quantitatively support.
  • Cross-disciplinary integration. By bridging landscape ecology (Fragstats metrics) and environmental epidemiology (two-stage time-series meta-analysis), the study creates a replicable template for multi-city, multi-exposure health research.

Core Methods

  • Landscape metric computation: five metrics — PLAND, PD, LPI, LSI, SPLIT — calculated using Fragstats v4.2 on EULUC-China green-space polygons within each city boundary. Metrics capture coverage, density, dominance, boundary complexity, and spatial fragmentation respectively. Each metric is categorized into tertile-based low/medium/high groups to avoid collinearity (VIF < 1.5 for all metrics).
  • Heatwave definition: four definitions combining two temperature percentile thresholds (92.5th and 95th of daily mean temperature) with two minimum-duration criteria (≥2 consecutive days, ≥3 consecutive days) — producing comparable heatwave events across cities with differing climate baselines.
  • Two-stage time-series meta-analysis: Stage 1 — city-specific quasi-Poisson regression with distributed-lag models estimating relative risk (RR) of heatwave exposure on daily mortality, controlling for long-term trends, day-of-week effects, and meteorological confounders. Stage 2 — random-effects meta-regression pooling city-specific RRs, stratified by landscape metric tertile groups.
  • Effect modification testing: multivariate Wald tests comparing cumulative RRs across landscape metric strata; between-group differences considered significant at P < 0.05 (two-sided).
  • Subgroup analyses: stratified by age group (5–64 years; 65+ years) and Köppen climate zone (temperate, continental, arid).
  • Sensitivity analyses: alternative heatwave definitions using daily maximum temperature; varying degrees of freedom; lag period extended to 14 days; controlling for daily PM2.5; alternative green space dataset (Global Forest Change v1.3); population-weighted ERA5-Land temperature replacing station data.

① Spatial accessibility as the mediating mechanism — directly measuring green-space reach

Extending this paperThe paper infers accessibility via the SPLIT index (more dispersed patches = broader accessibility) but never measures actual residential walking distance to the nearest park. A direct extension: compute 2SFCA or R2SFCA green-space accessibility scores for all 265 cities using the EULUC-China park polygons and WorldPop population grids, then test whether the accessibility score mediates (or replaces) SPLIT in the meta-regression. This would translate the paper's landscape-ecology framing into a concrete service-area measure and test whether the "dispersion" story is really an "accessibility" story.

Added value to my researchMy 2SFCA and R2SFCA toolkit — built for healthcare facility access — can be directly re-parameterized for parks: supply = cooling capacity (green area × canopy density), demand = population × heat vulnerability index, distance decay calibrated to observed park visitation. This paper's 265-city mortality outcome database becomes the ground-truth endpoint for validating whether my accessibility scores actually predict protective health effects at city scale — a rare opportunity to close the loop between access measurement and health outcome.

② Explainable GeoAI to unpack spatial heterogeneity of green-space health benefits

Extending this paperThe paper's meta-regression finds that three of five landscape metrics modify heatwave mortality at the national pooled level, but concedes that subgroup analyses by climate zone lack statistical power. A spatial extension: train a geographically weighted or gradient-boosted model predicting city-level RR from all five landscape metrics plus auxiliary urban covariates (building density, road imperviousness, socio-economic indicators, PM2.5), then use SHAP values to map which metric dominates heat protection in which city typologies. This would reveal whether SPLIT matters more in hot-arid cities and PLAND more in humid ones — turning the paper's pooled estimates into spatially explicit policy guidance.

The landscape metrics become spatial features; heatwave-mortality RR becomes the target variable — a textbook case for my GeoAI-for-health methodology, yielding both a published model and a methodology paper on spatial feature attribution in environmental epidemiology.

③ Optimizing green-space placement as a multi-objective facility-location problem

Extending this paperThe paper identifies that dispersed small parks (high SPLIT, regular boundaries) outperform concentrated large ones for heat-mortality reduction, but stops short of prescribing where to add or resize parks within a city. Extending it: recast green-space planning as a facility-location optimization — given a fixed budget (area to add), place new park parcels to maximize SPLIT gain while minimizing LSI, subject to equity constraints (coverage of high-vulnerability populations). The paper's mortality-RR estimates provide the objective function weights. This closes the gap between its descriptive findings and actionable spatial planning.

Added value to my researchI have experience optimizing EV charging station placement under efficiency–equity trade-offs. Green-space siting is structurally identical: supply nodes (parks) serving demand nodes (population), with a health benefit objective that the paper now quantifies in RR units. My optimization framework — integer programming with equity constraints and spatial interaction terms — can be repurposed directly, and the result would be a GeoAI tool for climate-adaptive urban park planning applicable far beyond China.

④ Integrating mobile-phone mobility data to reveal who actually accesses urban parks during heatwaves

Extending this paperThe EULUC-China dataset used in the paper was itself built partly from Tencent mobile-phone location request data, yet the study does not leverage mobility data to measure actual park visitation. A powerful extension: use high-frequency mobile-phone GPS traces or signaling data to measure actual park visit rates during heatwave days per city, and model whether observed visit rates mediate the SPLIT–mortality relationship. This would confirm (or challenge) the paper's theoretical mechanism — that dispersed parks provide cooling "refuges" that people actually use — with behavioral evidence.

Applying this to park visitation during extreme heat events creates a new use case: a heat-stress mobility signature that captures when and where people seek refuge, which can be integrated into an agent-based model of heat exposure to simulate the population-level mortality impact of different park configurations — directly extending this paper's findings into a behavioral simulation framework.

⑤ Cross-city generalization via a physics-informed spatiotemporal model of green-space cooling

Extending this paperThe paper is limited to 265 Chinese cities and acknowledges that results may not generalize to other climates, lifestyles, and health systems. Extending it: build a physics-informed spatiotemporal model that encodes biophysical cooling mechanisms (transpiration flux as a function of PLAND, boundary conditions captured by LSI, and fragmentation captured by SPLIT) as structural constraints in a graph neural network trained jointly across diverse global cities. The paper's Chinese estimates serve as one training domain; ERA5-Land temperature data and WorldPop provide the global input features. This would produce a transferable green-space health model valid across climate zones.
