Regional ambient temperature is associated with human personality

Wenqi Wei · Jackson G. Lu · Adam D. Galinsky · Han Wu · Samuel D. Gosling · Peter J. Rentfrow · Wenjie Yuan · Qi Zhang · Yongyu Guo · Ming Zhang · and 16 more Published 2017-11-27 DOI 10.1038/s41562-017-0240-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across two large-scale studies in China (N = 5,587, 59 cities) and the United States (N = 1,660,638, 12,499 ZIP codes), individuals who grew up in regions with temperatures closer to the psychophysiological comfort optimum of 22 °C scored consistently higher on all five personality dimensions — particularly the socialization/stability factor (Alpha: agreeableness, conscientiousness, emotional stability) and the personal growth/plasticity factor (Beta: extraversion, openness). These effects held after controlling for selective migration, subsistence style, pathogen prevalence, demographics, and GDP, suggesting that ambient temperature is an independent environmental driver of the geographical variation in human personality observed worldwide — and implies that ongoing climate change may bring measurable shifts in population-level personality profiles.

Why It Matters

  • Identifies a physical-environment driver of personality geography. Previous theories (subsistence style, selective migration, pathogen prevalence) focused on social and biological mechanisms; this paper adds ambient temperature as a primary, directly measurable environmental antecedent — offering a new causal layer upstream of existing explanations.
  • Cross-national replication at unprecedented scale. Converging evidence from two culturally distinct countries — a city-level Chinese sample and a ZIP-code-level US sample of over 1.6 million respondents — substantially strengthens the claim beyond single-country studies.
  • Climate change implications for human character. If personality is shaped by ambient temperature during upbringing, then the global warming trajectory implies foreseeable, measurable shifts in population-level personality distributions — a novel and policy-relevant dimension of climate-change consequences.
  • Methodological advance in personality geography. By analyzing at the lowest feasible geographical units (city and ZIP code) within countries and explicitly controlling for confounders, the paper overcomes the between-country cultural confounding that plagued prior cross-national work.

Core Methods

  • Temperature clemency operationalization: a non-linear transformation −|mean temperature − 22 °C| captures distance from the psychophysiological comfort optimum; robustness version uses min/max temperatures.
  • Multilevel (hierarchical) modelling: individuals (level 1) nested within cities / ZIP codes (level 2); accounts for intra-cluster dependence and unequal sample sizes; conducted separately for China and US.
  • Individual-level controls: age, gender, education (US only), and a computed acquiescent response style score to neutralize yea/nay-saying bias.
  • City/ZIP-code-level controls: GDP per capita, population density, agricultural subsistence proxies (rice/wheat farming area or employment sector fractions), influenza incidence (China), humidity and wind speed (US).
  • Conditional random forest (machine learning): variable-importance analysis (Fig. 3) used to identify statistically significant predictors of each Big Five dimension and the Alpha/Beta composites, providing a non-parametric complement to multilevel regression.
  • Effect size standardization: partial effect sizes reported as t-to-r transformations for cross-predictor comparability (Fig. 2).
  • Reverse-causality control (Study 1): restricted to students who spent youth in birthplace and whose birthplace matched ancestral home (jiguan), excluding selection effects.

① Map temperature clemency onto urban micro-climates to detect intra-city personality gradients

Extending this paperThe paper works at the city or ZIP-code level, treating each as a single temperature value. Yet within large cities, the urban heat island effect can create temperature variations of 3–5 °C across neighbourhoods — exceeding the inter-city variation used in the paper's US study. Extending this paper with fine-grained satellite-derived land surface temperature maps (e.g., MODIS/Landsat) at the sub-ZIP level would test whether the temperature–personality link scales down to the neighbourhood, and would reveal whether the 22 °C optimum holds within cities or only across them. This matters especially for growing megacities under climate change, where intra-urban warming may diversify personality micro-geographies.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal analysis toolkit — remote sensing data fusion and spatiotemporal kernel density estimation — is well suited to constructing neighbourhood-level thermal exposure grids. Coupling those grids with geotagged social media sentiment (like the Weibo dataset used in the air-pollution–happiness study) would let me test whether thermally stressed urban micro-zones exhibit systematically lower expressed extraversion and openness, feeding directly into my urban health geography work on environmental exposures and subjective well-being.

② Replace the static temperature average with time-varying climate exposure trajectories in childhood

Extending this paperThe paper uses a 40-year average temperature as the "ambient" exposure for each city, which collapses the entire developmental period into a single scalar. But personality development is not uniform: the formative windows (early childhood vs. adolescence) likely differ in temperature sensitivity. Extending the paper by constructing year-by-year temperature trajectories matched to each participant's birth year and birthplace — and using growth-curve or survival modelling — would reveal which developmental stage is the critical window for temperature's effect on personality. It would also enable testing whether warming trends (rather than absolute level) drive personality change over cohorts.

Added value to my researchThis longitudinal thermal-exposure design maps naturally onto my interest in physics-informed spatiotemporal models: I could encode a developmental "thermal budget" as a cumulative state variable in a graph network, where the paper's 22 °C-distance measure becomes a time-integrated deviation signal. Such a physics-grounded state variable would be more interpretable — and more transferable across cities — than a purely learned embedding.

③ Test whether the climate–personality link mediates the known effect of heat exposure on learning and economic outcomes

Extending this paperA parallel corpus paper shows that heat exposure inhibits learning and human capital formation. The present paper shows that clement temperature promotes openness to experience and conscientiousness — traits strongly linked to academic engagement and learning. A direct extension: formally test whether personality (especially Beta/Alpha factors) is a mediator of the heat–human-capital pathway. This would require a three-variable multilevel mediation model (temperature → personality → educational attainment/learning) using data from both studies, extending the causal chain the paper only hints at in discussion.

Added value to my researchThis mediation framework is directly relevant to my health geography work: if temperature shapes personality which in turn shapes health behaviours and care-seeking, it provides a mechanistic route for integrating climate variables into my spatial accessibility models. A spatially explicit mediation model — where both the exposure (temperature) and the outcome (healthcare use) vary across ZIP codes — would be a natural application of my MGWR/GWR methods with a behavioural mediator.

④ Apply explainable ML to disentangle temperature clemency from correlated ecological and cultural confounders

Extending this paperThe paper's conditional random forest analysis (Fig. 3) identifies temperature clemency as the top predictor but does not decompose the interaction structure between temperature, subsistence style, pathogen prevalence, and GDP. SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values applied to a richer multi-country gradient-boosted model would quantify the marginal contribution of temperature clemency across different economic and ecological regimes — revealing, for instance, whether the temperature effect is moderated by GDP (perhaps clemency matters more in poor, outdoor-dependent economies) or by pathogen load. This would sharpen the paper's claim of "temperature as antecedent" versus "temperature as correlated proxy."

⑤ Link the paper's city-level personality profiles to urban mobility patterns and public-space use

Extending this paperThe paper's mechanism — that clement temperatures encourage outdoor exploration and social contact, which then shapes personality — implies a testable behavioural fingerprint: cities with higher Beta scores (extraversion, openness) should exhibit more diverse, longer-range mobility trajectories and higher visit rates to public and recreational spaces. Coupling the paper's city-level personality scores with anonymized mobile-phone GPS trajectories or aggregated location data would close the loop on the proposed mechanism, turning the claimed behavioural pathway (temperature → outdoor activity → personality) into a directly observed mediating variable rather than a theoretical conjecture.
