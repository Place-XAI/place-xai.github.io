Understanding and combatting misinformation across 16 countries on six continents

Antonio A. Arechar · Jennifer Allen · Adam J. Berinsky · Rocky Cole · Ziv Epstein · Kiran Garimella · Andrew Gully · Jackson G. Lu · Robert M. Ross · Michael N. Stagnaro · Yunhao Zhang · Gordon Pennycook · David G. Rand Published 2023-06-29 DOI 10.1038/s41562-023-01641-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 16 countries on 6 continents (N = 34,286; 676,605 observations), analytic thinking, accuracy motivations, and valuing democracy consistently predict better COVID-19 misinformation discernment in every country tested. Subtly prompting people to think about accuracy and providing minimal digital literacy tips broadly reduce misinformation sharing intentions across countries. Aggregating as few as 15 layperson ratings per headline reliably differentiates true from false claims via the "wisdom of crowds" — suggesting that the psychological architecture of the misinformation problem, and its solutions, may be cross-culturally universal.

Why It Matters

  • First genuinely global RCT on misinformation psychology. With 34,286 participants across 16 countries spanning all six inhabited continents, this is one of the largest simultaneous cross-cultural misinformation experiments, moving the field far beyond WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic) samples.
  • Discovers universal cognitive predictors. Analytic thinking and accuracy-related motivations predict truth discernment in every single country — establishing cross-cultural psychological regularities rather than culturally idiosyncratic findings.
  • Validates scalable, low-cost interventions globally. Simple accuracy prompts and minimal digital literacy tips both improve sharing discernment across countries, giving social media platforms concrete, implementable tools without culture-specific customization.
  • Unlocks crowdsourced fact-checking at scale. Only 10–15 lay ratings per headline achieve over 80–90% AUC in distinguishing true from false claims in all countries — a practically deployable alternative to resource-scarce professional fact-checking worldwide.
  • Identifies differential vulnerability. Younger, less urban, lower-SES participants and those in more collectivist or higher-corruption contexts show lower truth discernment — pinpointing exactly where anti-misinformation interventions and digital-literacy resources should be prioritized.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Experimental corpus
45 COVID-19 news headlines (30 false, 15 true) sourced from the WHO's COVID-19 myth-busting list and country-specific fact-checking websites; each participant rated a random sample of 10 true + 10 false headlines (one at a time).
Participants
34,286 respondents with at least one rating (676,605 total observations); 33,480 with a complete 20-item set. Recruited via Lucid Marketplace with country-level age-and-sex quota matching. Median completion time 15:42 min. Data collection ran 63 days (22 February – 25 April 2021).
Countries & languages
16 countries across 6 continents: Argentina, Australia, Brazil, China, Egypt, India, Italy, Mexico, Nigeria, Philippines, Russia, Saudi Arabia, Spain, UK, USA, South Africa. Survey administered in 9 languages (Arabic, English, Hindi, Italian, Mandarin, Portuguese, Russian, Spanish, Tagalog) with professional translation and back-translation verification.
Survey instrument
71-item Qualtrics questionnaire; 20 individual-difference measures (3-item CRT, analytic style self-report, accuracy motivations, World Values Survey ideology items, trust, demographics); four randomized conditions (Accuracy, Sharing, Prompt, Tips).
Cultural validation
World Values Survey national estimates used to calibrate samples on four cultural value items in most countries.
Open data & code
All data, analysis code (R 3.6.1, Stata 15), pre-registration, and materials openly deposited at osf.io/g65qu.

Core Methods

  • Pre-registered multi-country randomized experiment: four conditions (Accuracy, Sharing, Prompt, Tips) with balanced random assignment within each of 16 countries, administered simultaneously during February–April 2021.
  • Random-effects meta-analysis: for each of 20 individual-difference predictors, a rating-level OLS regression (two-way robust SEs clustered on subject and headline) is run per country; a random-effects meta-analysis pools estimates and quantifies cross-country heterogeneity via I².
  • Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT): 3-item validated measure of analytic thinking (combined with self-report analytic style) used as the primary cognitive predictor of truth discernment.
  • Experimental treatment-effect estimation: conditions compared using uncontrolled between-subjects comparisons (randomization removes confounding); sharing discernment = mean sharing of true minus mean sharing of false headlines.
  • Wisdom-of-crowds AUC analysis: bootstrap sampling of k raters (k = 1–25) per headline; AUC = probability that aggregate crowd correctly ranks a true headline above a false one — computed for each country and each k.
  • Country-level moderation: three-way interaction multilevel models (headline veracity × individual difference × country-level variable) testing GDP, HDI, collectivism, power distance, and corruption as moderators of individual-level effects.
  • Software: R 3.6.1 / RStudio 2022.07.0+548 and Stata 15; code at osf.io/g65qu.

A direct extension: geo-code participants at the sub-national level and link their truth-discernment scores to fine-grained urban–rural gradients, street connectivity, broadband coverage, and neighborhood amenity access. ④ Turn the wisdom-of-crowds AUC curve into an adaptive crowd-sampling optimizer

Extending this paperFig. 6 demonstrates that AUC rises steeply with the first 10–15 raters per headline and then plateaus — a classic diminishing-returns curve. But the paper treats crowd size as the only optimization variable. Extending it: model crowd composition (analytic-style distribution, country mix, expertise level) alongside size, and derive an adaptive stopping rule — given a target AUC threshold and the current rater pool's profile, when should the platform stop soliciting additional ratings? This is a sequential decision problem in which each rating has a marginal cost (user fatigue, resource) and a marginal AUC gain that depends on the analytic heterogeneity of the existing pool. The paper's open data and within-country AUC curves provide the calibration data for such a model.
