Green roofs save energy in cities and fight regional climate change

Indira Adilkhanova · Mat Santamouris · Geun Young Yun Published 2024-02-12 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00035-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Coupling urban climate modeling (WRF) with urban building energy modeling (CitySim) and empirical data for Seoul, the study shows that the cooling efficacy of nonirrigated extensive green roofs is strongly governed by meteorological conditions — not a constant effect. Their city-scale cooling nonetheless cuts monthly urban building energy use intensity (EUI) by up to 0.64 kWh m−2 (7.7%), establishing green roofs as a credible nature-based solution that addresses urban overheating and building energy demand simultaneously.

★

Why It Matters

  • Closes a scale gap. Most green-roof studies measure thermal performance at the single-building level; this work quantifies the effect at the whole-city scale, where cooling feeds back into regional climate and aggregate energy demand.
  • Conditions the benefit on weather. By showing that nonirrigated extensive green roofs depend heavily on meteorological conditions (radiation, humidity, soil moisture), it warns against assuming a fixed savings number in design codes and incentive schemes.
  • Gives planners a hard number. A monthly EUI reduction of up to 0.64 kWh m−2 (7.7%) is a directly usable figure for cost–benefit and decarbonization planning of building stock.
  • Couples mitigation and adaptation. Green roofs are framed as one lever that simultaneously fights urban heat (adaptation) and energy/emissions (mitigation) — a rare dual win for climate-resilient cities.

Core Methods

  • Urban climate modeling: mesoscale WRF (v4.3) with urban canopy treatment, forced by ERA-Interim, simulates diurnal meteorology, air and surface temperature under each green-roof scenario.
  • Urban building energy modeling (UBEM): CitySim Pro (EPFL) translates the WRF-derived microclimate and the registry-based building stock into building-by-building energy demand and city-scale EUI.
  • Scenario comparison: baseline vs. nonirrigated extensive green-roof scenarios contrasted for cooling and energy effects across days (Figs. 3–4) and across months (Figs. 5–6).
  • Statistical analysis with empirical data: modeled fields validated against KMA AWS observations and metered gas/electricity consumption; sensitivity of green-roof efficacy to meteorological drivers quantified.
  • Disaggregation by building category: monthly EUI change reported per building type, exposing heterogeneity in who benefits most.

① From one city to a transferable, multi-city cooling–energy emulator

Extending this paperThe WRF + CitySim pipeline is calibrated on a single city (Seoul) and is computationally heavy to rerun. A direct extension: train a surrogate/emulator that maps (building stock, morphology, meteorology, green-roof coverage) → (city-scale cooling, EUI savings), then transfer it across the corpus of cities for which urban-scale nature-based cooling has been quantified — turning a bespoke case study into a fast, generalizable screening tool.

② Make the weather-dependence a learned response function

Extending this paperThe paper's headline insight — green-roof efficacy is strongly governed by meteorology — is reported descriptively. Extending it: fit an explicit response surface of cooling/EUI savings versus radiation, humidity, soil moisture and irrigation, so the benefit becomes a function planners can evaluate ex ante for any climate, rather than a single Seoul number that may not transfer to humid or arid cities.
