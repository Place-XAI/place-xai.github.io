Maternal metabolomic signatures for risk of preterm and early term birth in African American women

Tan, Youran · Liang, Donghai · Eick, Stephanie M. · Ren, Mengyuan · Walker, Douglas I. · Hu, Xin · Li, Zhenjiang · Taibl, Kaitlin R. · Corwin, Elizabeth · Hill, Cherie C. · Jones, Dean P. · Dunlop, Anne L. Published 2026-06-16 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00150-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

High-resolution untargeted metabolomics of maternal serum reveals distinct, phenotype-specific metabolic profiles that differentiate spontaneous from medically indicated preterm and early term birth among African American women. Perturbations in amino acid pathways (arginine, proline, methionine, branched-chain amino acids) dominate spontaneous early birth, while acylcarnitines and aldosterone characterize medically indicated birth. Thirteen metabolites were internally validated, supporting their potential as clinical biomarkers for pregnancies at elevated risk — a critical advance for a population experiencing PTB at nearly double the rate of non-Hispanic White women.

Why It Matters

  • Addresses an urgent, intractable health disparity. Non-Hispanic Black women experience preterm birth at nearly twice the rate of non-Hispanic White women — a gap that has not narrowed for decades. This study is one of the first MWAS studies exclusively in an African American cohort with rigorous internal validation.
  • Phenotypic granularity that prior studies lack. By separating spontaneous from medically indicated early birth across two gestational windows, the study reveals largely non-overlapping metabolic etiologies — a critical distinction for targeted prevention strategies.
  • Actionable biomarker panel. The six-metabolite panel for spontaneous PTB (AUC = 0.77) and 13 internally validated metabolites provide a foundation for prospective clinical biomarker development and targeted antenatal screening.
  • Captures exposome signals. Detection of xenobiotic metabolites (phthalates, cocaine-related compound) in untargeted metabolomics integrates environmental exposure data without requiring targeted assays, broadening the framework for health-risk profiling.
  • Open data for replication. Metabolomics data deposited in Metabolomics Workbench (ST002351) enables independent validation and meta-analysis across cohorts.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Study cohort
Atlanta African American Maternal-Child Cohort (2014–2018) — recruited from two prenatal clinics in Atlanta, Georgia. 279 participants in the discovery dataset and 251 in the internal validation dataset; all self-identified as African American or Black, singleton pregnancies, 18–40 years, without chronic conditions.
Outcomes
64 preterm births (PTB, <37 weeks), 54 early term births (ETB, 37–38 weeks), 161 healthy full-term births (HFTB, ≥39 weeks) in discovery. Births classified as spontaneous or medically indicated: sPTB (n=48), miPTB (n=16), sETB (n=40), miETB (n=14).
Biospecimen
Non-fasting venous serum samples collected at two prenatal windows: early pregnancy (8–14 weeks) and later pregnancy (24–30 weeks). Samples stored at −80 °C.
Metabolomics platform
Liquid chromatography–high-resolution mass spectrometry (Thermo Scientific Q-Exactive HF); each sample run in triplicate. Two complementary methods: HILIC with positive ESI (13,616 features) and C18 reversed-phase chromatography with negative ESI (11,900 features).
Feature filtering
Metabolic features retained if detected in >15% of samples, median CV <30% across technical replicates, and Pearson ρ >0.7. Final dataset characterized by m/z, retention time, and ion intensities.
Annotation databases
Human Metabolome Database (HMDB), METLIN, and the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG); mass error tolerance 10 ppm. Level 1 confirmation via in-house reference library with authentic standards.
Metabolomics data
Raw and processed data deposited in Metabolomics Workbench under study ID ST002351 (Emory Clinical Biomarker Laboratory). Clinical outcome and covariate data available under restricted access; code available from corresponding author (D.L.) within 10 business days.
Time window
Cohort enrolment 2014–2018; serum collected at gestational ages 8–14 weeks and 24–30 weeks.

Core Methods

  • Metabolome-wide association study (MWAS): logistic regression models for each metabolic feature (log2-transformed intensity) against each of four early birth phenotypes (sPTB, miPTB, sETB, miETB) and two composite categories (sPETB, miPETB), adjusting for infant sex, maternal age, parity, prenatal BMI, substance use, and education.
  • Multiple comparison control: false discovery rate correction using Benjamini–Hochberg (FDR q <0.20). Primary inference on raw P <0.05 with sensitivity analyses at P <0.01 and P <0.005.
  • Metabolic pathway enrichment: mummichog v2.0 with permutation-based EASE score (conservative Fisher's exact test) mapping significant m/z features to HMDB/KEGG pathways without requiring prior metabolite identification.
  • Metabolite annotation and confirmation: m/z matching to adduct forms (10 ppm tolerance), retention time and isotope distribution inspection, with level 1 confirmation against authentic in-house standards.
  • Predictive modeling: ROC analysis with binary logistic regression (80/20 train/test split, fixed random seed); multi-metabolite panel logistic regression; random forest classifier for variable importance ranking.
  • Internal validation: same analytical pipeline applied to an independent set of 251 participants from the same cohort; 13 level-1 metabolites validated at P <0.05 and 6 at FDR q <0.20.
  • Sensitivity analyses: stratification by medical indication; alternative P-value thresholds; multiple SES covariate specifications (education-only vs. all SES indicators vs. individual SES variables) to assess robustness to confounding.

① Geospatially stratify metabolomic risk by neighborhood environment and healthcare access

Extending this paperThe paper identifies metabolic signals of PTB risk within an African American cohort in Atlanta but treats all participants as spatially homogeneous — there is no spatial dimension in the MWAS models despite participants coming from two socioeconomically distinct prenatal clinics. A direct extension: geocode participant residential addresses and link them to neighborhood-level environmental stressors (air pollution, green space, food access, social vulnerability) and spatial accessibility to prenatal care. Then run spatially stratified or geographically weighted MWAS models to test whether the metabolite–birth associations are spatially non-stationary — that is, whether the arginine/proline pathway signal is stronger in neighborhoods with high pollution burden or low access to prenatal care.

Added value to my researchThis is precisely the intersection of my spatial accessibility methods (R2SFCA, 2SFCA) and health geography work. I can quantify spatial accessibility of prenatal care for each participant and use it as a MWAS covariate or moderator, testing whether poor access to care not only worsens birth outcomes (as in EHR reliability literature) but also shapes the metabolic profile of risk. My MGWR infrastructure handles spatial non-stationarity in continuous outcomes; extending it to metabolic feature-by-environment interaction studies is a direct methodological contribution.

② Integrate environmental metabolomics with racial stressor exposomics to decompose disparity mechanisms

Extending this paperThe paper captures xenobiotic metabolites (phthalates, cocaine metabolite) in untargeted MWAS, but SES covariates are used only as confounders, not as structural variables. An important extension: bring structural racism exposure metrics (e.g., area-level discrimination indices, redlining legacies, environmental justice burdens) into the MWAS framework as effect modifiers. This would quantify what fraction of the amino-acid pathway perturbations in the African American cohort are attributable to chronic stress pathways vs. environmental chemical co-exposures vs. healthcare access barriers, directly speaking to the mechanistic pathway from racism to birth disparity.

Combining my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation (for exposure surface construction) with the MWAS framework tested here would let me build an exposome-metabolome-birth outcome causal chain anchored in geography — advancing both health geography and environmental epidemiology.

③ Build an explainable multi-omics predictive panel combining metabolomics with environmental co-exposure data

Extending this paperThe paper's best individual metabolite achieves AUC = 0.74 and the six-metabolite panel reaches AUC = 0.77 — modest but clinically promising. A direct extension: augment the metabolite feature set with diet-omics, lipidomics, and environmental exposome data (the authors explicitly call for lipidomics integration), and replace the parametric logistic regression + random forest pipeline with an explainable gradient-boosting ensemble (e.g., XGBoost + SHAP values). This would improve predictive performance while quantifying, for each participant, the relative contribution of amino acid pathways vs. acylcarnitines vs. environmental chemicals — transforming a statistical signal into an interpretable risk score.

④ Apply mass spectral prediction tools to accelerate metabolite annotation in under-studied populations

Extending this paperA known bottleneck in this paper is that only 39 of the tens of thousands of detected features were annotated to level 1 (confirmed identity), necessarily limiting the scope of the validated biomarker panel. The majority of the MWAS signal lives in unannotated features. A direct extension: use computational mass spectral prediction models — which predict fragmentation patterns for novel compounds from molecular structure — to generate in silico reference spectra for candidate features in the unannotated long tail, potentially expanding the confirmed metabolite list by an order of magnitude without additional analytical standards.

Collaborating on such annotation pipelines would enrich my FAIR/open-science work and expand my methods portfolio into computational biochemistry.

⑤ Link metabolomic PTB risk scores to spatial disparities in PM2.5 exposure across Atlanta neighborhoods

Extending this paperOxidative stress and inflammatory amino acid pathways are this paper's dominant biological theme — and PM2.5 exposure is a well-established inducer of exactly these pathways. The paper does not include environmental pollution as a covariate, despite Atlanta being a city with documented air quality disparities between predominantly Black and White neighborhoods. A targeted extension: merge validated metabolite risk scores (especially arginine/proline and methionine/cysteine pathway perturbation indices) with residential PM2.5 exposure at the census-tract level, testing whether pollution burden mediates or amplifies the racial disparity in metabolic PTB risk.
