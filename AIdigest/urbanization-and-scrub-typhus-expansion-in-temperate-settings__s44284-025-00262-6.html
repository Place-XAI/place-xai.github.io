Urbanization and scrub typhus expansion in temperate settings

Zhe Lou · Jinjing Wu · Xiaochen Wang · Wei Zhao · Haowei Wang · Zhifeng Li · Jianli Hu · Ruiyun Li Published 2025-06-24 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00262-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Scrub typhus (ST) — long endemic only to southern, tropical China — is surging northward into temperate Eastern China. Using 16,775 case records from Jiangsu Province (2006–2023), the study shows infections surged ~80% after 2014 and spread to more counties, with cases shifting from rural toward suburban and urban areas (urban cases dominating in 2022–2023). Higher urbanization and human footprint raise the urban fraction and odds of ST, while population aging partially counteracts the spread into cities — yet urban patients face a longer onset-to-diagnosis delay (7 vs. 4 days in rural areas).

★

Why It Matters

  • Reframes a "rural" disease as an urban one. ST has long been treated as a farmers' zoonosis; this work documents a persistent rise in suburban and urban infections, shifting where surveillance and clinical vigilance must focus.
  • Quantifies the urbanization–disease link locally. Per-unit increases in urbanization (+6.2% odds) and human footprint (+22.3% odds) of urban ST occurrence give planners and CDC officials concrete, finer-scale evidence rather than global generalizations.
  • Surfaces a counter-intuitive demographic brake. Aged and super-aged counties show markedly lower odds of urban cases — likely because the oldest old do less outdoor/agricultural work — adding nuance to how aging societies reshape disease geography.
  • Exposes a treatment-delay equity gap. Urban patients wait longer for diagnosis (median 7 days vs. 4 in rural areas), pointing to low urban awareness of an "imported" disease and motivating public-knowledge campaigns.

Each record includes residential address, age, sex, occupation, and onset/diagnosis dates.

Core Methods

  • Spatiotemporal epidemiology: annual/city/county aggregation of case records to characterize incidence trends, seasonality (Oct–Nov peak), endemic-city identification, and county-level spatial diffusion.
  • Urban–rural disparity analysis: stratify cases by urbanicity; rescale annual fractions to a relative fraction (RF = Fc / mean Fc) fluctuating around 1 to compare across regions and years; compare onset-to-diagnosis intervals across urban/suburban/rural.
  • Generalized additive model (GAM): models the fraction of urban cases as smooth functions of urbanization rate, human footprint, mean temperature, and precipitation days, with aging level as a categorical term.
  • Multivariate logistic regression: models the occurrence (presence) of urban cases per county-year on the same covariates, reporting odds ratios with 95% CIs.
  • Statistical framing: two-sided tests, P<0.05 significance; aging-society stratification (aging / aged / super-aged) to quantify how demographic structure modulates urban risk.

① Move from county-year regression to a spatiotemporal diffusion forecaster

Extending this paperThe paper documents that ST diffused into more counties after 2014 but models risk with static GAM/logistic regressions on a 24-county panel — it describes spread without forecasting where it goes next. A direct extension: cast the county adjacency network as a graph and learn a spatiotemporal diffusion model (graph neural network or mechanistic SIR-on-network) that ingests urbanization, human footprint, and ERA5 climate to predict the next front of expansion, mirroring how fine-scale density predicts arbovirus wave dynamics.

② Replace the GAM smooth terms with explainable machine learning

Extending this paperThe paper's urbanization/human-footprint effects are captured by GAM smooths and a logistic model — interpretable but constrained to additive, low-order interactions. Extending it with an explainable ML ensemble (gradient boosting + SHAP, or MGWR for spatial non-stationarity) would expose local, interacting determinants — e.g. where aging dampens urban risk versus where climate amplifies it — that a single global GAM averages away.

③ Turn the diagnosis-delay gap into an accessibility problem

Extending this paperThe paper finds urban patients wait 7 days vs. 4 days (rural) from onset to diagnosis and attributes it to low awareness — but never tests spatial access to care. A natural extension: overlay healthcare-facility locations and travel times on the urbanicity strata to disentangle awareness from physical access, and ask whether longer urban delays reflect care-seeking behavior or first-contact misdiagnosis of an "imported" disease.
