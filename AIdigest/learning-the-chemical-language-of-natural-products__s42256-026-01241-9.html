Learning the chemical language of natural products

Xu Guo · Celia M. Rava · Allison S. Walker Published 2026-05-07 DOI 10.1038/s42256-026-01241-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

NaFM, a scaffold-aware foundation model for natural products, is pretrained via subgraph masking and contrastive learning to capture the unique chemical language of biosynthetically derived molecules. It generalizes across a broad array of downstream tasks — natural product classification, source-organism taxonomy prediction, biosynthetic protein prediction, and bioactivity prediction — outperforming task-specific models on most benchmarks. The approach demonstrates that natural-product-specific pretraining strategies, rather than generic molecular pretraining, are essential to unlock performance on this structurally distinctive chemical class.

Why It Matters

  • Addresses a data-scarce, structurally unique chemical class. Natural products have greater scaffold diversity and complexity than typical synthetic molecules, and labeled datasets are small — NaFM's pretraining strategy is specifically engineered to cope with this regime.
  • Unifies a fragmented tooling landscape. Existing tools are siloed by task (one model for taxonomy, another for bioactivity, another for gene-cluster annotation). NaFM is the first single foundation model to cover all of these simultaneously.
  • Demonstrates scaffold-aware masking as a transferable pretraining innovation. Masking entire scaffold subgraphs (not single atoms) forces the model to learn biosynthetic logic, not just atom connectivity — a principled approach transferable to other scaffold-rich chemical families.
  • Opens the door to virtual screening at natural-product scale. Bioactivity prediction from NaFM can be applied directly in virtual screening workflows, potentially accelerating antibiotic and pharmaceutical discovery pipelines where natural products are most productive.
  • Honest about limitations and next steps. The article clearly flags that evaluation is purely computational and that performance degrades with small pretraining data — setting a credible research agenda for the community.

Core Methods

  • Scaffold-aware subgraph masking: during pretraining, entire subgraphs of the Bemis–Murcko scaffold (containing multiple atoms and bonds) are masked and predicted — forcing the model to learn biosynthetic structural logic rather than local atom-level statistics.
  • Contrastive learning: a contrastive objective is combined with masked pretraining to pull together representations of structurally related natural products (same scaffold family) while pushing apart dissimilar ones, improving generalizability across downstream tasks.
  • Graph neural network encoder: molecular structures are encoded as molecular graphs (atoms = nodes, bonds = edges); the encoder learns node and edge embeddings via message-passing, informed by the scaffold-aware masking scheme.
  • Multi-task fine-tuning: the pretrained NaFM backbone is fine-tuned on four downstream tasks (taxonomy classification, taxa prediction, biosynthetic protein prediction, bioactivity prediction), each with a task-specific head.
  • Benchmarking: NaFM is compared against task-specific models including NPClassifier (taxonomy), demonstrating superior performance on most classes; the γ-lactam-β-lactone exception is explicitly discussed as a data-scarcity failure mode.

① Extend scaffold-aware masking from molecules to spatial graphs

Extending this paper NaFM's core innovation — masking subgraphs of the scaffold rather than single atoms — is a graph-level pretraining strategy that encodes domain priors (biosynthetic logic) into the self-supervised objective. A direct extension of this paper would be to apply the same scaffold-aware contrastive pretraining to heterogeneous molecular graphs spanning multiple biosynthetic gene cluster (BGC) families, where the "scaffold" maps to the core polyketide/terpenoid backbone. This would require curating a joint pretraining corpus from databases such as antiSMASH, MIBiG, and LOTUS, and testing whether the scaffold-aware objective transfers across BGC classes — an explicit next step the commentary calls for ("expanding the scale of pretraining by collecting substantially larger collections").

Added value to my research The scaffold-aware masking principle translates directly to spatial graph pretraining in GeoAI: instead of biosynthetic scaffolds, the analogous "scaffold subgraph" is the street-network community (e.g., a neighborhood catchment identified by community detection). Masking entire subgraphs of the spatial graph during pretraining of a GNN-based accessibility model would force the model to learn the structural logic of urban accessibility catchments — a physics-informed spatial pre-training strategy applicable to my health-facility network models.
