Probabilistic weather forecasting with machine learning

Ilan Price · Alvaro Sanchez-Gonzalez · Ferran Alet · Tom R. Andersson · Andrew El-Kadi · Dominic Masters · Timo Ewalds · Jacklynn Stott · Shakir Mohamed · Peter Battaglia · Remi Lam · Matthew Willson Published 2024-12-04 DOI 10.1038/s41586-024-08252-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

GenCast, a conditional diffusion model built on a graph-transformer architecture, generates ensembles of stochastic 15-day global weather forecasts at 0.25° resolution in approximately 8 minutes. Trained on 40 years of ERA5 reanalysis, it outperforms ENS — the top operational ensemble forecast of ECMWF — on 97.2% of 1,320 evaluated targets, with superior skill at predicting extreme weather events, tropical cyclone tracks and wind power production, while maintaining well-calibrated probabilistic uncertainty across all lead times.

★

Why It Matters

  • First ML ensemble to definitively surpass operational NWP. Prior ML weather models achieved competitive deterministic skill but failed at ensemble/probabilistic forecasting. GenCast is the first purely ML method proven more skilful than ENS across 97.2% of all variable-level-leadtime targets.
  • Sharp, physically realistic samples — not blurry means. Unlike MSE-trained ML models that produce blurry conditional means, GenCast's diffusion process yields sharp, spectrally realistic individual trajectory samples that match ERA5 power spectra even at 15-day lead time.
  • Captures joint spatiotemporal structure crucial for applications. The model outperforms ENS on pooled regional evaluations (98.1% of 5,400 targets), wind power forecasting (about 20% better CRPS out to 2 days), and tropical cyclone track probability — all of which require correlated multi-variable, multi-location forecasts.
  • 8-minute inference is operationally transformative. ENS requires substantial HPC time; GenCast's 8-minute runtime on a Cloud TPUv5 — with the ensemble generated in parallel — could democratize access to high-quality ensemble weather guidance for low-resource meteorological agencies.
  • Open code and weights lower the reproduction barrier. The model weights and code are released on GitHub (google-deepmind/graphcast), enabling the research community to directly build on GenCast without retraining from scratch on 40 years of ERA5.

Core Methods

  • Conditional diffusion model on the sphere: GenCast models P(Xt+1 | Xt, Xt−1) as a diffusion process, iteratively refining a spherical noise sample into a forecast state using an ODE solver (DPMSolver++2S) with N = 20 refinement steps per time step.
  • Graph-transformer denoiser architecture: Encoder maps 0.25° latitude–longitude grid data to an icosahedral mesh (6-times refined, 41,162 nodes); Processor applies 16 graph-transformer blocks with 32-hop neighbourhood attention (feature dim 512); Decoder maps back to the grid. Architecture derived from GraphCast.
  • Spherical noise distribution: Rather than i.i.d. Gaussian noise, GenCast samples isotropic Gaussian white noise on the sphere (flat spherical harmonic power spectrum), better respecting the spherical geometry of global atmospheric fields.
  • Autoregressive ensemble generation: Independent noise samples at each 12-h step produce independent ensemble trajectories. A single 15-day, 50-member ensemble is generated in about 8 minutes on a Cloud TPUv5.
  • Two-stage resolution training: Stage 1 pre-trains at 1° resolution (2M steps, 32 TPUv5 instances, ~3.5 days); Stage 2 fine-tunes to 0.25° (64,000 steps, ~1.5 days). GNN weight reuse across resolutions is enabled by the architecture design.
  • Probabilistic verification metrics: Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS), spread/skill ratio, rank histograms, Brier skill score, Relative Economic Value (REV) curves, and spatial neighbourhood pooled CRPS at 6 spatial scales (120 km to 3,828 km).
  • Cyclone tracking: TempestExtremes v2.1 applied to all gridded forecasts; paired position error and track probability heatmaps evaluated against IBTrACS-matched ERA5 ground truth.

① Downscale GenCast ensembles to city-scale for urban heat and health applications

Extending this paperGenCast operates at 0.25° (~28 km) — far too coarse to resolve urban heat islands, street-level wind corridors, or neighborhood-scale air quality. The paper itself notes that scaling to the upgraded ENS resolution of 0.1° is a future step, but even that remains too coarse for urban policy. A direct extension: apply generative statistical downscaling — itself a diffusion-model task — to GenCast ensemble members, conditioning on land-cover and urban morphology fields, to produce kilometer-scale probabilistic urban climate trajectories from GenCast's global stochastic ensemble. This would make the paper's probabilistic framework actionable at the city scale without retraining the global model.

Added value to my researchCity-scale probabilistic weather fields would directly power my spatiotemporal health geography work: extreme-heat probability forecasts tied to neighborhood-level demographic vulnerability (elderly populations, green-space access, housing type) could drive dynamic spatial prioritization of cooling centers and telehealth outreach — a concrete GeoAI pipeline bridging GenCast uncertainty envelopes to 2SFCA-style service-area accessibility scores under heat stress.

② Replace ERA5 initialization with a learned data-assimilation prior for observation-sparse regions

Extending this paperGenCast's probabilistic skill depends critically on high-quality ERA5 initialization; regions with sparse in-situ observations (oceans, polar areas, much of sub-Saharan Africa) have lower ERA5 quality. The paper acknowledges that NWP-based data assimilation remains essential. A key extension: replace the fixed ERA5 initial condition with a diffusion-model-based data assimilation step that treats observation scarcity as explicit uncertainty, producing a distribution over initial states that naturally propagates into GenCast's ensemble — making the full pipeline end-to-end probabilistic and observation-aware rather than deterministic-start probabilistic-forecast.

Added value to my researchThe same "observation-sparse prior" logic applies to my multi-source data fusion work: mobile-phone records, social media, and remote sensing data are also spatially irregular and heterogeneous in quality. Framing the fusion as a diffusion-based conditional inference — rather than a deterministic feature concatenation — would let my spatiotemporal prediction models propagate data-quality uncertainty through to downstream outputs, directly improving spatial prediction reliability in data-poor urban/rural regions.

③ Adapt GenCast's graph-transformer denoiser as a spatiotemporal backbone for place-based prediction

Extending this paperGenCast's key architectural innovation is the graph-transformer Processor: each node on the icosahedral mesh attends to its 32-hop neighborhood, efficiently encoding multi-scale spatial dependencies without fixed-radius kernels. This is architecturally general. Extending it: treat the globe as a multi-resolution spatial graph where "weather variables" are replaced by any spatiotemporal field — disease incidence, mobility flows, economic activity — and inherit GenCast's learned spatial correlation structure as a universal spatiotemporal graph prior for transfer learning across prediction tasks with limited local training data.

Added value to my researchMy physics-informed graph learning agenda would benefit directly: the graph-transformer denoiser, pre-trained on ERA5's rich spatial-correlation patterns, could be fine-tuned as a geospatial foundation model backbone for tasks like disease surveillance or healthcare access prediction. The icosahedral mesh naturally represents irregular geographies (urban vs. rural density) better than regular grids, which aligns with my interest in spatially non-stationary MGWR-style modeling.

④ Apply GenCast's REV (Relative Economic Value) framework to optimize healthcare resource pre-positioning under extreme weather risk

Extending this paperGenCast introduces REV curves as a decision-making lens: for a given cost/loss ratio, does the forecast provide positive value over climatology? The paper shows GenCast's REV advantage over ENS is largest at small cost/loss ratios — precisely where the cost of preparation is low relative to the loss from unpreparedness. This is directly applicable to public-health decisions. A paper extension: build a spatial decision pipeline that maps GenCast's 7-day extreme-heat/wind probability forecasts through REV thresholds to actionable, location-specific preparedness triggers, and quantify the expected-value gain vs. a static climatological alert system across diverse geographies.

⑤ Use GenCast's probabilistic wind ensemble to optimize wind-farm siting and grid-management equity

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that GenCast's 50-member ensemble gives approximately 20% better regional wind power CRPS than ENS out to 2 days, with maintained improvements to 7 days across 5,344 wind farms. The experiment is deliberately simplified (no curtailment, 10 m wind speed). Extending it: integrate GenCast wind-power ensemble predictions into a stochastic unit-commitment and reserve-dispatch optimizer that explicitly treats forecast uncertainty — following the paper's own framing that "leveraging forecast uncertainty can improve decision-making" — and evaluate whether the skill gain translates to measurable reductions in spinning reserves and fossil backup activation.

Added value to my researchMy EV charging-station efficiency-vs-equity optimization work faces an analogous problem: stochastic spatial demand (EV arrivals) must be matched to supply (charging stations) under uncertainty. GenCast's probabilistic wind ensemble provides a structural template — the same stochastic optimization framing could be adapted to spatially distributed EV charging demand forecasts, incorporating uncertainty to balance equity (siting stations in underserved areas) against efficiency (peak-load management), mirroring the cost/loss trade-off GenCast already handles.
