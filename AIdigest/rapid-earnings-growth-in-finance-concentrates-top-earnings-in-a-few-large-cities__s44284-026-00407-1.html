Rapid earnings growth in finance concentrates top earnings in a few large cities

Nils Neumann · Olivier Godechot · Lasse Folke Henriksen · Are Skeie Hermansen · Feng Hou · Naomi Kodama · Zoltán Lippényi · Silvia Maja Melzer · Halil Sabanci · Max Thaning · Donald Tomaskovic-Devey · et al. Published 2026-04-24 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00407-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Since the 1980s, the spatial concentration of top earnings in the global north has been driven less by globalization than by financialization. Using administrative linked employer–employee data for ten countries (1989–2019), the authors show that rapid earnings growth in finance concentrates top earnings in the few "financial cities" that host the national stock exchange. On average, workers in financial cities account for 65% of the rise in the national top-1% earnings share, and financial sector jobs alone explain ~26% of national and ~50% of local top-earnings growth there — a pattern that extends well beyond New York, London and Tokyo to smaller hubs like Frankfurt, Stockholm, Oslo and Madrid. The driver is urban sectoral specialization and finance-sector rent-sharing, not merely city scale or skill sorting.

★

Why It Matters

  • Re-explains a major macro-trend. The resurgence of spatial inequality in rich countries is reframed: it is not just a spatial echo of national top-earnings growth or globalization, but a consequence of where one sector — finance — clusters its labor market.
  • Cross-national, register-grade evidence. With nearly one billion employer–employee year observations across ten countries, the finding is far more robust than single-country case studies and reveals consistent dynamics across very different institutional settings.
  • Beyond "global cities." By showing the mechanism also operates in non-global hubs (Oslo, Stockholm, Copenhagen, Madrid), it generalizes the global-cities thesis from globalization to financialization plus sectoral specialization.
  • Direct policy stakes. The concentration of top earnings is linked to reduced intergenerational mobility and to political fragmentation/populism — making the spatial geography of finance a lever for equity and democratic stability.
  • A transferable analytic template. The counterfactual "set finance earnings to zero" decomposition can be applied to any spatially concentrated industry (e.g., tech), turning a descriptive trend into a sector-attributable accounting framework.

Core Methods

  • City pairing design: match each country's financial city to the closest comparison city (population, employment, GDP share) to net out scale and urban-growth effects (Germany: Frankfurt vs Hamburg).
  • Three concentration measures: (1) a city's absolute contribution to the rise in the national top-1% share; (2) the overrepresentation of a city's earnings in national top-1% shares via an odds ratio of local earnings shares; (3) local earnings concentration (local top-5% odds ratio).
  • Sectoral decomposition: decompose within-city change into the portion explained by financial sector jobs vs other sectors (equations 8–10).
  • Counterfactual estimand: compare observed trends against a counterfactual where finance earnings are mechanically set to zero in both cities; the difference quantifies finance's contribution (log-linear trend models, equations 11–12).
  • Robustness regressions: country-level OLS panels with country & year fixed effects, lagged predictors and Driscoll–Kraay standard errors, controlling for population, GDP per capita, FDI outflows and employment-share differences.
  • Mechanism probe: rule out employment growth (stagnant finance employment) and use France's occupational detail to show financial-market roles (e.g., 85% of market managers in Paris vs 1.5% in Lyon) drive rent-sharing.

④ Fuse occupational detail with mobile/RS data to localize financial-market labor

Extending this paperThe authors' sharpest mechanism evidence (Paris vs Lyon) exists only because France has occupational data distinguishing financial-market from commercial-banking roles; elsewhere this is missing. Extending it: impute the financial-market labor footprint in other countries by fusing register data with firm POIs, building-occupancy, commuting and mobile-phone signals — recovering the within-city geography the registers cannot see.

⑤ Add a temporal-dynamics / forecasting layer to the static decomposition

Extending this paperThe paper notes high year-to-year volatility in local top-earnings series and that finance's premium "attenuated" in North America as practices spilled into other sectors — yet the analysis is largely a static, period-bounded decomposition. Extending it: build a spatiotemporal model of contagion/spillover (finance wage norms diffusing across sectors and cities) to forecast when and where concentration peaks and reverses.
