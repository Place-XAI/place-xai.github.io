Machine learning-enabled globally guaranteed evolutionary computation

Bin Li · Ziping Wei · Jingjing Wu · Shuai Yu · Tian Zhang · Chunli Zhu · Dezhi Zheng · Weisi Guo · Chenglin Zhao · Jun Zhang Published 2023-04-10 DOI 10.1038/s42256-023-00642-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

EVOLER, a two-stage framework marrying low-rank representation learning with particle swarm optimization, is the first evolutionary computation method with a theoretical guarantee of convergence to the global optimum for non-convex problems exhibiting low-rank structure. Validated on 20 benchmark functions and two real-world tasks — power grid economic dispatch and nanophotonics inverse design — EVOLER finds the global optimum with probability approaching 1, while reducing required function evaluations by 5–30-fold compared with state-of-the-art PSO variants, overcoming the 50-year-old uncertainty problem in evolutionary computation.

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a 50-year-open problem. Evolutionary computation has lacked any theoretical guarantee of finding the global optimum; EVOLER provides the first mathematically proven lower-bound probability of convergence for non-convex, rank-restricted problems.
  • Overcomes the No Free Lunch trap. Classical PSO variants each exploit specific geometric structures and fail on others; EVOLER's low-rank subspace identification is structure-agnostic, achieving best performance across all 20 diverse benchmark functions simultaneously.
  • Unlocks energy-critical applications. On a 6-unit power dispatch problem, EVOLER finds the provably optimal economic cost ($USD 15,303.872 per 1,260 MWh) that classical methods miss — scaling to global power demand, the annual savings could reach ~$1.1 billion.
  • Enables rapid nanophotonics design. Inverse design of multilayer thin-film nanostructures with 10x faster computation than classical PSO and consistently reaching the global optimum — opening paths to reliable biosensor and optical switch design.
  • Open-source and replicable. Full MATLAB code and datasets are publicly released, enabling replication and extension across diverse optimization domains.

Core Methods

  • Stage 1 — Low-rank representation learning: randomly sample s rows and columns of the d-dimensional problem space (s ≈ O(r log r), where r is the intrinsic rank); reconstruct the full space via cross-approximation: F̂ = C·W·R, where W is the Moore–Penrose pseudo-inverse of the sampled intersection block. Extends to d-dimensional tensors via structured random sampling and mode-product reconstruction.
  • Stage 2 — Attention subspace exploration: identify x̂opt = argmin F̂ to define an attention subspace A around the learned near-global optimum; initialize PSO particles in A via Gaussian sampling; run canonical or local-version PSO within this small subspace.
  • Theoretical convergence guarantee: derive a closed-form lower bound probability Pr{xT→x*} > (1−ε)·(2/π)·arctan(Δf/ΔE(s)), linking convergence probability to the fitness gap Δf between global and next local optimum, and the representation error bound ΔE(s) which shrinks as s grows.
  • Benchmarking: 500 independent random-restart trials per function; comparative evaluation against 8 state-of-the-art PSO variants (MPCPSO, MCPSO, CLPSO, NPSO, DPSO, DNSPSO, GCPSO, CPSO-H/S); convergence curves measured in function evaluations (not wall time) to fairly compare across methods with different per-generation evaluation counts.
  • Scalability: hierarchical exploration applied to d = 30 problems, reducing tensor rank estimation complexity; multiscale down-sampling strategy for memory efficiency.

① Apply EVOLER's low-rank guarantee to spatial facility-location optimization

Extending this paperEVOLER proves global optimality for any function with low-rank structure and demonstrates this on 2D–30D benchmark problems plus power dispatch. The next direct extension is to apply it to combinatorial spatial optimization — such as optimal facility placement on a road network — where the objective (e.g., total weighted access time) is evaluated on a spatial grid and frequently exhibits low-rank structure due to spatial autocorrelation. The paper's current two real-world demos (power, nanophotonics) are purely numeric; adding a geographic demo would reveal whether urban accessibility cost surfaces are also low-rank, activating EVOLER's guarantee for a broader class of planning problems.

If the spatial cost surface is low-rank (likely, given spatial autocorrelation), EVOLER could replace my current genetic algorithm / greedy heuristics with a theoretically guaranteed optimizer, resolving the uncertainty problem inherent in my current facility-location pipeline and enabling confident global-equity claims.

② Extend EVOLER to hyperparameter optimization in geospatial ML models

Extending this paperEVOLER currently targets black-box function optimization where function evaluations are expensive and the landscape is non-convex. This is exactly the setting of hyperparameter tuning for large ML models. The paper briefly mentions aerodynamics and other high-dimensional settings but stops at d = 30 in experiments. A natural extension: use EVOLER as a sample-efficient hyperparameter search engine for neural architectures, where the validation-loss surface is the unknown low-rank function. The low-rank assumption is plausible because hyperparameter landscapes are known to exhibit low effective dimensionality — aligning with the paper's own theoretical premise.

③ Combine EVOLER's low-rank stage with free-energy-based combinatorial solvers

Extending this paperEVOLER's Stage 1 identifies a continuous attention subspace; its Stage 2 refines within it via PSO. However, many real-world planning and design problems are combinatorial (discrete variable types, assignment constraints, network topology). A direct paper extension: replace PSO in Stage 2 with a combinatorial solver (e.g., a free-energy machine) that operates within the low-rank-identified subspace, yielding globally guaranteed discrete optimization — a gap the current framework cannot address for problems like network design or facility assignment with integer constraints.

④ Use EVOLER's low-rank landscape learning as a spatial prediction surrogate

Extending this paperStage 1 of EVOLER is essentially a rank-constrained matrix/tensor completion from strategically sampled rows and columns — a sample-efficient spatial interpolation method. The paper uses it purely to identify a subspace for optimization, but the reconstructed F̂ is itself a global prediction of the unobserved surface. A direct extension: treat Stage 1 as a spatiotemporal surrogate model — reconstruct a high-dimensional spatial variable (e.g., fine-grained pollution, disease prevalence) from a sparse structured sample of row/column observations, then use the proven error bounds to quantify prediction uncertainty at unobserved locations.
