Mapping the local effectiveness of mass drug administration for malaria using transportability methods

Michelle E. Roh · Yanwei Tong · Gabriella Barratt Heitmann · Junran Jia · El-hadji Konko Ciré Ba · Jean Louis Ndiaye · Ari Fogelson · Paul Milligan · Amadou Seck · Abdoulaye Diallo · Aminata Colle Lo · Michael Baiocchi · Roly Gosling · Adam Bennett · Michelle S. Hsiang · Jade Benjamin-Chung Published 2026-03-24 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00094-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A doubly robust transportability model combining machine learning with fine-scale remote sensing data successfully generalized the effect of mass drug administration (MDA) from a 60-village Senegalese trial to 116 non-trial communes where seasonal malaria chemoprevention is standard care. MDA was estimated to reduce malaria incidence by 36–65% in 74 communes during the intervention year, with greater impact in areas of higher precipitation, denser vegetation and lower temperatures. Effects were not sustained in the post-intervention year, confirming MDA's short-lived nature and providing a scalable, data-driven framework for subnational intervention targeting.

Why It Matters

  • Bridges internal and external validity of RCTs. Cluster randomized trials are gold standards for efficacy but inherently limited in scope. This paper shows how causal transportability theory, paired with satellite data, can systematically generalize trial findings across an entire country.
  • Provides subnational precision for intervention targeting. Rather than a single national pooled estimate, the method produces commune-level effectiveness maps — exactly the granularity public health planners need to prioritize where MDA would be cost-effective.
  • Demonstrates the power of free remote sensing data. CHIRPS precipitation, MODIS temperature and vegetation index, and WorldPop demographic data — all publicly accessible — are sufficient to characterize the effect modifiers that drive spatial heterogeneity in MDA impact.
  • Confirms and quantifies MDA's temporal limitation. The finding that effects disappear in the post-intervention year (none reaching significance in 116 communes) directly informs WHO policy on when and how repeatedly MDA should be deployed.
  • Offers a transferable template. The doubly robust estimator plus elastic net feature selection pipeline is generic enough to be applied to other environmentally mediated diseases and other intervention trials anywhere in the world.

Core Methods

  • Transportability framework (doubly robust estimator): combines an inverse-probability-of-trial-participation model with an outcome model; consistent if either model is correctly specified (Dahabreh et al. 2020). Applied at commune-month level, producing separate transport estimates for each of the 116 eligible non-trial communes.
  • Elastic net regression (glmnet, α=0.5): used for both the trial-participation model (binomial family) and the outcome model (quasi-Poisson family). Ten-fold cross-validation selected lambda; the "relax" parameter was set to true to refit without penalty after variable selection. This mitigates overfitting in the small-n (60 clusters) setting.
  • Effect modifier screening pipeline: covariates screened for (1) positivity violations (no overlap between trial and target), (2) multicollinearity within variable groups (pairwise Pearson >0.7), and (3) association with outcome (Poisson likelihood ratio test, P < 0.2). Final covariates: precipitation, temperature, EVI, proportion <10 years old (varying by commune).
  • Effect heterogeneity analysis: mixed-effects Poisson regression at village-month level, with ratio- and additive-scale interaction tests (RERI), stratified by binary above/below median of each effect modifier.
  • Non-parametric bootstrap (1,000 iterations): resampling commune-months within trial datasets and months within non-trial communes to produce 95% CIs for each transported estimate.
  • Positivity restriction: transportability restricted to communes where predicted trial-participation probability ≥0.75 to avoid causal identification failures.
  • Software: R 4.1.0 (glmnet 4.1.2) for transportability analyses; Stata 16 for effect modification; Google Earth Engine for environmental data aggregation.

① Extend transportability to human-mobility-adjusted effect modifiers

Extending this paperThe paper's transportability models rely exclusively on environmental and demographic covariates obtained from satellite data, explicitly noting that population mobility patterns were a limitation not accounted for. A direct extension would integrate mobile-phone or GPS-derived mobility metrics — particularly cross-commune travel flows that seed malaria importation — as additional effect modifiers in the elastic net models. Because mobility fundamentally shapes how fast a parasite reservoir is re-seeded after MDA, communes with high in-flow from high-transmission zones would show dampened transported effectiveness, a pattern invisible to the current remote sensing stack. Mobility layers could be sourced from anonymized call detail records, the same data type used in malaria importation studies.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal mobility analysis toolkit — built on GPS trajectories and mobile-phone records — can directly supply these origin-destination flow matrices for Senegalese communes. Treating mobility as a spatially varying effect modifier in a doubly robust framework is methodologically analogous to my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation work: both require carefully specifying how a spatial covariate enters a non-parametric model. This would also extend my health geography work beyond static accessibility metrics toward dynamic, exposure-weighted accessibility that reflects where people actually go.

② Apply stable causal learning to make transported estimates robust to unmeasured confounders

Extending this paperThe paper's doubly robust estimator is consistent only if either the participation model or the outcome model is correctly specified — but both depend on the assumption that all effect modifiers have been measured. The authors acknowledge this is likely violated: healthcare system capacity, vector species distributions, and local behavioral factors are not in any satellite layer. "Stable learning" methods, which explicitly identify and down-weight spurious (environment-specific) correlations during training, could make the elastic net feature selection more robust to unobserved heterogeneity across communes. This would directly address the paper's sixth limitation and potentially extend the method to a broader set of communes currently excluded due to positivity concerns.

Added value to my researchMy explainable ML work for spatial prediction regularly confronts the same problem: models trained in one city fail to generalize to another because spurious local correlations are baked in. Stable learning as a regularization strategy is directly applicable to my MGWR/ensemble pipelines for predicting health outcomes across heterogeneous spatial contexts — offering a theoretically grounded route to cross-area transferability that complements my current spatial cross-validation approaches.

Cross-ref (corpus): Stable learning establishes some common ground between causal inference and machine learning — 10.1038/s42256-022-00445-z

③ Pool multi-trial transportability to extend coverage across West Africa

Extending this paperThe paper explicitly recommends, as a future direction, using "pooled datasets from multiple trials of the same intervention" to provide greater variability across epidemiological settings. The current framework is constrained to Senegalese SMC communes because the trial was in Senegal. Extending it: concatenate data from multiple MDA trials across Mali, Burkina Faso, Guinea and DRC — all countries with SMC programs — into a single multi-source target population. The doubly robust estimator naturally accommodates a non-nested design where trial clusters from different countries are pooled. The key challenge is harmonizing the remote sensing feature extraction across trial sites with different baseline transmission seasons, which would require the same CHIRPS/MODIS pipeline but with country-specific lag structures informed by local vector biology.

Added value to my researchThis multi-source data fusion challenge directly maps onto my FAIR/open-science and multi-source data fusion research direction. Fusing heterogeneous trial datasets — each with different sampling frames, eligibility criteria, and covariate definitions — requires the same metadata standardization and reproducibility infrastructure I work on for mobile-phone and remote sensing data integration. It would also give me a concrete large-scale application of my spatiotemporal feature engineering pipeline at continental scale.

④ Map health facility travel-time accessibility jointly with transported MDA effectiveness

Extending this paperTravel time to the nearest health facility was an explored but ultimately excluded effect modifier because it was collinear with other covariates and lacked sufficient overlap with the trial site. However, it remains the clearest proxy for MDA coverage in real-world delivery: districts where communities are far from health posts have systematically lower MDA coverage (the paper notes moderate-to-high correlations between travel time and coverage). A productive extension would construct a joint map pairing each commune's transported MDA effectiveness with its 2SFCA-based healthcare accessibility score — creating a two-dimensional prioritization surface that identifies where MDA is both effective and operationally feasible to deliver at scale.

Added value to my researchThis is an almost direct application of my 2SFCA/R2SFCA healthcare accessibility methods, previously applied to primary care and mental health, now applied to the delivery accessibility of a mass intervention in West Africa. Mapping effectiveness × accessibility jointly creates a natural extension of my "spatial accessibility of care" work into the intervention delivery domain — and the result (a commune-level bi-variate choroplet) is exactly the kind of policy-facing spatial product my public-health geography pipeline is designed to produce.

⑤ Adapt active learning to sequentially select which communes to run new trials in

Extending this paperThe paper's framework currently transports estimates from one existing trial. Thirteen communes could not receive estimates in the intervention year due to positivity violations — their covariate profiles were too far from the trial site. Rather than waiting for a new trial to happen elsewhere, active learning for causal models could sequentially recommend which commune should be the next trial site to maximally reduce uncertainty in transported estimates across all 433 communes. The selection criterion would directly minimize the average width of the 95% CIs in the transportability output — turning the current backward-looking analysis into a prospective trial-design tool that informs where the next MDA trial should be conducted to fill the evidence gap.

Added value to my researchActive learning for spatial experimental design bridges causal inference and my agent-based modeling work. For my broader research on spatiotemporal processes, the same sequential site-selection idea applies to siting new environmental sensors, health monitoring stations, or survey clusters — any setting where the cost of data collection is high and information gain per site matters. The commune-as-agent framing also aligns with my Monte Carlo simulation methods for spatial sampling.
