Patterns of medication use across society from national primary care dispensing data

Caroline E. Dale · Rohan Takhar · Andrew Lambarth · Andrew Mason · Nathalie Conrad · Christopher Tomlinson · Spiros Denaxas · Mamas A. Mamas · Andrew D. Morris · Munir Pirmohamed · Kamlesh Khunti · Cathie Sudlow · Angela M. Wood · Naveed Sattar · Reecha Sofat Published 2026-06-15 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00134-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Analysing 5.8 billion primary care medication dispensings for 52.6 million individuals in England (November 2019 – December 2024), this study shows that the COVID-19 pandemic disrupted medication initiation differently across drug classes — gastrointestinal and mental health medications remained persistently below pre-pandemic levels, while cardiovascular and diabetes medications recovered and surpassed them. Stark inequities are revealed: women receive up to twice the antidepressant volume of men in young adulthood, Bangladeshi and Pakistani groups have dispensing rate ratios up to 2.5-fold higher by age 60, and the most deprived quintile has rates twice those of the least deprived from age 40. By age 70, 42% of the population are on five or more concurrent medications. These findings demonstrate the power of linked medicines intelligence for monitoring health disparities and directing post-pandemic clinical pathway recovery.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Primary data
NHS Business Services Authority (NHSBSA) primary care dispensing data — accessed within NHS England's Secure Data Environment (SDE) service for England. Coverage: 52.6 million individuals; 5.8 billion dispensed medications; study period 1 November 2019 to 31 December 2024 (61 months). Medications coded to BNF chapters 1–15. NHSBSA data available in the SDE from April 2018; November 2019 used as study start to allow a washout period after linkage.
Demographics
NHS England SDE curated demographics asset (BHF Data Science Centre Health Data Science Team) — harmonizes primary and secondary care records to provide month/year of birth, sex, and ethnicity (18 groups) per individual. Data sources include GDPPR (General Practice Extraction Service Data for Pandemic Planning and Research), prioritizing primary care records.
Deprivation
Index of Multiple Deprivation 2019 (IMD) at Lower Layer Super Output Area (LSOA) level, linked from GDPPR records nearest to December 2024. Categorized into quintiles (quintile 1 = most deprived).
Mortality
Office for National Statistics (ONS) Civil Registration of Deaths — used to censor individuals who died during 2024, contributing half a person-year to the rate denominator. Deaths registry covers England and Wales since 1993.
Study population
53.2% women; 27.9% aged 60+ years; 79.9% British/Irish/any other white background; 20.7% in IMD quintile 1 (most deprived). Individuals with missing birth date, sex, or at least one GDPPR record excluded.
Medicines coding
British National Formulary (BNF) hierarchical coding: chapter (organ system) → section → paragraph → chemical substance (9th BNF code character). Chapters 1–15 analysed; wound dressings and appliances excluded.
Polypharmacy definition
Five or more distinct chemical substances within a rolling 3-month (84-day) window, consistent with established UK definition (Guthrie et al., Scotland).
Analysis tools
Databricks PySpark 3.5.0, SQL, Python 3.11.11, R. Poisson models with offset for person-months at risk.

Core Methods

  • Poisson regression for incident dispensing rates: incident medication initiation (first BNF chapter/section/paragraph/chemical substance dispense) modelled by Poisson regression per calendar month, with log(person-months) as offset. Fitted separately per stratum of sex, age band, IMD quintile, and ethnic group to reveal subgroup trends.
  • Prevalent medication dispensing by cross-section (2024): cross-sectional rates of all prevalent medications per individual by age (in years), calculated as total dispensings divided by living, GDPPR-active individuals in 2024, with half-year adjustment for deaths in 2024.
  • Log rate ratios (log RR) for ethnic and deprivation comparisons: each ethnic group compared to the British/Irish/any other white background baseline; each IMD quintile compared to the least deprived (quintile 5). Visualized as heatmaps across age and BNF chapter.
  • Polypharmacy surveillance: rolling 3-month window of distinct chemical substances per individual per month throughout the study period. Polypharmacy threshold: five or more chemical substances in 84 consecutive days. Longitudinal tracking from November 2019 to December 2024 and cross-sectional snapshot for December 2024.
  • BNF hierarchy drill-down: analyses conducted at four levels of the BNF hierarchy (chapter, section, paragraph, chemical substance) to disentangle within-chapter heterogeneity and identify specific driver substances (e.g., sertraline within the CNS antidepressants section).
  • Living dashboard: a dynamic Shiny-based dashboard (MeThRx) built to enable near-real-time monitoring of medication counts, incident and prevalent rates, stratified by age, sex, IMD, ethnicity, and geographic region, supporting continuous update as NHSBSA uploads monthly.

② Link the paper's ethnic dispensing rate ratios to spatial healthcare accessibility gaps

Extending this paperThe paper finds that Bangladeshi and Pakistani groups have dispensing RRs up to 2.5-fold higher than white British individuals by age 60 — yet it explicitly attributes this to higher disease prevalence, access barriers, and adherence issues without disentangling them. A direct extension: link the SDE medicines data to primary care appointment data and geographic accessibility metrics, testing whether the elevated dispensing rate is co-located with areas of poor GP access (and thus delayed diagnosis leading to more severe disease at first dispensing) or good GP access (more detection). ③ Build a mobility-weighted medicines-demand model from the paper's dispensing trends

Extending this paperThe paper's Poisson models are purely temporal (month-level) and demographic (sex, age, ethnicity, deprivation). Yet medication initiation — especially for mental health and chronic diseases — is known to be spatially clustered near GP surgeries, and population mobility changes during lockdowns demonstrably altered access patterns. Extending the paper: incorporate monthly mobility data (e.g., from mobile phone records or Google Community Mobility Reports) as time-varying covariates in the Poisson model, testing whether the pandemic-era dips in CNS and mental health prescribing are explained by reduced mobility to GP surgeries rather than genuine reduction in disease incidence. This would sharpen the paper's causal interpretation of pandemic effects on clinical pathways.
