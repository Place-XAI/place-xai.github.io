Response and sensitivity of urban plants with different seed dispersal modes

Zhiwen Gao · Yingji Pan · Kun Song · Yanyi Yang · Mingming Zhuge · Tian Wu · Tiyuan Xia · Yuandong Hu · Liangjun Da · Ellen Cieraad Published 2024-12-11 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00169-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 16 cities in Yunnan (China's most biodiverse province), the richness of spontaneous urban plants is shaped jointly by seed dispersal mode, climate, dispersal limitation and habitat quality. Autochory (self/ejection dispersal) dominated in 13 of 16 cities while hydrochory (water dispersal) was rarest. Plant numbers were higher in colder, more-humid climates but fell with greater dispersal limitation and lower habitat quality — and dispersal modes differed in their sensitivities (autochory most sensitive to dispersal limitation, hydrochory most sensitive to habitat quality and climate). The fix: improve habitat quality and build green corridors to conserve urban biodiversity.

Why It Matters

  • Treats dispersal mode as a first-class explanatory variable. Most urban-biodiversity studies pool all plants together; here the response to the urban environment is shown to be contingent on how a species moves its seeds — a trait-based lens that explains why some species persist in cities and others do not.
  • Spotlights spontaneous, unmanaged flora. These plants — not planted by people — are an underrated but vital reservoir of urban biodiversity, and the study quantifies what actually controls their distribution.
  • Connects mechanism to actionable planning. Because autochory is bottlenecked by dispersal limitation and hydrochory by habitat quality, the work yields concrete levers: green corridors to relieve dispersal limitation and habitat-quality improvement for water-dispersed species.
  • Anchored in a biodiversity hotspot. Yunnan's exceptional species pool makes it a stringent test bed, strengthening the generality of the convergent-strategy finding.

Core Methods

  • Dispersal-mode classification: assign each spontaneous species to one of four seed dispersal modes (autochory, anemochory, zoochory, hydrochory) and tabulate composition per city (Sankey, Fig. 3).
  • Driver modeling: relate species richness to three driver families — climate, dispersal limitation (connectivity/isolation), and habitat quality — using regression / model-averaging.
  • Interaction model: test whether driver effects differ by dispersal mode via contrast interaction estimates between modes (Fig. 4).
  • Separate averaged models: fit a model per dispersal mode and average candidate models to rank predictor importance for each mode (Fig. 5).
  • Sensitivity comparison: quantify which dispersal mode is most sensitive to which stressor (e.g., autochory ↔ dispersal limitation; hydrochory ↔ habitat quality & climate).

① Model dispersal limitation as a habitat-connectivity graph, not a scalar

Extending this paperThe paper finds autochory is bottlenecked by dispersal limitation, but represents that limitation with summary patch metrics rather than the actual network of green patches between which seeds must travel. A direct extension: encode each city's green-space mosaic (from the CLCD land cover used here) as a weighted connectivity graph and re-fit richness against graph-theoretic flow/centrality measures, so "green corridors" become quantitatively prioritizable edges rather than a qualitative recommendation.

② Replace model-averaging with explainable ML to map mode-specific sensitivity surfaces

Extending this paperThe paper's averaged linear models (Fig. 5) report which driver matters per dispersal mode but assume largely additive, stationary effects. Extending it with explainable gradient-boosting / SHAP would expose non-linear thresholds and driver interactions — e.g., the humidity level at which hydrochory's habitat-quality sensitivity switches on — turning the qualitative "sensitivities varied" into continuous response curves per mode.

Added value to my researchThis is my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction line applied to a new outcome: the same SHAP-based attribution I use for telehealth/primary-care access transfers directly to attributing biodiversity richness, broadening my methods portfolio into ecology.

③ Reframe "habitat quality" as an accessibility/2SFCA problem for non-human users

Extending this paperThe paper treats habitat quality and dispersal limitation as separate drivers, yet conceptually a seed's chance of colonizing a patch is a supply–demand–distance problem: source-patch "supply", target-patch "demand", and dispersal-distance decay. Extending it: compute a two-step floating catchment over the green-patch network with mode-specific decay kernels (short for autochory, long for anemochory), yielding a single colonization-accessibility index that unifies the paper's two drivers.

Added value to my researchDirectly generalizes my 2SFCA/R2SFCA framework: dispersal mode supplies an empirical basis for mode-specific distance-decay functions, the very parameters I usually assume — a rare ecological validation of accessibility kernels.
