The burden of malaria-attributable maternal anaemia and the impact of preventive treatment across sub-Saharan Africa

Leuba, Sequoia I. · Verity, Robert · Gutman, Julie R. · Weiss, Daniel J. · Cairns, Matt · Williams, John E. · Khairallah, Carole · Bojang, Kalifa · Dodd, James · Noor, Abdisalan M. · Taylor, Steve M. · Otieno, Kephas · Coulibaly, Sheick Oumar · Kayentao, Kassoum · Kariuki, Simon · Greenwood, Brian · Desai, Meghna · Chandramohan, Daniel · Tagbor, Harry · ter Kuile, Feiko O. · Madanitsa, Mwayiwawo · Walker, Patrick G. T. Published 2026-02-25 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00068-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Malaria in pregnancy remains a major but underquantified driver of maternal anaemia across sub-Saharan Africa. In 2023, an estimated 13.1 million pregnancies were exposed to Plasmodium falciparum; without preventive measures, this would have caused 2.41 million cases of moderate-to-severe anaemia. While transmission declines since 2000 have helped, the corresponding reduction in anaemia burden is disproportionately small — a 32% drop in exposure yielded only a 22% decline in anaemia — because declining transmission has eroded pregnancy-specific immunity in multigravid women, shifting risk from primigravidae to all gravidity groups. Intermittent preventive treatment with sulfadoxine-pyrimethamine (IPTp-SP) averted ~1.10 million cases in 2023, making it an indispensable intervention whose disruption risks rapid, broad resurgence of severe maternal anaemia.

Why It Matters

  • First continent-wide, gravidity-resolved quantification of malaria-attributable maternal anaemia. Previous estimates (e.g., a 2007 review attributing 26% of severe cases to malaria) were pre-control-scale-up and lacked spatial or gravidity granularity; this study provides 2023 estimates down to country level and by gravidity.
  • Reveals a hidden cost of successful malaria control. As transmission has fallen, multigravid women have acquired less immunity, meaning any resurgence of transmission could produce broader and more severe anaemia across all pregnancy orders — not just among first-time mothers.
  • Directly quantifies IPTp-SP impact and coverage gaps. The finding that current 55% two-dose coverage averted ~1.10 million anaemia cases — while full coverage could avert ~1.45 million — gives policymakers a precise number for the cost of under-coverage.
  • Timely warning amid global health funding cuts. Disruptions to malaria control historically produce rapid transmission rebounds; this paper quantifies the maternal-health stakes of such scenarios, which are increasingly plausible under current funding constraints.
  • Open, reproducible science. All modelling code is publicly available, supporting verification, country-specific re-runs, and integration into other health burden frameworks.

Core Methods

  • Hierarchical Bayesian Hb dynamics model: models individual-level Hb concentration as a function of gestational age, malaria infection (PCR), gravidity, and inferred prior-pregnancy malaria exposure history. A power-law function φ(e) = (1 + (e/ν)^κ) captures immunity decay across successive infected pregnancies. Fitted via MCMC using the drjacoby R package with 1,000+ effective samples per parameter.
  • Censoring correction: studies that excluded women with severe anaemia (Hb < 7 g/dl) at enrolment were corrected using a latent-censoring extension of the Bayesian model, marginalizing over the unobserved excluded distribution to recover population-level Hb trajectories.
  • Malaria-in-pregnancy transmission model: a cohort-based model simulating exposures across a woman's reproductive life (age 15–49), linked to country-level PCR prevalence estimates and gravidity-specific fertility rates (DHS/MIS). Exposure history drives immunity level at each pregnancy.
  • Spatially explicit burden estimation: Hb and transmission models were integrated across a 0.2-degree Africa-wide grid aligned to MAP prevalence posteriors; 100 realizations of slide prevalence per cell captured epidemiological uncertainty. Outputs weighted by local fertility rates and WorldPop population density, then scaled to WHO country-level pregnancy counts.
  • Counterfactual analysis: re-ran 2023 simulations using 2000 MAP prevalence surfaces to estimate how many anaemia cases would have occurred under pre-intervention transmission levels, attributing observed reductions to malaria control scale-up.
  • IPTp-SP efficacy estimation: maximum-likelihood calibration to Cochrane-reviewed RCT Hb outcomes. IPTp-SP was modelled as a constant proportional mitigation (53.7%) of malaria-attributable Hb loss; impact under observed 2023 coverage was calculated using WHO country-level coverage estimates (55% receiving ≥2 doses), assuming multiplicative per-dose effect.
  • Mixed-effects regressions with natural cubic splines and random intercepts were used to disentangle maternal age from gravidity in determining malaria's effect on Hb; model selection via AIC and likelihood ratio tests.

① Integrate fine-resolution mobility data to improve within-country exposure heterogeneity

Extending this paperThe current spatial model operates at 0.2-degree (~5 km²) grid cells, using population density from WorldPop and MAP prevalence surfaces that smooth sub-district variation. In high-burden countries like Nigeria and DRC — which together account for 38% of estimated cases — transmission intensity varies sharply at scales smaller than the model grid. A direct extension: link human mobility traces (GPS or mobile phone call-detail records) to the existing MAP prevalence surface to derive person-weighted exposure that captures commuting and seasonal migration patterns. This would replace the static grid-cell assignment with mobility-adjusted exposure estimates, narrowing the uncertainty intervals currently spanning 30–50% of the point estimate.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and mobile-phone data pipelines could serve as the mobility layer here. The fusion of gridded disease-prevalence surfaces with origin-destination mobility flows is directly analogous to my work on hospital utilization and telemedicine spatial access — the methods transfer cleanly to malaria-exposure mapping. This would also extend my 2SFCA accessibility framework to an "exposure catchment" formulation where the supply-side is malaria transmission risk rather than healthcare facility capacity.

② Spatially explicit targeting: identify high-return IPTp scale-up locations via a geospatial optimization

Extending this paperThe paper estimates that current 55% two-dose IPTp coverage averted 1.10 million anaemia cases, while universal coverage would avert 1.45 million. However, the model treats coverage as nationally uniform, missing the sub-national heterogeneity in both IPTp uptake and transmission intensity. A natural extension is to re-run the burden model under alternative coverage allocation scenarios — holding total doses constant but spatially reallocating them toward high-endemicity, low-coverage districts. This geospatial targeting problem (maximize averted anaemia cases per additional dose delivered) is directly solvable given the existing grid-level infrastructure of this paper.

Added value to my researchThis is a health-geography facility-location problem: optimizing the allocation of a finite supply (IPTp doses) across heterogeneous demand (pregnant women at varying transmission risk). My EV-charging efficiency-vs-equity optimization framework generalizes directly — the objective becomes maximizing averted severe anaemia cases subject to equity constraints (e.g., ensuring minimum coverage in high-fertility, hypoendemic settings where immunity is also low). Explainable ML could also identify which district-level features (ANC attendance, road access, fertility rate) are most predictive of coverage shortfalls.

③ Apply causal machine learning to disentangle non-malarial drivers and strengthen the attributable fraction

Extending this paperThe paper acknowledges that it does not account for nutritional deficiencies, HIV, or helminth co-infections as independent contributors to Hb variation. The estimated malaria-attributable fraction (and its wide credible interval) would be sharpened by a causal inference approach — specifically a doubly robust estimator or targeted learning framework applied to the multi-country individual-level data, treating malaria infection as a binary exposure and conditioning on the rich set of covariates already available (gestational age, gravidity, country, enrolment Hb). This would produce a marginal structural model estimate of the population-average causal effect, which may either tighten or re-centre the current 95% CrI spanning 1.98–3.04 million cases.

Added value to my researchCausal inference for spatially structured observational health data is a methodological gap I am interested in bridging with geospatial foundation models. The challenge of isolating malaria's Hb effect from spatially correlated confounders (nutrition, co-infections, health system access) mirrors the challenge of separating built-environment effects from neighbourhood selection in my urban health work. Transfer-learning a geospatial foundation model fine-tuned on this dataset could serve as the nuisance-function estimator in the doubly-robust pipeline.

④ Model immunity erosion dynamics to project resurgence risk under funding-cut scenarios

Extending this paperThe paper's most important but least-developed finding is that declining transmission has eroded multigravid immunity, so that any resurgence of transmission would produce a broader anaemia burden across all gravidity groups rather than concentrating in primigravidae. The current counterfactual compares 2000 vs. 2023 transmission — a backwards-looking comparison. A forward-looking extension: simulate trajectories of maternal anaemia burden under alternative funding scenarios (e.g., 25%, 50% reduction in IPTp coverage; 10%, 20% rebound in transmission) for 2025–2035, using the paper's own integrated model. This would convert the paper's warning into quantified resurgence curves, the kind of projections WHO programme planners need.

Added value to my researchSpatiotemporal projection of health burden under policy perturbation is methodologically analogous to my agent-based modeling and SIR-based epidemic work. The immunity-acquisition structure in this paper — where past malaria exposure in each pregnancy modulates future susceptibility — is essentially a memory kernel that could be formalized as a physics-informed ODE layer in a neural ODE or graph-neural-ODE framework, one of my active research directions. The resulting model would produce spatially explicit resurgence risk maps — a GeoAI product directly relevant to malaria-programme decision support.

⑤ Link maternal anaemia burden to facility access to identify spatial double-burden zones

Extending this paperThe paper generates country-level and grid-level estimates of malaria-attributable anaemia prevalence but does not cross-reference these with the availability of ANC facilities where IPTp is delivered, or with emergency obstetric care that treats severe anaemia complications. Overlaying the paper's malaria-attributable anaemia risk surface with spatial access measures (travel time to ANC, obstetric emergency care) would identify double-burden zones — areas with both high disease risk and low care access. These are the true priority areas for combined malaria-control and health-system strengthening investments.

Added value to my researchThis is the natural intersection of this paper with my core research: 2SFCA/R2SFCA-based spatial accessibility measurement applied to ANC and obstetric care facilities in sub-Saharan Africa, overlaid on the malaria anaemia burden surface produced by this paper. The resulting double-burden vulnerability index is directly producible from open data (OSM facility locations, WorldPop, MAP) and would integrate my spatial accessibility methods with this paper's disease burden framework. It could also reveal whether IPTp coverage shortfalls are primarily supply-side (no facility nearby) or demand-side (facilities exist but are underutilized), informing targeted programme design.
