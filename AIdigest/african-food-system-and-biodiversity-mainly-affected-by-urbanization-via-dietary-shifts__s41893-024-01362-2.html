African food system and biodiversity mainly affected by urbanization via dietary shifts

Koen De Vos · Charlotte Janssens · Liesbet Jacobs · Benjamin Campforts · Esther Boere · Marta Kozicka · David Leclère · Petr Havlík · Lisa-Marie Hemerijckx · Anton Van Rompaey · Miet Maertens · Gerard Govers Published 2024-05-30 DOI 10.1038/s41893-024-01362-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Urban area expansion in Africa is not the primary threat to food production — direct cropland losses stay below 1% through 2050 because land-use displacement compensates most losses, shifting pressure instead onto natural lands. The bigger story is dietary change: urban rice consumption 1.5× that of rural households increases continental rice demand by up to +9.2%, production by +8.0%, imports by up to 2 Mt, and agricultural methane emissions by up to +12 MtCO₂-equivalent yr⁻¹, underscoring the need to treat urbanization as a systemic socio-metabolic driver rather than a simple land-cover conversion.

Why It Matters

  • Reframes the urbanization-environment narrative. Contrary to prevailing assumptions, urban sprawl is unlikely to cause major food production losses in Africa by 2050 — displacement effects absorb most direct losses. The real risk lies in dietary change, a dimension typically excluded from land-use models.
  • Quantifies the dietary-shift externality at continental scale. By coupling observed household-level rice consumption patterns (LSMS surveys) with a global partial equilibrium model (GLOBIOM), the study translates behavioral changes into concrete environmental outputs: +8% rice production, +2 Mt trade, +12 MtCO₂e CH₄ per year.
  • Reveals displacement as a hidden land-use dynamic. Compensation of converted cropland leads to up to 2 Mt of additional natural-land loss by 2050 — an effect that models ignoring displacement systematically miss, leading to both false reassurance on food security and underestimation of biodiversity risks.
  • Provides multi-scale evidence. Effects that appear minor at continental scale can be severe locally (up to −4.5% Biodiversity Intactness Index for the Lagos–Ibadan corridor), requiring spatially differentiated planning responses.
  • Actionable for policy across SSP scenarios. Running three Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSP1, SSP2, SSP3) reveals that governance and sustainability choices fundamentally alter outcomes, giving planners quantified leverage points.

Core Methods

  • Spatially explicit urban expansion mapping: overlay of SSP-aligned urban expansion projections (1 km) with CGLS-LC100 land cover in Google Earth Engine to compute per-simulation-unit land-cover conversion fractions at each time step (2000–2050).
  • Land-use displacement accounting: GLOBIOM is modified to introduce urban expansion as an exogenous land-cover change prior to land-allocation optimization, allowing the model to endogenously simulate cropland, grassland, and forest displacement — separating direct from indirect LUC effects (equations 3–6 in the paper).
  • Household survey analysis (LSMS): per-capita annual rice consumption estimated from 7-day recall data for urban and rural households separately; urban/rural ratios aggregated to regional level using country population weights (equations 8–10).
  • Urban demand disaggregation in GLOBIOM: the representative-consumer demand function disaggregated into separate urban and rural components using observed urban/rural rice ratios (a_reg) and SSP-aligned urban/rural population projections — enabling demand effects of urbanization to be isolated from expansion effects (equations 11–15).
  • Scenario analysis (four model runs per SSP): (1) SSP baseline; (2) expansion effect only; (3) demand/dietary effect only; (4) net urban effect (both) — compared across SSP1, SSP2, SSP3 to bound uncertainty.
  • Biodiversity Intactness Index (BII) calculation: land-use-area-weighted average of fixed BII values per land-use × simulation-unit combination, tracking change relative to baseline (equation 7).
  • Production loss estimation: direct losses = converted area × predicted yield; displacement-adjusted losses = difference in production between urban-expansion and standard model runs (equations 5–6).

① Spatially explicit dietary-shift modeling: from country-level to intra-urban gradients

Extending this paperThe paper's dietary disaggregation treats each region as having a single urban/rural split ratio (a_reg) derived from LSMS survey data, which it then applies uniformly across the entire region. A direct and powerful extension would be to replace this flat urban/rural binary with a continuous urban gradient — using the same spatially explicit urban expansion maps to weight consumption shift as a function of urban density class (peri-urban, intermediate, high-density core). This would reveal whether dietary-driven environmental impacts are concentrated in rapidly densifying secondary cities or dispersed across diffuse urban sprawl, and would directly inform targeted food-system interventions at the sub-regional scale.

Added value to my researchThis spatial refinement maps cleanly onto my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and GWR/MGWR toolkit: the urban/rural dietary ratio a_reg becomes a spatially varying coefficient over the urban density field, enabling local estimation of dietary-shift intensity rather than regional averages. For my urban analytics work on healthy cities and food environments, this produces a finer-grained geography of food system vulnerability that could be coupled with health accessibility models.

② Couple displacement-adjusted LUC models with urban evolutionary dynamics for predictive scenario refinement

Extending this paperThe paper's urban expansion projections are externally prescribed from Chen et al.'s SSP-aligned cellular automata model and then fed one-way into GLOBIOM. A richer extension would establish a bidirectional feedback loop: food-system stress (producer price increases, import dependency) modifies migration incentives and urban growth patterns that are themselves already governed by the scaling rules of African urban systems. The corpus paper on evolutionary urban systems in Africa demonstrates that African cities follow Zipf's law, Gibrat's law, and Jacobs' externalities — incorporating these growth rules into the urban expansion scenarios would produce more internally consistent projections of where urban growth occurs and how fast, with cascading effects on the displacement model's spatial outputs.

Added value to my researchFor my work on urban agglomeration, regional collaboration, and network community detection, this coupling creates a framework for analyzing how food-system shocks propagate through African urban hierarchies — an applied GeoAI problem linking agricultural economics to urban system dynamics. The Zipf/Gibrat parameters from the evolutionary urban systems paper serve as physics-informed priors for the urban growth model, analogous to my physics-informed graph learning approach.

③ From SSP scenario ensembles to learned uncertainty quantification over land-use futures

Extending this paperThe paper uses three discrete SSP scenarios (SSP1, SSP2, SSP3) to bound uncertainty, but the space of plausible futures is continuous and high-dimensional. The key finding — that dietary-shift effects dominate expansion effects — holds across all three SSPs, but the magnitude varies substantially (e.g., demand effect on rice production ranges from +5% to +12% across SSPs). An extension using global urban land simulation data (aligned with the corpus paper mapping urban land for the 21st century) as a richer ensemble of urban expansion trajectories, combined with an emulator trained on GLOBIOM outputs, would produce continuous posterior distributions over food and biodiversity outcomes — moving from point scenarios to probabilistic sustainability projections.

④ Integrate biodiversity extinction debt into BII projections: temporal ecology meets spatial modeling

Extending this paperThe paper's BII estimates acknowledge but do not model extinction debt and colonization credit — the lag between habitat loss and actual species loss (or gain). For the Lagos–Ibadan corridor, where BII drops 4.5% under SSP1, ignoring extinction debt means the paper may underestimate long-run biodiversity loss because species extinctions will continue to accumulate for decades after the habitat conversion event. A direct extension would couple the GLOBIOM-BII outputs with the temporal extinction debt framework established in the multidecadal urbanization-biodiversity corpus paper, producing lagged BII trajectories that distinguish committed loss (extinction debt) from realized loss — giving planners a fuller account of biodiversity risk under urban expansion.

Added value to my researchThe extinction debt concept is structurally analogous to the temporal lag effects I analyze in spatiotemporal health models — where past environmental exposures have delayed health outcomes. Applying my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation to model the accumulation of extinction debt over space and time, weighted by habitat fragmentation geometry, would operationalize this extension and connect urban ecology to my health geography toolkit. This is also a natural application for agent-based modeling of species population dynamics on fragmented urban-edge habitats.

⑤ Link urbanization-driven agricultural methane emissions to urban carbon accounting and climate co-benefits

Extending this paperThe paper shows that urban dietary shifts increase African agricultural CH₄ emissions by up to +12 MtCO₂-equivalent yr⁻¹ by 2050 — yet this is framed purely as a negative externality. An extension would ask: what urban-side interventions (dietary guidance, urban rice supply chain efficiency, urban agriculture) most efficiently reduce this agricultural carbon footprint, and how do these interact with the well-documented relationship between urban population density and carbon emissions? Linking the paper's demand-side methane projections to an urban CO₂ emissions model (as demonstrated in the corpus paper on urban density and CO₂) would complete the urbanization–climate feedback loop and reveal co-benefit trade-offs between urban densification (reduces per-capita transport emissions) and dietary intensification (increases agricultural CH₄).

Added value to my researchFor my urban analytics and data science work, this creates a multi-objective optimization problem: city planners trying to minimize total GHG emissions must trade off urban form (density vs. sprawl) against dietary interventions. Framed as a spatial optimization, this maps onto my EV charging efficiency-vs-equity optimization structure but applied to carbon co-benefits. The multi-source data fusion approach (combining dietary survey data, emission inventories, and urban form metrics from remote sensing) is directly within my methodological toolkit.
