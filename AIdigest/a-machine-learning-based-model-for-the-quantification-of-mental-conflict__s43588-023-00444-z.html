A machine learning-based model for the quantification of mental conflict

Yuki Konaka · Honda Naoki Published 2023-05-15 DOI 10.1038/s43588-023-00444-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Mental conflict — long considered purely subjective — can now be quantified from behavioral time-series. By extending the Free Energy Principle (FEP) with a meta-parameter governing the balance between reward-seeking and curiosity, and pairing it with a machine learning decoder, the authors show that rats' reward–curiosity conflict can be tracked moment-to-moment from observed choices: animals exhibited negative average curiosity (preferring certain rewards) yet adaptively spiked curiosity in response to sudden environmental changes, demonstrating active inference with autonomously controlled exploration.

★

Why It Matters

  • First quantitative window into subjective mental conflict. The work transforms an inherently private psychological state (the tension between wanting a sure reward versus satisfying curiosity) into a continuous, time-resolved signal readable from observable behavior alone.
  • Bridges computational theory and translational psychiatry. By decoding curiosity dynamics without access to the animal's brain, the framework opens a path toward quantitative behavioral biomarkers for conditions such as ADHD (excess curiosity), autism (reduced curiosity), and social withdrawal (hikikomori) — all without neuroimaging.
  • Unifies reward and information-maximization theories. Standard reinforcement learning (RL) maximizes reward; the FEP maximizes information gain. This paper provides a single parameterized model that interpolates between the two, capturing irrational or exploratory decision-making neither theory alone can explain.
  • Observer-side decoding: the decoder operates from the external observer's perspective — no invasive neural recording needed — making it directly applicable to any behavioral experiment or clinical interview enhanced with behavioral logging.

Core Methods

  • Extended Free Energy Principle (FEP): the standard FEP (which maximizes the sum of reward and environmental information gain) is augmented with a time-varying meta-parameter that controls how much an agent weights curiosity versus reward at each moment — enabling a formal, continuous representation of conflict.
  • Machine learning decoding: the temporal dynamics of the curiosity parameter are decoded from observed behavioral sequences (choices), treating the animal as a black box and inferring its internal conflict state from the outside. The 68% confidence interval on estimated curiosity is reported, providing uncertainty quantification.
  • Active inference test: the authors develop a method to discriminate curiosity-driven exploration from random exploration, demonstrating that rats engage in deliberate (active) inference rather than stochastic wandering.
  • Slot machine paradigm: using a two-armed bandit with intermittently switching reward probabilities as a controlled behavioral probe that reliably induces and varies the reward–curiosity conflict.
  • Inverse problem framing: the ML step solves an inverse FEP — inferring hidden belief states (curiosity level, recognition confidence) of the experimental subject purely from observable behavior, analogous to observer-side state estimation.

① Scale the inverse-FEP decoder to multi-timescale conflict dynamics

Extending this paperThe paper introduces a single meta-parameter that smoothly interpolates between reward and curiosity at one temporal resolution. However, real decision-making involves multiple nested timescales — within-trial fast reactions, episode-level habit formation, and long-run life strategy. A direct extension: decompose the meta-parameter into a hierarchy of timescales, each decoded separately, and test whether the brain arbitrates between them as recent neuroscience suggests (multi-timescale RL in corticostriatal circuits). This would make the conflict decoder richer and more neurobiologically grounded.

Added value to my researchThe multi-timescale idea maps onto my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation work: just as behavioral conflict operates across nested time horizons, spatial access patterns (commute, weekly errand, occasional hospital visit) operate across nested spatial-temporal scales. The inverse-FEP hierarchy could inspire a multi-scale accessibility decoder that infers latent travel-demand conflict (distance vs. time vs. cost) from observed mobility traces — relevant for 2SFCA catchment design under multi-modal transport.

② Replace the single meta-parameter with a learned recurrent network that discovers cognitive strategies

Extending this paperThe paper's conflict parameter is a scalar estimated by fitting a single equation to behavioral data. This limits expressiveness: real animals may switch between qualitatively different cognitive strategies (exploitation, rule-following, novelty-seeking) not captured by a one-dimensional dial. Extending the paper: replace the scalar meta-parameter with a tiny recurrent neural network (RNN) trained to decode latent strategy from behavioral sequences, then interpret the RNN's hidden states as conflict dimensions. This follows recent work showing that minimal RNNs can discover human-interpretable cognitive strategies in choice tasks.

Added value to my researchFor my explainable ML agenda, this is a direct template: a small interpretable RNN acting as a state decoder whose hidden states correspond to named conflict axes (reward preference, curiosity drive, uncertainty aversion). In health-geography terms, the same architecture could decode latent access-seeking strategies from patient GPS trajectories — revealing when a person switches from routine GP visits to urgent care-seeking, informing R2SFCA's dynamic catchment shape.

Cross-ref (corpus): Discovering cognitive strategies with tiny recurrent neural networks — 10.1038/s41586-025-09142-4

③ Apply distributional RL to model not just mean curiosity but the full distribution of conflict states

Extending this paperThe paper reports a point estimate of curiosity with a 68% confidence interval, but the full distribution of reward expectations — not just the mean — may be the neurally relevant signal. An extension: reformulate the conflict meta-parameter within a distributional RL framework, where separate pathways encode optimistic and pessimistic reward expectations, and the curiosity drive emerges from their disagreement. This would connect the paper to the emerging neuroscience of distributional RL encoded in opponent striatal circuits.

Added value to my researchDistributional thinking transfers directly to spatial health equity: instead of a point estimate of healthcare access, compute the full distribution of access levels for a population cell, and define equity as the tails of that distribution narrowing. This distributional R2SFCA would provide richer equity metrics than current mean-access scores — and the optimistic/pessimistic split mirrors spatial optimization scenarios (best-case redistribution vs. worst-case congestion).

④ Make the conflict decoder interpretable: open the ML black box for clinical psychiatry

Extending this paperThe paper's ML decoder is presented as a proof of concept but the article does not discuss what features of the behavioral sequence drive the decoded curiosity value — the decoder is essentially a black box relative to the clinician. Extending it: apply post-hoc explainability methods (SHAP, attention maps) to identify which behavioral signatures (reaction times, switch frequency, streak length) are the most informative predictors of the conflict state. This would make the framework usable for diagnostic interpretability and regulatory transparency in clinical settings, avoiding the pitfall that opaque ML models for high-stakes decisions invite.

⑤ Transfer the reward–curiosity conflict model to spatial decision-making and digital health accessibility

Extending this paperThe paper focuses on a temporal slot machine, but spatial choices (which clinic to visit, which route to take, whether to try a new facility) involve the same reward–curiosity trade-off: known nearby options (certain reward) versus unexplored farther options (curiosity-driven). Extending the framework to spatial decision tasks would allow decoding the temporal dynamics of facility-choice conflict from GPS or app-interaction logs — enabling a spatially-situated version of the conflict quantifier applied to urban or healthcare mobility.
