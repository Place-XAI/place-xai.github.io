A framework to evaluate machine learning crystal stability predictions

Janosh Riebesell · Rhys E. A. Goodall · Philipp Benner · Yuan Chiang · Bowen Deng · Gerbrand Ceder · Mark Asta · Alpha A. Lee · Anubhav Jain · Kristin A. Persson Published 2025-06-23 DOI 10.1038/s42256-025-01055-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Matbench Discovery establishes the first prospective, task-relevant benchmark for ML-guided crystal stability prediction, using 215,488 hypothetical inorganic structures as a test set larger than the training set. Universal interatomic potentials (UIPs) — which learn from forces and stresses in addition to energies — decisively outperform energy-only predictors, achieving F1 scores of 0.57–0.82 and discovery acceleration factors (DAF) up to 6× on the 10,000 most stable candidates. Critically, the paper reveals that strong regression metrics (MAE, R²) do not guarantee good classification performance: even accurate models generate unexpectedly high false-positive rates when predictions land near the 0 eV/atom stability threshold — the “triangle of peril.”

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a critical disconnect in materials ML evaluation. The community has long optimized for MAE/RMSE on formation energies, but this paper quantitatively shows these metrics can be misleading: models with strong regression performance can still yield high false-positive rates when predictions lie near the thermodynamic stability boundary.
  • Establishes a prospective benchmark at unprecedented scale. By using a test set (~215,488 structures) larger than the training set (~154,000 MP crystals), Matbench Discovery uniquely tests out-of-distribution generalization in the regime that matters for real discovery campaigns.
  • Introduces the Discovery Acceleration Factor (DAF) as a task-aligned metric. The DAF directly quantifies how many times more efficient ML-guided screening is compared to random selection — a practically meaningful metric missing from prior benchmarks.
  • Ships as open infrastructure. The Python package, online leaderboard with adaptive user-defined metric weighting, and Figshare-hosted datasets constitute a living community benchmark, not a one-time study.
  • Clears the path for autonomous materials discovery. Demonstrating that the top UIPs approach the theoretical maximum DAF of ~6.5× establishes that ML pre-screening now provides clear positive ROI before expensive DFT validation.

Core Methods

  • Prospective benchmarking design: test data generated after training data using the same computational discovery workflow (prototype substitution + DFT relaxation), producing a realistic covariate shift rather than retrospective cross-validation splits.
  • IS2RE task (initial structure to relaxed energy): all models must predict the convex hull distance of the relaxed structure given only the unrelaxed input structure — avoiding the circular dependency that would arise from requiring already-relaxed inputs.
  • Classification-first evaluation: primary metric is F1 score for stable/unstable classification at the 0 eV/atom hull distance threshold, complemented by precision–recall curves as a function of campaign length and the Discovery Acceleration Factor (DAF = precision / prevalence).
  • Triangle of peril analysis: rolling MAE plotted as a function of DFT hull distance to visualize the zone where a model's average error exceeds the distance to the classification boundary — revealing systematic false-positive bias near the stability threshold.
  • Universal interatomic potentials (UIPs) as surrogate relaxers: UIPs emulate DFT relaxation using learned force fields, enabling energy prediction from unrelaxed structures; trained simultaneously on energies, forces, and stresses.
  • Protostructure deduplication: WBM test set cleaned by Wyckoff-position protostructure matching against MP training set; 11,175 overlapping and 524 pathological structures removed before evaluation.

Extending this paperThe paper's central insight — that regression metrics (MAE, R²) can be misleading when predictions near a decision boundary drive costly downstream errors — is entirely general. A direct extension of the framework: port the “triangle of peril” visualization and DAF-style metric to any ML pipeline where continuous predictions are ultimately thresholded for binary decisions. The leaderboard's adaptive user-defined metric weighting could also be generalized to support asymmetric cost matrices reflecting domain-specific false-positive vs. false-negative penalties beyond materials science.

③ Translate UIP multi-signal supervision (energy + force + stress) to physics-constrained GeoAI graph models

Extending this paperThe paper's most consequential finding is that UIPs outperform energy-only models because they learn from forces and stresses in addition to total energies — a richer physical supervision signal that captures the potential energy surface more faithfully. A direct extension within materials benchmarking: train next-generation UIPs that simultaneously predict phonon spectra (dynamic stability) alongside thermodynamic hull distance (static stability), closing the paper's acknowledged gap of not screening for dynamically unstable candidates, which the authors identify as the next benchmarking priority.

Added value to my researchThe UIP lesson translates to physics-informed graph learning for urban systems: instead of supervising only on an output variable (e.g., healthcare utilization or facility demand), I can add auxiliary physical constraints — conservation of total demand, service-area contiguity, capacity ceilings — analogous to force/stress supervision in UIPs. Encoding these priors into a GNN rather than applying them post-hoc would produce more robust out-of-city generalization in my R2SFCA and EV-charging models, in the same way that physical signal diversity drives UIP transferability across chemical space.

④ Leverage the adaptive leaderboard design for reproducible open-science GeoAI benchmarking

Extending this to Matbench Discovery: incorporate uncertainty quantification metrics (calibration curves, expected calibration error) as first-class leaderboard dimensions, since models deployed in prospective screening must be well-calibrated in addition to accurate — especially when budgets are fixed and the cost of validating a false positive is high.

⑤ Use the paper's false-positive cost asymmetry framework to design equity-aware spatial accessibility metrics

Extending this paperThe paper finds that all models are asymmetrically biased toward false positives — predicting unstable materials as stable more often than the reverse — and attributes this to the MP training set's disproportionate fraction of stable structures. A testable extension: train UIPs on datasets augmented with high-energy disordered structures (Alexandria, OMat24) and measure whether the error asymmetry in the triangle of peril decreases, directly linking training distribution shape to false-positive bias. This would close the paper's own theoretical gap between its empirical observation and its mechanistic explanation.

Added value to my researchIn spatial health equity, false-positive facility siting (labeling an area as well-served when it is not) harms underserved populations more than false negatives harm already-served ones — an asymmetric cost structure that mirrors the paper's materials discovery setting. The paper's framework — modeling prediction error as a function of distance to a decision boundary, then mapping the asymmetry of false-positive vs. false-negative costs — gives me a principled design for equity-weighted accessibility metrics that penalize systematic over-prediction of access in low-income or minority neighborhoods, connecting my 2SFCA/R2SFCA work to the paper's cost-asymmetric evaluation philosophy.
