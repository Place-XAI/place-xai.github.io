Multiomics and deep learning dissect regulatory syntax in human development

Betty B. Liu · Selin Jessa · Samuel H. Kim · Yan Ting Ng · Soon Il Higashino · Georgi K. Marinov · Derek C. Chen · Benjamin E. Parks · Li Li · Tri C. Nguyen · Austin T. Wang · Sean K. Greenleaf Published 2026-04-08 DOI 10.1038/s41586-026-10326-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The Human Development Multiomic Atlas (HDMA) profiles chromatin accessibility and gene expression from 817,740 fetal cells across 12 organs, mapping over 1 million candidate cis-regulatory elements and 203 cell types. Deep learning (ChromBPNet) models decode a lexicon of 508 regulatory motifs, uncovering both 'hard' syntax rules (fixed motif spacing/orientation) and 'soft' rules (flexible arrangements) that mediate transcription factor cooperativity. Disruption of these motifs by genetic variants produces concordant directional effects on gene expression, linking fetal regulatory grammar directly to human disease risk.

Why It Matters

  • First multi-organ, multi-modal single-cell atlas of human fetal development. Previous datasets were restricted to bulk tissue, single organs, or single modalities; HDMA spans 12 organs at single-cell resolution with simultaneous ATAC-seq and RNA-seq from the same cells.
  • Decodes the grammar of gene regulation. The 508-motif lexicon plus hard/soft syntax rules provide a first comprehensive map of how transcription factors cooperate to control chromatin accessibility in a cell-type-specific manner during development.
  • Connects non-coding variants to disease through fetal biology. By overlapping GWAS fine-mapped variants with fetal-specific regulatory elements, the paper identifies 28 fetal-only disease-relevant variants — demonstrating that adult-onset disease risk can originate in developmental regulatory logic invisible to adult genomics.
  • Foundational open resource. All processed data (fragment files, counts matrices, ChromBPNet models, motif instances, genomic tracks) are deposited at Zenodo and raw sequencing data in SRA (BioProject PRJNA1402391), enabling community reuse at scale.
  • Negative regulatory logic. Discovery of 15 ubiquitous motifs that reduce chromatin accessibility — enriched near nucleosome dyads and for upregulating eQTL variants — adds a previously underappreciated repressive dimension to our understanding of regulatory syntax.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Primary atlas
HDMA (Human Development Multiomic Atlas) — 817,740 fetal cells profiled with SHARE-seq (simultaneous chromatin accessibility + gene expression per cell). Spans 12 organs (adrenal gland, brain, eye, heart, liver, lung, muscle, skin, spleen, stomach/oesophagus, plus thymus and thyroid), post-conception weeks 10–23, from 23 individuals across 76 tissue samples.
Regulatory elements
1,032,273 chromatin-accessible cis-regulatory elements (caCREs) — each 500 bp, collectively covering ~17% of the genome. 85.1% overlap ENCODE v4 candidate CREs; 14.9% are novel, with brain- and eye-derived elements disproportionately represented.
ENCODE v4 cCREs
Reference regulatory element database downloaded from downloads.wenglab.org/Registry-V4/GRCh38-cCREs.bed; used for comparison and adult peak sets from matched cell types.
VISTA enhancer DB
3,272 VISTA enhancers with hg38 coordinates (accessed 24 January 2024); 851 overlap HDMA organs; 766 intersect with caCREs. Used for in vivo enhancer validation.
GTEx eQTL data
GTEx v8 tissue-specific eQTL data across organs matched to HDMA fetal organs; used to validate directional effects of positive/negative motif disruption on gene expression.
GWAS fine-mapping
CAUSALdb — aggregates credible sets from 1,483 GWASs spanning 349 distinct traits; intersected with HDMA caCREs and fetal motif instances to prioritize disease-relevant variants.
Deep learning models
ChromBPNet — 945 convolutional neural network models (189 cell types × 5-fold cross-validation) trained to predict base-resolution ATAC-seq profiles from 2,114 bp DNA sequence context. Median Pearson correlation 0.78 between predicted and observed log-read counts in held-out test chromosomes.
Reference genome

Processed data

Core Methods

  • SHARE-seq single-cell multiomics: scalable split-and-pool combinatorial barcoding captures chromatin accessibility (ATAC-seq) and gene expression (RNA-seq) simultaneously from the same cells, enabling true paired multiomics at scale across 12 fetal organs.
  • Iterative cell-type annotation: Seurat + ArchR iterative dimensionality reduction and clustering (Louvain algorithm) across RNA and ATAC modalities; hierarchical annotation into 203 L1, 134 L2, and 37 L3 cell clusters; chromVAR for transcription factor motif enrichment.
  • Activity-by-Contact (ABC) model: links 1,032,273 caCREs to gene promoters within each cell-type cluster using pseudobulk ATAC-seq signal and power-law 3D contact frequency estimates; top 1% of ABC-linked genes defined as Highly Linked Genes (HLGs).
  • ChromBPNet deep learning: deep convolutional neural networks predict base-resolution ATAC-seq profiles (shape + magnitude) from 2,114 bp local DNA sequence context; trained with 5-fold cross-validation per cell type; DeepLIFT interpretation of per-nucleotide contribution scores.
  • De novo motif discovery (TF-MoDISco): aggregates high-contribution sequence segments into recurrent 30 bp patterns (CWMs); iterative clustering with gimmemotifs consolidates 6,362 raw motifs into 508 non-redundant lexicon motifs across 189 cell types; TOMTOM annotation against JASPAR/CIS-BP.
  • Fi-NeMo motif instance identification: contribution-aware sparse linear regression identifies predictive genomic instances of each motif in each cell type; ~839,544 instances per cell type on average.
  • In silico marginalization (tangermeme): exhaustive evaluation of joint vs. independent motif effects across all spacing (0–200 bp) and orientation arrangements; synergy defined as significant deviation from log-additive independence; hard syntax = spike at specific arrangement; soft syntax = broad distance preference up to ~100 bp.
  • g-chromVAR disease enrichment: quantifies cell-type-specific enrichment of GWAS fine-mapped variants in HDMA caCREs; 131 significant cell types identified (FDR < 0.05) across 1,483 GWASs.
  • NucleoATAC nucleosome inference: infers nucleosome positions from ATAC fragment size distribution; used to characterize positional bias of positive vs. negative motifs relative to nucleosome dyads.

① Transfer the ChromBPNet interpretability pipeline to spatial omics for geospatial foundation model fine-tuning

Extending this paperChromBPNet's DeepLIFT attribution pipeline identifies causal nucleotide-level features from a high-dimensional sequence space — essentially solving an attribution problem over a structured input. The paper's motif lexicon is a direct product of model interpretability applied to sequence "coordinates." A direct extension: apply the same contribution-score attribution framework to spatial transcriptomics data, where the "sequence" is a tissue section grid, to identify spatial co-expression patterns (analogous to composite motifs) that govern cell-type identity across tissue domains. This would extend the hard/soft syntax framework from DNA sequence space to physical tissue space.

ChromBPNet's training strategy — predict a high-resolution signal from a local context window with strong regularization — maps directly onto spatiotemporal kernel prediction: predicting fine-grained spatial outcomes (disease incidence, service demand) from local neighborhood feature vectors. The DeepLIFT attribution pipeline becomes an explainability layer for GeoAI models, identifying which spatial features causally drive predictions rather than merely correlating — directly advancing my explainable ML for spatial prediction agenda.

② Extend hard/soft motif syntax to a grammar of spatial co-location rules for urban facility networks

Extending this paperThe paper's central finding — that transcription factor cooperativity operates under either hard (precise spacing) or soft (flexible arrangement) syntax constraints — is a general principle about how pair-wise proximity and orientation of elements governs emergent function. A direct extension: test whether similar syntax classes exist for regulatory element triples or higher-order combinations, and whether the hard/soft syntax boundary shifts across developmental time (PCW 10 vs. 23), using the paper's in silico marginalization framework applied longitudinally across the developmental window already profiled.

Added value to my researchThe hard/soft syntax distinction maps onto a fundamental problem in urban accessibility analysis: does the co-location of two amenities (clinic + pharmacy) within a neighborhood produce super-additive health access benefits (hard syntax — specific proximity required), or does their effect decay gradually with distance (soft syntax — flexible arrangement)? This frames my 2SFCA/R2SFCA work as a motif synergy problem: the distance-decay function encodes the "syntax" of service co-location, and the question of whether healthcare facility pairs exhibit hard vs. soft co-location syntax for health outcomes becomes testable using paired facility access data.

③ Use negative motif logic to identify "accessibility-suppressing" spatial features in GeoAI models

Extending this paperOne of the paper's most surprising findings is that 15 ubiquitous motifs reduce chromatin accessibility — they are repressors embedded within accessible regions, enriched near nucleosome dyads rather than peak summits. The paper identifies them via negative contribution scores from DeepLIFT. A direct extension: systematically test whether negative motifs cluster in specific genomic contexts (e.g., constitutively accessible housekeeping regions vs. inducible enhancers), and whether their disruption in GWAS variants produces disproportionately large gene expression effects — since removing a brake may be more disruptive than removing an accelerator in a finely tuned developmental program.

Added value to my researchThe negative motif concept translates directly to spatial suppressor features in GeoAI: variables that systematically reduce a predicted health or accessibility outcome even when other favorable features are present. In my MGWR/GWR frameworks, I typically model direct positive and negative regression coefficients, but the ChromBPNet framing suggests a deeper question: are there spatial "anti-patterns" — specific feature combinations that suppress outcomes even within otherwise high-access neighborhoods? Identifying such spatial suppressors via model interpretation could reveal hidden barriers to healthcare access (e.g., physical highway barriers, zoning discontinuities) that conventional regression misses.

④ Apply the HDMA fetal-only variant discovery pipeline to spatial health disparity mapping

Extending this paperThe paper's approach of comparing variant overlap between fetal-specific and adult ATAC-seq peaks reveals 28 disease variants active exclusively during development — invisible to adult-only genomic studies. The pipeline (g-chromVAR enrichment → SuSiE PIP filtering → adult peak comparison → ChromBPNet variant effect prediction) is a general framework for identifying context-specific regulatory activity. A direct extension: apply this pipeline across all 349 GWAS traits in CAUSALdb, not just the curated 79 studied here, and build a tissue × disease enrichment atlas that maps which fetal organs contribute disproportionately to which disease classes — a developmental atlas of disease origins.

Added value to my researchThis "context-specific signal" framework directly parallels my work on spatial health disparities: just as the paper identifies variants that matter only in fetal contexts (invisible in adult data), spatial health patterns can have neighborhood-specific drivers that are invisible in city-wide or regional analyses. ⑤ Build a transferable sequence-to-function foundation model anchored in HDMA's cell-type diversity

Extending this paperThe paper trains 945 ChromBPNet models independently per cell type and fold, with no weight sharing across cell types. This misses the opportunity to learn a unified model that extrapolates to unseen cell types by conditioning on cell-type identity embeddings. A direct extension: train a multi-task or conditional foundation model where the 189 ChromBPNet models share a common backbone (sequence encoder) with cell-type-specific prediction heads, and condition on cell type embeddings derived from the RNA modality of the same HDMA atlas. This would enable zero-shot prediction of accessibility in cell types outside the 189 profiled — a transferable sequence-to-function operator grounded in the paper's own atlas.

Added value to my researchMy agenda explicitly includes fine-tuning geospatial foundation models on multi-source data. The HDMA architecture — shared encoder + task-specific heads conditioned on context embeddings — is exactly the design pattern I pursue for geospatial foundation models: a shared spatiotemporal encoder (satellite imagery + street networks) with location-specific prediction heads conditioned on urban context embeddings (density, function, demographics). The HDMA approach demonstrates that this design scales to hundreds of heterogeneous "tasks" (cell types) while maintaining high per-task fidelity — validating the architecture for my GeoAI use case and providing a concrete blueprint for multi-task geospatial prediction across heterogeneous urban contexts.
