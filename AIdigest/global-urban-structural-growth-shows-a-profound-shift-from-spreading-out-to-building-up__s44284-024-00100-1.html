Global urban structural growth shows a profound shift from spreading out to building up

Steve Frolking · Richa Mahtta · Tom Milliman · Thomas Esch · Karen C. Seto Published 2024-08-05 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00100-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 1,550+ cities observed over three decades (1990s–2010s) with independent satellite radar backscatter (a proxy for building volume) and Landsat-derived built fraction, the world's cities show a profound shift from lateral expansion ("spreading out") to vertical development ("building up"): built-fraction growth rates decelerated in most regions and large cities while backscatter growth rates accelerated in essentially all of them. Globally, rapid outward growth area fell ~56% while upward growth area rose ~400% from the 1990s to the 2010s. The transition is universal but time-lagged across regions, occurring earliest and most strongly in Asian (especially Chinese) cities, with major consequences for material/energy use and urban climate.

★

Why It Matters

  • First multidecadal, global measure of urban verticality. Most prior 3D-urban remote-sensing work was a single snapshot, a single decade, or a single city; this study reconstructs three decades of upward-vs-outward growth for more than 1,550 cities in 151 countries.
  • Two independent signals beat any single metric. Combining radar backscatter (sensitive to building volume / corner reflectors) with Landsat built fraction lets the authors disaggregate growth into four regimes — slow, outward, upward, and up-and-out — that neither dataset reveals alone.
  • Quantifies a planetary regime change. Outward (low-rise sprawl) was the dominant rapid-growth mode in the 1990s in every region except East Asia; by the 2010s it dominated nowhere, replaced by upward and up-and-out growth — a fundamental rewiring of how cities accumulate physical capital.
  • Direct hooks to sustainability. Building volume is the lever behind embodied carbon, material demand, operational energy, and local microclimate; an independent 3D growth record can constrain global material-stock and emissions models that currently hinge on sparse assumptions.

Global — 1,567 Morphological Urban Areas (MUAs) in 151 countries (5 MUAs excluded for missing data → 1,550+ analyzed), aggregated to 12 regions. Total of 34,880 urban grid cells at 0.05° lat/lon (~5 km, ~25 km² each), covering ~890,000 km².
Time window
Annual time steps 1993–2021; decadal trends computed per decade (1990s, 2000s, 2010s) because the three radar sensors are not directly continuous.
Radar backscatter (PR)
Seasonal urban backscatter at 0.05° from three scatterometers — ERS (1993–2000), QSCAT (1999–2009), ASCAT (2007–2021) — converted to power-return ratio; used as a proxy for building volume.

WSF-evo (World Settlement Footprint evolution, settlement fractional cover 1985–2015), Landsat-derived, aggregated to 0.05°. WSF-3D (building height/volume/fraction, 2012/2019 status) used for spatial validation of backscatter, not for trends.

Core Methods

  • Two-signal fusion: pair independent radar backscatter (PR, ∝ building volume via dihedral corner-reflector scattering) with Landsat built fraction (BF, lateral/infill growth) to discriminate growth types one signal cannot resolve.
  • Decadal trend estimation: compute grid-cell ΔBF and ΔPR rates per decade; do not stitch a continuous PR series — instead intercalibrate ASCAT→QSCAT and ERS→QSCAT using overlapping-year PR ratios to remove inter-sensor offsets.
  • Four growth regimes: bin grid cells by (low/high ΔBF) × (low/high ΔPR) into slow, outward (area-dominant), upward (height-dominant), and up-and-out (rapid 3D) growth.
  • Acceleration/deceleration tracking: measure whether each decade's BF and PR growth rate sped up or slowed down relative to the previous decade, at regional, subregional, and city scales.
  • k-means growth typologies: cluster all 34,880 grid cells per decade on initial-state + growth-rate features (e.g., budding, outward, stabilized, upward, slow-/fast-up-and-out), then trace inter-decadal transitions (Sankey).
  • Independent validation: correlate aggregate PR/BF against IEA and Chinese statistical floor-area series, and PR against WSF-3D building volume/height across 12 representative cities.

① Downscale the 5-km backscatter signal with a foundation-model fusion layer

Extending this paperThe paper's headline limitation is its coarse ~5–10 km backscatter resolution: a single 25 km² cell mixes new low-rise, new high-rise, and replacement, so no neighborhood-scale or smaller-city analysis is possible and no universal backscatter→volume factor exists. A direct extension: fuse the three-scatterometer time series with high-resolution SAR and WSF-3D using a geospatial foundation model to learn a downscaling operator that recovers building-volume change at street-block scale while preserving the multidecadal trend the paper already validated.

② Reconcile this volume-based shift with the divergence/dispersion view of 3D growth

Extending this paperThe paper measures the rate shift from outward to upward but treats each cell's regime independently and aggregates to regions. A natural extension: pair its backscatter-derived volume trends with the spatial distribution of vertical growth (concentration vs. dispersion of tall buildings within a city), testing whether "building up" is converging on cores or spreading — directly engaging a contemporaneous corpus result on built-up-volume divergence.
