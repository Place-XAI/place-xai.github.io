Mortality from short-term exposure to particulate matter, nitrogen dioxide and ozone across Europe

Zhao-Yue Chen · Hicham Achebak · Hervé Petetin · Wenzhong Huang · Raúl Fernando Méndez Turrubiates · Nadia Beltrán · Fabien Peyrusse · Yuming Guo · Carlos Pérez García-Pando · Joan Ballester Published 2026-05-13 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00124-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using daily mortality and air pollution exposure data from 653 regions across 31 European countries (2003–2019), short-term exposure to PM2.5, coarse particulates, NO2, and ozone each raises mortality risk by 0.54–1.14% per 10 µg/m³. Their combined effect accounts for an estimated 146,517 annual premature deaths (95% CI: 119,992–173,771). Vulnerability is strongly heterogeneous: younger males face higher risk from particulates and NO2 than young females, while elderly females face steeper age-related risk increases. Critically, uniform-risk models systematically underestimate mortality burdens in Eastern and Southeastern Europe, revealing geographic and demographic inequities that demand targeted air quality policy and early-warning systems.

Core Methods

  • Distributed lag quasi-Poisson regression (single-pollutant): region-specific time-series models estimating the lagged association between daily pollutant concentrations and mortality, controlling for temperature and other meteorological confounders.
  • Two-stage meta-regression with BLUP: Best Linear Unbiased Predictions pool region-specific relative risks to obtain continent-wide and country-level estimates while retaining geographic heterogeneity.
  • Weighted Quantile Sum (WQS) regression (multi-pollutant): joint model estimating the combined attributable mortality from all four pollutants simultaneously, avoiding collinearity issues of traditional multi-pollutant regression.
  • Attributable fraction / burden calculation: region-specific relative risks converted to attributable death counts using local population and baseline mortality rates; aggregated to national and European totals with 95% confidence intervals via Monte Carlo simulation.
  • Subgroup stratification: analyses stratified by sex, age group, cause of death (cardiovascular, respiratory), and degree of urbanization (urban/peri-urban/rural) to characterize demographic heterogeneity in vulnerability.
  • Sensitivity analysis: alternative exposure estimates from CAMS reanalysis used to verify robustness; comparison of local vs. uniform risk assumptions to quantify the estimation bias from assuming homogeneous European-average risks.

① Replace uniform risk pooling with geographically weighted multi-pollutant modeling

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that a single European-average risk estimate systematically underestimates burden in Eastern and Southeastern Europe — but it stops at documenting the bias rather than correcting it structurally. A natural extension is to replace the two-stage BLUP meta-regression with a geographically weighted regression (GWR/MGWR) framework that allows risk coefficients to vary continuously in geographic space. This would eliminate the discrete region-pooling step entirely, letting the exposure–response surface adapt to local climatic, demographic, and built-environment contexts simultaneously, and producing smoother spatially explicit burden maps.

Added value to my researchMGWR is a core method in my toolkit (GWR/MGWR for spatial non-stationarity). Adapting it to time-series quasi-Poisson count models — where the "outcome" is daily mortality, not a cross-sectional attribute — would extend MGWR into a spatiotemporal health-burden estimator. The resulting geographically varying risk surfaces would also serve as spatially explicit inputs to my 2SFCA-based health-access models, linking pollution vulnerability to healthcare accessibility gaps in the same spatial framework.

② Integrate satellite-derived PM estimates to extend the framework beyond EARLY-ADAPT mortality registers

Extending this paperThe study's mortality data are restricted to 31 countries with national statistical agency agreements (EARLY-ADAPT), which excludes much of Eastern Europe and neighboring regions where burden is likely highest. The exposure pipeline, however, already uses machine-learning (QML) models trained on ground monitors and reanalysis inputs. An extension: couple the QML exposure layer with satellite-derived aerosol optical depth (AOD) downscaled via deep learning to impute PM fields where ground monitors are sparse, then combine with cause-of-death data from the Global Burden of Disease to extend the framework to non-EARLY-ADAPT countries.

③ Add healthcare accessibility as a mediating layer between pollution exposure and mortality outcome

Extending this paperThe paper attributes mortality solely to acute pollution exposure, but the probability that a pollution-triggered cardiovascular or respiratory event results in death is not independent of access to emergency and specialist care. Extending this paper: introduce healthcare accessibility (hospital density, ambulance response, ICU capacity) as a regional moderator in the meta-regression. Regions with low healthcare access would show amplified pollution-attributable mortality even at identical exposure, decomposing the Eastern European excess into pollution load versus care-system deficit.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct bridge to my spatial accessibility of primary and emergency care research using 2SFCA/R2SFCA. Quantifying the care-access deficit's contribution to pollution mortality — even approximately — creates a new evidence basis for facility-location policy: reducing travel time to emergency care in high-burden Eastern European NUTS regions could be as life-saving as tightening emission standards.

④ Deploy explainable ML to disentangle the demographic drivers of heterogeneous vulnerability

Extending this paperThe paper documents sex- and age-stratified risk heterogeneity but cannot identify why younger males are more vulnerable to particulates or why elderly female risk escalates faster — the BLUP meta-regression is an estimator, not an explainer. An extension: fit a gradient-boosting or random forest meta-model on the 653 region-specific risk estimates, using as features regional socioeconomic indicators, healthcare infrastructure, housing type, ambient temperature, and urbanization class. SHAP decomposition of the meta-model would attribute each region's excess risk to specific context features, turning descriptive heterogeneity into mechanistic hypotheses.

⑤ Build an urban-scale early-warning system coupling the paper's regional risk surfaces with real-time air quality forecasts

Extending this paperThe paper explicitly calls for "air quality policy and public health early-warning systems" but provides only retrospective burden estimates. The next step: operationalize the paper's region- and demographic-specific relative risks as dynamic risk multipliers fed by CAMS real-time air quality forecasts. For each forecast day, multiply predicted exposure concentrations by the local BLUP risk estimates to produce a 48-hour mortality-risk alert at NUTS-region resolution, stratified by age-sex group. This converts a retrospective epidemiological study into a proactive public health instrument.
