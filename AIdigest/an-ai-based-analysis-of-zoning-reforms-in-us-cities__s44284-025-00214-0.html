An AI-based analysis of zoning reforms in US cities

Arianna Salazar-Miranda · Emily Talen Published 2025-04-04 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00214-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using natural language processing to read the actual zoning ordinances of over 2,000 US census-designated places, the study detects the linguistic fingerprint of form-based codes (FBCs) — reforms that regulate physical urban form rather than segregating land use. FBCs are already widely adopted (with strong regional variation) and are statistically associated with higher floor-to-area ratios, narrower and more consistent setbacks, smaller plots, better walkability, shorter commutes, and more multifamily housing. The work demonstrates that NLP can turn unstructured legal text into a measurable, nationwide indicator of zoning reform and its built-environment outcomes.

Why It Matters

  • Makes zoning law machine-readable at national scale. Zoning codes are long, idiosyncratic legal PDFs; this is the first study to quantify FBC adoption across 2,000+ places directly from the text rather than via hand-coded surveys.
  • Connects paper policy to physical outcomes. It links a code's language to measured urban form, mobility and housing (FAR, setbacks, plot size, walkability, commute length, multifamily share), closing the gap between what cities write and what they build.
  • Speaks directly to climate and sustainability. Use-segregating "Euclidean" zoning is tied to car dependence and sprawl; quantifying the reform that counters it gives planners evidence for compact, mixed-use change.
  • Reusable, reproducible method. Code is open (github.com/ariannasm/Zoning), so the FBC-similarity pipeline can be re-run on new ordinances as cities reform.

Core Methods

  • NLP on legal text: apply natural language processing to municipal zoning ordinances to identify linguistic patterns indicative of form-based-code principles (form/design language vs. use-segregation language).
  • FBC-similarity score: construct a continuous measure of how closely each place's code resembles FBC language, enabling national mapping (Fig. 4) and "high-FBC" classification.
  • Thematic / topic analysis: contrast key themes between FBC and non-FBC documents (Figs. 1–2) and relate FBC similarity to regulatory stringency (Fig. 3).
  • Outcome regression: OLS estimates linking high-FBC status to urban-form (FAR, setbacks, plot size), mobility (walkability, commute), and housing (multifamily share) measures (Fig. 5).
  • Geospatial integration: join text-derived FBC scores to parcel, footprint and street-network data to measure built outcomes per place.

① Upgrade the FBC-language detector from bag-of-words NLP to an LLM ordinance reader

Extending this paperThe paper detects FBC principles via linguistic-pattern matching over ordinance text, which can miss negation, cross-references and clause-level conditionality typical of legal writing. A direct extension: replace the similarity score with a large-language-model reader that extracts structured FBC attributes clause by clause (frontage rules, build-to lines, use tables), turning each ordinance into a machine-checkable schema and sharpening the "high-FBC" label the paper's regressions rely on.

Added value to my researchThe same LLM-as-policy-parser approach lets me convert health-facility siting regulations and Certificate-of-Need statutes into structured covariates for my accessibility models — making institutional text a first-class input alongside POIs and mobility data.

② Move from FBC-as-cause correlation to a causal, design-led planning loop

Extending this paperThe paper's outcome links are OLS associations (Fig. 5), vulnerable to selection — reform-minded places may already be walkable. Extending it: pair the FBC score with quasi-experimental designs (adoption timing, matched controls) and then close the loop with a generative planner that proposes the physical layout a given FBC implies, testing whether the code's intent is buildable.

Added value to my researchThis connects to my agent-based and explainable-ML work: I can simulate how a regulation change propagates into accessibility surfaces, then use SHAP/attribution to show which code provisions actually move equity outcomes.

Cross-ref (corpus): Spatial planning of urban communities via deep reinforcement learning — 10.1038/s43588-023-00503-5

③ Translate "improved walkability" into facility-access and grocery-provisioning gaps

Extending this paperThe paper reports that FBC places have better walkability and shorter commutes, but treats walkability as an index, not as access to specific destinations. Extending it: re-express each place's outcome as the number and placement of amenities needed to make the FBC promise real — e.g., how many groceries, clinics or schools a high-FBC code actually delivers within walking distance.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my 2SFCA/R2SFCA agenda: I can compute supply-demand catchments inside FBC vs. non-FBC places and quantify whether form-based reform narrows healthcare and food-access deserts, not just composite walk scores.

Cross-ref (corpus): Hundreds of grocery outlets needed across the United States to achieve walkable cities — 10.1038/s41467-025-61454-1

④ Use the FBC map as a national lens on sprawl, infrastructure cost and equity

Extending this paperThe paper produces a national FBC-similarity surface (Fig. 4) but stops at form/mobility/housing outcomes. Extending it: overlay the FBC map on infrastructure-service cost and sprawl metrics to test whether form-based reform lowers the per-capita cost of delivering water, transit and utilities — turning a code indicator into an infrastructure-economics instrument.

Added value to my researchThis feeds my urban-agglomeration and efficiency-vs-equity line: FBC scores become a regulatory covariate in MGWR models of how compactness trades off service cost against accessibility equity across regions.

Cross-ref (corpus): Urban sprawl is associated with reduced access and increased costs of water and sanitation — 10.1038/s44284-025-00338-3

⑤ Couple zoning-reform text with housing and mobility outcomes for equity policy

Extending this paperThe paper links FBCs to a higher multifamily share, hinting at affordability effects but not measuring who benefits. Extending it: connect the FBC indicator to longitudinal housing and intergenerational-mobility data to test whether form-based reform broadens access to opportunity, controlling for prior advantage — a natural-experiment view of zoning as a social-mobility lever.
