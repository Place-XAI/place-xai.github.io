The Blursday database as a resource to study subjective temporalities during COVID-19

Maximilien Chaumon · Pier-Alexandre Rioux · Sophie K. Herbst · Ignacio Spiousas · Sebastian L. Kübel · Elisa M. Gallego Hiroyasu · Şerife Leman Runyun · Luigi Micillo · Vassilis Thanopoulos · Esteban Mendoza-Duran · Anna Wagelmans · Ramya Mudumba · Ourania Tachmatzidou · Nicola Cellini · Arnaud D'Argembeau · Anne Giersch · Simon Grondin · Claude Gronfier · Federico Alvarez Igarzábal · André Klarsfeld · Ljubica Jovanovic · Rodrigo Laje · Elisa Lannelongue · Giovanna Mioni · Cyril Nicolaï · Narayanan Srinivasan · Shogo Sugiyama · Marc Wittmann · Yuko Yotsumoto · Argiro Vatakis · Fuat Balcı · Virginie van Wassenhove Published 2022-08-15 DOI 10.1038/s41562-022-01419-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The Blursday database — collected across nine countries from 2,840 participants using 14 questionnaires and 15 behavioural tasks during COVID-19 lockdowns — shows that subjective isolation (not objective stringency) most strongly warps the felt passage of time: the more isolated participants felt, the slower time passed. Objective mobility and stringency indices independently shape retrospective duration estimates and temporal orientation, and retrospective duration estimation obeys an inter-individual central tendency (Vierordt's law) that is significantly less pronounced during lockdown than outside it. The open, FAIR-compliant database provides a uniquely longitudinal, cross-cultural resource for studying how social disruption re-calibrates human temporal cognition.

Why It Matters

  • First large-scale, multi-country database of subjective time during a global disruption. Blursday captures 2,840 participants across nine countries on four continents tested longitudinally from the peak of 2020 lockdowns through post-lockdown control sessions — a historically unprecedented naturalistic experiment that no lab study could replicate.
  • Disentangles objective from subjective confinement. By pairing government stringency and Google mobility indices with participant-reported isolation scores, the study shows that felt isolation — not official rules — most powerfully governs the flow of experienced time over days to months.
  • Discovers an absolute temporal prior (~15 minutes) in free retrospective duration estimates. The inter-individual central tendency towards a 15–20 minute indifference interval across unrelated participants tested on very different durations is a non-trivial psychophysical finding with implications for interval timing theory.
  • Ships as reusable open infrastructure. All tasks, questionnaires (in five languages), raw trial-level data, and a Shiny GUI are openly available (CC BY 4.0, OSF + GitHub), enabling researchers to add control data and run new analyses without repeating the study.
  • Provides cross-cultural, longitudinal baselines for mental health, sleep, personality and timing jointly. No prior resource combined timing tasks and psychometric measures at this geographic and temporal scale, creating a rich platform for co-variation analyses across domains.

Core Methods

  • Online longitudinal experiment: participants recruited via institutional newsletters and social media; study hosted on Gorilla Experiment Builder; pseudo-randomized (Latin Square) task ordering across participants; multi-language deployment in 12 countries.
  • Retrospective duration estimation: participants estimate elapsed time since last login (1 min – 5 h range), tested at multiple within-session points; log-linear regression of subjective vs. clock duration captures the slope (power exponent) separately per session.
  • Visual analogue scale (VAS) ratings: passage of time over "last few days," subjective temporal distances to past (first lockdown day) and future (next week, next month), rated 0–100; Likert alternatives for some conditions.
  • Linear mixed-effects models (LMM): primary inference framework; fixed effects include stringency index, mobility index, subjective confinement score, age, and hour of day; random intercept per participant for repeated-measure outcomes; ANOVA on model coefficients to assess significance.
  • Vierordt's law / central tendency analysis: regression coefficient of log(clock duration) on log(retrospective duration) compared across sessions and to the theoretical identity to quantify deviation from ideal observer; indifference interval identified as the duration at which over- and underestimation balance.
  • Subjective confinement index: confirmatory factor analysis on 5 UCLA Loneliness Scale items most relevant to lockdown yielding a "felt isolation" score (5 = most isolated, 20 = least isolated); used as a continuous predictor alongside objective indices.
  • Outlier handling: three criteria applied to retrospective duration (clock duration 1 min – 5 h; subjective estimate 12 s – 25 h; relative error within central 95th percentile per country/session); ~8.8% of data removed.

Expanded Ideas — this paper's extensibility & added value to my research

① Embed geo-tagged mobility traces into the Blursday framework to spatialize temporal distortion

Extending this paperBlursday uses country-level Google mobility indices as lockdown covariates, averaging over entire national populations. A direct extension: link Blursday participants' self-reported confinement levels to fine-grained GPS or smart-card mobility traces at the individual or neighbourhood level, so that temporal distortion can be explained by local mobility collapse rather than a national average. This would reveal whether temporal distortion is driven by absolute movement loss or by loss of routine structure, and whether certain urban geographies (e.g., dense vs. dispersed neighbourhoods) are disproportionately disorienting.

Added value to my researchThis bridges temporal cognition to my spatiotemporal kernel density and mobility-data methods: individual-level mobility traces (GPS trajectories, mobile-phone records) become the exposure variable linking where people can go to how they experience time — an unexplored GeoAI linkage between space-human coupling and psychophysiological outcomes directly relevant to urban health policy.

② Replace country-level lockdown indices with spatially granular policy and mobility surfaces

Extending this paperThe paper's primary statistical models rely on national stringency and transit-station mobility indices, which mask enormous within-country variation — a city under strict local lockdown may score the same nationally as one with looser enforcement. Extending the Blursday framework: substitute these coarse covariates with city- or neighbourhood-level policy enforcement intensity and fine-grained mobility change data (e.g., urban walking patterns derived from mobile-device data), then re-fit the LMMs. This would test whether the paper's key finding — that felt isolation governs temporal distortion more than objective stringency — is diluted or sharpened when the objective indices better capture local reality.

Added value to my researchConstructing such granular policy-exposure surfaces requires multi-source data fusion (mobile phone records, POI visitation, satellite imagery of pedestrian activity) and spatiotemporal modelling — the core toolkit of my research program. The result would be a spatially explicit "lockdown exposure" covariate applicable to any urban-health regression, paralleling my 2SFCA service-area delineation logic but applied to restriction intensity rather than facility access.

Cross-ref (corpus): Effect of COVID-19 response policies on walking behavior in US cities — 10.1038/s41467-021-23937-9

③ Apply explainable ML to discover which behavioural and demographic predictors drive temporal distortion heterogeneity

Extending this paperThe Blursday statistical analyses use pre-specified linear mixed models with a small set of hypothesized covariates (stringency, mobility, subjective confinement, age, hour of day). The database, however, contains dozens of additional predictors — sleep quality (PSQI), personality traits (BFI-10), time perspective (ZTPI), mindfulness (FMI), depression/anxiety (HADS), circadian type (rMEQ/μMCTQ), and detailed demographics. Extending the paper: apply tree-based ensemble methods (gradient boosting, random forests) with SHAP or LIME explanations to the full feature space to discover which combinations of psychometric and lockdown variables jointly predict each temporal distortion outcome, without pre-specifying the model structure.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my explainable ML agenda to a new domain: predicting subjective time distortion rather than health-service utilization or housing prices. The heterogeneity discovered (e.g., that high-anxiety + night-owl chronotype amplifies temporal dilation under strict lockdown) would generate hypotheses testable in future controlled studies and demonstrates that my GeoAI method stack transfers to cognitive outcomes, broadening its health-policy reach.

④ Build a cross-cultural psychophysical scaling law: how does temporal prior strength vary with lockdown intensity?

Extending this paperThe paper's most theoretically striking result is an inter-individual central tendency (~15 min) that weakens during lockdown — participants conformed less to Vierordt's law under confinement. What the paper cannot determine with its current analyses is whether this modulation itself follows a systematic law: does the central-tendency attenuation scale linearly with stringency? Does it differ by culture? Extending the paper: fit the Vierordt exponent (the regression slope of log-retrospective-duration on log-clock-duration) as a country- and session-specific outcome variable, then model it as a function of stringency, mobility, and cultural variables — effectively estimating a second-order psychophysical scaling law across societies.

Added value to my researchQuantifying how an environmental intervention (lockdown) shifts a psychophysical law across cultures echoes my spatially varying coefficient models (MGWR/GWR): both ask "how does a parameter vary across contexts?" Treating the Vierordt exponent as a spatially/culturally varying regression coefficient would create a methodological bridge between psychophysics and geographically weighted modelling — a novel cross-disciplinary application.

⑤ Integrate Blursday's temporal disorientation measures into urban accessibility and mental health geography

Extending this paperBlursday demonstrates that mobility restriction warps time perception and temporal orientation — but the database contains no spatial data on participants' urban contexts (neighbourhood density, walkability, proximity to green space, access to services). Extending the paper: geo-code participants to their city/neighbourhood (even at NUTS-3 or metropolitan statistical area level) and join Blursday's temporal distortion outcomes to urban form and service accessibility metrics. The hypothesis: participants in car-dependent, low-walkability, service-poor environments would experience steeper temporal dilation during lockdown because their pre-lockdown routines depended on longer daily movement ranges, and lockdown erased them more completely.

Added value to my researchThis directly merges my two research pillars — urban accessibility modelling (2SFCA, POI proximity, 15-minute city metrics) and health geography (mental health, digital divides) — with a new outcome variable: pandemic-induced temporal disorientation. A spatially explicit model of where temporal distortion is worst would identify high-risk urban morphologies for future public-health policy, linking built-environment design directly to cognitive well-being during crises.
