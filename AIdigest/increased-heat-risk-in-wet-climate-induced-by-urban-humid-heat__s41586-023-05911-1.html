Increased heat risk in wet climate induced by urban humid heat

Zhang, Keer · Cao, Chang · Chu, Haoran · Zhao, Lei · Zhao, Jiayu · Lee, Xuhui Published 2023-04-26 DOI 10.1038/s41586-023-05911-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Cities in wet climates (summer precipitation >570 mm) experience a novel hazard: the urban area raises wet-bulb temperature (Tw) by 0.17 ± 0.14 °C on summer daytimes, primarily because urban canopies suppress the dynamic turbulent mixing that would otherwise lower humidity. Although small in absolute terms, this increment is enough to generate two to six additional dangerous heat-stress days per summer for urban residents — and this urban-humid-heat penalty will worsen under future climate projections.

★

Why It Matters

  • Overturns a standard assumption. The conventional view is that urban dry islands (UDI) partially compensate for urban heat islands (UHI), relieving heat stress. This paper shows that compensation fails in wet climates — the UHI wins — flipping heat-stress expectations for billions of city residents in humid regions.
  • Pinpoints a physical mechanism. By decomposing the wet-bulb island into dynamic (turbulence) and diabatic (heat storage) contributions, the paper identifies reduced aerodynamic mixing — not extra sensible heating — as the dominant driver of elevated Tw in wet climates. This gives engineers a concrete target for mitigation.
  • Quantifies a climate-type threshold. The 570 mm summer-precipitation boundary cleanly separates cities where urban living increases versus decreases humid heat stress, enabling globally consistent urban climate health policy based on measurable precipitation climatology.
  • Links to public-health outcomes. The study translates the Tw increment directly into extra heat-stress days, connecting atmospheric science to epidemiology and providing a metric that health authorities and urban planners can act on immediately.

Core Methods

  • Wet-bulb temperature (Tw) calculation: Tw is derived from paired dry-bulb temperature and relative humidity at urban and rural stations, providing a single integrated heat-stress metric that accounts for both temperature and humidity.
  • Urban wet-bulb island decomposition: the urban–rural Tw difference (ΔTw) is analytically decomposed into a UHI contribution and a UDI contribution, isolating the net heat-stress effect of urbanization across climate types.
  • Climate-wetness stratification: stations and model grids are binned by summer precipitation to reveal the precipitation threshold (~570 mm) above which ΔTw turns from negative (relief) to positive (extra stress).
  • Diagnostic thermodynamic analysis: within CESM2, the urban Tw increment is further decomposed into dynamic mixing (turbulence suppression by urban roughness) and diabatic heating (heat storage, sensible, and latent fluxes) terms, attributing causation mechanistically.
  • Heat-stress day counting: extra heat-stress days are computed by applying empirical heat-stress indices (wet-bulb globe temperature or equivalent) to the modelled Tw increment under present and projected future climate conditions.
  • Robustness testing: Extended Data Fig. 9 reports random omission of site pairs to verify that results do not depend on any particular subset of stations.

① Map the 570 mm precipitation threshold onto a fine-grained urban population-exposure layer

Extending this paperThe paper identifies a global climate threshold (570 mm summer precipitation) but reports results at the city or model-grid scale, not the sub-city scale. A direct extension: overlay the urban wet-bulb island intensity with high-resolution gridded population data to compute person-weighted extra heat-stress days for every climate zone. This would turn the paper's aggregate 2–6 extra days figure into a spatially explicit exposure surface, revealing which neighbourhoods and demographic groups (elderly, outdoor workers) bear the greatest burden — and whether the intra-city heterogeneity mirrors the social inequity observed for surface UHI.

② Integrate urban green space as a humidity-modulating design variable in the wet-climate Tw budget

Extending this paperThe paper shows that reduced turbulent mixing — not excess sensible heat — is the primary driver of elevated daytime Tw in wet climates. A logical extension is to ask: can urban green space restore that mixing and, equally importantly, does evapotranspiration from vegetation amplify or dampen Tw in wet vs. dry cities? The sibling paper on humid heat regulation by urban green space across a climate wetness gradient (Nature Cities, 2024) provides exactly this information. Combining both frameworks would produce a design rule for vegetation planning that is climate-specific: cool with trees in dry cities, but select species carefully in wet cities where adding moisture may not help.

This feeds into my EV-charging and public-facility efficiency-equity optimization work as a generalizable framework for multi-objective urban infrastructure placement.

③ Apply explainable machine learning to predict city-level Tw increment from morphological and climate features

Extending this paperThe paper's CESM2 model requires substantial computational resources and is run at coarse spatial resolution. A lightweight extension: train an explainable ML model (e.g., gradient-boosted trees with SHAP) on the paper's own station-pair dataset — using urban morphology metrics (building height, sky-view factor, impervious fraction), background climate variables (summer precipitation, vapour pressure, wind speed), and GHS-BUILT density — to predict ΔTw for any city. SHAP values would then verify whether the paper's theoretical primacy of dynamic mixing is reflected empirically in feature importance, and the trained model would generalize the paper's findings to cities without full climate model simulations.
