Publisher Correction: Automating turbulence modelling by multi-agent reinforcement learning

Guido Novati · Hugues Lascombes de Laroussilhe · Petros Koumoutsakos Published 2021-01-07 (Correction); Original article: 2021-01-04 DOI 10.1038/s42256-021-00293-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

This is a Publisher Correction that fixes swapped figure captions (Figs. 2–6) in the original article 10.1038/s42256-020-00272-0. The underlying paper demonstrates that multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) can automate the discovery of turbulence closure models for large-eddy simulation (LES) without supervised learning, matching DNS statistics and generalizing across unseen Reynolds numbers and grid resolutions with greater stability than classical Smagorinsky-based models.

★

Why It Matters

  • First MARL-based closure model for turbulence: breaks from the tradition of supervised learning (which requires differentiable DNS targets) by letting agents optimize long-term statistical fidelity — inherently handling compounding errors that a priori supervised models cannot.
  • Generalizes out-of-distribution: trained on Reλ ∈ {65–163}, the learned policy remains stable and accurate at Reλ = 205 — beyond the training range — and transfers across grid sizes (323, 643, 1283) without retraining.
  • Eliminates manual coefficient tuning: the Smagorinsky constant Cs — historically hand-tuned — is replaced by a spatially adaptive, learned control policy, reducing problem-specific engineering intuition.
  • Experimental-data pathway: because the RL reward only requires statistical measurements (e.g. energy spectra), training can in principle bypass DNS entirely and use experimental measurements, opening a route to real-world turbulence model discovery.
  • Open-source, reproducible infrastructure: simulation solver CubismUP_3D, RL library smarties, and coupling code all released on GitHub, enabling community extension to other conservation-law closures.

Core Methods

  • Multi-agent RL (MARL) for closure discovery: Nagents cooperating agents are spatially distributed on the LES grid; each agent samples the Smagorinsky eddy-viscosity coefficient Cs2(x, t) from a shared Gaussian policy conditioned on local (velocity-gradient invariants) and global (energy spectrum) flow state.
  • ReF-ER (Remember and Forget Experience Replay): controls the pace of policy updates via a trust-region mechanism (KL-divergence penalization), stabilizing training in the multi-agent setting where individual agents' actions confound credit assignment.
  • V-RACER off-policy optimization: off-policy policy gradient with importance-sampling weights (Retrace) for sample efficiency; mini-batches of B = 512 from a replay memory of 106 steps; updated with Adam (lr = 10−5) for 107 gradient steps.
  • Reward design: two reward functions tested — rLL (maximizes log-likelihood of LES energy spectrum against DNS distribution) and rG (minimizes Germano identity error locally). rLL outperforms, confirming that matching long-term statistics beats minimizing instantaneous residuals.
  • DNS-to-LES comparison: energy spectrum log-likelihood (equation 2) as the primary objective; additional evaluation on velocity structure functions S2(r) and integral quantities (kinetic energy, integral length scale, dissipation rates) not seen during training.
  • Generalization testing: evaluation at Reλ = {60, 82, 111, 151, 190, 205} — all outside the training set — and at grid resolutions 643 and 1283 trained only on 323.

① Scale the MARL closure framework to spatially heterogeneous urban flows via graph neural networks

Extending this paperThe MARL framework places agents on a regular Cartesian grid suited to isotropic, periodic turbulence. Real-world engineering and urban-physics flows (street-canyon turbulence, ventilation, wind around buildings) are geometrically irregular and boundary-condition-heavy. A direct extension: replace the Cartesian agent grid with a graph-structured MARL in which nodes are mesh cells of an unstructured CFD solver and edges encode adjacency. This would let cooperating agents share local state via message-passing and learn SGS closures that respect the graph topology of urban geometries — generalizing the original paper's closure paradigm from periodic boxes to arbitrary domains.

Added value to my researchGraph-structured MARL is a natural entry point for my physics-informed graph learning agenda: the neighborhood aggregation operator in a GNN directly mirrors the spatial stencil of the LES agent, allowing physical constraints (e.g. incompressibility, energy conservation) to be encoded as graph-level loss terms. For urban-health GeoAI, this opens a path to simulate air-pollution dispersion at street level using RL-learned closure policies rather than hand-tuned atmospheric models — enriching spatial exposure assessment in my health-geography work.

② Replace the fixed Smagorinsky architecture with a sparse, interpretable policy for mechanistic insight

Extending this paperThe paper trains a 6,211-parameter feed-forward neural network and explicitly notes that "the present policies may not be transferable to another flow solver." The authors flag sparse RL as a future direction for mechanistic insight. A concrete extension: apply sparse policy distillation — train the dense MARL policy then distill it into a sparse symbolic regression or decision-tree policy that expresses Cs2 as a compact function of local invariants. This would test whether a handful of invariant combinations suffice, potentially recovering or extending Kolmogorov's phenomenology in a data-driven way.

Added value to my researchThe distillation pipeline mirrors my use of explainable ML for spatial prediction: just as I extract SHAP feature importances from gradient-boosted trees to identify which urban variables drive health outcomes, sparse RL distillation would reveal which turbulence invariants the policy actually relies on — a physics analogue of spatial feature attribution for GeoAI interpretability.

③ Transfer the ReF-ER stability mechanism to spatiotemporal kernel density estimation with long memory

Extending this paperThe paper's most technically novel contribution is ReF-ER: a trust-region experience-replay mechanism that prevents runaway policy changes by penalizing KL-divergence from prior policies. In the MARL setting this is critical because agents share a policy and their interactions produce highly correlated updates. The same instability pathology arises whenever a model must be updated online from correlated, streaming observations — a regime common in sequential spatial processes. An extension: embed ReF-ER-style trust-region constraints into an online spatiotemporal model (e.g. a streaming kernel density estimator over mobile phone trajectories), using the trust region to prevent catastrophic forgetting of earlier spatial patterns while adapting to new data.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal kernel density estimation work (e.g. for pedestrian health exposure) currently uses fixed bandwidths updated in batch. A ReF-ER-inspired online update rule would let the KDE bandwidth adapt continuously as mobility data streams in while bounding how much each new batch can shift the estimated density surface — a principled alternative to sliding-window averaging that preserves temporal coherence.

④ Use the experiment-driven reward principle to train accessibility models from real-world mobility data without GPS ground truth

Extending this paperA key insight of the paper is that RL reward functions can be defined from statistical measurements rather than ground-truth labels: energy spectra can come from experiments, bypassing the need for DNS. The identical logic applies whenever you have a measurable aggregate statistic but lack fine-grained labels. Extending the paper: apply the same statistics-as-reward principle to other PDE-governed systems (e.g. shallow-water equations for flood modelling or diffusion equations for pollutant transport) where experimental sensor data can serve as reward, removing the computational burden of full-resolution reference simulation.

Added value to my researchIn 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility modelling, the "ground truth" for catchment-level demand is rarely observable directly — only aggregate statistics (utilization rates, appointment volumes) are available from clinic records. The paper's statistics-as-reward paradigm suggests training a policy that adapts catchment delineation parameters to match observed utilization statistics across healthcare facilities, without requiring individual-level GPS data. This would convert my supply-side accessibility model into a data-calibrated demand-side one.

⑤ Apply cross-resolution generalisation testing as a validation protocol for geospatial foundation models

Extending this paperThe paper designs a systematic out-of-distribution generalisation test: train on one grid resolution, evaluate on two coarser and finer grids, and report log-likelihood at each (Fig. 6e). This cross-resolution benchmark is methodologically independent of the turbulence domain and could be formalised as a general protocol for evaluating ML closure models in any PDE system. An extension: develop a resolution-invariance benchmark suite analogous to Fig. 6e for other physics-based ML models (e.g. neural weather forecasting), measuring how accuracy degrades as you deviate from training resolution and using that degradation curve as a model-selection criterion.

Added value to my researchGeospatial foundation models (e.g. fine-tuned remote-sensing encoders) face an analogous resolution-invariance problem: a model trained on 10 m satellite imagery should degrade gracefully when applied to 30 m or 100 m data. Adopting the paper's cross-resolution log-likelihood protocol as a resolution-robustness benchmark for geospatial FM fine-tuning would provide a principled evaluation metric beyond standard accuracy, directly relevant to my data-science and reproducibility research theme around open science and FAIR geospatial models.
