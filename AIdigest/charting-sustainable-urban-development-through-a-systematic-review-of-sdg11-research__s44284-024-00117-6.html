Charting sustainable urban development through a systematic review of SDG11 research

Abdulaziz I. Almulhim · Ayyoob Sharifi · Yusuf A. Aina · Shakil Ahmad · Luca Mora · Walter Leal Filho · Ismaila Rimi Abubakar Published 2024-08-27 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00117-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A bibliometric analysis of 21,153 publications (2016–2022, 229,182 citations) shows SDG11 research has grown into a multidisciplinary field dominated by affordable housing, urban transport, governance and public space, while urban safety, inclusion, cultural/natural heritage (SDG11.4), urban–rural linkages (SDG11.a) and support for least-developed countries (SDG11.c) remain under-studied. China, the USA and the UK produce 47.71% of the output, exposing an Anglo–American hegemony and a Global South research gap precisely where 90% of future urban growth will occur.

Why It Matters

  • First global scientometric map of SDG11. Most prior reviews lump all 17 SDGs together; this is the first comprehensive, goal-specific stocktake of "sustainable cities and communities" at the halfway point to 2030.
  • Names the blind spots. By quantifying thematic imbalance, it shows that safety, inclusion and heritage — the social-justice core of SDG11 — are crowded out by environmental and economic topics, risking the perpetuation of existing disparities.
  • Exposes the geography of knowledge. Mapping authors, institutions and country collaborations reveals a Global North concentration that mismatches where urbanization pressure is greatest (Africa, Asia).
  • Actionable agenda for funders and policymakers. Translates patterns into priorities — urban governance, localizing targets, cross-sectoral integration, and inclusive digitalization/smart-city development.

Core Methods

  • Bibliometric / scientometric analysis: quantitative text-mining of publication metadata to trace trends, themes and contributors across 21,153 SDG11 documents.
  • Boolean search design: a long SDG11-aligned query (city/urban terms AND housing/transport/governance/resilience/heritage/green-space terms) executed in WoS TS fields for 2016–2022.
  • Term co-occurrence mapping: VOSviewer keyword networks (with a thesaurus file to merge synonyms) to surface thematic clusters and their evolution across the two periods.
  • Co-citation & collaboration networks: identify influential journals, institutions and country-to-country collaboration structures (Fig. 3).
  • Sensemaking interpretation: a qualitative layer over the quantitative results to move from description to insight, bridging bibliometrics and systematic review.
  • PRISMA workflow: transparent four-stage identification/screening/eligibility/inclusion reporting (Fig. 4).

① Ground the bibliometric blind spots in measured spatial outcomes

Extending this paperThe paper diagnoses an imbalance of attention (housing/transport/governance dominate; safety, inclusion and SDG11.a urban–rural linkages lag) purely from publication counts. A direct extension: cross-walk each SDG11 sub-target's research volume against an actual on-the-ground indicator (e.g., measured transport accessibility), to test whether the topics cities study most are the ones where they perform worst — turning a literature map into a research-vs-reality audit.

Added value to my researchThis is a template for my SDG11.2 work: pair an accessibility metric (2SFCA/R2SFCA-style service-area delineation for primary care, transit) against thematic coverage, so my health-accessibility studies are framed as filling an evidenced gap rather than an arbitrary case.

② Replace keyword co-occurrence with LLM-based thematic extraction

Extending this paperThe paper's themes come from VOSviewer keyword co-occurrence plus a hand-built thesaurus — sensitive to author keywording and synonym choices. Extending it: re-run the same 21,153-document corpus through an LLM embedding + topic-model pipeline on full abstracts, and compare the recovered SDG11 sub-target structure to the keyword map, quantifying how much the "imbalance" is a measurement artifact.

Added value to my researchThis connects to my fine-tuning of foundation models for geospatial text: an LLM SDG-classifier over abstracts becomes a reusable tool for screening multi-source literature in my health-geography reviews, with explainability checks on the assigned topics.
