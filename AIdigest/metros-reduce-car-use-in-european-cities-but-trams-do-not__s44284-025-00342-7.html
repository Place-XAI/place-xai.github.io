Metros reduce car use in European cities but trams do not

Rafael Prieto-Curiel (Complexity Science Hub, Vienna) Published 2025-11-07 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00342-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 378 European cities, those with a metro have a significantly lower share of car journeys than cities with only a tram or no rail system at all. Crucially, trams alone do not deliver the same car-substitution effect — once city size and other factors are accounted for, tram-only cities look much like cities with no rail. The high-capacity, grade-separated nature of metros, particularly in medium and large cities, is what shifts trips out of cars, captured through the simple ABC framework (Active mobility, Bus/public transport, Cars).

Why It Matters

  • Settles an expensive policy question with comparable data. Metros cost orders of magnitude more than trams; the paper provides cross-city evidence on which rail investment actually displaces car trips, informing multi-billion-euro infrastructure decisions.
  • Distinguishes mode type from mode presence. Simply "having rail" is not enough — the result that trams do not reduce car share challenges a common assumption that any rail transit is climate-progressive.
  • Uses a harmonized, transparent metric. The ABC framework collapses messy, incomparable mobility surveys into three intuitive shares, making 378 cities directly comparable — a reusable standard for modal-share research.
  • Directly relevant to decarbonization and health. Car journeys drive emissions, sprawl, air pollution and sedentary lifestyles; identifying the infrastructure that actually moves the modal needle is a lever for healthier, lower-carbon cities.

Core Methods

  • ABC harmonization: reduce heterogeneous mobility surveys to three comparable shares — Active mobility (A), Bus/public transport (B), Cars (C) — enabling like-for-like comparison across 378 cities.
  • Group comparison: stratify cities into metro, tram-only and no-rail classes and compare their ABC distributions (Fig. 3).
  • Controlling for confounders: examine modal-share differences while accounting for city size and other determinants, isolating the metro effect from the simple correlation between large cities and rail.
  • Descriptive + inferential statistics: test whether the lower car share in metro cities is statistically significant relative to tram-only and no-rail cities (Fig. 4).
  • Global rail characterization: place the European sample in context with worldwide metro/tram coverage, length, station counts and ridership (Fig. 1).

① Move from "metro present/absent" to a network-topology dose-response

Extending this paperThe paper's explanatory variable is essentially a categorical label (metro / tram-only / none), which conflates a 2-line system with a dense 14-line network. A direct extension: replace the binary with continuous network-topology features — coverage, line count, station density, grade-separation, connectivity and ridership — and fit a dose-response linking rail structure to car-share reduction. This converts the paper's contrast into a graded law and would reveal whether a sufficiently extensive tram network can match a small metro.

Added value to my researchThe same topology-to-behavior mapping feeds my explainable-ML spatial prediction: an MGWR/ML ensemble with SHAP attribution that quantifies how much each transit-network feature locally buys in car-trip displacement, spatially non-stationary across European city types.

② Add demand-side accessibility behind the supply-side modal share

Extending this paperThe ABC framework measures realized modal share but not the accessibility that produces it — how many jobs/services each resident can actually reach by metro vs tram within a time budget. Extending the paper with a 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility layer over the same 378 cities would test whether metros lower car share because they raise reachable-opportunity counts, supplying the causal mechanism the descriptive comparison lacks.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my R2SFCA service-area delineation to transit: compute metro vs tram accessibility catchments and relate them to the car-share gap, transferring my healthcare-access methods to mobility equity.

③ Translate car-share reductions into emissions and health co-benefits

Extending this paperThe paper stops at modal share; it never closes the loop to the harms (emissions, pollution, sedentary disease) it motivates with. A natural extension: couple the metro-induced car-share reduction to a transport-emissions / energy-demand model and a health-impact model, producing per-city avoided-CO₂ and avoided-disease estimates that turn the result into a cost-benefit case for metro investment.

④ Stress-test the metro advantage for resilience, not just mean car share

Extending this paperThe paper compares average modal shares but says nothing about robustness: a metro-dependent city may suffer disproportionately when a line fails. Extending it: build the multimodal transport graph for each city and evaluate network resilience under node/edge removal, asking whether the cities that most reduce car use are also the most fragile — adding a resilience dimension to the metro-vs-tram verdict.

Added value to my researchThis pairs with my physics-informed graph learning and network community-detection work: a GNN that jointly predicts car-share reduction and resilience over the transit meta-graph, attributing which structural motifs deliver both.

⑤ Generalize the ABC contrast beyond Europe and beyond rail

Extending this paperThe analysis is European; CitiesMoving is worldwide. Extending it to Global-South and Asian cities — and to non-rail alternatives such as BRT, superblocks and remote-work-driven demand changes — would test whether "metros beat trams" is a universal regularity or a function of European urban form and income. A foundation-model fine-tuned on the harmonized ABC labels could even impute modal share for cities lacking surveys.
