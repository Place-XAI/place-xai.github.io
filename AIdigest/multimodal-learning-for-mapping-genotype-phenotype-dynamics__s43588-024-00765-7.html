Multimodal learning for mapping genotype–phenotype dynamics

Farhan Khodaee · Rohola Zandie · Elazer R. Edelman Published 2025-01-28 DOI 10.1038/s43588-024-00765-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The authors introduce PolyGene, a multimodal BERT-based foundation model trained on nearly 500,000 human cells across 24 tissues from the Tabula Sapiens atlas, which jointly embeds gene expression (genotype) and phenotypic metadata (cell type, tissue, age, sex) into a unified manifold via a novel integrated genetics pretraining objective. The resulting contextualized embeddings resolve cellular heterogeneity more finely than gene-expression-only baselines, uncover cross-tissue biomarkers including an unexpected kidney–tongue molecular similarity, and reveal the polyfunctionality of individual genes — demonstrated by discovering two functionally distinct roles of the VWF gene in endothelial cells (canonical hemostasis and a secondary reactive-oxygen-species response). PolyGene consistently outperforms scGPT on tissue and cell-type classification on an independent 75,000-cell lung–liver atlas.

Why It Matters

  • Bridges the genotype–phenotype gap at atlas scale. Jointly masking and reconstructing both gene expression tokens and phenotype labels in a single self-supervised pass makes the century-old genotype–phenotype mapping problem tractable computationally, without requiring any labeled contrast pairs.
  • Reveals hidden biological structure in embeddings. Contextualized embeddings expose tissue subclusters invisible to raw-expression UMAP, and tissue-similarity hierarchies (cardiac atrium ↔ ventricle, kidney ↔ tongue) align with or predict known clinical connections.
  • Operationalizes gene polyfunctionality. By clustering VWF embeddings in endothelial cells, the model identifies a secondary reactive-oxygen-species function alongside the canonical hemostasis role — a new precision-targeting strategy for drug discovery and cell therapy.
  • Demonstrates context-dependent gene-network restructuring. Power-law degree-distribution shifts in endothelial-cell gene networks between donors under 40 and over 60 propose testable vascular-aging candidates (KCNH8, DNAJA4, EGLN3).
  • Supplies a reusable genomics pretraining recipe. The model, tokenizer, and training code are publicly released on GitHub and Zenodo, enabling the community to fine-tune PolyGene for any downstream transcriptomic task.

Core Methods

  • Multimodal BERT-style transformer (PolyGene): gene expression tokens and phenotype label tokens (tissue, cell type, age, sex) are concatenated into one input sequence; both are randomly masked and jointly reconstructed via masked-language-modeling loss, enabling bidirectional attention across genotype and phenotype modalities simultaneously.
  • Gene tokenization via expression binning: continuous expression values are normalized by dispersion rate and discretized into B equal-width bins; unexpressed genes (value < 0.1) are padded, not masked, so training focuses on biologically meaningful signal. Fixed positional encoding by gene identity (not expression rank) resolves the loss-function ambiguity that affects prior methods.
  • Soft-label ordinal regression loss: a modified cross-entropy spreads probability mass across adjacent bins, improving calibration for ordinal expression levels; the paper reports 9% precision / 19% recall improvement over hard labels after 10 epochs.
  • Contextualized embedding extraction: final transformer layer outputs per-gene and per-phenotype embeddings; cosine similarity and Euclidean distance between these are used for tissue hierarchy analysis, differentially embedded gene (DEG) identification via Wilcoxon rank-sum test, and polyfunctionality clustering.
  • Scale-free gene-network construction: gene interaction networks are built by thresholding cosine similarities between gene embeddings; power-law degree distributions characterize topology and detect aging-related restructuring in endothelial cells.
  • Polyfunctionality detection: within-cell-type clustering of a single gene's embeddings across all cells reveals functionally distinct subclusters; validated by GO enrichment with Fisher's exact test and FDR correction (demonstrated for VWF and CD55).
  • Benchmark comparison: precision, recall, and F1 for tissue and cell-type classification compared against scGPT on the independent held-out lung–liver–blood atlas.

① Fine-tune PolyGene on disease-specific atlases to build a phenotype-conditional cell-state predictor

Extending this paperPolyGene is pretrained on healthy Tabula Sapiens cells and benchmarked on a healthy lung–liver atlas; it is explicitly positioned as a foundation model for downstream fine-tuning, yet no clinical fine-tuning experiment is demonstrated. A direct extension would fine-tune the released model on disease atlases (e.g., tumor microenvironments or chronic-kidney-disease biopsies) using the same integrated-genetics masking strategy — introducing a "disease-status" phenotype token alongside sex, age, and tissue. Because PolyGene already encodes four phenotype modalities, adding one more requires minimal architectural change and should produce embeddings capable of discriminating pathological cell states from healthy counterparts, enabling cross-tissue biomarker transfer to diseased tissues. The corpus paper by Theodoris et al. on predicting drug responses via transfer learning from foundation models provides an experimental blueprint for exactly this fine-tuning workflow.

Added value to my researchFine-tuning geospatial foundation models is one of my declared research directions. PolyGene's fine-tuning protocol — freeze pre-trained weights, inject a new context token, apply the same masking loss — is architecturally analogous to fine-tuning a geospatial foundation model (e.g., SatMAE or GeoCLIP) on local health-outcome prediction with limited labeled data. The key lesson from PolyGene: phenotype-token injection during pretraining dramatically reduces fine-tuning data requirements. I can apply the same design to spatial foundation models by injecting urban-context tokens (neighborhood type, socioeconomic tier, land-use class) during pretraining on satellite imagery or POI data, then fine-tuning on small labeled health-outcome datasets.

② Extend PolyGene's multimodal embedding to spatial transcriptomics for intra-tissue geography

With spatial coordinates encoded, the model's contextualized embeddings would simultaneously capture gene function, tissue context, and intra-tissue positional neighborhood — enabling deconvolution of cell–cell interaction effects invisible in dissociated-cell datasets. ③ Apply PolyGene's polyfunctionality clustering to detect context-dependent roles of spatial covariates

Extending this paperThe paper's most creative contribution is "polyfunctional genes" — entities whose contextualized embeddings form multiple distinct functional clusters even within a single cell type, detected by clustering embeddings across micro-contexts. This concept is not specific to genomics: any transformer trained with multi-context masking produces per-entity contextualized embeddings that may reveal condition-dependent functional multiplicity. A direct extension of the paper would formalize a statistical criterion for polyfunctionality — e.g., BIC-selected Gaussian mixture plus minimum inter-cluster distance — making it a screening tool applicable beyond genomics. The corpus paper on partially shared multi-modal embeddings for cell-state representation provides a complementary framing of how shared vs. private embedding components can isolate modality-specific vs. shared functional signals.

Added value to my researchIn spatial health analysis, certain covariates exhibit "spatial polyfunctionality": green space or transit density may correlate positively with health outcomes in dense urban cores (amenity access) yet negatively in peripheral areas (proxy for urbanization-related stressors). PolyGene's polyfunctionality workflow gives me a model for discovering such spatial regime shifts empirically from embeddings rather than presupposing them through MGWR bandwidth choices. I could train a spatial transformer on multi-city health data, cluster the contextualized embeddings of spatial covariates across neighborhood contexts, and identify which features are "polyfunctional" — directly extending my MGWR/GWR and explainable-ML agenda with a data-driven regime-detection step.

④ Upgrade PolyGene's static scale-free network analysis with physics-informed graph neural networks

Extending this paperThe paper constructs gene interaction networks from embedding cosine similarities and reports power-law degree distributions in endothelial cells, tracking slope changes between young (<40 yr) and old (>60 yr) donors. However, the network is static (one snapshot per phenotype group), and the power-law analysis only tracks slope shifts at two endpoints without modeling the mechanistic process driving the change. A direct extension: treat the gene interaction network as a dynamical system and learn a physics-informed graph neural network that encodes known regulatory priors (e.g., transcription-factor hub constraints) as soft penalties, trained on PolyGene embeddings across multiple age brackets to predict network restructuring trajectories — moving from descriptive (the network changes) to predictive (the network will change via these mechanisms). The corpus paper on Geneformer scaling provides a pre-trained network biology foundation model that could supply the initial node embeddings for this GNN.

Added value to my researchPhysics-informed graph learning is one of my core methodological directions. The PolyGene gene-network setup is structurally identical to my urban health network problems: nodes are entities (genes / facilities / neighborhoods), edges are learned similarity scores, and the network evolves with an external covariate (age / policy change). The workflow — (1) use a pretrained foundation model to produce rich node embeddings, (2) construct a similarity graph, (3) train a physics-informed GNN with conservation priors, (4) forecast network evolution — directly validates a paradigm I can apply in urban health: neighborhoods as nodes, spatial accessibility similarity as edges, and a physics prior encoding the 2SFCA supply–demand balance to constrain how accessibility networks restructure under facility closures or population growth.
