Unsupervised pretraining in biological neural networks

Lin Zhong · Scott Baptista · Rachel Gattoni · Jon Arnold · Daniel Flickinger · Carsen Stringer · Marius Pachitariu Published 2025-06-18 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09180-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Most of the neural plasticity observed in the mouse visual cortex after task learning is replicated by unsupervised (unrewarded) exposure to the same stimuli, demonstrating that perceptual learning in the brain is primarily driven by unsupervised representation learning. The medial higher visual areas (HVAs) are the main locus of this plasticity, obeying visual rather than spatial rules. Only anterior HVAs carry a unique reward-prediction signal specific to supervised training. Crucially, this unsupervised pretraining accelerates subsequent supervised task learning — a behavioural finding that directly parallels the effectiveness of pretraining in artificial neural networks.

★

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a fundamental debate in neuroscience. Whether perceptual learning in sensory cortex is supervised or unsupervised has been contested for decades; this experiment, recording up to 90,000 neurons simultaneously, provides the clearest evidence that the majority of plasticity arises from unsupervised exposure alone.
  • Identifies a functional dissociation across the visual hierarchy. Medial HVAs perform unsupervised representation learning of visual statistics; anterior HVAs uniquely encode reward predictions for supervised/reinforcement learning — linking cortical anatomy to distinct learning algorithms.
  • Bridges neuroscience and AI. The finding that unrewarded stimulus exposure accelerates subsequent task learning is a direct biological analogue of self-supervised pretraining in large language and vision models, grounding AI methods in brain biology.
  • Validates unsupervised learning theories at scale. Large-scale mesoscope recordings — population sizes far exceeding prior work — provide statistical power to show that plasticity due to task feedback is the exception, not the rule.
  • Opens a path to mechanistic accounts of pretraining. By showing that natural-image statistics — not spatial/reward signals — drive cortical reorganization, the work constrains which synaptic learning rules are responsible, motivating future mechanistic studies.

Core Methods

  • Large-scale two-photon mesoscopy: a custom mesoscope with temporal multiplexing recorded simultaneously from up to 90,000 GCaMP6s-expressing excitatory neurons across V1 and multiple HVAs in head-fixed, freely running mice; z and xy drift corrected online via a custom ScanImage module.
  • Selectivity index d′: signal-detection-theory index comparing mean and standard deviation of responses across two corridors; selective neurons defined as |d′| ≥ 0.3; used to construct 2D spatial histograms of selective neuron density across the cortical sheet.
  • Coding direction analysis: top 5% of corridor-selective neurons define a population coding vector; projections of test stimuli onto this axis yield a similarity index (range: −1 to 1) quantifying visual categorical generalization across areas and cohorts without requiring labels.
  • Sequence correlation analysis: neurons sorted by preferred spatial position in the trained corridor; sequence correlations tested against new corridors to dissociate spatial from visual learning — spatial hypothesis predicts high correlation, visual hypothesis predicts near-zero.
  • Reward-prediction index d′late vs early: compares trial-averaged activity on late-reward vs. early-reward trials (matched for stimulus drive and reward outcome), isolating anticipatory ramping signals; cells with d′late vs early ≥ 0.3 identified as reward-prediction neurons, localized to anterior HVAs in task mice only.
  • Retinotopic atlas alignment: convolutional encoding model (200 kernels) fit to natural-image responses with expectation-maximization; kriging interpolation then aligns each mouse's spatial preference map to a reference atlas, enabling cross-mouse area comparisons.
  • Rastermap visualization: unsupervised neuron reordering by response similarity to discover functional clusters — here used to identify the reward-prediction population without prior anatomical assumptions.
  • Controlled cohort comparison: three matched imaging cohorts (task, unsupervised, unsupervised-gratings) with matched VR experience and running speed; behavioural pretraining experiment adds a no-pretraining control and a passive-reward day-0 baseline.

① Transfer the pretraining paradigm to inform geospatial foundation model design

Extending this paperThe paper's core finding — that unrewarded exposure to naturalistic (not grating) stimuli reorganizes visual cortex and accelerates supervised task learning — provides a neuroscience-grounded theory of what makes pretraining effective: semantic richness and naturalistic statistics, not mere temporal co-occurrence. A direct extension would vary the statistical complexity of pretraining stimuli (gratings → textures → natural scenes → videos) and track how coding-direction similarity index saturates on subsequent tasks, producing a "pretraining efficiency curve" that maps stimulus statistics to representation quality. This would identify the minimum complexity needed for effective pretraining, with direct implications for what pretraining corpora artificial neural networks should use.

Added value to my researchMy work on fine-tuning geospatial foundation models maps directly to this question: just as natural-image statistics — not arbitrary patterns — drive biological pretraining, the semantic richness of the pretraining corpus (remote sensing imagery vs. synthetic tiles vs. street-level photos) likely governs how well a geospatial foundation model fine-tunes on spatial prediction tasks (healthcare accessibility, mobility, land use). This paper provides a neuroscience-grounded hypothesis I can operationalize when designing pretraining data pipelines for GeoAI, specifically favoring multi-modal, semantically varied geospatial inputs over spatially homogeneous ones.

② Design supervision-efficient spatial learning agents using the biological two-module architecture

Extending this paperThe paper dissociates two circuits: a large unsupervised-plasticity module in medial HVAs (feature extraction independent of labels) and a small supervised module in anterior HVAs (reward-prediction signal). This two-module architecture predicts that adding only a minimal reward-prediction head on top of frozen unsupervised representations suffices for task learning — matching linear probing experiments in AI. Extending the paper: systematically vary the ratio of unsupervised pretraining duration to supervised fine-tuning duration (analogous to the 10 days pretraining vs. 5 days task training in Fig. 5), and measure the resulting learning curve compression ratio. This would produce a biologically-informed "pretraining-to-fine-tuning ratio" law.

Added value to my researchIn spatiotemporal agent-based modeling for urban health, agents must learn spatial policies from a combination of unlabeled mobility traces and sparse reward signals (health outcomes). The biological two-module design supports a practical architecture: pretrain agent representations on large unlabeled GPS/POI/trajectory corpora unsupervisedly, then fine-tune only a lightweight policy head on labelled outcomes — reducing the label annotation burden for spatial health models and making agent-based simulations more data-efficient. This generalizes my existing MGWR/ensemble pipeline toward an unsupervised pretraining stage before supervised spatial regression.

③ Apply the coding-direction similarity index as an explainability metric for geospatial embeddings

Extending this paperThe paper's coding-direction analysis and similarity index are purpose-built to ask: "Do representations of category X generalize to a new exemplar of the same category, or collapse into the category of Y?" This is a biologically interpretable measure of representational geometry. Extending the paper: measure whether the orthogonalization of leaf2 (Fig. 3h) depends on the perceptual distance between leaf1 and leaf2 (e.g., LPIPS score), producing a law relating stimulus dissimilarity to the degree of neural orthogonalization — and testing whether this law is universal across species or cortical areas.

Added value to my researchGeospatial embedding models used for spatial prediction (encoding census tracts, grid cells, or POI clusters) often suffer from representation collapse in low-diversity regions, where nearby features map to near-identical embeddings. The coding-direction similarity index is directly transferable as an explainability diagnostic: project embeddings onto the coding axis of contrasting urban contexts (e.g., high-density vs. low-density neighborhoods, urban vs. rural health facilities), compute the similarity index, and use it to quantify representation collapse without requiring ground-truth labels. This operationalizes explainable ML for spatial data in a way grounded in interpretable geometric reasoning.

④ Model the spatial topography of plasticity as a physics-informed graph neural network

Extending this paperThe paper's 2D selectivity maps (Fig. 1i) reveal that unsupervised plasticity is spatially organized: medial HVAs gain the most selectivity, V1 changes modestly, lateral HVAs show novelty responses, and anterior HVAs encode reward predictions exclusively. This spatial structure is described qualitatively; a formal extension would model the cortical sheet as a graph (neurons as nodes, anatomical adjacency as edges) and train a physics-informed graph operator to predict post-learning selectivity maps from pre-learning states and stimulus statistics — yielding a predictive model of cortical reorganization that could be tested on new animals or stimulus sets without additional experiments.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my physics-informed graph learning agenda to neuroscience data: treat the retinotopic atlas as a spatial graph with known connectivity priors (short-range excitation, long-range inhibition) and learn graph convolutions constrained by these anatomical priors. The same architecture is immediately applicable to urban spatial graphs (transit networks, street graphs), where physics priors (flow conservation, spatial autocorrelation decay) constrain the GNN predictions — making the method transferable between neuroscience and urban analytics within a single codebase.

⑤ Translate the visual-not-spatial coding rule into a cross-city transfer principle for GeoAI

Extending this paperThe paper's central methodological insight is distinguishing visual from spatial coding: the same medial HVA neurons respond to leaf1's visual statistics in new spatial positions (leaf2 corridor), demonstrating that the representation is anchored to visual content, not to spatial location. A direct extension: systematically vary the spatial configuration while holding visual content constant (as the paper partly does with swap stimuli in Fig. 5 test 3), and vary visual content while holding spatial configuration constant, to fully map the visual-spatial generalization boundary across all cortical areas. This would reveal which areas are "content encoders" vs. "place encoders" in a clean 2x2 factorial design.

Added value to my researchIn multi-source data fusion for urban health (mobile phone records, social media, remote sensing, POIs), the fundamental question is whether a learned representation generalizes across source modalities (analogous to visual statistics) or only within a fixed spatial context (analogous to spatial codes). The visual-not-spatial principle from this paper suggests that GeoAI models pretrained on semantically rich but spatially variable inputs (e.g., global street-level imagery, multi-city POI distributions) should generalize to new cities better than models trained on location-specific tabular features — a design principle for cross-city transfer directly testable with my MGWR/ensemble pipeline on healthcare accessibility datasets from multiple cities.
