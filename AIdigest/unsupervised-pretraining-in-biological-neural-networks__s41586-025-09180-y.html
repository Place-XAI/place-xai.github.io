Unsupervised pretraining in biological neural networks

Lin Zhong · Scott Baptista · Rachel Gattoni · Jon Arnold · Daniel Flickinger · Carsen Stringer · Marius Pachitariu Published 2025-06-18 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09180-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Most neural plasticity in the mouse visual cortex after task learning is in fact driven by unsupervised learning: unrewarded exposure to the same stimuli replicates nearly all the cortical changes seen in task-trained mice. The plasticity is highest in medial higher visual areas (HVAs) and follows visual rather than spatial coding rules. A distinct reward-prediction signal, exclusive to supervised training, appears in anterior HVAs. Critically, unsupervised pretraining with naturalistic stimuli accelerates subsequent task learning in behavioural experiments, bridging biological and artificial neural network pretraining principles.

★

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a decades-long ambiguity. Perceptual learning has been studied for decades, but whether it stems from supervised or unsupervised mechanisms was unknown. This is the first direct, large-scale experimental test separating the two in the same animals with identical stimuli.
  • Population-scale evidence. Simultaneous recordings of up to 90,000 neurons per session across multiple visual areas provide a level of statistical confidence and areal coverage not previously achieved in this context.
  • Validates the pretraining analogy to AI. Just as unsupervised pretraining accelerates learning in deep networks, it demonstrably accelerates mice's task learning — establishing a biological parallel to a central tenet of modern AI.
  • Dissects regional specialization. Medial HVAs implement unsupervised feature learning; anterior HVAs hold the task-specific reward-prediction signal. This areal decomposition constrains theories of supervised and reinforcement learning in cortex.
  • Behavioural prediction confirmed. The neural findings make a testable prediction — pretraining should speed later task acquisition — that the authors validate in a dedicated cohort, closing the loop between neural mechanism and behaviour.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Animal subjects
19 GCaMP6s-expressing mice (TetO-GCaMP6s × CaMK2a-tTa) for imaging experiments; 23 C57 female mice for behaviour-only experiments (Fig. 5 cohort). 13 male + 6 female imaging mice, aged 2–11 months.
Imaging scale
89 recording sessions total. Simultaneous populations of 20,547 to 89,577 neurons per session recorded with a custom two-photon mesoscope across primary visual cortex (V1) and higher visual areas (AL, LM, MMP, RL, RLL, RSP, PM, AM, MMA and retrosplenial cortex).
Experimental cohorts
Three imaging cohorts: task mice (n = 4–5), unsupervised mice exposed to naturalistic stimuli (n = 6–9), unsupervised mice exposed to gratings (n = 3). Three behaviour-only cohorts (Fig. 5): VRn pretrained (n = 11), VRg pretrained (n = 7), no pretraining (n = 5).
Visual stimuli
Naturalistic texture images (leaf, circle, rock, brick) cropped from large photographs; grating stimuli (0° and 45°) used as controls. Presented in 4 m virtual reality corridors via three LED tablet screens covering 270° field of view.
Imaging software

Analysis stack
Python 3 with NumPy, scikit-learn, matplotlib, jupyter-notebook. Calcium imaging timescale of decay = 0.75 s. Retinotopic mapping via convolutional encoding model + kriging interpolation.

Core Methods

  • Two-photon mesoscopy: custom mesoscope enabling wide-field cellular-resolution imaging across multiple visual cortical areas simultaneously; temporal multiplexing approximately doubles recorded neuron counts.
  • Neural selectivity index (d'): standardised effect-size metric computed from corridor response distributions; neurons with |d'| ≥ 0.3 classified as selective. Enables comparison of selectivity across cohorts, areas, and time points.
  • Coding direction analysis: population-level linear readout axis defined by the difference between mean responses of top-5% leaf- and circle-selective neurons; similarity index quantifies how novel stimuli project onto this axis.
  • Reward-prediction index (d'late vs early): novel discrimination index contrasting neural activity on early- vs. late-cue reward trials, isolating anticipatory/ramping signals from reward and stimulus drive.
  • Rastermap visualisation: unsupervised neural reordering algorithm applied to large raster plots to discover structured population-level signals (e.g., the reward-prediction cluster in anterior HVAs).
  • Retinotopic mapping: convolutional encoding model fit to responses to ~5,000 natural images; kriging interpolation aligns spatial maps across mice to a common reference atlas.
  • Sequence correlation analysis: spatial tuning curves computed via odd/even trial splits; Pearson correlation between preferred positions in different corridors distinguishes visual vs. spatial coding.
  • Behavioural pretraining paradigm: controlled cohort experiment (3 groups, 5-day task, with/without 10-day VR pretraining) measuring anticipatory lick rates as a performance metric.

Expanded Ideas — this paper's extensibility & added value to my research

① Build a compact neural encoding model from the paper's mesoscope recordings

Extending this paper The paper identifies which visual areas change and what they encode, but does not build a predictive model of neural responses to arbitrary stimuli. A natural extension is to fit a compact deep convolutional model to the paper's population recordings — both the task and unsupervised cohorts — asking whether the same model architecture can predict responses in both conditions, and whether the divergence in anterior HVAs (reward-prediction) requires task-specific modules. The paper's retinotopic mapping pipeline (convolutional encoding model, step 1 of Methods) is already a stepping stone toward this goal.

Added value to my research My work on geospatial foundation models involves pretraining on large unlabelled spatial datasets before fine-tuning on specific urban tasks — directly analogous to the unsupervised-then-supervised paradigm proven here. The finding that naturalistic (not grating) pretraining is critical mirrors debates in geospatial AI about whether pretraining on generic imagery (Sentinel-2) versus domain-specific imagery (street-view, POI maps) transfers better to urban health predictions. This paper provides a biological proof-of-concept that the content structure of the pretraining domain matters.

② Use the paper's d' selectivity framework as a transferable feature-quality metric for spatial embeddings

Extending this paper The paper operationalises "representation quality" as the fraction of neurons with |d'| ≥ 0.3 discriminating two corridor identities — a clean, threshold-free measure of population-level feature selectivity. Extending it to artificial networks trained on the same VR stimuli would test whether state-of-the-art self-supervised visual models (DINO, SimCLR) develop similar areal hierarchies when their hidden units are mapped back to the stimulus space, creating a direct biological–artificial alignment benchmark.

Added value to my research In explainable GeoAI, I need to measure how well a spatial embedding layer has learned to separate urban health outcomes across neighbourhoods. The d' framework can be adapted as an area-level feature selectivity index: for each spatial grid cell or community area, compute d' between high- and low-health-outcome zones from the embedding activations. This gives an interpretable, neuroscience-grounded diagnostic of whether a geospatial model has acquired the right spatial feature hierarchy.

③ Formalize the paper's pretraining benefit as a sample-efficiency law for biological and artificial learners

Extending this paper Figure 5 shows that VRn-pretrained mice learn faster, but the paper does not quantify how many supervised trials are "saved" or whether the benefit scales with pretraining duration. Extending it: vary pretraining duration (1, 5, 10, 20 days) and measure the number of supervised trials to criterion — fitting a sample-efficiency curve analogous to neural-scaling laws. Does 10 days of pretraining halve the supervised trials needed? Does the relationship saturate? This would produce a quantitative law connecting unsupervised experience to supervised efficiency in biological systems.

Added value to my research My work on geospatial foundation model fine-tuning faces the same trade-off: how much labelled data (e.g., healthcare visit records, socioeconomic surveys) is needed to adapt a pretrained spatial encoder to a new city? A biological scaling-law analogue would justify a principled data-efficient fine-tuning strategy: pretrain on large unlabelled mobility or remote-sensing data, then fine-tune with only a small labelled set — with the pretraining-to-fine-tuning ratio guided by the biological law.

④ Dissect the reward-prediction signal's role in multi-timescale reinforcement learning

Extending this paper The paper identifies a ramping reward-prediction signal in anterior HVAs that is exclusive to supervised training. However, it characterises this signal primarily at a single timescale (trial-level). Extending it: probe whether the anterior HVA signal carries multi-timescale reward predictions — immediate, episodic, and across-session — as predicted by temporal-difference models at different scales. The paper's d'late vs early index could be generalised to compare activity in early vs. late days of training, not just early vs. late within a trial.

Added value to my research Healthcare and transportation accessibility decisions involve multi-timescale feedback: a new clinic opens (immediate), patient flows shift over weeks, and long-run population health changes unfold over years. A multi-timescale reward-prediction framework borrowed from neuroscience could inform agent-based models of healthcare-seeking behaviour: agents learn from immediate travel-cost feedback, medium-term outcome feedback (treatment success), and long-term population health signals — exactly the architecture uncovered in anterior HVAs.

Cross-ref (corpus): Multi-timescale reinforcement learning in the brain — 10.1038/s41586-025-08929-9

⑤ Transfer the neural orthogonalization finding to spatial representation disentanglement in GeoAI

Extending this paper One of the paper's most striking findings (Fig. 3) is that exposure to a new stimulus (leaf2) causes its neural representation to become orthogonal to the existing leaf1–circle1 coding axis — a form of representational disentanglement that emerges from unsupervised exposure alone. This "orthogonalization by exposure" could be tested in artificial networks: does contrastive self-supervised pretraining on naturalistic stimuli produce similarly orthogonal representations for semantically related but visually distinct categories, compared to pretraining on grating-like synthetic data?

Added value to my research In spatiotemporal analysis of urban systems, I routinely face the challenge of disentangling correlated spatial signals — for instance, distinguishing healthcare accessibility from socioeconomic deprivation in neighborhood embeddings, where both signals co-vary strongly. The paper's unsupervised orthogonalization mechanism motivates a contrastive spatial pretraining strategy: expose a GeoAI model to many city configurations unsupervised, so that it develops orthogonal embeddings for accessibility, deprivation, and mobility — making downstream supervised tasks (e.g., predicting mental health outcomes) more sample-efficient and interpretable via the resulting disentangled representation space.
