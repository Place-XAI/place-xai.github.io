Gendered movement ecology and landscape use in Hadza hunter-gatherers

Brian M. Wood · Jacob A. Harris · David A. Raichlen · Herman Pontzer · Katherine Sayre · Amelia Sancilio · Colette Berbesque · Alyssa N. Crittenden · Audax Mabulla · Richard McElreath · Elizabeth Cashdan · James Holland Jones Published 2021-01-04 DOI 10.1038/s41562-020-01002-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using the largest GPS corpus ever collected in a traditional subsistence society — 2,078 person-days of movement among Hadza hunter-gatherers in Tanzania — this study demonstrates that men walked roughly 60% further per day, explored three to four times more landscape, followed more sinuous routes and were far more likely to travel alone than women. These gendered differences match predictions from foraging ecology (rarer, more mobile animal prey vs. abundant plant foods) and imply that the sexual division of labour co-evolved with divergent spatial behaviour and, likely, spatial cognition.

★

Why It Matters

  • Largest GPS corpus in any traditional society. At 2,078 person-days across 15 camps and 179 participants, this study sets a new empirical standard for quantifying human movement in non-industrial contexts — far exceeding all prior GPS-based forager studies.
  • Bridges evolutionary theory and precision spatial data. By grounding predictions in foraging ecology (Harestad and Bunnel's day-range model), the study transforms sex differences in space use from description into causal inference.
  • Gender segregation on the landscape is stark and early-emerging. Only 13.6% of total visited land was shared by both sexes; the gender gap in daily travel appears by age 6, with implications for developmental environments and spatial cognition.
  • Disease ecology, epidemiology and public health implications. Men's more expansive and solitary travel increases their exposure to novel pathogens and zoonoses — a finding with direct relevance to causal models of sex-differentiated immunity and disease burden.
  • Comparative baseline for non-human primates. Cross-species comparison shows that Hadza men's gender difference in daily range (~62%) greatly exceeds that of chimpanzees (~20%), grounding hominin cognitive evolution in observed behaviour.

Core Methods

  • GPS data collection & processing: devices set to 5-second sampling interval; data stored in MySQL; tracks filtered by speed thresholds and visual inspection; all data interpolated to uniform 5-second intervals. Pedestrian-only tracks (vehicle/bicycle days excluded).
  • Hierarchical Bayesian generalized additive mixed models (GAMMs): fit with brms (models 1–6) and mgcv::bam (model 7 — sociality sample too large for brms). Smooth functions estimated via penalized thin-plate regression; individual and camp treated as varying intercepts.
  • Raster-based land exploration: each GPS track overlaid on a 10 m × 10 m raster; unique cells visited tallied per day and cumulatively. Male vs. female cumulative exploration modelled with smooth functions over days of observation (model 4).
  • Minimum convex polygons (MCPs): 95% MCPs computed per camp and gender using adehabitatHR to characterize home-range area; male-to-female MCP ratio averaged 4:1 across 12 camps.
  • Sinuosity measurement: for each track, outbound and inbound bouts (≥500 m from camp) split at furthest point; sinuosity = actual path length / straight-line distance. Geographically weighted sinuosity computed per 10 m × 10 m cell to assess spatial distribution of turning behaviour.
  • Inter-individual proximity analysis: for each of 193,244 sociality sample points, straight-line distances computed to all campmates with GPS fixes within ±60 seconds. Binary proximity (within 5 m) modelled as a function of distance from camp, age and device count (model 7).
  • Effect sizes and model comparison: Cohen's d computed from 1,000 posterior-predictive draws at each age; WAIC used for model selection (e.g., child-dependency model vs. null).

① Decode the ecology of daily range using a learned foraging model rather than a fixed analytic formula

Extending this paperThe paper's core prediction about daily range derives from Harestad and Bunnel's metabolic-needs/resource-density ratio — a hand-coded formula applied uniformly. A direct extension: replace this static formula with a data-driven foraging model that learns how resource density, mobility and landscape heterogeneity jointly predict observed GPS range across the 15 camps. The corpus paper on physiography and foraging mobility in Sahul pioneers exactly this approach — combining time-evolving landscape surfaces with Lévy walk foraging dynamics to model human mobility from first principles. Extending the Hadza study in this direction would allow the model to generalize across seasons, camps and ecological gradients without assuming the ratio holds everywhere.

Added value to my researchThis forager-ecology model is structurally analogous to my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation of urban demand: both ask how the density of a target resource (food patches vs. patients/users) shapes the spatial distribution of an agent's movement. A learned foraging model for the Hadza could seed a demand-surface-conditioned mobility prior for agent-based accessibility models — for instance, calibrating how far patients realistically travel as a function of local healthcare density in 2SFCA catchment delineation.

Cross-ref (corpus): Physiography, foraging mobility, and the first peopling of Sahul — 10.1038/s41467-024-47662-1

② Extract universal scaling laws of individual exploration from the GPS corpus

Extending this paperThe paper reports that cumulative land exploration grows as a smooth function of observation days (model 4), but does not fit a scaling exponent or compare the form of this growth law to that of non-foraging human populations. Extending it: fit power-law and logarithmic exploration curves to the Hadza data and test whether the exploration exponents match those of urban smartphone users or non-human primates. The corpus paper "Evidence for a conserved quantity in human mobility" proposes that a conserved mobility quantity governs individual exploration across cultural contexts — testing whether Hadza men and women fit or deviate from this conserved quantity would directly extend the universality debate.

Added value to my researchIf Hadza foragers follow the same exploration law as urban commuters, this validates the use of urban mobility flow data (mobile phone CDRs, social media traces) as a proxy for baseline human movement — directly strengthening my multi-source data fusion methodology when GPS ground truth is unavailable in health-geography studies. It also suggests that 2SFCA catchment sizes derived from smartphone mobility data are ecologically grounded rather than arbitrary.

Cross-ref (corpus): Evidence for a conserved quantity in human mobility — 10.1038/s41562-018-0364-x

③ Map the gender landscape-segregation finding onto urban spatial segregation analytics

Extending this paperOne of the paper's most striking results is that only 13.6% of visited landscape is shared between sexes — yet this gender segregation metric is computed with ad-hoc raster union operations. A methodological extension: apply the random-walk-based segregation index developed in "Quantifying ethnic segregation in cities through random walks" to the Hadza GPS corpus. This would give scale-free, comparable segregation scores and allow the paper to test whether gender segregation varies predictably with camp size, ecosystem productivity or season. It would also allow direct comparison with socioeconomic segregation in urban settings.

Added value to my researchSpatial segregation measurement is directly relevant to my health geography work: the same random-walk segregation index could quantify how healthcare access is segregated by neighbourhood income or race in urban health atlases. The Hadza case provides a behavioural-ecology grounding for why segregation emerges from differential resource ecology — a causal explanation that could sharpen spatial equity narratives in urban health accessibility papers.

Cross-ref (corpus): Quantifying ethnic segregation in cities through random walks — 10.1038/s41467-022-33344-3

④ Contextualise Hadza mobility within the global mobility distribution using smartphone baselines

Extending this paperThe paper benchmarks Hadza step counts against 717,527 smartphone users from Althoff et al. (2017) but uses only the aggregate means. A richer extension: use the corpus paper "Mapping global variation in human mobility" — which maps mobility from 300 million+ smartphone users covering 65% of Earth's populated surface — to situate Hadza GPS ranges, daily distances and sinuosity within the global distribution of human movement. This would allow the paper to test whether Hadza movement sits at an ecological extreme or falls within the natural variation of rural populations worldwide, and would make the cross-cultural comparison statistically grounded rather than anecdotal.

Added value to my researchGlobal smartphone mobility data are a key layer in my multi-source data fusion toolkit. Understanding where forager mobility sits relative to the global distribution informs how GPS ground-truth data from diverse societies can calibrate mobility models trained on urban smartphone data — directly relevant to transferring geospatial foundation models from data-rich cities to data-scarce rural health contexts.

Cross-ref (corpus): Mapping global variation in human mobility — 10.1038/s41562-020-0875-0

⑤ Build an explainable spatial model of who travels alone vs. in groups as a function of landscape and ecology

Extending this paperThe paper shows that men were far more likely to travel alone (median distance to nearest neighbour = 63.3 m for men vs. 3.2 m for women) and that women increase group proximity as a safety response when far from camp. These sociality patterns are currently modelled using a single Bayesian GAMM with gender and distance from camp as predictors, ignoring landscape-level features (vegetation, terrain, Datoga presence zones). A direct extension: incorporate spatial covariates — e.g., proximity to Datoga pastoralist territories, vegetation cover, water sources — into an explainable machine learning model of group size and proximity. SHAP values would reveal which spatial features most explain women's increased sociality at distance.

Added value to my researchThis explainability framework maps directly onto my GeoAI agenda: using SHAP-driven explainable ML to decompose which spatial features drive health-seeking behaviour (e.g., which neighbourhood attributes predict solo vs. group trips to a clinic). The Hadza case demonstrates that even in non-urban contexts, spatial proximity between individuals is predictable from landscape features — validating the use of explainable spatial models to disentangle safety, resource-ecology and social-norm drivers of human movement across diverse settings.

Cross-ref (corpus): The scales of human mobility — 10.1038/s41586-020-2909-1
