Regulatory grammar in human promoters uncovered by MPRA-based deep learning

Barbadilla-Martínez, Lucía · Klaassen, Noud · Franceschini-Santos, Vinícius H. · Breda, Jérémie · Yücel, Hatice · Hernández-Quiles, Miguel · van Lieshout, Tijs · Urzua Traslaviña, Carlos G. · Boi, Minh Chau Luong · Akbarzadeh, Maryam · Hermana-Garcia-Agullo, Celia · Gregoricchio, Sebastian · de Haas, Marcel · Straver, Roy · Derks, Sarah · Zwart, Wilbert · Voest, Emile · Franke, Lude · Vermeulen, Michiel · de Ridder, Jeroen · van Steensel, Bas Published 2026-02-04 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-10093-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

PARM (Promoter Activity Regulatory Model) is a cell-type-specific convolutional deep-learning model trained on massively parallel reporter assay (MPRA) data that accurately predicts autonomous promoter activity from DNA sequence alone across 30,607 human promoters in 10 cell types. By applying in silico saturation mutagenesis genome-wide, PARM reveals a complex positional grammar of transcription factor binding — including distinct positional preferences for activating versus repressive factors and context-dependent combinatorial motif–motif interactions — while remaining experimentally and computationally lightweight enough to model cell-type- and stimulus-specific promoter regulation without large-scale epigenome datasets.

★

Why It Matters

  • Decodes regulatory grammar at scale. PARM is the first approach to systematically map functional transcription factor binding sites — not just sequence motifs — across all 30,607 human promoters genome-wide and in 10 distinct cell types, revealing a rich positional grammar that simple sequence scanning misses (~20-fold fewer functional sites than naive motif scanning).
  • Highly economical and transferable. PARM's promoter-focused MPRA library (~4 million fragments, ~5 million cells per replicate) is roughly 240-fold cheaper than genome-wide MPRA; each model trains in ~1 day on a single GPU — making cell-type- and stimulus-specific deep-learning models genuinely accessible for any lab.
  • Uncovers new biology. The study confirms known TF biology (TBP at −30 bp, GATA in blood cells) while discovering unexpected findings: TBP is not universally functional across cell types; CTCF has orientation-specific functional bias; repressive regulatory sites are enriched downstream of the TSS while activating ones peak near −50 bp; and NRF1/NFYA/SP1 can repress transcription when bound downstream of the TSS.
  • Designs fully synthetic promoters. PARM's genetic algorithm generated synthetic 232 bp promoter sequences with activities comparable to the strongest natural human promoters and no significant similarity to any human genomic sequence — demonstrating it has learned biologically meaningful grammar rather than memorizing sequences.
  • Immediate clinical relevance. PARM correctly predicted cancer-associated TERT promoter mutations (C250T, C228T), matched eQTL effect directions from GTEx blood data, and has already been applied to identify rare functional promoter mutations in cancer patients.

Core Methods

  • Promoter-focused MPRA: human gDNA randomly fragmented to ~300 bp, enriched for promoter-overlapping fragments by hybridization capture (127,575 custom probes), cloned into barcoded promoter-less reporter vectors, transfected into cells, and quantified by sequencing RNA/DNA barcode ratios. Produces 4 million unique fragments covering 30,607 TSSs at 151-fold average coverage.
  • PARM — lightweight 1D CNN with self-attention pooling: architecture adapted from the first layers of Enformer; 742,337 parameters (vs. >30M in Borzoi). Input: one-hot-encoded 600 bp DNA sequence. Separate model per cell type, trained with AdamW optimizer using cosine learning rate schedule. Cross-validated across 6 folds (no sequence overlap ± 2,600 bp). Training time: ~1 day on one NVIDIA Quadro RTX6000 GPU.
  • In silico saturation mutagenesis (ISM): for all 30,607 promoters, each nucleotide is replaced by the three alternatives and PARM predicts the activity change. Importance score = reference activity − mean mutant activity. Applied genome-wide to build regulatory site (RS) maps accessible via interactive browser.
  • Regulatory site identification: ISM profiles scanned against HOCOMOCO v11 TF motifs using Pearson's correlation (r ≥ 0.75) plus an information-content-weighted IS score cutoff (FDR < 5%), identifying functional binding sites — not merely sequence matches. FIMO used for comparison as naive sequence scanner.
  • Synthetic promoter design via genetic algorithm: 200 random 232 bp sequences evolved for 300 generations by random mutagenesis (prob. 0.8) and two-point crossover (prob. 0.5) with tournament selection, using PARM as fitness function. Implemented in DEAP-ER v2.0.0. Ten independent instances run; resulting sequences experimentally validated by MPRA.
  • Unknown motif discovery: RSs without HOCOMOCO/JASPAR2024 matches clustered by TOMTOM pairwise similarity (Pearson correlation, min overlap 6 bp); novel TFs identified by DNA affinity purification from K562 nuclear extracts followed by quantitative mass-spectrometry on Orbitrap Exploris 480 (dimethyl labelling).
  • Motif insertion experiments: systematic in silico insertion of consensus and two negative-control mutated motifs for NRF1, NFYA, SP1, and YY1 at 15 positions (−160 to +63 bp relative to TSS, step 14 bp) in all 30,607 promoters; subset of 19 promoters per TF validated by synthetic MPRA library in 4 cell types.

① Transfer PARM's "lightweight targeted-data" philosophy to spatial health prediction

Extending this paperPARM's key architectural insight is that a compact CNN trained on carefully targeted functional data (4M focused MPRA fragments instead of thousands of epigenome maps) achieves competitive precision while remaining deployable per cell type at low cost. A direct extension of the paper: apply PARM models to disease-relevant cell types (vascular endothelial, neuronal, hepatic) using patient-derived MPRA libraries to systematically identify functional regulatory variants from GWAS loci — the paper already demonstrates this with 1,836 eQTLs from GTEx blood. Scaling to tissue-specific GWAS for cardiovascular or metabolic disease would substantially broaden clinical impact and test whether PARM's lightweight design advantages hold across diverse disease contexts.

Added value to my researchThe targeted-data-over-scale philosophy translates to my explainable ML for spatial health prediction: rather than aggregating all available spatial covariates into massive ensemble models, training focused models on spatially stratified sub-populations (e.g., within specific healthcare catchment areas) could achieve similar gains in precision. PARM's ISM analog — systematically perturbing each nucleotide to find functional variants — is the exact structure of spatial sensitivity analysis: systematically varying each spatial covariate (clinic location, transit route, housing density) to isolate causal effects on accessibility. This aligns my SHAP-based workflows with a more rigorous perturbation-based causal framework.

② Build a multi-cell-type shared backbone via cross-cell-type transfer learning

Extending this paperPARM currently trains a separate independent model per cell type, requiring a fresh MPRA experiment for each. Yet the paper's own data show promoter activities are highly correlated across cell types (R = 0.78–0.95), suggesting a shared low-dimensional regulatory space exists. A natural extension: use the 10 existing PARM cell-type models to learn a shared representational backbone (via multi-task or meta-learning) that can be rapidly fine-tuned to new cell types from fewer MPRA fragments — analogous to fine-tuning foundation models. This would also reveal which sequence features encode invariant regulatory grammar versus cell-type-specific deviations, converting PARM's per-cell-type analyses into a single transferable model of human promoter regulation.

Added value to my researchThis directly parallels my work on fine-tuning geospatial foundation models: a shared spatial embedding fine-tuned to local city contexts is structurally identical to a shared promoter embedding fine-tuned per cell type. Methodological insights from PARM — which architectural bottlenecks enable efficient fine-tuning without forgetting shared representations — transfer directly to my geospatial foundation model experiments, where the cost of labeled spatial data is equally a limiting factor. PARM's 10-cell-type multi-task setup could serve as a benchmark for comparing multi-task vs. sequential fine-tuning strategies.

③ Encode the positional grammar as geographically weighted kernels for interpretable modeling

Extending this paperPARM's ISM reveals that TF activity depends on precise position relative to the TSS — activating RSs peak at −50 bp, repressive ones are enriched downstream. This positional grammar is currently described empirically from ISM outputs but not encoded as a structured architectural prior. An extension: reformulate PARM's CNN with position-conditioned kernels — convolution filters whose weights vary explicitly with distance from the TSS — making the learned positional preferences explicit, inspectable parameters rather than latent features. This would enable principled synthetic promoter design with controlled positional logic, and provide a mechanistically interpretable model of why the −50 bp position is privileged for activation.

Added value to my researchThis is the structural analog of my MGWR/GWR methodology: just as MGWR fits spatially varying bandwidths to capture scale-dependent spatial processes, a position-conditioned PARM fits regulatory-distance-varying kernels capturing position-dependent promoter grammar. The mathematical framework of geographically weighted regression — fitting local models within adaptive bandwidth windows — could be imported into sequence modeling directly. This bridge between spatial statistics and genomics could generalize: any problem with a privileged reference coordinate (TSS, spatial centroid, disease hotspot) admits position-conditioned or location-conditioned kernel approaches.

④ Model stimulus-driven regulatory rewiring as a dynamic TF–promoter graph network

Extending this paperPARM detects rewiring of TF regulatory interactions after stimuli by comparing RS maps between control and perturbed conditions — but treats each promoter independently. An extension with substantially higher explanatory power: construct a TF–promoter bipartite graph per condition where edge weights are PARM-predicted RS strengths, then apply graph community detection to identify co-regulated gene modules and the TF hub nodes driving stimulus-specific rewiring. This would reveal not just which individual TFs change but how the entire regulatory network topology reorganizes — identifying bottleneck TFs whose gain or loss connects large gene modules. The three stimulus conditions (heat shock, nutlin-3a, PMA) provide three natural comparison graphs.

Community detection on this graph identifies regulatory "neighborhoods" — gene clusters co-governed by the same TF hub — structurally identical to detecting functional urban regions from commuting flows. ⑤ Apply PARM's in silico perturbation logic as a spatial counterfactual framework for health geography

Extending this paperPARM's most powerful analytical mode is in silico insertion/mutagenesis: systematically replacing each nucleotide or inserting each TF motif to predict causal activity effects, then validating a targeted subset experimentally. This perturbation-predict-validate loop is what elevates PARM's inferences above correlative epigenome-based models. A direct extension: apply PARM per-patient to model personalized transcriptional responses — the paper already demonstrates this with TERT cancer mutations and a patient-derived colorectal organoid, and notes it has been applied to identify rare functional promoter mutations in cancer. Expanding to panels of cancer-associated non-coding variants across patient cohorts would create a population-scale regulatory variant interpreter.

Added value to my researchPARM's perturbation-predict-validate loop is a direct template for spatial counterfactual analysis in health geography: instead of nucleotide substitutions, systematically vary spatial covariates (remove a clinic, relocate a transit stop, change population density) and predict the causal effect on healthcare accessibility using an explainable ML model. The ISM importance score formula — importance = reference prediction minus mean across perturbations — is directly adaptable as a spatial sensitivity index for 2SFCA catchment design, attributing causal influence to each spatial feature rather than correlational importance. This creates a bridge between my R2SFCA methodology and PARM's rigorous model-based causal interpretation, grounding accessibility policy recommendations in counterfactual reasoning rather than observational correlation.
