Long-term public health impact of doxycycline post-exposure prophylaxis on syphilis transmission

Wang, Zihao · Nikitin, Dariya · Dickens, Borame L. · Wee, Liang En · Chio, Martin T. W. · Tan, Rayner Kay Jin · Ejima, Keisuke · Wang, Yi · Fisman, David N. · Whittles, Lilith K. · Lim, Jue Tao Published 2026-03-20 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00092-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Over a 15-year horizon, doxy-PEP programmes targeting high-risk MSM (more than 5 partners per year) at the point of syphilis diagnosis are by far the most efficient enrolment strategy, averting 2.50 cases per enrolment in Singapore and 4.60 in England. Broader enrolment of all MSM attending sexual health clinics averts more cases in total but with very low efficiency (as few as 0.02–0.04 cases per enrolment). Frequent STI screening (every 3–4 months) substantially amplifies benefit, and the potential emergence of tetracycline-resistant Neisseria gonorrhoeae must be actively monitored as a key risk of widespread doxy-PEP uptake.

Why It Matters

  • First long-run, multi-setting quantification of doxy-PEP impact. While clinical trials show short-term efficacy, this is among the first modelling studies to project 15-year population-level outcomes across two contrasting epidemiological settings (Singapore and England), bridging trial evidence and policy.
  • Efficiency vs. reach trade-off made explicit. The study quantifies — not just describes — the efficiency penalty of broad enrolment, providing decision-makers with concrete cases-averted-per-enrolment figures to weigh equity against resource efficiency.
  • Behavioural realism encoded in the model. Screening frequency, uptake, adherence, and discontinuation are all embedded as calibrated parameters rather than fixed assumptions, making the findings robust across real-world variation.
  • Antimicrobial resistance risk flagged proactively. By coupling the benefit model with the risk of tetracycline-resistant gonorrhoea, the study directly informs programme monitoring requirements — a needed translational bridge for guideline bodies.
  • Open code and data. Full model code and calibration data are publicly available via GitHub, enabling reproducibility and adaptation to new settings.

Core Methods

  • Behavioural transmission-dynamic compartmental model: a multi-stage SEIR-type model tracking syphilis progression through incubation, primary, secondary, early latent, late latent, and tertiary compartments, stratified by sexual activity level (low vs. high) and doxy-PEP enrolment status (N, X, D, M strata).
  • Bayesian calibration: model parameters (transmission rates, natural history, behavioural rates) are calibrated to historical incidence data from Singapore and England using Bayesian inference, producing 95% credible intervals for all projected outcomes.
  • Behavioural realism: four programme-relevant behavioural parameters are explicitly modelled — doxy-PEP uptake probability, screening frequency, adherence level, and discontinuation rate — enabling sensitivity analysis across realistic behavioural scenarios.
  • Enrolment strategy comparison: five alternative programme strategies are compared over 15 years, including diagnosis-at-risk targeting (high-risk MSM), clinic-wide enrolment (all MSM at sexual health clinics), and age-group-based strategies, each evaluated on both total cases averted and cases averted per enrolment.
  • Counterfactual scenario analysis: results are stress-tested against varying background epidemiological conditions and future behavioural changes to assess robustness.
  • Resistance risk framing: programme efficacy results are contextualised against the risk of tetracycline-resistant Neisseria gonorrhoeae emergence, using published resistance surveillance data.

① Spatialize the transmission model: from national to sub-city sexual health clinic catchments

Extending this paperThe current model operates at the national level (Singapore as a whole, England as a whole), treating geography as homogeneous. Yet doxy-PEP programme reach is inherently spatial: which sexual health clinics MSM attend is determined by proximity and transport access, and high-risk MSM are not uniformly distributed across urban space. A direct extension would embed the transmission-dynamic model within a spatial accessibility framework — delineating catchment areas of sexual health clinics in Singapore and across English cities, assigning MSM sub-populations by area, and simulating how geographic variation in clinic access shapes programme enrolment and eventual syphilis burden. This would reveal whether the efficiency advantage of high-risk targeting holds across all clinic catchments or is diluted in geographically underserved areas.

② Replace point estimates with ML-predicted behavioural parameters from mobile and social media data

Extending this paperThe paper's Bayesian calibration is constrained by the availability of published survey and surveillance data on MSM behavioural parameters (screening frequency, uptake, adherence). These are sparse, lagged, and subject to reporting bias. Extending the paper: use multi-source digital trace data — anonymised mobile-app activity, social media sentiment around sexual health campaigns, pharmacy dispensing records — to generate real-time, spatiotemporally resolved behavioural estimates as dynamic model inputs. This would replace the paper's static behavioural distributions with time-varying forcing functions, allowing the model to capture how adherence drifts over programme lifetime or spikes after public health campaigns.

③ Extend the programme-efficiency metric to a multi-city transferability benchmark

Extending this paperThe paper calibrates two settings (Singapore and England) and shows that optimal strategy is robust across these two contrasting epidemiological contexts. However, it cannot yet answer: "given a new city or country's baseline syphilis incidence, MSM population size, and clinic density, which strategy should be adopted without re-running the full Bayesian calibration?" Extending this: treat each calibrated setting as a training point in a meta-learning or transfer learning framework, where a fast-adaptation model predicts the optimal enrolment strategy and expected cases-averted-per-enrolment from a small set of observable epidemiological indicators, enabling rapid deployment guidance for new settings.

④ Couple the doxy-PEP model with a tetracycline-resistance evolution sub-model using agent-based simulation

Extending this paperThe paper flags tetracycline-resistant Neisseria gonorrhoeae as the primary risk requiring monitoring but does not formally model resistance dynamics — this is a stated limitation. ⑤ Embed screening frequency as an accessibility-constrained variable informed by healthcare facility access

Extending this paperThe paper shows that maintaining STI screening every 3–4 months substantially amplifies the impact of doxy-PEP, but treats screening frequency as a fixed behavioural parameter. In reality, screening frequency is shaped by spatial access to sexual health clinics: individuals living farther from clinics screen less often, independent of motivation. Extending the paper: parameterize screening frequency as a function of travel time to the nearest sexual health clinic (from a spatial access model), and examine how the cases-averted efficiency surface shifts when geographic access inequality is accounted for — potentially revealing that targeting high-risk MSM in geographically underserved areas requires co-intervention on access.
