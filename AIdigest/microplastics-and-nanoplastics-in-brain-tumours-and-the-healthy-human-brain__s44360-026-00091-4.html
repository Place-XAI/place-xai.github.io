Microplastics and nanoplastics in brain tumours and the healthy human brain

Runting Li · Fa Lin · Xiaoli Zhao · Junyu Wang · Miaomiao Teng · Chengjun Li · Li Ma · Huan Zhong · Yu Chen · Wenli Tang · Ke Wang · Jiaqi Sun · Zhenshan Song · Jionghao Xue · Minghao Liu · Yitong Jia · Genhuan Yang · Qingyuan Liu · Zenghui Qian · Wenying Qiu · Naili Wang · Zhen Chen · Wei Zhang · Shuai Kang · Hao Wang · Bobo Cao · Guozhi Cao · Shuo Wang · Chao Ma · Fengchang Wu · Dabiao Zhou · Yuanli Zhao · Jizong Zhao · Xiaolin Chen Published 2026-04-20 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00091-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Microplastics and nanoplastics (MNPs) are ubiquitous in the human brain: detected in 99.4% of 156 diseased samples from 113 brain-tumour patients and 100% of 35 healthy brain samples from five post-mortem donors. MNP concentrations are significantly higher in peri-tumoural tissue than in healthy brain, suggesting the compromised blood–brain barrier in cancer facilitates MNP entry. A positive correlation between microplastic surface area and tumour proliferation (Ki-67 index) is reported, though causality remains to be established.

★

Why It Matters

  • First evidence of MNPs in the living human brain. Prior studies confirmed MNPs in peripheral organs (arteries, placenta, colon); this paper for the first time demonstrates their presence in intraoperatively collected human brain tissue, closing a major evidence gap.
  • Elevated MNP burden at the tumour interface. The peri-tumoural microenvironment accumulates significantly more MNPs than healthy brain, implicating the disrupted blood–brain barrier as an entry route — a mechanistic hypothesis with direct therapeutic relevance.
  • Correlation with tumour proliferation. The positive association between total microplastic surface area and Ki-67 index suggests a potential biological link between MNP exposure and tumour aggressiveness, warranting prospective causal investigation.
  • Rigorous contamination controls. The study reproduces the actual operating-room environment to quantify intraoperative contamination from surgical materials (gelatin sponges, infusion sets, specimen bottles), validating that tissue signals are not artefactual.
  • Population-level exposure questionnaire. Data from 108 patient questionnaires on lifestyle, diet and occupational exposure allow preliminary identification of external factors associated with brain MNP burden, opening an epidemiological line of enquiry.

Core Methods

  • LDIR spectroscopy: Laser Direct Infrared imaging identifies, counts, and sizes individual microplastic particles in tissue sections, providing particle-level abundance (particles/g) and diameter distributions. Spectral matching scores of 0.80–0.85 and >0.85 cross-validated against O-PTIR.
  • Py-GC/MS and nanoscale Py-GC/MS: Pyrolysis coupled with gas chromatography / mass spectrometry quantifies MNP mass concentrations (μg/g) and enables polymer-type identification; nanoscale variant extends sensitivity to nanoplastics not visible by LDIR.
  • Intraoperative contamination control: Systematic blank testing of all surgical consumables (specimen bottles, syringes, saline, gelatin sponges, surgical patties, air exposure) in three surgically reproduced settings; no contamination detected in air controls.
  • Tissue compartment comparison: Spatial stratification of samples into intra-axial vs extra-axial, and peri-tumoural vs healthy, with two-sided Mann–Whitney U tests for concentration and diameter differences.
  • Survival analysis: Kaplan–Meier curves stratified by median MP abundance, surface area, and Py-GC/MS content; log-rank tests; 1-year overall survival follow-up (n = 113 all patients; n = 56 glioma).
  • Restricted cubic spline (RCS) regression: Dose–response modeling of MP surface area / abundance against tumour molecular markers (IDH status, 1p/19q codeletion, MGMT methylation, WHO grade, Ki-67 index) in glioma and meningioma separately.
  • Questionnaire-based exposure analysis: Spearman correlation and linear regression linking 108 lifestyle/dietary/occupational variables to MP abundance to identify significant external exposure factors.

① Spatial exposomics: map external MNP exposure geographically and link to brain burden

Extending this paperThe paper's 108-questionnaire analysis identifies individual-level exposure correlates but does not geolocate patients or compute spatial exposure indices. A direct extension: geocode patient residential histories and link them to spatially explicit MNP exposure surfaces — air microplastic deposition maps, drinking-water contamination grids, and dietary exposure rasters — to build a spatial exposome for brain MNP accumulation. The pesticide-cancer spatial Bayesian framework in the corpus (Nature Health, 10.1038/s44360-026-00087-0) provides an immediate methodological template for estimating spatially resolved mixture exposures at country scale and attributing cancer risk.

② Explainable ML to decode the questionnaire exposure-response curve

Extending this paperThe paper uses Spearman correlation and linear regression on 108 questionnaire responses — a low-dimensional, assumption-heavy approach. An extension: train a gradient-boosted ensemble on the full questionnaire feature set with SHAP-based attribution to identify non-linear dose-response thresholds and interaction effects (e.g., duration of exposure × diet type) that linear regression misses. This converts the paper's descriptive questionnaire analysis into an actionable risk-factor ranking with confidence intervals and individual-level predictions.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my explainable ML for spatial prediction agenda. The questionnaire dataset is analogous to multi-source survey data I work with (social media, mobile phone, POI). Applying SHAP-driven ensemble models here would generate a transferable pipeline for environmental health factor attribution — and the geographic component of residence and exposure source naturally maps onto my MGWR/GWR spatially varying coefficient framework.

④ Physics-informed graph learning to model MNP transport across the blood–brain barrier

Extending this paperThe paper hypothesizes that MNPs exploit the disrupted blood–brain barrier (BBB) in tumours, but provides no dynamic model of particle transport. An extension: represent the cerebral vasculature as a graph network and encode known biophysical transport equations (Stokes-Einstein diffusion, pore-size exclusion, enhanced permeability in glioblastoma) as physics constraints. A physics-informed graph neural network (PI-GNN) trained on the paper's per-region MNP concentration data would learn residual transport pathways not captured by first principles — producing a mechanistic accumulation model. This is the type of hybrid physical–data-driven approach now being applied to complex nanoparticle heterostructures in materials science (corpus, Nature Computational Science, 10.1038/s43588-025-00917-3).
