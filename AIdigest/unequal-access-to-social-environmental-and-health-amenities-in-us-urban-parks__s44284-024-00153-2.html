Unequal access to social, environmental and health amenities in US urban parks

Richelle L. Winkler · Jeffrey A. G. Clark · Dexter H. Locke · Peleg Kremer · Myla F. J. Aronson · Fushcia-Ann Hoover · Hogyeum Evan Joo · Daniele La Rosa · KangJae Jerry Lee · Susannah B. Lerman · Hamil Pearsall · Timothy L. V. Vargo · Charles H. Nilon · Christopher A. Lepczyk Published 2024-11-08 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00153-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 122,988 urban parks in the contiguous USA, both the spatial distribution of parks and the amenities they offer track neighborhood demographics. Neighborhoods within a 10-min walk of a park are systematically whiter than the rest of the same city, and parks in predominantly white neighborhoods are cooler in summer with more tree cover than parks serving more Hispanic and Black residents. These amenity inequities persist across all US census divisions, showing that unequal access to high-quality parks is a nationwide pattern rather than a local quirk.

Why It Matters

  • Moves park equity from "how many acres" to "what quality." Past work mostly counted park presence or proximity; this study shows that even where access exists, the environmental and health quality of the park differs by race/ethnicity — a second, hidden layer of inequity.
  • National scale with consistent measurement. A single reproducible pipeline applied to ~123k parks lets the same metric be compared across the whole country, separating a structural pattern from city-specific anecdotes.
  • Connects greenspace, heat, and public health. By linking park demographics to summer land surface temperature and tree canopy, it ties distributional justice directly to climate exposure and the documented health benefits of urban nature.
  • Actionable for investment targeting. Identifying which parks underserve which populations gives planners a concrete map for where amenity upgrades (shade, tree planting, cooling) would most reduce disparity.

Core Methods

  • Park identification: extract all urban parks from ParkServe and retain those whose 10-min walkshed intersects an urban area (Figs. 1–2).
  • Network walksheds: delineate pedestrian 10-min service areas per park and tally the race/ethnicity population inside each from block-group demographics.
  • Representativeness metric: compare each group's share within a park's walkshed against its share in the whole urban area, yielding an over-/under-representation index (Figs. 3–5).
  • Amenity characterization: attach summer land surface temperature (Landsat) and tree canopy cover (NLCD) to each park; classify parks as above- vs. below-average amenity.
  • Regional stratification: repeat all comparisons across the nine census divisions to test whether disparities are national or regionally contingent.

① From supply-side walksheds to competition-aware park accessibility

Extending this paperThe paper measures access as a binary 10-min walkshed and counts who lives inside it, but it ignores competition for the same park and crowding when several neighborhoods share one green space. Recasting the analysis with a 2SFCA/R2SFCA formulation — where each park's amenity "supply" (acreage, tree canopy, coolness) is divided among all populations within reach — would turn the paper's presence metric into a quality-weighted, demand-adjusted accessibility score, sharpening the equity verdict where high-quality parks are scarce and oversubscribed.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct testbed for my R2SFCA service-area delineation: parks become "facilities," amenity layers become a multi-attribute supply vector, and racial/ethnic block groups become competing demand — a clean, national-scale benchmark for quality-weighted green-space 2SFCA.

② Explain the amenity gap, don't just measure it

Extending this paperThe paper documents that white-neighborhood parks are cooler and greener but stops at description. Extending it with an explainable ML model (gradient boosting + SHAP, or MGWR) predicting park summer LST and tree canopy from neighborhood demographics, density, historical zoning and park age would identify which drivers — and where — produce the disparity, separating contemporary income effects from legacy structural ones.

Added value to my researchSquarely my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction line: a national park dataset with environmental targets and rich covariates is an ideal case for spatially varying SHAP and MGWR coefficient surfaces that map local mechanisms of environmental injustice.

Cross-ref (corpus): Contemporary income inequality outweighs historic redlining in shaping intra-urban heat disparities in Los Angeles — 10.1038/s41467-025-59912-x

③ Couple park coolness to the city-wide heat-equity field

Extending this paperThe paper treats each park's summer LST in isolation. A natural extension embeds parks within the urban heat island field of their host city, quantifying the cooling service each park delivers to its surrounding walkshed and how that cooling is distributed across racial/ethnic groups — converting a static amenity attribute into a measured per-capita cooling benefit.

Added value to my researchConnects park accessibility to my healthy-cities / climate-exposure work: spatiotemporal kernel density of heat exposure can be offset by modeled park-cooling catchments, giving an equity-of-cooling layer for public-health policy.

Cross-ref (corpus): Green spaces provide substantial but unequal urban cooling globally — 10.1038/s41467-024-51355-0

④ Replace residential walksheds with mobility-revealed park use

Extending this paperThe paper assumes the people near a park are the people who use it — a residence-based proxy. Fusing mobile-phone / GPS trajectories would reveal actual visitation and travel distances to high- vs. low-amenity parks, testing whether nearby residents of color are simply closer or genuinely visiting, and exposing exposure disparities the static walkshed misses.

Added value to my researchDirectly leverages my multi-source data fusion (mobile-phone, social-media, RS) and behavioral-accessibility agenda: realized park visitation becomes a learned distance-decay function that re-weights the paper's representativeness index.

Cross-ref (corpus): Big mobility data reveals hyperlocal air pollution exposure disparities in the Bronx, New York — 10.1038/s44284-024-00093-x

⑤ Optimize amenity investment as an efficiency–equity facility problem

Extending this paperThe paper diagnoses inequity but offers no allocation strategy. Casting it as a spatial optimization — where a fixed budget of tree-planting / shade / cooling interventions is allocated across the 122,988 parks to maximize equity gain per dollar — would convert the diagnostic into a prescriptive planning tool, with a Pareto frontier between mean amenity improvement and disparity reduction.
