Unforeseen high continental-scale soil microbiome homogenization in urban greenspaces

Xin Sun · Jake M. Robinson · Manuel Delgado-Baquerizo · Anton Potapov · Haifeng Yao · Biao Zhu · Alexei V. Tiunov · Linxiu Zhang · Faith Ka Shun Chan · Scott X. Chang · Martin F. Breed · Nico Eisenhauer · Stefan Scheu · Zhipeng Li · Yong-Guan Zhu Published 2025-08-04 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00294-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 13 Chinese cities and four land uses (city parks, residential areas, adjacent forests and farmlands), urban greenspaces consistently host higher local (alpha) richness of soil bacteria, protists and fungi — but not metazoa — than farmlands and forests, an effect closely tied to higher soil pH. Yet at the continental scale these urban communities are more homogeneous (lower beta diversity) than farmland communities. The paradoxical signature — locally richer, regionally more similar — means urbanization drives biotic homogenization and more synchronous responses to environmental stressors, a hidden fragility for the resilience of city soils.

Why It Matters

  • Reframes "more biodiversity" as a warning, not a win. Higher local richness in urban soils looks reassuring, but the paper shows it coexists with continental-scale homogenization — cities are converging on the same soil microbiome, eroding the regional turnover that underpins resilience.
  • Compares cities against the right baselines. By measuring city parks and residential soils alongside adjacent farmlands and forests, it isolates the urban signal from generic land management and answers a previously open question.
  • Identifies a tractable lever — soil pH. The elevated urban richness tracks elevated pH, giving managers a measurable, modifiable handle rather than an abstract "urbanization effect."
  • Synchronous risk is the real concern. Homogeneous communities respond in unison to droughts, heatwaves and pollution, raising the chance of correlated, city-wide losses of soil function — a systemic risk for the soils that cities of the future will depend on.

Core Methods

  • Stratified comparative sampling: paired soil sampling across four land uses (parks, residential, forest, farmland) replicated over 13 cities to separate the urban signal from land management and climate.
  • Cross-kingdom amplicon sequencing: high-throughput sequencing of bacteria, protists, fungi and metazoa, with taxonomy assigned via SILVA/UNITE/PR² reference databases in QIIME pipelines.
  • Alpha vs. beta diversity decomposition: contrast local richness (alpha) against community turnover (beta) to detect the "locally rich, regionally homogenized" signature (Figs. 2–3).
  • Diversity accumulation curves: land-use-resolved accumulation curves (relative to forests) quantify how richness saturates differently across urban vs. natural soils (Fig. 3).
  • Biodiversity–environment modeling: relate diversity to soil/environmental predictors — pH foremost — to identify the abiotic drivers and their land-use dependence (Figs. 4–5).
