Reusability report: Unpaired deep-learning approaches for holographic image reconstruction

Yuhe Zhang · Tobias Ritschel · Pablo Villanueva-Perez Published 2024-02-15 DOI 10.1038/s42256-024-00798-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A reusability assessment of FMGAN — a physics-parameterized, unpaired GAN for holographic image reconstruction — confirms its reproducibility and out-of-distribution generalizability on polystyrene microsphere data, but reveals a critical limitation: when an explicit forward optical model is unavailable (e.g., with unknown blurring PSFs), PhaseGAN outperforms FMGAN because its dual-generator design can learn the optical system response. Adding the PSF model explicitly to FMGAN's propagator (FMGAN†) restores performance, demonstrating that incorporating domain physics into unpaired learning is both a strength and a requirement for robustness in non-ideal imaging conditions.

Why It Matters

  • Unlocks holographic reconstruction without paired training data. Acquiring paired (hologram, ground truth) datasets in biomedical imaging is often impossible due to dose constraints or sample degradation; unpaired methods remove this barrier while retaining reconstruction quality.
  • Systematic reusability audit, not just a replication. The study goes beyond reproducing published numbers — it stress-tests three non-perfect optical regimes (Poisson noise, Gaussian blurring, non-uniform blurring) and a wide propagation-distance range, yielding a map of FMGAN's actual operating envelope.
  • Identifies a concrete failure mode and its fix. FMGAN generates image hallucinations when faced with unknown optical PSFs; adding the PSF explicitly to the forward propagator (FMGAN†) eliminates the artefacts — actionable guidance for practitioners deploying unpaired GANs on non-ideal optical systems.
  • Benchmarks three competing unpaired methods head-to-head. CycleGAN, PhaseGAN, and FMGAN are evaluated on the same datasets and metrics (FSIM, PCC, MAE of propagation distance), establishing clear conditions under which each method should be preferred.

Core Methods

  • Unpaired GAN framework: cyclic image-to-image translation between hologram (detector) and complex-field (object) domains using forward and backward generators; no pixel-level pairing between hologram and ground truth is required during training.
  • FMGAN's physics parameterization: the forward generator is replaced by a differentiable physical propagation model (Rayleigh-Sommerfeld diffraction law) with propagation distance as a learnable parameter, stabilizing training and enabling out-of-distribution generalization.
  • FMGAN† extension (this study): blurring PSF (Gaussian or non-uniform) is added explicitly into the physical forward propagator, converting unknown optical aberrations into a known, differentiable model component.
  • Evaluation metrics: Feature Similarity Index (FSIM) and Pearson Correlation Coefficient (PCC) for amplitude and phase reconstruction quality; Mean Absolute Error (MAE) for propagation-distance estimation. Five independent runs per method with different random seeds for statistical robustness.
  • Fourier Ring Correlation (FRC) loss: spectral constraint added to PhaseGAN's training to enforce frequency-space consistency; PhaseGAN* (without FRC) is reported as an ablation baseline.
  • Network architectures: U-Net for CycleGAN and PhaseGAN; squeeze-and-excitation U-Net for FMGAN. ADAM optimizer with mini-batch size of 8; learning rates decayed on schedule throughout training for all methods.

① Replace the GAN adversarial loop with self-supervised physics-consistency training

Extending this paperFMGAN's core strength is its differentiable physical propagator; its core weakness is that GAN-style adversarial training remains unstable when the propagator is incomplete (unknown PSF), leading to hallucinations (Fig. 2). A direct extension of the paper is to replace the discriminator-based adversarial loop with a self-supervised physics-consistency loss: enforce that the reconstructed complex field, when re-propagated through the forward model, reproduces the observed hologram — no discriminator, no paired data, and no fragile min-max training dynamics. This approach directly addresses the paper's own "Future directions" call for exploring alternative loss functions and would eliminate the hallucinations identified as FMGAN's primary failure mode.

Added value to my researchThe self-supervised physics-consistency principle transfers directly to spatiotemporal geospatial inverse problems: just as re-propagating a reconstructed wavefield validates the inferred complex field, running a spatial interaction model (gravity model, SIR epidemic) forward from a predicted state validates spatiotemporal reconstructions from sparse sensor data. This establishes a physics-closure loss paradigm for GeoAI — an underexplored direction that could improve my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and agent-based modeling when ground truth is unavailable.

② Unify the three non-perfect optical systems under a single differentiable rendering pipeline

Extending this paperThe paper tests Poisson noise, uniform blur, and non-uniform blur as three independent perturbations, each requiring a bespoke FMGAN† modification with a hand-crafted PSF. A more powerful extension is to embed the entire optical forward chain inside a differentiable rendering pipeline where noise, PSF shape, tilt, and vignetting are all learnable parameters of a single differentiable simulator. This enables FMGAN to jointly infer reconstruction quality and optical system parameters from raw hologram data alone, eliminating the need for prior PSF knowledge and collapsing the paper's three separate experiments into a single self-calibrating model that adapts to any non-perfect optical configuration.

Added value to my researchDifferentiable simulation is an emerging paradigm in GeoAI: if remote sensing noise models and spatial resolution degradation functions are parameterized as differentiable layers, they can be learned end-to-end alongside spatial prediction tasks. This supports my multi-source data fusion work (mobile phone + remote sensing) by treating sensor noise and systematic biases as learnable calibration components rather than fixed preprocessing steps, improving transferability of geospatial models across sensing platforms.

③ Apply a causal lens to diagnose why FMGAN hallucinates spurious beads

Extending this paperThe paper documents that FMGAN generates spurious polystyrene beads under Gaussian blurring (red circles, Fig. 2b) — a hallucination symptom of the model learning dataset-specific bead-density correlations rather than physics. Extending the paper through a causal perspective: model bead-presence as a confounding variable in the joint distribution of hologram domain and reconstruction domain, then apply interventional data augmentation (randomizing bead count and arrangement during training) to break spurious correlations. This reframes FMGAN's observed failure mode not as an architectural limitation but as a distributional bias correctable through causal training protocols.

Added value to my researchMy explainable ML work relies on model outputs being trustworthy and not artefacts of dataset bias. The causal audit framework maps directly onto spatial prediction models trained on geographically unbalanced samples: healthcare access models overfit to urban training data show analogous hallucinations in rural areas. Applying the same causal deconfounding protocol — treating city type or population density as a potential confounder — provides a principled framework for detecting and mitigating geographic dataset bias in GeoAI.

④ Multi-task pretraining across all three figshare object types to reduce data requirements

Extending this paperThe paper uses only the microsphere dataset while the Lee et al. figshare repository also contains red blood cell and histological slide datasets, explicitly set aside. Practical deployment of FMGAN in biomedical labs depends on how few samples are needed per new object type. Extending the paper: pretrain a shared encoder backbone on all three figshare object types using multi-task self-supervised physics-consistency objectives (shared propagation physics, object-type-specific decoders), then fine-tune on a new target with minimal samples. This leverages the paper's own declared data availability and could substantially reduce the stated 600-image training requirement.

Added value to my researchThe pretraining-then-fine-tuning paradigm aligns directly with my agenda for fine-tuning geospatial foundation models: just as hologram types share underlying wave physics, cities and regions share spatial interaction and accessibility physics. Multi-task pretraining across multiple cities — then fine-tuning on a target city with sparse local data — is the same strategy applied to urban health geography models, and this paper provides a clear worked example of the paradigm's conditions of success and failure.

⑤ Formalize OOD reliability assessment with a tri-axis evaluation protocol

Extending this paperThe paper evaluates OOD generalization by testing propagation distances outside the training range, but holds object class and optical system fixed. A rigorous extension would apply a formal tri-axis OOD protocol distinguishing: (1) covariate shift (different distances, same object), (2) semantic shift (different object types: red blood cells vs. beads), and (3) domain shift (different optical setups). Reporting FMGAN's FSIM/PCC/MAE across all three axes would deliver the principled OOD reliability map that practitioners need before deploying unpaired holographic reconstruction in new experimental facilities, matching the level of rigor now demanded for scientific ML benchmarks.

Added value to my researchFormal OOD evaluation is central to my open-science/FAIR reproducibility agenda and to deploying GeoAI models across heterogeneous geographic contexts. A spatiotemporal model trained on Chinese cities and applied to European cities faces precisely this tri-axis challenge — covariate shift (different density distributions), semantic shift (different urban morphologies), and domain shift (different data collection systems). The vocabulary and protocol from holographic imaging's OOD taxonomy provides a ready-made framework for GeoAI transferability assessments.
