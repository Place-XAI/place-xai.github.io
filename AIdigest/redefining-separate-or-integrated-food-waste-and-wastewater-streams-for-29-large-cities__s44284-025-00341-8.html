Redefining separate or integrated food waste and wastewater streams for 29 large cities

Xu Zou · Zi Zhang · Chengyu Xiao · Liezhong Fan · Yifeng Feng · Hongxuan Wang · Jiayan Deng · Chukuan Jiang · Feixiang Zan · Hongxiao Guo · Guanghao Chen Published 2025-11-06 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00341-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Cities usually treat solid food waste and wastewater as two separate systems, missing a chance for resource integration. The authors built the Urban Biowaste Flux (UBF) model — coupling mechanistic bioprocesses with life-cycle assessment — to quantify material flows, energy, cost and greenhouse-gas (GHG) emissions for city-specific waste, and validated it on Hong Kong before applying it to 28 more large cities. Whether to divert food waste into the sewer hinges on its moisture: net cost rises linearly with moisture, and a threshold of about 50 kg per capita per year marks where sewer integration becomes cost-effective. Optimized, locale-specific strategies can cut overall emissions for targeted cities by up to 69% versus today's separate systems.

Why It Matters

  • Breaks a silo that wastes resources. Solid-waste and wastewater utilities are typically planned, financed and regulated apart; this is one of the first city-scale quantifications of what merging the two biowaste streams actually buys in cost and carbon.
  • Gives planners a single, transferable number. The ~50 kg/capita/year food-waste moisture threshold is a crisp decision rule for whether a given city should keep streams separate or push food waste down the sewer.
  • Large, concrete mitigation potential. Up to 69% lower emissions from optimized strategies is a city-policy-scale lever in a sector (organic waste + wastewater) that is a major source of urban methane.
  • Ships as an open, reusable tool. The UBF model (Excel + VBA) is released on GitHub, so other cities can re-run the analysis on their own waste composition and tariffs — the research is also infrastructure.

Core Methods

  • Urban Biowaste Flux (UBF) model: a city-scale framework that couples mechanistic bioprocess models with life-cycle assessment (LCA) to track material flows, energy use, costs and GHG emissions for food waste + wastewater.
  • Stoichiometric mass balances: treatment processes (e.g. anaerobic digestion / co-digestion, sludge handling, incineration, landfill) represented via stoichiometric equations and mass-balance simulation parameterized by city-specific inputs.
  • Scenario comparison: systematic contrast of separate vs. integrated management across four treatment strategies (Fig. 4), reporting energy, cost and emissions for each.
  • Sensitivity / correlation analysis: relating food-waste characteristics (especially moisture) to sustainability outcomes, yielding the linear cost–moisture relationship and the ~50 kg/cap/yr threshold (Fig. 3).
  • Policy optimization: deriving optimized, locale-specific strategies per city (Fig. 5) and quantifying the maximum achievable emissions reduction (up to 69%).
  • Validation-then-transfer: calibrate/validate on Hong Kong, then apply the same model to 28 additional cities — an explicit transferability test.

① Spatialize the UBF model onto the sewer network as a graph operator

Extending this paperUBF currently treats each city as a single lumped node — one composition, one tariff, one moisture value. But food-waste diversion to the sewer is fundamentally a network problem: where disposers are installed, how flows aggregate toward treatment plants, and where pipes saturate all vary intra-city. A direct extension: re-cast UBF's mass-balance on the actual collection/sewer graph and learn a physics-informed graph operator mapping (per-district waste field, network topology, plant locations) → cost/emissions, with the paper's stoichiometric conservation laws encoded as soft constraints so it extrapolates to un-modeled districts.

② Turn the 50 kg/cap/yr moisture threshold into an explainable, transferable predictor

Extending this paperThe paper's headline rule — sewer integration pays off above ~50 kg moisture per capita per year — is read off correlation plots (Fig. 3). Extending it: train an explainable ML model (e.g. gradient boosting + SHAP) across the 29 cities to predict the break-even threshold from city covariates (climate, diet, tariff structure, plant mix), exposing which factors move the threshold and giving cities outside the sample a defensible, attributable estimate instead of a single global constant.

③ Add an explicit efficiency–equity axis to the optimized strategies

Extending this paperThe paper optimizes for cost and aggregate emissions (Fig. 5) but is silent on who bears the burden — food-waste disposer retrofits, tariff changes and plant siting fall unevenly across neighborhoods and income groups. Extending it: reframe the optimization as a multi-objective (cost × emissions × distributional equity) problem and trace the Pareto surface, so policymakers see the equity cost of the cheapest-carbon solution.

④ Fuse multi-source data to replace static, surveyed city inputs

Extending this paperUBF's per-city inputs (composition, moisture, tariffs) come from static surveys / databases that age quickly and miss seasonal swings (e.g. diet-driven moisture changes).
