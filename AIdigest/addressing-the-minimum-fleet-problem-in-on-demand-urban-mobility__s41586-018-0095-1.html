Addressing the minimum fleet problem in on-demand urban mobility

Vazifeh, M. M. · Santi, P. · Resta, G. · Strogatz, S. H. · Ratti, C. Published 2018-05-23 DOI 10.1038/s41586-018-0095-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

By recasting taxi dispatching as a minimum path cover problem on a directed acyclic vehicle-shareability network, this paper proves that New York City's taxi fleet of ~7,748 vehicles can be reduced by 40% (offline optimal) or 30% (real-time near-optimal) — without any passenger delay, ride sharing, or regulatory change. Serving 150 million trips over one year with only ~4,627 vehicles is achievable through smart, centralized dispatching alone, opening a direct path to urban fleet right-sizing for autonomous vehicles.

Core Methods

  • Vehicle-shareability network construction: each trip becomes a node; a directed edge (Ti → Tj) exists if a vehicle can travel from Ti's drop-off to Tj's pick-up within the connection-time budget δ (set to 15 min). Network is proven to be a directed acyclic graph (DAG) by the arrow of time.
  • Minimum path cover on a DAG: the minimum fleet size equals the minimum path cover of the shareability network, which is equivalent to a maximum bipartite matching problem — solvable in O(|E|·|N|1/2) time using the Hopcroft–Karp algorithm.
  • Real-time (online) batch dispatching: trip requests are batched in 1-minute windows; a weighted bipartite matching maximizes trips served within a tolerable delay (max Δt = 3 or 6 min). Fleet is set to N = Nmin × 1.2 (20% inflation over the offline optimum).
  • Travel-time estimation: GPS-calibrated, hour-of-day per-segment travel times derived from historical trip data using a least-squares heuristic (from Santi et al. 2014). Routing uses these segment speeds on the Manhattan OSM network.
  • Bin-packing model for fleet scaling: a simple stochastic bin-packing analogy explains the observed plateau in fleet size despite rising trip counts — vehicles have a finite spatiotemporal capacity, and new trips fill existing capacity before forcing new vehicles.
  • Multi-operator analysis: random trip partitioning simulates oligopolistic markets; two operators incur a 4–6% fleet-size penalty, three operators 6–10%, versus the monopolistic global optimum.

① Transfer the vehicle-shareability network to multi-city generalization via graph learning

Extending this paperThis paper solves the minimum fleet problem for a single city (NYC, 2011) and asserts the approach is general, but never tests it on other urban forms. The vehicle-shareability network's structure — density of edges, path-cover size, void-ratio profile — will vary with city geometry, street topology, and demand patterns. A direct extension: train a graph neural network (GNN) on the shareability network itself as a surrogate for the Hopcroft–Karp solver, and use transfer learning across multiple cities. The paper's trip-to-node encoding and connection-time parameter δ provide a clean, city-agnostic feature schema. This would let operators of any city predict optimal fleet size without re-running the full combinatorial algorithm from scratch.

A fleet-sizing GNN trained on NYC could transfer to Chinese cities (e.g., Jinan, where GPS taxi data is available), providing a GeoAI mobility module for my spatiotemporal analysis toolbox.

② Extend the framework to healthcare on-demand transport: accessibility meets dispatching

Extending this paperThe paper frames its problem as serving any "point-to-point mobility" demand and explicitly notes the method generalizes beyond taxis. Non-emergency medical transport (NEMT) — trips from home to clinics, dialysis centers, or mental health facilities — is a structurally identical problem: fixed origin-destination pairs, time-window constraints, no ride sharing assumed. Extending this paper to NEMT would add a service-type priority layer (some trips cannot tolerate even a 3-minute delay) and a spatial equity objective — the minimum-fleet solution should not deprive low-income or remote populations of rides even when it is "efficient" to do so. The paper's multi-operator analysis section already shows how fleet fragmentation degrades efficiency, which maps directly onto NEMT providers that operate in siloed county-level contracts.

Added value to my researchMy health geography work on spatial accessibility of primary care (2SFCA/R2SFCA) measures whether facilities are reachable but not whether the physical trip is reliably served. Combining my accessibility scores with this paper's fleet-sizing math would produce a mobility-supply-aware accessibility metric: a zone's effective accessibility is the product of a facility being reachable in principle and a vehicle being available in practice. This bridges my service-area delineation work with operational transportation modeling.

③ Incorporate spatial equity into the minimum fleet objective

Extending this paperThe paper's optimality criterion is purely operational: minimize the number of vehicles subject to zero passenger delay. It is silent on where within the city service degrades first when a smaller fleet is deployed. In practice, thinly served peripheries (low trip density) and dense cores operate under different void-ratio regimes, and a global 40% reduction could systematically disadvantage outer boroughs or off-peak populations. A direct extension: add a spatial Gini constraint on per-zone service rates, converting the pure efficiency problem into an efficiency–equity Pareto frontier. The paper's δ parameter and the bin-packing model already supply the levers; the Pareto tracing is the missing step.

Added value to my researchThis mirrors precisely my work on EV-charging station efficiency–equity optimization and my use of spatial entropy as an equity index. The minimum-fleet framework provides a new domain — ride-hail dispatching — where I can test whether my MGWR-based spatial heterogeneity measures predict which zones face the steepest equity penalties under a constrained fleet, connecting explainable ML to transport operations.

④ Couple fleet sizing with demand forecasting using spatiotemporal kernel density models

Extending this paperThe paper's online version operates on a 1-minute batching horizon, knowing only the current and very near-future demand. Its performance degrades with demand burst events (sports matches, concerts) that inflate fleet requirements unpredictably. The paper identifies this as a limitation but proposes no solution. A direct extension: replace the paper's flat historical-baseline fleet-size estimator Nmin with a spatiotemporal demand forecast — e.g., a kernel density estimation of pick-up intensity conditioned on time-of-day, day-of-week, and event calendars — so that Nmin is updated dynamically ahead of known surges. This would tighten the 20% fleet-inflation buffer (x = 1.2) used in the online model.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal kernel density estimation (KDE) methods, developed for crime hotspot and health-event prediction, transfer directly to taxi pick-up intensity forecasting. A joint KDE–shareability-network pipeline would be a concrete GeoAI product: a demand-adaptive dispatcher that combines my spatiotemporal analytics with the paper's combinatorial solver. The NYC TLC dataset provides a ready benchmark, and the paper's own source data CSV files enable direct replication.

⑤ Link fleet optimization to transit network resilience and multimodal integration

Extending this paperThe paper optimizes a single on-demand fleet in isolation and notes that future work should extend to "multiple coexisting fleets of various types." Taxi/ride-hail services act as feeder or substitute modes for public transit; when a subway line fails, taxi demand spikes in precisely the spatiotemporal pattern the paper's void-ratio analysis reveals. Extending the framework: model the ride-hail fleet as a resilience buffer for multimodal transit, where the shareability network is rebuilt dynamically as subway disruptions are detected, and the minimum fleet for surge demand during failures is pre-computed from historical disruption patterns. The paper's multi-operator section already shows how demand fragmentation affects fleet size — disruption-induced demand shifts are the same problem in a different costume.

This opens a path to integrate my SIR-like network spreading models with transport operations research.
