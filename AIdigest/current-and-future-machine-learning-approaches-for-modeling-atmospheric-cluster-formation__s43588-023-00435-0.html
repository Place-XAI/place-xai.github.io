Current and future machine learning approaches for modeling atmospheric cluster formation

Jakub Kubečka · Yosef Knattrup · Morten Engsvang · Andreas Buchgraitz Jensen · Daniel Ayoubi · Haide Wu · Ove Christiansen · Jonas Elm Published 2023-06-26 DOI 10.1038/s43588-023-00435-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The Δ-ML (delta-ML) approach — training a surrogate on the energy difference between cheap (semi-empirical) and expensive (DFT or coupled-cluster) methods — can dramatically accelerate configurational sampling of atmospheric molecular clusters, the rate-limiting step toward new aerosol particle formation. The publicly released Clusteromics I–V datasets (more than 23,000 equilibrium configurations at the ωB97X-D/6-31++G(d,p) level) provide the first purpose-built training corpus; a coupled-cluster-quality Δ-ML model on a subset of 11,749 clusters achieves MAE below 0.1 kcal mol-1. The key unresolved challenge is extrapolation to non-equilibrium geometries, which requires explicit gradient information and non-equilibrium structures in training; future ML-driven molecular dynamics and reactive free-energy prediction on acid–base clusters are the most transformative near-term targets for linking molecular-scale nucleation to global climate models.

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the largest computational bottleneck in aerosol science. Configurational sampling requires millions of quantum chemical evaluations; the Δ-ML screening step cuts DFT calculations by orders of magnitude, unlocking acid–base cluster compositions that were previously computationally out of reach.
  • Directly reduces the largest uncertainty in global climate forcing. New particle formation (NPF) contributes up to half of all cloud condensation nuclei (CCN); aerosol–cloud interactions remain the biggest uncertainty in IPCC radiative forcing estimates. Even 1–3 nm changes in cluster growth behavior alter CCN counts by up to 50%.
  • Ships open, reusable scientific infrastructure. The Clusteromics I–V datasets and the JKCS/JKQC/JKML toolchain are freely available on GitHub and an atmospheric cluster database, making the approach immediately reproducible and extensible by the community.

The paper charts an explicit end-to-end pathway: ML-accelerated cluster thermochemistry → cluster distribution dynamics (MAFOR, ARCA box) → chemical transport models (ADCHEM) → global climate models (EC-EARTH, ECHAM) — turning molecular physics into actionable climate policy input.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Clusteromics I–V
Five QC datasets of atmospheric molecular cluster configurations (sulfuric acid, methanesulfonic acid, nitric acid, and organic acid systems with bases including ammonia, methylamine, and dimethylamine). Together comprising more than 23,000 equilibrium cluster configurations at ωB97X-D/6-31++G(d,p). Freely available at the atmospheric cluster database and released across ACS Omega 2021–2023 (refs. 27–31).
CC-level subset
Jensen et al. computed PNO–CCSD(F12*)(T)/cc-pVDZ-F12 binding energies for 11,749 equilibrium (acid)1–2(base)1–2 clusters from Clusteromics I–III — enabling Δ(DFT|CC)-ML at coupled-cluster accuracy with MAE below 0.1 kcal mol-1 (ref. 87).
SA–AM extrapolation
Sulfuric acid–ammonia clusters from (SA)1(AM)1 to (SA)30(AM)30 used to test size extrapolation; preliminary results show error scaling almost linearly with size (~6 kcal mol-1 at n=30) when non-equilibrium training structures are included. Not separately released.
Reference ML benchmarks
GDB-17 (166.4 billion organic molecules; Reymond); QM7 (7,165 molecules, ≤7 non-H atoms); QM9 (133,885 molecules, ≤9 non-H atoms); ANI-1/1x/1ccx/2x (up to 20 million off-equilibrium organic-molecule conformations via normal-mode sampling). These are methodological reference points, not primary training data for atmospheric clusters.
Software / tools
JKCS (Jammy Key for Configurational Sampling) — open-source multi-step sampling; JKQC — thermochemical property extraction; JKML — user-friendly ML interface. Available on GitHub (ref. 33/34). The GPU-accelerated QML code (ref. 39) and ABCluster (ABC algorithm) are companion tools.

Core Methods

  • Δ-ML (delta-ML) screening: train a surrogate on the energy difference between a cheap method (PM7 or GFNi-xTB) and an expensive target (DFT or DLPNO–CCSD(T)); use the Δ-ML predictor as a cheap single-point filter to reduce DFT optimization calls by excluding high-energy configurations early.
  • Multi-step configurational sampling pipeline: ABCluster/OGOLEM/basin-hopping → PM7/xTB optimization → Δ(xTB|DFT)-ML screening → DFT optimization + frequency → Δ(DFT|CC)-ML single-point (replacing DLPNO–CCSD(T)). Each ML insertion step filters out candidates, reducing total computation.
  • Kernel ridge regression (KRR) with FCHL representation: primary method; O(N3) scaling in training size; local atomic descriptors enable size extrapolation by decomposing total energy into transferable atomic contributions; demonstrated MAE below 1 kcal mol-1 (Δ-ML) and below 2 kcal mol-1 extrapolation to (SA)7(W)10 clusters.
  • Gaussian process regression (GPR): probabilistic ML providing built-in uncertainty quantification; proposed as an active-learning oracle — training points are selected to minimize prediction variance on the target system, enabling intelligent dataset pruning.
  • Neural networks (NNs): O(N) scaling, suitable for large datasets and MD force fields; currently shown to have large errors (>18 kcal mol-1 RMSE) for large SA–DMA clusters when trained naively; identified as the path toward ML-driven reactive molecular dynamics.
  • Extrapolation benchmarks: three axes systematically tested — (i) cluster size: errors scale ~linearly after adding non-equilibrium training data; (ii) cluster configuration (equilibrium→non-equilibrium): catastrophic failure without gradient training (MAE ~50 kcal mol-1); (iii) interactions and molecules: feasible for closely related chemical systems.

① Replace the paper's static database curation with uncertainty-driven active learning

Extending this paperThe paper proposes GPR uncertainty quantification as a training-data selection signal but leaves it as a future direction. A concrete extension: embed an active-learning loop directly in the multi-step sampling pipeline — after each Δ-ML evaluation, rank un-labeled configurations by predicted variance and selectively route only the most informative candidates to DFT. This replaces the current post-hoc heuristic dataset pruning (Fig. 3) with a principled online strategy that grows the Clusteromics corpus toward the specific cluster composition being studied. The uncertainty-driven dynamics paradigm for interatomic potentials in the corpus provides a direct blueprint, demonstrating that uncertainty-guided MD sampling reduces DFT calls by 60–80% for similar molecular systems.

Added value to my researchThe active-learning-over-expensive-oracle pattern is directly analogous to my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation work: instead of allocating mobile-phone sensing or survey resources uniformly across space, an uncertainty-guided sampler directs data collection to high-entropy spatial regions — areas where the health or mobility model is most uncertain. Translating the GPR variance criterion from cluster configuration space to geographic space gives a principled spatial active-sampling strategy that could halve data collection cost for fine-resolution public-health studies.

② Replace FCHL with learned graph-neural-network encoders to solve the non-equilibrium failure

Extending this paperThe paper's key failure mode is catastrophic accuracy loss for non-equilibrium geometries (~50 kcal mol-1 MAE) because FCHL encodes only the local atomic environment and is sensitive to bond-length and angle changes away from minima. A direct extension: replace FCHL-KRR with a message-passing graph neural network (GNN) trained on mixed equilibrium + normal-mode-sampled non-equilibrium Clusteromics data. GNNs encode full bond topology and propagate information across multiple bond distances, making them naturally more robust to geometry perturbations. This specifically targets the gap between Fig. 5's blue-square (equilibrium) and red-circle (non-equilibrium) performance — the paper's clearest open problem.

Added value to my researchMolecular GNN representations are the chemistry equivalent of spatial graph learning for urban networks in my research. My physics-informed graph learning work on transportation and health-facility networks uses the same message-passing backbone; the representation design lessons — handling variable node counts, encoding pairwise distances, enforcing symmetry — transfer directly to irregular geospatial graphs (road networks, census catchment boundaries). The molecular GNN literature on handling non-equilibrium geometries informs my handling of missing or noisy spatial edges in urban accessibility graphs.

③ Train a multi-task ML model jointly targeting ΔE, ΔH, ΔS, and ΔG across all Clusteromics levels

Extending this paperThe paper trains separate Δ-ML models for electronic energy at each theory level, while free energies (ΔG) — the physically relevant quantity for NPF rates — remain a flagged future extension. A structured extension: train a multi-task learning (MTL) model that simultaneously outputs electronic energy at semi-empirical, DFT, and coupled-cluster levels plus enthalpy, entropy, and Gibbs free energy from a single shared molecular encoder. Corpus work shows MTL reaches coupled-cluster accuracy on molecular electronic structures by transferring signal across correlated tasks — especially valuable here where high-level ΔG labels are scarce but the thermochemical targets are strongly correlated through the Hessian.

Added value to my researchMulti-task learning across correlated spatial outcomes is central to my GeoAI prediction pipeline: spatiotemporal models that simultaneously predict health outcomes, mobility patterns, and socioeconomic indicators from shared remote-sensing representations generalize better than single-task models, particularly in data-sparse regions. The molecular multi-task insight — that a shared encoder reduces label requirements for expensive targets via transfer from cheaper correlated tasks — directly motivates multi-output architectures in my healthcare accessibility and 2SFCA models where some outcomes (e.g., utilization rates) are scarce while correlated covariates (POI density, travel time) are abundant.

⑤ Pre-train a self-supervised foundation model on Clusteromics + QM9 then fine-tune per composition

Extending this paperEvery time the paper's workflow targets a new cluster composition (e.g., SA–TMA or SA–guanidine), it requires building and training on a new labeled subset of Clusteromics from scratch. A frontier extension: pre-train a self-supervised molecular foundation model (masked-atom prediction or contrastive) jointly on Clusteromics I–V, QM9, and ANI-2x, then fine-tune on each new cluster composition with only tens of labeled QC calculations. This amortizes the dataset-construction cost across the entire atmospheric cluster chemical space — a direct solution to the "chemical complexity" bottleneck the paper identifies — and would naturally generalize across all three extrapolation regimes (size, configuration, interactions) via the shared pre-trained representation.

Added value to my researchThis is precisely the geospatial foundation model fine-tuning paradigm I am developing: pre-train on massive multi-source data (satellite imagery, GPS mobility, OSM networks), then fine-tune with small labeled datasets for specific urban-health tasks (telehealth digital divides, EV charging equity gaps, 2SFCA service-area calibration). The molecular case provides a rigorous benchmark for a key design question I face: how many fine-tuning labels are needed to reach task-specific accuracy that matches fully supervised training from scratch? Answering this for molecular systems informs analogous experiments in the geospatial domain.
