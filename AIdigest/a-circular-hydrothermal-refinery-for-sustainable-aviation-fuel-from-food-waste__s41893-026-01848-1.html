A circular hydrothermal refinery for sustainable aviation fuel from food waste

Buchun Si · Zixin Wang · Jamison Watson · Sabrina Summers · Yalin Li · Siying Yu · Hong Yang · Zhibin Yang · Joshua S. Heyne · Jinyue Jiang · Zhiyong Jason Ren · Hemin Ma · Chaoyuan Wang · Pengsen Wang · Yuanhui Zhang Published 2026-06-03 DOI 10.1038/s41893-026-01848-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

An integrated, closed-loop hydrothermal refinery converts urban food waste into a 100% drop-in sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) — meeting all flash point, viscosity and freezing-point constraints of Jet A without blending — while a paired electrochemical stage recovers high-purity acetic acid (91.6% of organics) and nutrients (>76.9%) from the problematic aqueous phase, simultaneously generating enough hydrogen to sustain the hydrotreating step. Techno-economic and life-cycle analyses show the system can reach market competitiveness at US$3.82 per gasoline gallon equivalent with renewable electricity, and achieves a net-negative carbon footprint of −8.5 kg CO₂e GGE⁻¹ — establishing a circular, carbon-negative pathway from urban organic waste to high-density aviation energy.

Why It Matters

  • Closes the hardest decarbonization gap in transport. Aviation accounts for roughly 2–3% of global CO₂ emissions and has no electrification shortcut; a carbon-negative, 100% drop-in SAF derived from waste directly addresses this hard-to-abate sector without requiring engine or infrastructure modification.
  • Solves the aqueous-phase bottleneck that has hampered HTL at scale. Rather than treating the HTL aqueous phase (HTL-AP) as a disposal problem, the framework converts it into saleable acetic acid, recoverable nutrients (nitrogen and phosphorus), and clean hydrogen — turning a liability into multiple revenue streams.
  • Transforms urban food waste from a cost centre to an energy asset. With over one-third of all food produced globally wasted, cities generate enormous stocks of wet organic material. This work provides a scalable, city-anchored refinery blueprint that can be sited at urban waste-processing hubs.
  • Demonstrates genuine life-cycle carbon negativity. Unlike most SAF pathways that merely reduce emissions, the integrated system with renewable electricity achieves negative net CO₂e — a rare verified claim grounded in detailed TEA/LCA modelling with open source data.
  • Sets a benchmark for waste-to-energy circularity. The paper's open-source TEA/LCA codebase (EXPOsan on GitHub) enables city planners and engineers to simulate cost and carbon trade-offs for their own waste streams, making the research directly actionable.

Core Methods

  • Hydrothermal liquefaction (HTL): food waste is processed at high temperature and pressure in an aqueous medium, producing a biocrude oil and an aqueous phase (HTL-AP). The paper optimizes HTL conditions to maximize biocrude yield and quality.
  • Catalytic hydrotreating / upgrading: the biocrude is upgraded over a heterogeneous catalyst to remove heteroatoms (N, O, S) and saturate aromatic rings, producing a cycloalkane-rich (70.5 wt%) fuel fraction meeting ASTM jet-fuel specifications as a 100% drop-in fuel.
  • Partial electrooxidation of HTL-AP: the problematic organic-laden aqueous phase is treated by controlled electrochemical oxidation, selectively converting mixed organics to high-purity acetic acid (91.6% of recovered organics) without fully mineralizing to CO₂.
  • Electrodialysis for nutrient recovery: paired electrodialysis selectively recovers nitrogen and phosphorus nutrients (>76.9%) from the treated HTL-AP, producing fertilizer-grade streams and closing the nutrient loop.
  • In-situ hydrogen generation: the electrochemical stage co-produces hydrogen (from the cathode) sufficient to supply the biocrude hydrotreating step, eliminating the need for external hydrogen purchases in the integrated system.
  • Techno-economic analysis (TEA): three refinery configurations are modelled using QSDsan/EXPOsan to calculate the minimum fuel selling price (MFSP) under baseline and sensitivity scenarios, identifying electricity cost as the dominant cost lever.
  • Life-cycle assessment (LCA): gate-to-gate and expanded system boundary LCA quantify global warming potential (GWP₁₀₀) and net carbon footprint; renewable electricity integration is the critical parameter for achieving carbon negativity.

② Close the nutrient loop: map recovered N/P back to urban agriculture potential

Extending this paperThe paper recovers >76.9% of nitrogen and phosphorus from the HTL aqueous phase but stops at the refinery gate — it does not ask where these nutrients go or whether they can displace synthetic fertilizer use at city scale. The corpus paper on building operating carbon dynamics across multiple cities provides exactly the city-scale energy data needed to parameterize such an integration.
