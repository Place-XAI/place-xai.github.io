The 15-minute city quantified using human mobility data

Timur Abbiasov · Cate Heine · Sadegh Sabouri · Arianna Salazar-Miranda · Paolo Santi · Edward Glaeser · Carlo Ratti Published 2024-02-05 DOI 10.1038/s41562-023-01770-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using GPS traces from 40 million US mobile devices across 420 urban areas, this paper finds that the median US resident makes only 14% of daily consumption trips locally (within a 15-minute walk). Local amenity access explains 84% of variation in local-trip behaviour across cities and 74% within cities. A 1961 NYC zoning natural experiment supports a causal link from access to usage. However, a critical trade-off emerges: encouraging local living increases experienced income segregation for low-income residents, who encounter far less socioeconomic diversity on short trips than on longer ones.

★

Why It Matters

  • Bridges the supply–demand gap in 15-minute city research. Prior work measured amenity proximity; this paper is the first large-scale US study to link supply-side access directly to observed trip behaviour, validating the core premise of the 15-minute city model with real GPS data from 13.6 billion POI visits.
  • Provides a credible causal foothold. Leveraging New York City's 1961 zoning resolution as a quasi-natural experiment yields instrumental-variable evidence that local access causally increases local-trip rates — moving the literature beyond correlation.
  • Reveals an uncomfortable equity trade-off. Promoting local living can deepen socioeconomic isolation for lower-income communities, whose only path to cross-class encounters runs through longer trips to wealthier areas. This reshapes policy design: local-living policies must be paired with social-mixing interventions.
  • National scale, granular resolution. Coverage of 420 urban areas and 150,159 census block groups in a single year (2019) gives the index unprecedented statistical power — a strong regional divide is revealed (Northeast ~34% local trips vs. South ~18%).
  • Directly informs land-use and zoning reform. Results suggest restrictive commercial zoning extends trip distances and carbon emissions, giving reformers an empirical lever: mixed-use zoning as a climate and mobility tool.

Core Methods

  • 15-minute usage index: share of consumption trips to 9 essential POI categories originating in a block group that fall within its 15-minute walking isochrone. Aggregated to urban-area level via population-weighted averaging.
  • 15-minute access index: for each block group, tally POIs per essential category within the walkshed; convert to percentile ranks across all block groups; weight by national average trip share per category; sum to a 0–100 composite index. Robust to alternative time thresholds (10/20/25 min) and unweighted versions.
  • OLS regression: 15-minute usage regressed on access at (a) urban-area level (n = 420) and (b) census block-group level (n = 150,159) with urban-area fixed effects, sociodemographic controls, transit frequency, and commute length.
  • Instrumental variable (IV) estimation: average commercial FAR permitted under the 1961 NYC Zoning Resolution used as instrument for current access across 5,582 New York City block groups, with 1960 Census pre-zoning controls (F-statistic = 187 at first stage).
  • Experienced segregation index: for each block group, compute visit-weighted income disparity between residents and co-located visitors across all POIs (following Xu 2022); scaled 0–100. Disaggregated by income quartile and trip length (</>15 min).
  • Non-parametric spline regression: city-specific splines fit to access–usage relationships across block groups to characterize local functional form (Fig. 4b).
  • Robustness checks: alternative time thresholds, unweighted access index, expanded POI universe (all SafeGraph POIs), transit-rich subsample, residential-stayers subsample (Opportunity Atlas), full POI universe segregation replication (Pearson r = 0.981).
  • Software: R 3.6.3, Python 3.7.7, Stata MP 16.

① Decompose the access–usage–segregation chain using mobility network community detection

Extending this paperThis paper establishes that local access drives local usage and that higher local usage raises experienced segregation for low-income residents — but these two findings sit in separate analyses. A direct extension: model the full causal chain as a structural mobility bipartite network, where block-group nodes are linked to POI nodes by trips and edge weights encode the income diversity of co-located visitors. Network community detection would reveal precisely which POI categories and trip-distance ranges most amplify or dampen the segregation effect, turning a two-part result into an integrated, mechanism-level account of how local living structures social exposure.

Added value to my researchThis network framing maps directly onto my spatiotemporal kernel density and community-detection toolkit: I can apply network community detection to the resident × POI bipartite graph to identify structurally segregated service sub-markets, then use explainable ML (SHAP over a spatial gradient boosting model) to attribute which neighbourhood features — density, zoning mix, transit score — govern community membership, producing an interpretable, policy-ready segregation diagnostic.

② Extend the 1961 NYC zoning IV into a multi-city quasi-experiment panel using AI-assisted archive mining

Extending this paperThe IV design is compelling but limited to New York City, which has an unusually well-documented zoning history. The paper acknowledges that findings may not generalize to less dense or differently regulated areas. A natural extension: systematically identify analogous zoning shock events — post-war urban-renewal designations, highway construction corridors, historic-district overlays — across many US cities, building a multi-city quasi-experiment panel. This would transform a single-city causal footnote into a transferable causal infrastructure, testing whether the access → usage coefficient is stable across regulatory regimes and density gradients.

③ Replace the fixed 15-minute threshold with a mobility-calibrated, spatially varying catchment

Extending this paperThe paper acknowledges the 15-minute cutoff is arbitrary and shows robustness at 10/20/25 min — but all alternatives are applied uniformly across all contexts. A principled extension: learn a spatially varying optimal threshold per neighbourhood or density regime by maximizing the local explanatory power of the access–usage relationship. This turns one universal cutoff into an adaptive, data-driven walkshed — acknowledging that "local" differs across low-density suburbs, mid-rise districts, and Manhattan-style grids — and sharpens the R² values reported in the paper into locally optimal, context-specific estimates.

④ Reformulate the segregation trade-off as a bi-level optimization objective for mixed-use POI placement

Extending this paperThe paper's experienced-segregation index is computed ex post as a diagnostic; it is never used to guide intervention design. A direct extension: reformulate the index as an objective in a bi-level optimization — the outer problem finds the optimal POI placement to increase 15-minute usage; the inner problem minimizes the resulting segregation penalty for low-income block groups. This bi-level design makes the paper's trade-off operational: instead of warning planners about the equity cost, the model resolves it jointly, revealing whether there exist POI configurations that simultaneously increase local-trip rates and reduce income isolation.

Added value to my researchThis bi-level structure directly parallels my EV-charging efficiency–equity optimization, extending it from a two-objective to a three-objective problem (coverage, carbon reduction, social mixing). The income-disaggregated segregation objective also connects to my health-geography work: experienced isolation is a measurable social determinant of health, so the same optimization formulation could serve equity-weighted healthcare-facility placement — aligning the paper's urban-sustainability framing with my healthcare accessibility research.

⑤ Transfer the behavioural usage index to data-scarce cities using satellite-derived built-environment proxies

Extending this paperThe paper's 15-minute usage index requires a SafeGraph-quality GPS panel — unavailable outside a small set of high-income countries. A critical extension: train a cross-city transfer model that predicts 15-minute usage from observable built-environment features (street-network density, building footprints, OSM POI counts, nighttime lights) using US cities as the labelled training set, then apply it globally. This would operationalize the paper's core empirical finding — access strongly predicts usage — as a transferable estimator that bridges the data gap between the US-centric paper and the worldwide 15-minute city policy debate.
