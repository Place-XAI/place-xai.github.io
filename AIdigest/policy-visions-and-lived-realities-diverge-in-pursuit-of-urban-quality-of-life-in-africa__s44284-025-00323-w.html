Policy visions and lived realities diverge in pursuit of urban quality of life in Africa

Niké Susan Wesch · Sonja De Beer · Carike Claassen · Roelof Petrus Burger · Selna Cornelius · Elizelle Juaneé Cilliers · Stuart John Piketh Published 2025-10-01 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00323-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

State planners and low-income communities in South Africa largely agree on what quality of life (QoL) is — but they frame and prioritize it very differently. The misalignment is not about which indicators matter, but about time, responsibility and space: communities need jobs, services and safety today, whereas the state plans for long-term, large-scale development. These "conflicting rationalities" — shaped by interpretation, power dynamics, and the uneven use of data in decision-making — are what weaken policy and fuel protest in the "protest capital of the world."

  • Grounds an abstract debate ("conflicting rationalities") in seven real communities. By pairing planning directives with interviews from planned and unplanned low-income settlements, it shows concretely where state logic and lived experience pull apart.
  • Names the politics of data. The paper argues spatial decisions are shaped by power and the uneven use of data — a direct, honest caution that quantitative indices and maps are interpreted, not neutral.
  • Speaks to the world's fastest-urbanizing continent. South Africa's protest dynamics are an extreme case of state–community divergence that is increasingly relevant across the rapidly urbanizing Global South.

Core Methods

  • Grounded theory (constructivist): an emergent QoL framework built bottom-up from data rather than imposing pre-existing categories; literature revisited iteratively for theoretical elaboration (Charmaz tradition).
  • Content analysis: inductive and elaborative coding to categorize social indicators related to QoL across index reports, directives and interviews.
  • Storyline analysis: tracing the underlying rationalities and implicit arguments that guide planning directives (the "conflicting rationalities" lens).
  • Case-study analysis: situating emergent themes in the lived, empirical realities of the seven communities (planned vs. unplanned settlements).
  • Triangulation across three perspectives: integrating index, state and community sources, with results visualized as a Sankey diagram of directive↔indicator engagement (Fig. 2) and a spatial map of case-study importance (Fig. 3).

① Quantify the "temporal–responsibility–spatial" divergence with geospatial indicators

Extending this paperThe paper diagnoses three axes of misalignment (time, responsibility, space) qualitatively across seven communities. A direct extension: operationalize each axis as a measurable geospatial signal — e.g., overlay the community priorities (jobs, services, safety) against mapped infrastructure deficits and slum footprints so the qualitatively asserted "spatial disconnect" becomes a quantified map. Pairing the interview themes with continent-scale deprivation mapping would let the framework scale beyond seven cases and test whether the divergence is universal across SA's urban hierarchy.

Added value to my researchThis is a template for my health-geography accessibility work: lived community priorities become the demand weights in a 2SFCA model, so service-area delineation reflects what residents actually prioritize today rather than planner-assumed need.

② Interrogate the "uneven use of data" with explainable AI on the planning pipeline

Extending this paperA central claim is that spatial decisions are shaped by the uneven use of data and power. The paper stops at naming this; it could be extended into a reproducible audit — train an interpretable model to predict which QoL indicators each directive actually privileges (the Sankey of Fig. 2), then use feature-attribution to expose which evidence is systematically down-weighted. That turns "data is used unevenly" from an assertion into an auditable, quantified bias map of the planning corpus.

③ Build a mixed-methods QoL index that fuses interviews with multi-source spatial data

Extending this paperThe paper derives an emergent QoL framework from interviews and index reports but keeps the data streams largely separate and qualitative. Extending it: fuse the grounded-theory indicators with multi-source spatial data (POIs, mobile-phone/mobility traces, remote sensing of informal settlements) to produce a community-weighted, spatially explicit QoL index — preserving the lived priorities the paper surfaced while making them comparable across neighborhoods.

⑤ Test transferability across Africa's evolving urban systems

Extending this paperThe findings come from one country, South Africa. The paper's "conflicting rationalities" thesis invites a transferability test: replicate the grounded-theory framework across cities at different positions in Africa's urban hierarchy and growth trajectory, to see whether the temporal/responsibility/spatial divergence scales with city size, formality, or urbanization speed — making the claim continental rather than national.
