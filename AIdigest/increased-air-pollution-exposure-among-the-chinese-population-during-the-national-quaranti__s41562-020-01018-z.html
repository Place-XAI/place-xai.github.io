Increased air pollution exposure among the Chinese population during the national quarantine in 2020

Huizhong Shen · Guofeng Shen · Yilin Chen · Armistead G. Russell · Yongtao Hu · Xiaoli Duan · Wenjun Meng · Yang Xu · Xiao Yun · Baolei Lyu · Shunliu Zhao · Amir Hakami · Jianping Guo · Shu Tao · Kirk R. Smith Published 2021-01-04 DOI 10.1038/s41562-020-01018-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Despite a significant decrease in ambient PM2.5 during China's COVID-19 national quarantine, the total population-weighted PM2.5 exposure increased by 5.7 μg m−3 (95% CI: 1.2–11.0 μg m−3). The increase was driven by a nationwide urban-to-rural migration before the Spring Festival whose reversal was frozen by the quarantine, trapping a larger fraction of the population in rural households relying on solid fuels. The quarantine deepened health inequality: lower-income, rural, and heating-region populations bore a disproportionate increase in exposure, while urban populations experienced a decrease — a finding that challenges the narrative that lockdowns unambiguously improve public health.

Why It Matters

  • Counters the "clean-air dividend" narrative. The widely reported ambient PM2.5 drop during lockdown was reversed at the population level once indoor exposure, household energy use, and migration dynamics were accounted for — a critical corrective to policy optimism.
  • Integrates three usually-separate disciplines. The paper links air-quality modelling, real-time human mobility data, and indoor exposure modelling into one coherent population-weighted exposure (PWE) framework — a methodological template for any crisis-driven environmental assessment.
  • Reveals the solid-fuel trap as an inequality amplifier. Rural-household solid-fuel use — not ambient air quality — dominates national PWE and is the mechanism by which the quarantine worsened health equity, with provincial PWE changes ranging from −19.0 μg m−3 (Tianjin) to +32.5 μg m−3 (Inner Mongolia).
  • Evaluates a real policy in real time. The paper demonstrates that China's Clean Heating Plan (launched 2017) reduced the quarantine-induced PWE increase in the heating region by roughly half, providing the first quantitative evaluation of this national campaign under a natural experiment.
  • Immediate relevance for pandemic preparedness. Confining people to homes that have poor air quality may create a second health crisis; findings generalize to any society with large indoor pollution burdens.

Core Methods

  • Population-weighted exposure (PWE) framework: integrates ambient PM2.5 (outdoor), indoor PM2.5 (via indoor exposure model), and time-activity patterns to compute daily population-weighted PM2.5 exposure at county and provincial level.
  • Ensemble deep-learning data fusion: merges CMAQ model output with hourly CNEMC ground observations to produce daily, spatially continuous ambient PM2.5 fields at 12 km resolution across mainland China.
  • Dynamic migration modelling: uses census origin-destination data (2010, scaled to 2020 figures) temporally allocated using Baidu real-time province-to-province mobility intensities, classifying migrants into four urban/rural direction categories.
  • Decomposition analysis: isolates the separate contributions of (1) ambient air quality change, (2) population migration, (3) household energy consumption, and (4) time spent indoors to the total PWE change between P1 and P2.
  • Counterfactual scenario comparison: "2019 migration" scenario (normal Spring Festival migration, no quarantine time-activity change) and "no migration" scenario to attribute quarantine effects on PWE.
  • CMAQ-Adjoint v5.0 source attribution: adjoint sensitivity analysis attributes outdoor PM2.5 contributions to eight sectoral emission categories (residential, transport, industry, power, agriculture, solvents, fugitive dust, fires) at province level.
  • Compartment-based indoor exposure model: calculates indoor PM2.5 from fuel-type energy consumption, indoor–outdoor infiltration factors, and time spent in kitchen, living room, and bedroom; validated against 28 field studies and 30 rural households.
  • Monte Carlo uncertainty propagation: 1,000,000 iterations propagating uncertainty from fused PM2.5, migration intensity, time-activity patterns, household energy, and infiltration factors into 95% CI on PWE.
  • Regression analysis with interaction term: county-level OLS regression of quarantine-induced PWE change on log(per-capita income) × epidemic severity index to quantify income-stratified inequality.
  • Clean Heating Plan counterfactual: scenarios evaluate the plan's phase-out of 44.4% of northern solid-fuel use by end-2019, estimating avoided PWE increase and inequality reduction.

① Replace static mobility allocation with individual-trajectory PWE tracking

Extending this paperThe paper's migration model assigns population fractions to rural/urban areas using Baidu aggregate-flow data and census proportions — it never tracks individual trajectories. A direct extension: replace the province-to-province flow allocation with individual mobility sequences (GPS or mobile-phone call-detail records), enabling person-level PWE trajectories that capture intra-city spatial heterogeneity in addition to urban–rural shifts. The paper's own decomposition framework (ambient + indoor + time-activity) could be recast as a per-trajectory integration. This would sharpen the inequality analysis by revealing who (not just which province) experiences the highest exposure.

Added value to my researchMy work on spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and mobile-phone record analysis maps directly onto this extension. Coupling GPS trajectory data with ambient PM2.5 grids produces a mobility-corrected environmental health index analogous to my 2SFCA travel-time weighting — swapping service supply for pollution concentration and travel mode for time-activity pattern. This would feed directly into health geography accessibility studies that need individual-level rather than area-level exposure estimates.

② Apply the PWE decomposition framework to the urban–rural air pollution gap in China

Extending this paperThe paper's finding that rural solid-fuel use dominates national PWE is framed in the context of one quarantine episode. A generalization: apply the same decomposition framework (ambient PM2.5 + indoor HAP + time-activity) to China's long-run urbanization trajectory to map how rapid rural depopulation reshapes the national indoor–outdoor PM2.5 burden over decades, and how infrastructure investment (high-speed rail, clean-heating policy) modulates it. Recent evidence suggests that air pollution in rural areas is comparable to or worse than in urban areas, making the paper's indoor-exposure lens even more critical over the long run.

Added value to my researchThis generalizes my urban agglomeration and regional-collaboration work: instead of measuring GDP or job-housing mismatch, I could measure the spatiotemporal propagation of indoor-pollution inequality along the urban hierarchy, using panel data and my MGWR framework to detect non-stationary income–exposure relationships that the paper's OLS regression cannot capture.

③ Integrate income-stratified PWE inequality into a spatial justice metric for China's clean-energy transition

Extending this paperThe paper quantifies a significant negative correlation between per-capita income and quarantine-induced PWE change across provinces, and shows Clean Heating Plan reduced that inequality by ~30%. But the analysis is a snapshot and uses linear OLS. Extending it: build a time-series inequality index (Gini or Atkinson) on provincial PWE and decompose its trend into contributions from (a) ambient improvements, (b) solid-fuel phase-out, (c) demographic shifts, and (d) income growth — constructing an environmental Kuznets curve test for China's indoor-pollution transition over 2010–2025.

I could recast the Clean Heating Plan as a facility-siting problem — optimizing coal-replacement subsidy allocation to minimize the Gini of provincial PWE — a direct methodological extension of my EV-charging equity work.

⑤ Extend the indoor–outdoor integration to compound climate-pollution exposure under future extreme events

Extending this paperThe paper's PWE framework combines indoor and outdoor PM2.5 but treats temperature as a fixed background. An extension for future relevance: couple the paper's indoor exposure model with a heat-stress layer, since extreme heat drives people indoors in summer while solid-fuel heating drives indoor pollution in winter — two opposing mechanisms that interact under climate change. In a warming China where humid-heat risk is rising faster in rural areas, the compound indoor-heat + indoor-pollution burden could be assessed using the paper's energy-mix data alongside climate projections.

Added value to my researchThis extends my spatiotemporal analysis work beyond PM2.5 toward compound environmental health risk: linking the paper's indoor PM2.5 model with heat-humidity indicators produces a multi-hazard exposure index across the urban–rural gradient. My SIR modelling and Monte Carlo methods map naturally onto propagating joint uncertainty in temperature and pollution projections, and the resulting spatial risk surface would inform my health geography work on vulnerable-population targeting.
