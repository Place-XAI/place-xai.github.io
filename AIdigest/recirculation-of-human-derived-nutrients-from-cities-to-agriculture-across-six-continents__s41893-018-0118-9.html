Recirculation of human-derived nutrients from cities to agriculture across six continents

John T. Trimmer · Jeremy S. Guest Published 2018-08-14 DOI 10.1038/s41893-018-0118-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

For 56 of the world's largest cities across six continents, human-derived nutrients in urban sanitation spatially align with surrounding cropland demand at highly variable distances — spanning two orders of magnitude (6 km in Rome to 329 km in Boston). High local cropland density and compact urban form (particularly in Africa, Asia, and Europe) are the strongest predictors of short transport distances. Recovering nutrients in concentrated crystal products (struvite, ammonium sulfate) reduces transport energy requirements below those of synthetic fertilizer production even in long-distance cities, and nutrient-intensive crop shifts can further reduce distances — identifying a spatial roadmap to close urban nutrient cycles and advance circular economies.

Why It Matters

  • First global spatial audit of urban-to-agriculture nutrient flows. Prior studies were either single-city or single-nutrient; this is the first comparative analysis across 56 diverse cities on all inhabited continents simultaneously for nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium.
  • Closes a critical knowledge gap in circular economy planning. Recovering nutrients from wastewater is technically feasible, but "finding markets" for recovered products has been the missing link — this paper quantifies how far those markets are from each city.
  • Offsets unsustainable fertilizer dependence. Less than 15% of excreted nitrogen is currently recycled; results show Cairo's recoverable nutrients could have offset all of Egypt's phosphorus fertilizer imports and Tokyo/Osaka-Kobe together could replace >72% of Japan's nitrogen fertilizer imports.
  • Identifies an equity opportunity. Lower-income countries correlate with shorter nutrient distances, suggesting that nutrient recovery can specifically support smallholder farmers in Africa and Asia who face yield gaps from nutrient and water limitations.
  • Provides decision levers for policy and technology selection. Two actionable strategies are quantified: shifting to nutrient-intensive crops (up to 76% distance reduction in Rome) and recovering concentrated crystal products (energy below synthetic fertilizer production thresholds in every city tested).

Core Methods

  • City-extent delineation: contiguous areas from GPWv4 meeting a city-specific population-density threshold, calibrated so the enclosed population matches the UN WPP figure. Each city projected into a locally appropriate UTM coordinate system for accurate metric distance calculations.
  • Nutrient recovery estimation: city population × country-level per capita excretion rates (derived from protein/caloric supply) × technology-specific recovery efficiencies for N, P, K across multiple sanitation options (struvite precipitation, adsorption, ion exchange, ammonia stripping, direct urine/faeces reuse).
  • Iterative nutrient allocation: for each nutrient independently, an iterative algorithm allocates recoverable urban nutrients to the nearest cropland cell demanding that nutrient (constrained to national borders), tracking mass applied and distance travelled until all recoverable nutrients are exhausted. Path distances are computed via least-cost overland routing using GTOPO30 elevation and a land-water mask.
  • Summary statistics: mass-weighted average distance and distance required to utilize 5/25/50/75/95% of recoverable nutrients per city and nutrient. Spearman rank-order correlations and Kruskal–Wallis tests used to identify drivers (cropland density, city area, population density, GDP, coastal proximity, continent).
  • Sensitivity analyses: (1) centralized vs. decentralized sanitation (1 facility per city vs. 1 per 100 km² grid cell); (2) 2030 population/affluence scenario using UN 2030 projections; (3) altered crop patterns replacing existing crops with the most nutrient-intensive nationally important crop.
  • Transport energy analysis: for each recovery product (wastewater, sludge, urine, crystal products), product mass per tonne of nutrient × average city distance × ecoinvent energy factor, compared against synthetic fertilizer production energy benchmarks to identify energy-competitive recovery strategies.

① Replace overland Euclidean routing with food-system network flows to close the urban-rural nutrient loop

Extending this paperThe paper's path-distance algorithm routes nutrients from city sanitation facilities to the nearest cropland cell via terrain-corrected overland distance, without considering actual road infrastructure, supply-chain intermediaries, or where recovered products are more likely to be transported given existing logistics networks. A direct extension: overlay the nutrient allocation on actual food-supply-chain networks (freight road graphs, market locations) to produce feasibility-weighted transport distances that incorporate realistic logistics costs — closing the gap the paper's own Google Maps quality-control check identifies (7–21% underestimate). The food-system network framing also naturally integrates nutrient recycling into the broader food trade flows studied in telecoupled food systems.

② Model urban sprawl's impact on nutrient co-location using spatiotemporal population projections

Extending this paperThe paper holds urban extent and land use constant in its 2030 sensitivity analysis, acknowledging that urban expansion may displace approximately 2% of global cropland globally (with larger local impacts around cities in Africa and Asia). A direct extension: couple the nutrient distance model with dynamic urban land-use projections (e.g., SLEUTH, LandScan futures, or GHS built-up area time series) to simulate how urban sprawl shrinks the nearby cropland available for nutrient application — generating a time-series of nutrient-transport distances rather than a static snapshot. This would reveal which cities face rapidly worsening co-location due to urban expansion on agricultural land.

③ Integrate rooftop food production into the urban nutrient cycle to reduce transport burden

Extending this paperThe paper treats agriculture as entirely rural and surrounding the city, noting that urban agriculture "accounts for a small fraction of urban diets." However, if nutrient-intensive crops (vegetables, herbs) grown within city limits on rooftops or urban parcels were included as close-proximity demand sinks, the effective transport distance for a fraction of recoverable nutrients would collapse to near zero. A model extension: use building-level GIS footprints and solar/agronomic suitability to estimate urban rooftop absorption capacity, then re-run the iterative allocation algorithm with an inner urban-agriculture tier before dispatching to rural cropland.

④ Apply explainable spatial ML to identify which city characteristics actually predict nutrient-recycling feasibility

Extending this paperThe paper's statistical analysis uses Spearman rank correlation to identify associations between nutrient transport distance and city characteristics (cropland density, GDP, coastal proximity, city area, population). While it finds cropland density is the strongest correlate, no model quantifies the interaction among factors or identifies which combination of urban characteristics creates the best conditions for nutrient reuse. A direct extension: train a MGWR or GWR model (or gradient-boosted ensemble with SHAP explanations) on the paper's 56-city dataset — and extend to a larger city sample via global datasets — to produce a spatially varying importance map of what drives feasibility, revealing regional heterogeneity in causal structure.

⑤ Frame nutrient-recovery facility siting as an equity-efficiency optimization across urban and peri-urban areas

Extending this paperThe paper evaluates two bounding scenarios for sanitation system centralization (fully centralized — one facility per city — vs. fully decentralized — one per 100 km² grid cell) and shows that centralization almost always increases transport distances. However, it does not optimize facility locations for any objective. A direct extension: recast the paper's problem as a p-median / facility-location optimization — given a fixed number of recovery facilities, where should they be placed to minimize nutrient transport distance while also ensuring equitable sanitation access across intra-urban wealth gradients? The resulting multi-objective Pareto frontier would give city planners a concrete spatial investment plan.
