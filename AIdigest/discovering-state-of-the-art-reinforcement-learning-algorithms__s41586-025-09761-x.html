Discovering state-of-the-art reinforcement learning algorithms

Junhyuk Oh · Gregory Farquhar · Iurii Kemaev · Dan A. Calian · Matteo Hessel · Luisa Zintgraf · Satinder Singh · Hado van Hasselt · David Silver Published 2025-10-22 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09761-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Machines can now autonomously discover state-of-the-art reinforcement learning algorithms that outperform all manually designed rules. The method — meta-learning over a large population of agents across diverse, complex environments — produced DiscoRL, which surpassed every existing RL rule on the Atari benchmark and generalised to held-out benchmarks (ProcGen, DMLab-30, Crafter, NetHack, Sokoban) it had never seen during discovery. Crucially, performance and generality improve as more diverse environments are used, suggesting that future RL algorithms for advanced AI may be machine-discovered rather than manually designed.

Why It Matters

  • First machine-discovered RL rule to beat the human-designed state of the art. Decades of attempts at automated RL discovery had not matched hand-crafted algorithms; this breaks that barrier decisively on the canonical Atari benchmark (IQM 13.86, exceeding MuZero).
  • Strong zero-shot generalisation. Disco57, meta-trained only on Atari, topped all existing published methods on ProcGen and reached top-3 on the NetHack NeurIPS 2021 Challenge without domain-specific reward shaping — evidence that the discovered rule captures genuinely transferable learning dynamics.
  • Performance scales with data (environments) and compute. Disco103, discovered on 103 environments (Atari + ProcGen + DMLab-30), improved on every benchmark over Disco57, demonstrating a tractable scaling pathway analogous to data scaling in supervised learning.
  • Discovery of semantically novel predictions. Analysis reveals that DiscoRL invents prediction targets distinct from value functions and policy entropy — it identifies salient upcoming events, suggesting the method can uncover genuinely new RL concepts rather than merely re-parameterising known ones.
  • Open science. Meta-parameters of Disco103 and all meta-training/evaluation code are released as open source at github.com/google-deepmind/disco_rl, enabling reproducibility and community extension.

Core Methods

  • Meta-learning via meta-gradient: a meta-network (LSTM-based) parameterises the RL update rule — it receives agent trajectory outputs and returns prediction targets. Meta-parameters are updated via meta-gradients that backpropagate through 20 agent-update steps (sliding window).
  • Expressive search space: the agent outputs a policy (π), observation-conditioned predictions y(s), action-conditioned predictions z(s,a), action values q, and auxiliary policy prediction p — supporting any RL rule representable as target functions over these quantities.
  • Population-based discovery: 128–206 parallel agents, each with its own environment instance; meta-parameters updated synchronously by averaging per-agent Adam-normalised meta-gradients.
  • Meta-optimization stabilization: advantage normalization across environments, separate per-agent Adam optimizers before aggregation, entropy regularization of predictions (L_ent), and KL regularization of policy targets (L_KL) to prevent collapse.
  • Meta-RNN for lifetime statistics: an additional recurrent module unrolled forward across agent update steps (not time steps) allows the meta-network to adapt targets to the agent's learning dynamics over its lifetime.
  • Evaluation metric: interquartile mean (IQM) of human-normalized scores across tasks — a statistically robust metric recommended for RL benchmarking to reduce sensitivity to outlier games.
  • Ablation analysis: systematic removal of predictions (y/z/q/p) and replacement of Atari by toy grid-worlds verifies each component's contribution and the critical role of complex environments.

④ Integrate distributional RL targets into DiscoRL's meta-network for uncertainty-aware spatial prediction

Extending this paperThe paper's meta-network discovers prediction targets but currently focuses on expected returns. A corpus-motivated extension: incorporate distributional RL targets (predicting the full return distribution rather than its mean) into the meta-network's discoverable search space. The paper's flexible functional form — any target computable from trajectory data — could encompass distributional targets, allowing the discovery process to find rules that reason about risk and uncertainty rather than just expected performance.
