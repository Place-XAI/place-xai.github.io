Multiple stakeholders drive diverse interpretability requirements for machine learning in healthcare

Fergus Imrie · Robert Davis · Mihaela van der Schaar Published 2023-08-10 DOI 10.1038/s42256-023-00698-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Machine learning in healthcare must serve five distinct stakeholder groups — model developers, medical researchers, regulators, clinicians, and patients — each requiring fundamentally different types of explanation. Five classes of post-hoc interpretability (feature-based, example-based, concept-based, model-based, and counterfactual) map non-redundantly onto these stakeholder needs. Clinical adoption of ML will only be achieved when the community moves beyond feature-importance methods alone and matches explanation type to stakeholder goal, supported by an open-source interpretability library that makes the full XAI toolbox accessible.

Why It Matters

  • Reframes interpretability as a multi-stakeholder design problem. Instead of asking "is this model interpretable?", the paper asks "interpretable to whom and for what purpose?" — a conceptual shift that de-silos ML research from clinical reality.
  • Exposes a critical gap in current practice. Despite five distinct explanation classes existing, the medical community has almost exclusively adopted feature-based methods (SHAP, LIME), leaving counterfactual, example-based, concept-based, and model-based tools underutilized.
  • Directly addresses regulatory readiness. With FDA and EU GDPR Article 13 requiring model transparency as a condition of clinical deployment, the paper maps each stakeholder's compliance burden to the XAI type that meets it — giving ML developers a structured path to regulatory approval.
  • Ships actionable infrastructure. An open-source interpretability library (vanderschaarlab/Interpretability) with visualization tools lowers the adoption barrier for both the ML and clinical communities.
  • Patient agency through counterfactuals. The paper foregrounds patients as a primary stakeholder who can act on counterfactual explanations — "what would I need to change to improve my prognosis?" — elevating shared clinical decision-making.

Core Methods

  • Conceptual taxonomy: a structured classification of post-hoc XAI methods into five mutually distinct classes — feature-based, example-based, concept-based, model-based, and counterfactual — each with a unique information-theoretic role and use case.
  • Stakeholder analysis: identification and characterization of five primary stakeholder groups in clinical ML (model developers, medical researchers, regulators, clinicians, patients), with their objectives, information needs, and trust-building requirements articulated in structured tables (Tables 1–2).
  • Mapping framework: a systematic cross-mapping of explanation type to stakeholder requirement, surfacing under-served pairings (e.g., counterfactuals for patient agency; model-based explanations for regulatory equation requirements; concept-based for developer shortcut detection).
  • Case-study synthesis: review and re-analysis of published clinical ML case studies to illustrate where each XAI type has succeeded or failed, including shortcut-learning detection, biomarker discovery via feature selection, and diagnostic aid accuracy.
  • Software engineering: design and release of a unified Python library encapsulating all five XAI classes with a shared API and interactive visualization platform, enabling cross-method comparison on the same model.

Expanded Ideas — this paper's extensibility & added value to my research

① Translate the paper's five-explanation taxonomy into a geospatial XAI framework for spatial prediction models

Extending this paperThe paper maps five XAI classes onto five healthcare stakeholder groups but operates exclusively in the tabular/feature-space domain. Its taxonomy is never tested against spatial models — where features are not independent (spatial autocorrelation), local explanations must vary geographically, and counterfactuals must be spatially plausible. Extending the paper: apply its full five-class XAI toolkit to spatially explicit ML models (e.g., gradient boosting with geographic covariates), defining what each explanation type means when observations are embedded in space — e.g., a counterfactual for a high-disease-burden census tract must specify a geographically realizable intervention, not merely a feature perturbation.

Added value to my researchMy explainable ML pipeline for spatial health prediction (disease burden, accessibility, telehealth utilization) overwhelmingly relies on SHAP feature importance — exactly the feature-based monoculture the paper identifies as insufficient. This extension would add example-based explanations (show policy-makers which similar tracts have analogous risk profiles), model-based explanations (distill my MGWR/GBRT into symbolic equations linking density and access to health outcomes), and counterfactual explanations (what spatial configuration change — new clinic, transit route — most efficiently shifts a prediction), giving health-policy stakeholders all five lenses the paper argues for.

② Integrate the paper's counterfactual explanations with 2SFCA accessibility models for patient-facing intervention guidance

Extending this paperThe paper identifies patients as a primary XAI stakeholder who can benefit from counterfactual explanations — "what would need to be different to change my prognosis?" — but only addresses this in terms of modifiable patient covariates (smoking, diet). The paper does not address spatial access barriers as counterfactual dimensions: a patient's prognosis prediction might change if a primary-care provider were within a 15-minute travel window, or if a telehealth option were available. Extending the paper: incorporate spatial access variables into its counterfactual generation pipeline, so patient-facing explanations can specify both behavioral and geographic interventions.

Added value to my researchMy 2SFCA/R2SFCA service-area delineation work produces spatial access scores per zone but does not yet connect to patient-level ML predictions. This idea directly bridges the two: the R2SFCA access score for a patient's location becomes a feature in a clinical ML model, and counterfactual explanations reveal the access threshold needed to shift a clinical prediction — quantifying, for health-policy purposes, what spatial access improvement is clinically meaningful. This is a concrete and novel bridge between geospatial health geography and clinical XAI.

Cross-ref (corpus): Causal inference and counterfactual prediction in machine learning for actionable healthcare — 10.1038/s42256-020-0197-y

③ Extend the paper's fairness/bias discussion into spatially stratified fairness auditing for clinical ML

Extending this paperThe paper flags fairness and bias as key regulatory concerns and notes that XAI can "assess fairness and bias as well as understand their origin." However, the paper does not operationalize spatial fairness: models trained on hospital-system data may be biased not only along demographic axes (race, sex) but along geographic axes (urban vs. rural, inner-city vs. suburb), leading to systematically worse predictions for underserved populations. Extending the paper: augment its regulator-stakeholder section with a spatial fairness audit protocol that stratifies model errors and XAI attributions by geographic unit, using the paper's feature-based and concept-based methods to diagnose spatially differential model behavior.

Added value to my researchSpatial stratification of model fairness is directly relevant to my telehealth digital-divide and healthcare-accessibility research: models trained on urban-dense populations may underperform in rural or periurban areas, and the paper's XAI toolkit provides a principled basis for diagnosing this. Combining the paper's bias-auditing framework with my MGWR geographically weighted approach — where model coefficients vary spatially — produces a spatially explicit fairness map: where is the model unfair, and which spatial factors explain it?

④ Use the paper's model-based explanation class to distill urban health prediction models into interpretable spatial equations

Extending this paperThe paper describes model-based explanations — symbolic regression and associative classifiers — as converting black-box ML into closed-form equations or logic rules. The paper's own example compresses a 115-variable ML model into 3 clinically interpretable rules via an associative classifier. This is applied only to tabular/clinical data; the paper does not explore symbolic regression for spatially explicit prediction models, where the recovered equation might explicitly encode geographic structure (density gradients, distance decay, network topology). Extending the paper: apply its model-based XAI class to spatial ML models, with the goal of recovering closed-form spatial interaction functions — learning, rather than assuming, the distance-decay or density-threshold law.

Added value to my researchMy physics-informed graph learning agenda aims to bake prior spatial knowledge into GNN architectures. The paper's model-based class provides the inverse operation: extract the spatial physics already captured implicitly by a trained black-box model (e.g., GBRT predicting health outcomes from POI density, transit access, population density). Applying symbolic regression to my trained spatial models could reveal whether and how a power-law distance decay or linear density threshold is embedded in their learned representations — turning empirical model fit into theoretical spatial law, and feeding those equations back as physics constraints in the next model generation.

⑤ Build a stakeholder-stratified XAI evaluation protocol tailored to urban health and facility-access policy

Extending this paperThe paper argues that different stakeholders need different XAI outputs but does not provide a formal evaluation protocol for whether a given explanation actually satisfies a given stakeholder's requirement. The paper's Table 2 maps stakeholder questions to explanation types qualitatively; extending it: design a stakeholder-stratified XAI evaluation benchmark for public-health ML, operationalizing Table 2 as measurable criteria — e.g., does a counterfactual satisfy patients? (plausibility + user study score), does a model-based explanation satisfy regulators? (equation parsimony + fidelity to the black box), does a feature explanation satisfy clinicians? (consistency with medical knowledge). This converts the paper's normative framework into a testable, reproducible methodology.

Added value to my researchMy data-science and reproducibility agenda (open science / FAIR) requires a standard evaluation protocol for XAI outputs targeted at urban-health and facility-access policy stakeholders — city planners, public-health officials, patients, funding bodies. Applying the paper's five-stakeholder lens to my geospatial foundation model fine-tuning work would require defining, for each stakeholder, what a valid explanation of a geospatial prediction looks like — creating a reusable evaluation harness for GeoAI interpretability that is currently absent from the field, and that would itself constitute a publishable methodological contribution.
