Emergency mental health co-responders reduce involuntary psychiatric detentions in the USA

Thomas S. Dee · Jaymes Pyne Published 2025-11-17 DOI 10.1038/s41562-025-02339-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A co-responder programme pairing mental health professionals with police officers on qualified emergency calls reduced involuntary psychiatric detentions by 16.5% (370 fewer detentions over two years; b = −0.180, 95% CI −0.325 to −0.034) in San Mateo County, CA — with no detectably harmful effect on calls for service, criminal offences, or arrests. Incident-level analysis shows co-responders cut the per-incident probability of an involuntary detention by 11.5 percentage points, and the programme also appears to reduce future mental health emergencies, yielding estimated annual health-cost savings of US$300,000–US$800,000.

★

Why It Matters

  • First credibly causal evidence on co-responder programmes. Prior studies acknowledged an inability to establish causation; this preregistered study uses two complementary quasi-experimental designs — a difference-in-differences panel and an incident-level fixed-effects approach — to generate causal evidence at both the community and incident levels.
  • Quantifies a spillover benefit. Co-responders reduced not just the disposition of immediate incidents but also the future rate of mental health emergency calls — suggesting the programme addresses underlying crises rather than just managing acute episodes.
  • High policy relevance at scale. Involuntary psychiatric detention rates vary 33-fold across US states; police-initiated detentions are rising. This study offers the clearest evidence yet that co-responder reform can reduce inappropriate detentions without increasing crime or disorder.
  • Demonstrates feasibility for adoption. The CWCRT programme operated across 26 treatment neighbourhoods spanning 4 cities in a single US county, showing that the model is organizationally viable for mid-sized municipalities — lowering the barrier to replication.
  • Health-economic bottom line. Estimated savings of US$300,000–US$800,000 per year in emergency-department and hospitalization costs provide a concrete cost-effectiveness argument for policymakers and funders.

Core Methods

  • Difference-in-differences (DiD) — total-effects design: neighbourhood-by-month panel (N = 2,592); binary treatment indicator for CWCRT availability; neighbourhood and month–year fixed effects; conditional maximum likelihood Poisson and negative binomial estimators with bootstrapped standard errors clustered at the neighbourhood level.
  • Event-study specification: semi-dynamic DiD with leads (12 months pre-treatment) and lags (up to 10+ months post-treatment) to test the parallel-trends assumption and detect time-varying effects. A joint test that pre-treatment trends are equal across treatment and comparison neighbourhoods yields P = 0.57–0.59 (non-rejection supports the identifying assumption).
  • Incident-level fixed-effects design: linear probability model on 4,117 CWCRT-eligible incidents; fixed effects for neighbourhood, month–year, day of week, and hour of day; identifies the effect of co-responder dispatch exploiting within-cell variation from clinician unavailability.
  • Randomization inference: 1,000 permutations of treatment assignment within neighbourhoods; none produce a point estimate as extreme as the observed estimate in either design, implying randomization-inference P < 0.001.
  • Robustness checks: increasingly saturated fixed effects (neighbourhood × month–year, neighbourhood × day-of-week, etc.); Callaway–Sant'Anna-style reweighting to correct for tacit weighting in fixed-effects specifications; alternative count estimators (negative binomial, Poisson without clustering); busy vs. non-busy hour subsamples.
  • Statistical software: Stata 18; all tests two-tailed; study approved by Stanford IRB (protocol IRB-760).

① Spatial accessibility mapping of co-responder service gaps across US cities

Extending this paperThis paper evaluates a single county's co-responder programme. It does not ask where in the USA similar programmes are absent, under-resourced, or covering the wrong neighbourhoods. A direct extension: apply the paper's DiD causal framework across a multi-city panel by combining GIS catchment delineation of CWCRT-style programmes with 2SFCA-based accessibility scores measuring the ratio of co-responder clinician capacity to mental health emergency demand per census tract. This would transform a causal point estimate into a national spatial deployment map — identifying where rollout would have the highest expected detention-reduction payoff.

The "supply" layer is co-responder clinician coverage (shift hours × travel-time catchment); the "demand" layer is geolocated mental health call density (from 911 CAD data). ② Spatially heterogeneous treatment-effect estimation with GWR/MGWR

Extending this paperThe paper's DiD specification imposes a single global treatment coefficient (b = −0.180). Yet San Mateo County contains dense urban cores (Daly City, South San Francisco) alongside lower-density suburban areas — and the paper's own event-study evidence shows noisy pre-treatment trends, hinting at heterogeneity. A natural extension: estimate the DiD treatment effect locally using a geographically weighted regression (GWR) or MGWR framework, treating each neighbourhood as a bandwidth-weighted local regression unit. This would reveal whether co-responder effectiveness varies with neighbourhood-level covariates such as poverty rate, existing psychiatric facility proximity, or residential density — turning the global estimate into a spatial surface.

Added value to my researchMGWR is a core method in my toolbox. Applying it to a preregistered policy DiD is a compelling methodological contribution: it demonstrates that spatial non-stationarity is not merely a modelling nuance but a policy-actionable signal — high-effect neighbourhoods warrant accelerated rollout, while low-effect ones warrant programme redesign. It also directly extends my work on spatially varying health determinants.

③ Predicting co-responder dispatch need using explainable machine learning on call characteristics

Extending this paperThe paper shows that co-responders reduce detentions but also documents a critical bottleneck: co-responders are sometimes unavailable (the incident-level variation exploited for identification). The paper cannot tell dispatchers which incoming calls most need a clinician. An extension: train an XGBoost or SHAP-interpretable gradient-boosting model on the 4,117 call-level records — using call type, neighbourhood, time-of-day, prior-call history, and residential density — to score each incoming call's probability of resulting in a detention absent a co-responder. This probabilistic triage score would optimize clinician allocation under capacity constraints.

Added value to my researchThis directly applies my explainable GeoAI methods (spatial ML + SHAP) to emergency resource allocation. The SHAP output would decompose which spatial and temporal features drive detention risk — connecting to my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation methods — while the allocation model parallels my EV charging-station efficiency-vs-equity optimization work. It also opens a collaboration vector with mental health systems using 911 CAD data.

④ Adding urban nature exposure as a structural confounder and preventive layer

Extending this paperThe paper focuses entirely on the acute emergency response pathway, with no account of the built or natural environment as a moderator of mental health crisis frequency. Yet the corpus shows that greenness exposure dose-dependently reduces mental health burden and emergency utilization. An extension of this paper: add neighbourhood-level green space coverage (NDVI, park area per capita, tree canopy) as a time-varying confounder in the DiD specification and, in a second step, test whether CWCRT's spillover benefit (reduced future crisis calls) is larger in low-greenness neighbourhoods — providing causal evidence for the synergistic value of pairing crisis response reform with urban nature investment.

Added value to my researchThis connects my urban analytics theme (healthy cities, green infrastructure) with spatial health geography and causal policy evaluation. Using remote sensing-derived NDVI as a time-varying covariate in a policy DiD is methodologically novel and aligns with my multi-source data fusion methods (remote sensing + administrative records). It would also generate SHAP attribution showing how much of the neighbourhood-level detention rate is explained by greenness vs. socioeconomic vs. programme factors.

⑤ Scaling CWCRT-style evidence with graph-learning on intercity healthcare networks

Extending this paperThe paper's authors caution against over-generalization: "successfully replicating and scaling… is likely to hinge on key design and implementation details." Yet the study cannot say which structural features of a city or health system predict replication success. A multi-city extension: construct a directed intercity and intra-city mental health care network graph (nodes = hospitals, community mental health centres, police precincts; edges = patient referral flows, co-responder deployment routes) and train a physics-informed GNN that predicts post-CWCRT detention reduction rates from network topology and node attributes. This transforms the paper's single causal estimate into a transferable prediction engine for programme adoption planning.

Added value to my researchThis is the most direct connection to my physics-informed graph learning agenda. The mental health emergency network is a real-world test case for my spatiotemporal graph operators: patient flow conservation laws and capacity constraints serve as physics priors, while CWCRT assignment is the intervention node. Combined with the intercity telemedicine network literature from the corpus, this opens a GeoAI-for-health-systems research direction where graph-based predictions guide where and when co-responder investment maximizes population-level impact.
