Advancing operational global aerosol forecasting with machine learning

Ke Gui · Xutao Zhang · Huizheng Che · Lei Li · Yu Zheng · Linchang An · Yucong Miao · Hujia Zhao · Oleg Dubovik · Brent Holben · Jun Wang · Pawan Gupta · Elena S. Lind · Carlos Toledano · Hong Wang · Zhili Wang · Yaqiang Wang · Xiaomeng Huang · Kan Dai · Xiangao Xia · Xiaofeng Xu · Xiaoye Zhang Published 2026-03-04 DOI 10.1038/s41586-026-10234-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

AI-GAMFS — a machine-learning system combining a vision transformer and U-Net backbone trained on 42 years of aerosol reanalysis (MERRA-2) — delivers global 5-day, 3-hourly aerosol forecasts in under 1 minute, outperforming the Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service (CAMS) and four physics-based regional dust models in forecasting aerosol optical depth and dust components. Against GEOS-FP it achieves lower global AOD RMSE, comparable dust skill, and substantially improved surface aerosol component forecasts over the USA and China — at approximately 360 times lower computational cost, marking a step-change in operational environmental forecasting.

Why It Matters

  • First operational ML system for global multi-component aerosol forecasting. Prior ML work targeted single metrics (total AOD) or depended on NWP inputs; AI-GAMFS simultaneously forecasts 12 aerosol optical and surface-concentration variables at global scale.
  • 360× speed-up over physics-based baselines. A 5-day global forecast takes ~39 seconds on a single GPU versus 4–6 hours for GEOS-FP, enabling near-real-time update cycles and reducing hardware barriers to operational deployment.
  • Outperforms dedicated dust models across East Asia. Against FMI-SILAM, CMA-CUACE/Dust, JMA-MASINGAR and KMA-ADAM3, AI-GAMFS improves DUAOD spatial correlation by 4.9–199.7% at 120-hour lead time, and can warn of dust-emission intensification in the Gobi Desert 3–4 days in advance — a capability beyond current regional models.
  • Directly relevant to public health and climate policy. Accurate aerosol forecasting reduces mortality from dust storms and wildfires, informs air-quality advisories, and reduces the dominant aerosol-related uncertainty in climate projections.
  • Fully operational and open. AI-GAMFS has been live on the China Science and Technology Cloud since 20 January 2025, with 8-times-daily updates freely accessible; dust forecasts are also distributed via the WMO Early Warning platform.

Core Methods

  • Architecture — Vision Transformer + U-Net backbone: three modules: (1) cube embedding — 3D spatiotemporal feature extraction from the input variable tensor; (2) vision transformer — multiheaded global self-attention capturing non-local aerosol–meteorology interactions; (3) cube unembedding — deconvolution/upsampling to original spatial resolution, with skip connections. ~1.2 billion parameters per base model.
  • Relay forecasting strategy: four base models (3-h, 6-h, 9-h, 12-h lead times) trained independently with identical configurations; at inference, chained in relay order prioritizing longer-lead models to minimize iteration count and error accumulation. Reduces average RMSE at 120-h lead by 15.1% over single 3-h autoregressive rollout.
  • Training: 80 epochs per base model (~10 days on 8× L40 GPUs); Adam optimizer (β1=0.9, β2=0.999), cosine annealing LR from 3×10−4, MAE loss function, 32-bit float, dropout 0.15. Rolling training on consecutive MERRA-2 time-step pairs.
  • Operational initialization: real-time GEOS-FP analysis fields bilinearly interpolated to 0.5°×0.625° grid; 5-day forecast in ~39 s on a single L40 GPU (vs. 4–6 h for GEOS-FP).
  • Evaluation metrics: latitude-weighted RMSE and spatial Pearson R against MERRA-2 (gridded); site-scale RMSE and R against AERONET, CARSNET, IMPROVE, EPA-CSN and CAWNET observations. Quadratic polynomial interpolation used to derive AOD at 550 nm from multi-wavelength AERONET data.
  • Stratified cross-validation: training set (1980–2021) partitioned into 7 contiguous 6-year subsets with iterative leave-one-out validation to confirm temporal robustness.

&circone; Extend AI-GAMFS from deterministic to probabilistic aerosol forecasts

Extending this paperAI-GAMFS delivers a single deterministic trajectory and acknowledges growing uncertainty with lead time — but provides no ensemble or confidence interval. A direct extension: equip AI-GAMFS with a probabilistic output head (e.g., a diffusion-based generative layer or multi-member ensemble spread mechanism) so each 3-hourly aerosol map carries calibrated uncertainty bounds. This would let public-health authorities issue probability-of-exceedance alerts for dust storms or wildfire smoke rather than binary warnings. The current relay architecture already separates model uncertainty (relay configuration choice) from initial-condition sensitivity — disentangling these into an explicit uncertainty budget is a tractable next step aligned with the probabilistic turn now standard in ML weather forecasting.

Added value to my researchCalibrated aerosol uncertainty fields are the environmental input layer for my spatial health-risk exposure models. Having forecast uncertainty envelopes on pollution concentrations allows propagation through a 2SFCA-style exposure–health model, moving from point predictions to risk distributions across census tracts — directly advancing my health geography work toward uncertainty-aware policy analysis and informing decisions about when to issue population advisories.

② Add a learned downscaling module to generate sub-city-block aerosol fields from AI-GAMFS 50-km output

Extending this paperAI-GAMFS operates at ~50 km — appropriate for regional early warnings but too coarse for intra-urban exposure mapping. A targeted extension: append a learned statistical downscaling module that uses AI-GAMFS 50-km forecasts as boundary conditions and fuses high-resolution land-use, road-network, building-height and localized emission-inventory features to produce 1-km (or finer) aerosol concentration fields. The MERRA-2 training chain already ingests satellite retrievals; the downscaling step could additionally assimilate dense ground-sensor networks (CARSNET, IMPROVE) via data-fusion layers — without rebuilding the forecasting backbone. This would close the gap between global-scale forecasting and local air-quality management without the cost of a full-resolution reanalysis.

③ Embed physicochemical inductive biases into AI-GAMFS via a physics-informed graph architecture

Extending this paperThe authors explicitly flag embedding physicochemical constraints as a key future direction, but the current system is purely data-driven. A concrete extension: replace the global self-attention grid with a physics-informed graph neural network where edges encode known aerosol transport pathways (prevailing wind trajectories, deposition rates, source-receptor relationships) and node features carry species mass-conservation constraints. The relay architecture’s modularity makes this tractable — each base model can be independently upgraded. The paper’s own finding that sea-salt (SS) forecasts degrade beyond 2-day lead (due to wind-speed accuracy) is precisely the gap that aerodynamic priors could close: encoding Stokes-drag deposition physics into graph edges would constrain SS dispersion independent of data-driven wind errors. Stratospheric dynamics, shown in the corpus to modulate long-range dust transport on multi-week timescales, could also be encoded as physically motivated long-range edges.

Added value to my researchThis aligns directly with my physics-informed graph learning research agenda: the same operator encoding atmospheric transport priors (mass conservation, wind-driven advection) can be repurposed as an inductive-bias encoder for spatiotemporal prediction in urban contexts — e.g., encoding traffic-flow conservation into a pollution-dispersion GNN for urban air-quality estimation. The aerosol domain provides a rigorous, well-labeled test bed for such methods before deployment in data-sparser urban settings.

Cross-ref (corpus): Stratospheric impacts on dust transport and air pollution in West Africa and the Eastern Mediterranean — 10.1038/s41467-022-35403-1

④ Couple AI-GAMFS aerosol forecasts to human-mobility models for dynamic population exposure estimation

Extending this paperAI-GAMFS forecasts where aerosols will be, but not who will be exposed. Extending the system: couple its 3-hourly aerosol concentration forecasts with a data-driven human-mobility model (learned from mobile-phone records or satellite imagery) to produce dynamic population-weighted exposure forecasts — a spatiotemporally resolved dose surface rather than a static ambient concentration map. This is especially powerful for wildfire smoke (BCAOD, OCAOD) and dust-storm (DUAOD) events where populations may move, shelter or evacuate. The AI-GAMFS 8-times-daily update cycle matches the timescale of daily activity patterns, making coupling technically straightforward. The result would be an integrated warning system that outputs both the aerosol hazard and the human exposure simultaneously.

Added value to my researchDynamic population exposure is the missing link between my GeoAI and health geography work streams. My 2SFCA models treat population as static; coupling with AI-GAMFS aerosol forecasts and mobility data would yield a time-varying respiratory-risk accessibility surface — identifying which neighborhoods face the worst pollution burden when residents are most exposed (morning commute during a dust event) and informing precision public-health advisories and telehealth intervention targeting.

⑤ Use AI-GAMFS component-resolved forecasts as explainable feature layers for urban emission-source attribution

Extending this paperAI-GAMFS disaggregates AOD into 5 components (sulfate, dust, BC, OC, sea salt) — a source-type resolution unavailable in most operational systems. A direct extension: feed these component-resolved 5-day forecasts as input features to an explainable ML attribution model (SHAP-augmented gradient boosting or interpretable attention transformer) relating each component’s forecast trajectory to downstream health outcomes (hospital admissions, mortality) and identifying which emission-source types dominate risk in each region and season. The paper already shows BC improvements are concentrated in the western US (wildfire zone) and sulfate improvements in the eastern US (anthropogenic zone) — providing a natural spatial stratification for such attribution. This turns a forecasting system into a policy-attribution tool.
