Learning is inhibited by heat exposure, both internationally and within the United States

R. Jisung Park · A. Patrick Behrer · Joshua Goodman Published 2020-10-05 DOI 10.1038/s41562-020-00959-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Heat exposure during school days causally reduces the rate of learning: each additional school day above 26.7 °C lowers test scores by 0.18–0.22% of a standard deviation internationally (PISA, 58 countries) and 0.07% domestically (SEDA, 12,000+ US districts). The effect is concentrated entirely on school days (not weekends or summer), confirming a learning-time mechanism. Impacts are two to three times larger for lower-income and minority students, meaning climate change will widen existing achievement and economic-mobility gaps.

Why It Matters

  • Upgrades climate-damage accounting. Most integrated assessment models measure climate impacts as level effects on GDP; this paper documents a growth-rate effect working through human capital accumulation — implying that existing social cost of carbon estimates are structurally understated.
  • Identifies a universal physiological channel. By separately estimating school-day vs. non-school-day heat, the paper isolates a cognitive-inhibition mechanism independent of agricultural shocks, nutrition, or incentive effects — applicable from Vietnam to the American South.
  • Reveals regressive climate burdens within wealthy nations. Even in the highly air-conditioned United States, Black and Hispanic students and those in lower-income districts bear three times the learning loss of white, higher-income peers — linking climate stress directly to longstanding racial/ethnic achievement gaps.
  • Provides policy-actionable magnitudes. A school year with 30 extra hot days reduces learning by ~2.1 standard deviations, equivalent to cutting teacher quality by 20% — translating temperature data into the language of education policy investment.
  • Dual-scale validation strategy. The combination of 58-country quasi-experimental evidence with near-universal US district-level data (270 million+ test records) creates a uniquely robust causal scaffold.

Core Methods

  • Quasi-experimental panel design: exploit year-to-year within-geography variation in temperature — plausibly random conditional on geography and year fixed effects — to identify causal effects on learning, avoiding cross-sectional confounds correlated with average climate.
  • Temperature binning: count school days per 5.5 °C bin (–17.7 to 60 °C) for each geography-year; regress test scores on bin counts following the canonical binned-temperature regression framework (Deschênes & Greenstone 2007 style).
  • Geography and year fixed effects: absorb all time-invariant country/district characteristics and global macro trends; continent-specific time trends in PISA regressions guard against spurious warming-trend confounds.
  • School vs. non-school day decomposition: split hot days into school-day weekdays, school-year weekends, and summer vacation days to test whether learning-time disruption — rather than broader economic or agricultural shocks — drives the result.
  • Heterogeneity analysis: stratify by per-capita income (above/below sample mean), subsidized lunch rate (>50% / ≤50%), student race/ethnicity (Black, Hispanic, non-Hispanic white), and grade band (elementary vs. middle school).
  • Robustness checks: controls for rice-growing-season heat (agricultural channel test), exam-day temperature, precipitation, demographic composition changes, and district school-funding levels; country-specific trends (Supplementary Table 9).
  • Clustered standard errors: at the geography level (country for PISA, district for SEDA) to account for serial correlation in temperature shocks over time.

② From annual temperature bins to fine-grained spatiotemporal heat-dose curves

Extending this paperThe paper's key causal variable is a simple annual count of days above 26.7 °C. This collapses considerable structure: the timing of heat within the school year, the intensity distribution above threshold, heat persistence (consecutive hot days), and nighttime temperatures that disrupt sleep. Extending the paper with a heat-dose response curve that accounts for cumulative exposure timing, day-sequence autocorrelation, and sub-daily heat patterns would sharpen effect estimates and reveal whether critical windows (e.g., exam-month heat) dominate.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal kernel density estimation methods can model heat-exposure density over the school calendar as a continuous spatio-temporal field rather than a binary day-count. Combined with my Monte Carlo simulation experience, this enables probabilistic heat-dose exceedance maps that update annually and can feed predictive models of district-level learning loss under climate projections.

③ Integrate mobility data to test teacher absenteeism as a mediating mechanism

Extending this paperThe paper shows that only school-day heat reduces learning but explicitly notes it cannot separate student-side effects (impaired cognition, absenteeism) from teacher-side effects (reduced teaching quality, teacher absenteeism). With mobile-phone or GPS trajectory data, one can observe population-level mobility to school buildings on hot days across income strata, directly testing the absenteeism channel the paper flags as a priority for future work — and decomposing the effect between supply (teacher) and demand (student) sides of the classroom.

⑤ Shade provision and green infrastructure as targeted adaptation: from observation to optimal placement

Extending this paperThe paper argues that investments in physical learning environments (electric infrastructure, air conditioning) are warranted but acknowledges the climate externality of energy-intensive cooling. A greener extension: combine its achievement-damage coefficients with street-level shade and urban heat simulations to estimate the cooling value of tree canopy and green infrastructure around schools. This would enable a cost–benefit optimization of shade investment per unit of learning loss avoided — extending the paper's policy conclusions toward nature-based solutions.
