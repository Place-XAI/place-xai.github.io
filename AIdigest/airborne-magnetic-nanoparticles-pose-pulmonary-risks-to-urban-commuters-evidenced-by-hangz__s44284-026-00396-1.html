Airborne magnetic nanoparticles pose pulmonary risks to urban commuters evidenced by Hangzhou and Zhengzhou subways

Weijun Li · Yunfeng Zhao · Hongyu Wang · Hongliu Cai · Lingtong Huang · Keliang Li · Liang Xu · Xiyao Chen · Shupeng Zhu · Yinxiao Zhang · Zongbo Shi · Jianmin Chen · Yinon Rudich · Xiaokun Ding Published 2026-02-23 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00396-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Subway air carries a substantial fraction of iron-oxide magnetic nanoparticles generated by wheel–rail and brake–wheel friction. Two forms dominate: α-Fe2O3 in settled surface dust and airborne Fe3O4 particles with a magnetic core wrapped in an amorphous SiO2 shell. Critically, the same nanoscale Fe3O4 particles were detected in the lung tissue of subway commuters, and controlled mouse inhalation experiments showed they induce pronounced lung injury — establishing friction-derived magnetic nanoparticles as a tangible public-health risk in urban transit.

Why It Matters

  • Closes the inhalation-to-injury loop. Most subway-air studies stop at measuring concentrations; this one traces the same Fe3O4 nanoparticle from the platform, into human lung tissue, and through to demonstrable injury in a mammalian model — rare end-to-end evidence of a specific urban pollutant.
  • Identifies a precise, actionable source. Pinpointing wheel–rail and brake–wheel friction as the origin tells operators exactly where to intervene (materials, braking systems, ventilation) rather than treating "subway dust" as a black box.
  • Highlights a structurally distinctive hazard. The core–shell (Fe3O4@SiO2) architecture is unusual; its magnetic, redox-active core may drive toxicity differently from generic PM2.5, so mass-based standards alone may understate the risk.
  • Speaks to billions of daily riders. Metro systems are central to low-carbon urban mobility; quantifying a health cost of using them informs how cities make transit both clean and safe to breathe.

Core Methods

  • Single-particle microscopy & phase analysis: TEM/HRTEM + SEM with EDS mapping and SAED to determine size distribution, core–shell morphology, elemental composition, and crystalline phase of individual subway nanoparticles.
  • Phase discrimination: distinguish settled α-Fe2O3 (surface dust) from airborne Fe3O4@amorphous-SiO2 core–shell nanoparticles.
  • Source attribution: dust-sample milling under varied conditions and car-braking experiments reproduce friction-derived Fe particles, linking them to wheel–rail and brake–wheel wear.
  • Human deposition detection: microscopy of commuter lung tissue to confirm inhalation and pulmonary deposition of Fe3O4 nanoparticles, contrasted against rural-population lung tissue.
  • Causal toxicology: controlled mouse inhalation exposure with histopathological assessment of lung injury, establishing a cause–effect link rather than mere association.

① From four sites to a network-wide, mobility-weighted exposure surface

Extending this paperThe study fixes a serious hazard but at only four sampling sites on three lines; it cannot say which riders accumulate the most Fe3O4. A direct extension: combine the paper's particle measurements with smart-card / mobile-phone trajectory data to build a network-wide, time-resolved personal-dose surface — weighting platform/in-car concentrations by realized passenger flows, dwell times and transfer hubs, so the friction hotspots map onto the populations actually exposed.

② Trade the friction hazard against the modal-shift benefit of metros

Extending this paperThe paper documents a health cost of riding the subway but does not net it against the well-known benefit — metros pull people out of cars and cut street-level pollution. Extending it: build a net-health ledger that places the in-cabin Fe3O4 dose on one side and the avoided ambient PM/NO2 exposure from reduced car use on the other, per line and per rider segment, to find when mitigation (ventilation, low-wear braking) flips the balance.
