Seeing racial avoidance on New York City streets

Bryce J. Dietrich · Melissa L. Sands Published 2023-05-04 DOI 10.1038/s41562-023-01589-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using publicly available NYC traffic camera feeds combined with a randomized field experiment across 3,552 pedestrians in two Manhattan neighbourhoods, this study documents systematic racial avoidance on city sidewalks: non-Black pedestrians (93% of the sample) give a statistically significant wider berth — on average ~4 inches (~3.4% of sidewalk width) — to phenotypically Black male confederates compared with phenotypically white non-Hispanic male confederates. The effect is consistent with and without covariate controls, and holds across both Midtown and the Upper East Side locations, offering among the largest-scale field-experimental evidence to date of implicit racial bias physically manifested in everyday urban space.

Why It Matters

  • Large-scale, naturalistic measurement of implicit racial bias. Prior studies relied on lab settings or small-scale qualitative methods; this paper produces an unobtrusive, high-resolution field measurement across 3,552 real urban pedestrians, making results far more generalizable.
  • Validates a surveillance-camera-based research paradigm. Openly accessible traffic-camera feeds are repurposed as a social-science observatory — a scalable, low-cost method replicable in any camera-equipped city worldwide.
  • Documents a measurable psychological and social harm. Chronic pedestrian avoidance experienced by Black individuals probably imposes a real psychological toll and may amplify implicit threat perceptions by law enforcement, with downstream consequences for policing and public safety.
  • Connects micro-scale behaviour to macro-scale segregation. The experiment shows that even in dense, diverse New York City — where inter-racial encounters are unavoidable — racial bias manifests in physical space, reinforcing structural patterns of residential and social segregation.
  • Opens new avenues for replication and extension. The standardized SPD metric and agent-based validation framework let future researchers replicate the study with different confederate demographics (gender, age, SES) or in any city with publicly accessible camera infrastructure.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Study sites
Two Manhattan neighbourhoods — Midtown East and the Upper East Side — selected for camera visibility, sidewalk width, and pedestrian volume. Sidewalk widths: 126 inches (Midtown) and 96.5 inches (Upper East Side).
Camera data
NYC Department of Transportation (NYC DOT) publicly available traffic camera feeds (nyctmc.org). Images recorded over 2 weekdays (~225 min of video per site, 5 × 45-min blocks per day), used for pedestrian tracking.
Experimental design
Randomized field experiment with 3 conditions block-randomized within 45-min blocks: Black confederates, white non-Hispanic confederates, and a no-confederate baseline. Pre-registered on EGAP (ID: 20170616AA) and OSF (osf.io/vtqez).
Pedestrian sample
N = 3,552 total pedestrians tracked (776 at Upper East Side, 4,632 captured at Midtown from which 3,419 were used in the main analysis); 93% phenotypically non-Black. Manual tracking via ImageJ (Fiji).
Measurement
Standardized Pedestrian Deviation (SPD): deviation of each pedestrian from confederate location as a proportion of sidewalk width. Validated using agent-based modelling and 3D scene reconstruction to account for camera-perspective distortions.
Outcome variable
Pixel-based distance measurements converted to inches using known sidewalk widths. Main statistic: pedestrians deviate on average ~3.4% of sidewalk width (~4 inches) more in the presence of Black versus white confederates.
Replication data
All data and R code (v4.1.0) available at Harvard Dataverse (DVN/Y9QEUK).

Core Methods

  • Randomized field experiment: three conditions (Black confederates / white confederates / baseline) block-randomized within 45-min time blocks to control for time-of-day variation in pedestrian flow; confederates trained to behave identically across conditions.
  • Unobtrusive passive measurement: NYC DOT public camera feeds used to record pedestrian movements without consent — justified by the public nature of the space and the fact that individual faces cannot be discerned.
  • Standardized Pedestrian Deviation (SPD): for each pedestrian frame, pixel distances to confederate and to a neutral baseline object are computed; their difference, normalized by sidewalk pixel width, gives a perspective-distortion-corrected deviation metric.
  • Agent-based modelling & 3D validation: simulated camera perspectives (including a "bird's-eye view" equivalent) used to confirm that SPD yields equivalent results regardless of the camera angle — a novel perspective-correction validation strategy.
  • OLS regression with bootstrapped clustered standard errors: average treatment effects (ATE) estimated via bivariate OLS; covariate-adjusted treatment effects (CTE) add pedestrian race, gender, and time-block fixed effects. Standard errors bootstrapped within 15-min randomization clusters using wild block bootstrap to account for within-cluster dependence.
  • Pedestrian race coding: on-site research assistant identified phenotypically Black pedestrians in real time; independently validated for one site using recorded video with a second coder, and inter-coder reliability reported in the Supplementary Information.

① Replace manual SPD coding with an automated computer-vision pipeline

Extending this paperThe study's bottleneck is manual pedestrian tracking in ImageJ: two research assistants coded all frames, limiting throughput to 225 min of video per site and introducing human subjectivity in race coding. A direct extension would couple the paper's SPD metric with modern pose estimation and multi-object tracking (e.g., OpenPose, ByteTrack) to automate distance extraction, and a phenotype classification model (trained with careful ethical guardrails) to automate race coding — enabling replication across hundreds of camera feeds city-wide rather than two selected sites. The surveillance camera prevalence work in our corpus maps exactly which NYC neighbourhoods already have public-facing cameras suitable for this pipeline.

Added value to my researchThis vision-to-behaviour pipeline is a concrete GeoAI application: a spatiotemporal kernel density layer over automated avoidance events could produce a fine-grained racial-avoidance heatmap of NYC — directly analogous to the spatial accessibility surfaces I compute with R2SFCA, and actionable for public health policy around street safety and mental health burden.

② Embed the experiment within a city-scale implicit bias geography

Extending this paperThe paper measures avoidance at two Manhattan corners; it cannot say whether the effect is stronger in more segregated or more homogeneous neighbourhoods. A natural extension: replicate the camera-experiment protocol across dozens of neighbourhoods, stratified by local racial composition and segregation index, and test whether the SPD treatment effect scales with the implicit bias scores documented at the city level. The corpus paper on implicit racial biases across US cities provides city-level IAT scores that could serve as ecological predictors in a multilevel model linking neighbourhood racial context → implicit bias → sidewalk avoidance magnitude.

Added value to my researchThis multilevel spatial framework maps directly onto my MGWR/GWR toolkit: fitting spatially varying coefficients for the neighbourhood-level predictors of avoidance would reveal where the bias-to-behaviour link is strongest — informing equity-focused urban health interventions and extending the spatial analysis of social determinants of mental health that underlies my health geography work.

Cross-ref (corpus): Implicit racial biases are lower in more populous more diverse and less segregated US cities — 10.1038/s41467-024-45013-8

③ Link sidewalk avoidance to GPS-measured experienced segregation

Extending this paperThe paper measures a momentary inter-personal distance; it cannot tell us whether individuals who avoid Black confederates also systematically avoid Black-majority neighbourhoods in their daily mobility. A powerful extension: cross-reference the SPD avoidance signal with GPS-derived experienced segregation trajectories of the same urban population. Do pedestrians who deviate most also visit less racially diverse spaces? The corpus offers two relevant mobility-segregation datasets — one on income segregation in US cities, another specifically on NYC foot-traffic patterns — that could provide the ecological GPS baseline needed to answer this question.

Added value to my researchFor my spatiotemporal analysis work, this proposes a novel cross-modal segregation metric: combining micro-scale camera-measured avoidance with macro-scale GPS mobility data creates a richer multi-resolution measure of experienced segregation — potentially applicable as an outcome variable in my health geography models where social isolation mediates health disparities.

Cross-ref (corpus): Urban mobility and the experienced isolation of students — 10.1038/s44284-023-00007-3

④ Test whether neighbourhood socioeconomic mixing moderates avoidance

Extending this paperThe authors find no significant difference in SPD between their two sites (Midtown commercial vs. Upper East Side residential), but their two-site design has limited power to detect neighbourhood-level moderation. Extending the experiment to a wider range of socioeconomic contexts — using the ring-and-pocket geography of US urban segregation as a sampling frame — would allow a proper test of whether the avoidance effect is attenuated in high-mixing pockets versus amplified in socioeconomically segregated rings. The corpus entry on socioeconomic mixing in US cities provides exactly such a typology, enabling a spatially stratified experimental design.

Added value to my researchThis experiment-meets-spatial-typology design generalizes to my urban analytics work on healthy cities and 15-minute neighbourhoods: understanding how micro-scale social behaviour varies across urban morphology types (rings vs. pockets) would inform where public-space investment could most effectively reduce bias — connecting social equity with physical accessibility.

Cross-ref (corpus): US cities are defined by rings and pockets with limited socioeconomic mixing — 10.1038/s44284-025-00350-7

⑤ Track how COVID-era mobility disruptions altered inter-racial encounter diversity — and what a "recovery" means for avoidance

Extending this paperThe paper's 2017 experiment predates COVID-19, a period when pedestrian density, mobility patterns, and neighbourhood composition of street users shifted dramatically. The corpus shows that pandemic mobility restrictions substantially reduced income diversity of urban encounters. An extension: replicate the camera-experiment in 2023–2025 to ask whether post-COVID behavioural norms (greater spatial distancing, changed pedestrian compositions) amplified or attenuated the racial-avoidance SPD effect — and whether recovery of encounter diversity tracks recovery of the avoidance baseline.

Added value to my researchThis longitudinal extension connects to my spatiotemporal analysis toolkit: applying spatiotemporal kernel density estimation to camera-extracted avoidance events before, during, and after COVID would yield a time series of spatial racial-avoidance intensity — a new type of public health surveillance signal that I could fuse with mobile phone and social media data streams in my multi-source data fusion work.
