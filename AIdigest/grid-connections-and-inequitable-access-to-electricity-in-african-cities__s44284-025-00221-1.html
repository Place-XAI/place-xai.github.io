Grid connections and inequitable access to electricity in African cities

Jessica Kersey · Civian Kiki Massa · June Lukuyu · Judith Mbabazi · Jay Taneja · Daniel M. Kammen · Veronica Jacome Published 2025-04-08 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00221-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

In Kampala's informal settlements, having a grid connection is not the same as having good electricity. Through surveys, interviews and remote power-quality monitoring across 25 informal settlements, the authors trace electrical and financial flows between the utility, informal supply intermediaries and end users, and identify 29 distinct "service arrangements." These arrangements deliver electricity that is highly variable and overall limited in affordability, reliability, voltage stability, autonomy and safety — so the grid, the supposed "gold standard," reproduces deep inequity for the city's most vulnerable residents, and binary metrics like the Multi-Tier Framework miss it entirely.

★

Why It Matters

  • Redefines what "electrified" means. Global energy-access targets (SDG 7) count grid connections as success. This paper shows a connection can still mean unaffordable, unsafe, intermittent power — the "last mile" of access is qualitative, not binary.
  • Makes the informal middlemen visible. By mapping service arrangements — the improvised electrical-and-financial relationships between utility, intermediaries and households — it surfaces a hidden governance layer that standard energy statistics never capture.
  • Exposes a measurement gap. It demonstrates concretely where the Multi-Tier Framework (MTF) fails to register precarity, autonomy and safety, pushing for richer, equity-sensitive access metrics.
  • Grounds policy in lived experience. Remote voltage monitoring plus household surveys link macro energy-poverty debates to the meter on the wall, giving utilities and regulators actionable, settlement-level evidence.

Core Methods

  • Mixed-methods design: integrates household surveys, semi-structured interviews, and quantitative remote power-quality monitoring into a single account of access.
  • Flow tracing: reconstructs the electrical and financial flows linking the utility → supply intermediaries → end users, the unit of analysis behind each arrangement.
  • Service-arrangement typology: inductively derives 29 unique configurations of connection type and payment recipient (six connection types; Figs. 1–2).
  • Multi-dimensional quality assessment: evaluates each arrangement on affordability, reliability, voltage stability, precarity, autonomy and safety — going well beyond the binary "connected/not connected."
  • Empirical voltage analysis: compares measured nominal voltage distributions across arrangements (Fig. 3) to ground qualitative claims in physical telemetry.
  • Critique of metrics: benchmarks findings against the Multi-Tier Framework to show what existing energy-access metrics systematically miss.

① Turn "service arrangements" into a multi-dimensional access index, then scale it spatially

Extending this paperThe paper's central contribution is qualitative — 29 service arrangements scored across affordability, reliability, voltage, precarity, autonomy and safety — but it stops short of a composite, comparable access index. A direct extension: fuse the survey + voltage telemetry into a multi-criteria score per household and spatially interpolate it across Kampala's informal settlements using geospatial mapping of slums, producing a settlement-level "quality-of-access" surface that the Multi-Tier Framework cannot generate.

Added value to my researchThis is exactly my health-geography toolkit applied to energy: building a multi-dimensional accessibility index and mapping it as a continuous service-area surface is the same machinery I use for spatial accessibility of primary care — energy access becomes another "service" amenable to 2SFCA-style provisioning analysis.

② Couple electricity precarity with health outcomes in the same settlements

Extending this paperThe paper documents unsafe, intermittent, smoky-fuel-displacing electricity but does not link it to health endpoints. An extension: pair the service-arrangement typology with respiratory, mental-health and injury indicators in the same communities, testing whether precarious arrangements predict worse health — converting an energy-justice study into an environmental-health one.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my space–human-systems-for-health agenda: it gives a concrete exposure pathway (energy precarity → health) to model with spatiotemporal methods, and connects to my work on how the urban built environment shapes mental-health and primary-care need.

③ Optimize the intermediary network for an equity–reliability trade-off

Extending this paperThe paper reveals that informal supply intermediaries mediate cost and reliability, but treats this network descriptively. Extending it: model the utility → intermediary → household graph and ask how to reallocate connections / metering to maximize reliability and affordability under an equity constraint — a facility/allocation optimization on a real social-electrical network.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly onto my EV-charging efficiency-vs-equity optimization: same objective structure (efficiency × equity over a spatial service network), now on electricity distribution, and a natural target for explainable ML to attribute which features drive inequitable outcomes.

④ Use remote sensing + telemetry to extrapolate beyond the 25 surveyed settlements

Extending this paperThe study is grounded but local (25 of 60 settlements, single city). An extension that honors its data: train a model that links the ground-truth service-arrangement labels and voltage telemetry to satellite nightlights, building footprints and infrastructure-deficit layers, then predict access quality across all African informal settlements — generalizing the title's claim with quantified uncertainty.

Added value to my researchThis is my multi-source data-fusion + GeoAI line: fusing sparse ground truth with remote sensing to scale a survey to continental coverage, fine-tuning geospatial foundation models, with explainable-ML attribution to keep predictions interpretable.

⑤ Reframe energy access as a spatial-accessibility problem alongside other essential services

Extending this paperThe paper isolates electricity, but informal settlements face simultaneous deficits (health care, green space, water). Extending it: embed the energy access index within a multi-service accessibility framework for the same neighborhoods, testing whether energy precarity co-locates with care and amenity deserts and whether a single deprivation gradient explains them.
