Learning functional properties of proteins with language models

Serbulent Unsal · Heval Atas · Muammer Albayrak · Kemal Turhan · Aybar C. Acar · Tunca Doğan Published 2022-03-21 DOI 10.1038/s42256-022-00457-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Large-scale protein language models — especially transformer-based architectures like ProtT5-XL and ProtALBERT — consistently outperform classical representations across four functional benchmarks (semantic similarity, ontology-based function prediction, drug-target family classification, and binding affinity estimation), even when sequence similarity between training and test sets is as low as 15%. The benchmarking platform PROBE provides a reproducible standard for evaluating any new protein representation method on these four tasks, accelerating progress toward solving open problems in protein science.

  • Establishes the first multi-task, multi-model standard for protein representations. Covering 23 learned and classical methods across 4 functional benchmarks, PROBE offers a unified, reproducible yardstick previously absent from the field.
  • Demonstrates generalization beyond sequence similarity. Top learned representations (ProtT5-XL, ProtALBERT) maintain strong performance even at the 15% sequence similarity split — below the "twilight zone" — implying capture of deeper structural/functional patterns.
  • Directly enables drug discovery and biomedicine. Accurate drug-target family classification and binding affinity estimation from sequence alone can shortcut costly wet-lab experiments and open new avenues in rational protein design.
  • Delivers open infrastructure. The PROBE codebase, datasets, pre-trained vectors, and an interactive web tool (PROBE.kansil.org) make every result reproducible and every new model immediately evaluable.

Core Methods

  • Protein representation collection: pre-computed or freshly generated representation vectors for 15 learned methods (small-scale: SeqVec, UniRep, CPCProt, etc.; large-scale transformers: ProtBERT-BFD, ProtT5-XL, ProtALBERT, ESM-1b, MSA-Transformer) and 8 classical baselines (BLAST, HMMER, AAC, APAAC, K-Sep, PFAM, rule-based methods). Mean pooling aggregates residue-level to protein-level vectors.
  • Semantic similarity inference: Spearman rank-order correlation between cosine/Manhattan/Euclidean representation similarities and GO-derived Lin semantic similarities across three datasets per GO aspect (MF, BP, CC).
  • Ontology-based protein function prediction (PFP): linear SVM (SGD, hinge loss) trained per GO-term group; 5-fold cross-validation; 275 GO terms selected to span 27 specificity/frequency groups across MF, BP, CC; evaluated by weighted F1-score.
  • Drug target protein family classification: multitask linear SVM (OneVsRest) with 10-fold cross-validation on four sequence-similarity-controlled splits (random, 50%, 30%, 15%); evaluated by F1-score, accuracy, and Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC).
  • Protein–protein binding affinity estimation: element-wise vector multiplication of protein pair representations; Bayesian Ridge Regression with 10-fold cross-validation against SKEMPI KD values; evaluated by Pearson correlation, MSE, MAE, compared against PIPR baseline.
  • Data leakage control: explicit sequence-similarity-based train/test splits (UniClust50/30, MMSEQ-15); methods with potential data leakage flagged in figures.

① Transfer the PROBE benchmarking philosophy to geospatial foundation models

Extending this paperPROBE's central contribution is a multi-task, method-agnostic benchmarking platform: it separates the quality of a representation from the power of the downstream classifier by using a deliberately weak linear SVM. This design principle is directly exportable. Extending this paper: apply the same representation-decoupling logic to geospatial sequence models (e.g., trajectory encoders, remote sensing patch embeddings), defining analogous benchmark tasks — spatial semantic similarity, land-use class prediction, and mobility-flow affinity — and constructing similarity-controlled train/test splits by geographic distance instead of sequence identity. This would give the geospatial AI community its own PROBE equivalent.

Added value to my researchMy fine-tuning agenda for geospatial foundation models currently lacks a principled evaluation standard. A PROBE-style spatial benchmark — with linear probe heads on frozen encoders — would let me fairly compare geospatial foundation models (SatCLIP, GeoCLIP, RemoteCLIP) on accessibility and health-geography tasks without conflating representation quality with classifier complexity. This directly advances the reproducibility and FAIR-data themes in my research.

② Integrate multi-source heterogeneous data for richer spatial representations

Extending this paperThe paper finds that Mut2Vec — which combines PPI networks, mutation profiles, and biomedical text — outperforms purely sequence-based models in biological process and cellular component prediction, pointing to multi-source fusion as a productive direction. A direct extension: construct holistic protein vectors by concatenating independently pre-trained representations from sequence, structural graphs (PDB), and functional text (literature), then training a unified multi-modal encoder using contrastive learning. The paper already proposes this direction (Supplementary Fig. 13); systematically evaluating it within PROBE's four benchmarks would validate the strategy.

Added value to my researchThe multi-source fusion lesson maps directly onto my spatiotemporal data fusion work (mobile phones, social media, remote sensing, POIs). Instead of late-fusion concatenation, I can adopt the protein-space approach of pre-training each modality independently and learning cross-modal alignment — a strategy that should improve predictions of spatial health outcomes where each data stream captures a different dimension of human activity.

③ Disentangle spatial representation vectors for explainable GeoAI

Extending this paperThe paper identifies model interpretability as a critical open challenge: most learned protein vectors are black boxes where it is unknown which vector dimension encodes which biological property. It calls for disentanglement frameworks that associate vector positions with measurable protein attributes. Extending this paper: adapt recent disentanglement methods (beta-VAE, concept activation vectors) to protein language model embeddings, then evaluate using PROBE's four tasks as ground-truth attribute probes — turning the benchmark into a disentanglement test bed.

④ Apply sequence-similarity-controlled evaluation to spatial accessibility studies

Extending this paperOne of the paper's most rigorous methodological contributions is the use of sequence-similarity-controlled train/test splits (UniClust50/30 and MMSEQ-15), which reveal that classical methods degrade sharply as similarity drops to 15%, while large transformers remain robust. This is a domain-general lesson about overfitting to superficial similarity rather than learning deep structure. Extending this paper: apply the same leakage-probing logic across all four PROBE benchmarks with even lower similarity thresholds to map the "extrapolation frontier" of each model class.

Added value to my researchIn spatial prediction tasks (e.g., predicting primary care accessibility in unseen census tracts or cities), I typically split by random geographic partitioning. The protein-field lesson suggests I should use spatial-autocorrelation-controlled splits — ensuring training and test areas are truly geographically dissimilar — to honestly assess whether my explainable ML models learn transferable patterns or just memorize spatial proximity. This rigour is directly applicable to MGWR and ML ensemble models I use for health and accessibility prediction.

⑤ Leverage DNA/sequence language model architectures for spatiotemporal trajectory encoding

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that transformer attention heads naturally discover sequence motifs and amino-acid co-variation patterns in an unsupervised way, enabling performance on tasks never seen during pre-training. This is the core of the transfer learning success story here. Extending this paper: the same pre-training strategy — next-token prediction on long biological sequences — could be applied to protein families not yet covered by existing models (e.g., disordered regions, membrane proteins), with PROBE providing immediate downstream evaluation.

Added value to my researchGPS trajectories and urban mobility sequences are structurally analogous to protein sequences: long symbolic strings with local motifs (activity clusters), long-range dependencies (commuting patterns), and a finite vocabulary (location categories, POI types). Pre-training a transformer on massive GPS trajectory corpora — using next-location prediction as the self-supervised task — and then evaluating frozen representations with linear probes on downstream tasks (health outcome prediction, telehealth access, job-housing balance) mirrors precisely what PROBE establishes. The paper's lesson that large-scale, multi-head attention outperforms all classical baselines on functional tasks suggests investing in trajectory language models for spatial health geography.
