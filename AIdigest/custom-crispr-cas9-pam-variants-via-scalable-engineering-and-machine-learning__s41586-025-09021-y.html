Custom CRISPR–Cas9 PAM variants via scalable engineering and machine learning

Core Conclusion

By combining high-throughput protein engineering (saturation mutagenesis + bacterial selection) with a trained neural network (PAMmla), the authors generated nearly 1,000 SpCas9 variants, then used PAMmla to predict PAM preferences for 64 million SpCas9 enzymes. Bespoke, PAM-specific editors identified this way outperform generalist enzymes (SpG, SpRY) in human cells while substantially reducing off-target editing. An in silico-directed evolution pipeline further enables user-directed design of allele-selective editors — demonstrated for the disease allele RHO P23H in human cells and mice — marking a paradigm shift from generalist to custom, target-specific Cas9 variants.

★

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the generalist-enzyme bottleneck. Current genome editing relies on a handful of relaxed-PAM SpCas9 variants (SpG, SpRY) that trade specificity for coverage; PAMmla replaces that trade-off with on-demand, target-tuned enzymes.
  • Scales machine learning to a near-infinite enzyme space. Training on ~1,000 experimentally characterized variants, PAMmla makes reliable predictions for 64 million SpCas9(6AA) sequence combinations — a >60,000-fold amplification of experimental throughput.
  • Reduces off-target editing without sacrificing on-target efficiency. Bespoke editors chosen by PAMmla show fewer GUIDE-seq2-detected off-target sites than SpG/SpRY — directly addressing the central safety concern in therapeutic gene editing.
  • Demonstrates allele-selective in vivo editing. The in silico-directed evolution (ISDE) approach designs an enzyme (MRRWMR/KRHWMR) that preferentially edits the mutant RHO P23H allele over wild type in living mice — a proof-of-concept for precision medicine.
  • Open platform for the community. PAMmla predictions for 64 million enzymes are queryable through a public webtool (pammla.streamlit.app) and training data on GitHub — democratizing bespoke enzyme selection.

Core Methods

  • Structure-informed saturation mutagenesis: six PAM-interacting (PI) domain residues (D1135, S1136, G1218, E1219, R1335, T1337) identified from SpCas9 crystal structures and randomized using NNS codons to create the SpCas9(6AA) library.
  • Bacterial positive selection (HT-PAMDA): E. coli selection system using a toxic ccdB gene adjacent to non-canonical 4-nt PAMs; surviving colonies harbor SpCas9 variants capable of recognizing that PAM. Performed for all 16 NG·NN PAM categories.
  • Neural network training (PAMmla): neural network (outperforming linear regression and random forests) trained on one-hot-encoded 6-amino-acid sequences → 64-PAM activity profiles; validated by 5-fold cross-validation stratified by Hamming distance. SHAP used for interpretability.
  • In silico prediction at scale: PAMmla applied to all 64 million SpCas9(6AA) sequence combinations to predict PAM preferences; top candidates prioritized for experimental validation in human cells.
  • In silico-directed evolution (ISDE): iterative, user-directed Cas9 design pipeline that applies PAMmla as an oracle, starting from 1,000 random sequences and evolving toward a user-specified target PAM profile; analogous to in vitro directed evolution but fully computational.
  • Human-cell validation: targeted amplicon sequencing (CRISPResso2 analysis) for nuclease editing and base editing (ABE8e, TadCBEd) in HEK 293T cells; GUIDE-seq2 for genome-wide off-target profiling.
  • In vivo allele-selective editing: subretinal AAV injection in heterozygous RHO P23H mice; editing outcomes quantified by amplicon sequencing of retinal tissue.

① From PAMmla's one-hot input to structure-aware graph representation

Extending this paperPAMmla currently encodes each SpCas9(6AA) variant as a one-hot amino-acid vector — discarding all structural context between the six PI-domain residues and their PAM contacts. A direct extension: replace the flat one-hot encoding with a graph neural network (GNN) over the PAM-interacting domain, where nodes are residues and edges encode distance and hydrogen-bond contacts from the crystal structures already used for library design. This would let PAMmla generalize beyond the SpCas9(6AA) scaffold to other Cas9 orthologs or multi-position libraries without retraining from scratch.

Added value to my researchThe challenge of extracting predictive signals from a small, structured feature set (6 residues → activity profile) mirrors my own use of physics-informed graph learning for spatiotemporal prediction, where node features (locations) interact via physical priors (network adjacency, mobility flows). The GNN-over-protein-structure design pattern — encoding domain knowledge as graph topology rather than flat features — directly transfers to my spatiotemporal kernel-density and spatial accessibility models, where graph-encoded geographic constraints can act as structural priors.

② Active-learning-guided experimental design to shrink the 64-million-enzyme gap

Extending this paperPAMmla was trained on ~1,000 variants selected by a structure-informed heuristic (which residues to vary) but without active learning — the training set was chosen before any model feedback. A powerful extension of the paper: embed PAMmla in an active learning loop where after each training round the model flags variants with highest predictive uncertainty (or highest predicted activity in underexplored PAM classes) for the next batch of HT-PAMDA experiments. This would close the 64,000-fold gap between predicted and experimentally validated enzymes more efficiently than random or heuristic sampling, and would naturally focus experimental budget on the regions of sequence space where the model is most likely to be wrong.

My mobile-phone and social-media data are sparse in rural and underserved areas — structurally identical to the underexplored PAM classes here. An uncertainty-guided sampling strategy adapted from this paper's domain could tell me which geographic regions or time windows to prioritize for additional ground-truth data collection, improving spatial prediction accuracy most efficiently per unit of annotation cost.

③ Generalizing ISDE: from enzyme-space search to spatial facility-siting optimization

Extending this paperThe in silico-directed evolution (ISDE) pipeline is essentially a black-box, ML-oracle-guided combinatorial search: starting sequences are mutated, scored by PAMmla, and the best variants propagated — a discrete analog of evolutionary strategies in continuous space. The paper shows this works for a 6-position, 206-dimensional sequence space. A methodological extension: replace the stopping criterion (plateau in top-10 predicted activity) with a multi-objective Pareto front (on-target activity + PAM stringency + off-target profile), and benchmark ISDE against Bayesian optimization with a Gaussian process surrogate — this would establish whether ISDE's design choices (number of random restarts, plateau rule) are optimal or merely convenient.

Added value to my researchISDE's structure — a trained oracle replaces expensive real-world evaluation, enabling large-scale combinatorial search — maps directly onto my EV charging-station efficiency-vs-equity optimization problem, where the "oracle" would be a trained spatial model predicting accessibility outcomes for a given station placement. Using a learned surrogate model to guide facility siting across millions of candidate configurations, rather than re-running accessibility computations from scratch, would make my optimization tractable at metropolitan scale.

④ SHAP interpretability from amino acid sequence to spatial feature attribution

Extending this paperPAMmla uses SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) to identify which of the six amino acid positions most drives activity toward each PAM class — Extended Data Fig. 4 shows position-specific SHAP values across all 16 NG·NN PAMs. A natural extension: use these SHAP attributions to constrain the next round of library design — fixing high-SHAP positions at their optimal amino acids and exploring only low-SHAP positions — reducing the effective search space by an order of magnitude and providing a mechanistic basis for the selections rather than exhaustive enumeration. This would also allow the paper's structural homology models (Extended Data Fig. 5) to be validated or refuted by predicted interaction energies.

Added value to my researchSHAP-constrained feature selection is immediately applicable to my explainable ML pipelines for spatial prediction (healthcare accessibility, housing prices, telehealth divide). I currently use SHAP post-hoc to interpret predictions; this paper's usage — feeding SHAP attributions forward into the next round of experimental or data-collection design — is a powerful upgrade. Knowing which spatial features carry the most predictive weight would let me focus expensive data-collection (e.g., ground-truthing POI quality, measuring actual travel times) on the feature dimensions SHAP identifies as most uncertain or most impactful.

⑤ Allele-selective editing as a prototype for spatially selective intervention design

Extending this paperThe paper's most striking result is an enzyme (MRRWMR) that preferentially edits the RHO P23H mutant allele over the wild-type allele — achieved because the P23H mutation shifts the PAM sequence at the target locus. This selectivity by genomic context concept generalizes: for any SNV-adjacent target, PAMmla could be queried to find enzymes that exploit the PAM difference introduced by the mutation. An extension: build a genome-wide pipeline that (i) identifies all ClinVar pathogenic SNVs that create a new non-NGG PAM, (ii) queries PAMmla for selective editors, and (iii) ranks them by predicted specificity — producing a pre-computed catalogue of allele-selective editing opportunities across thousands of disease alleles.

Added value to my researchThe conceptual bridge to my work is the idea of context-selective models: just as MRRWMR is specific to one allele's context, my GeoAI models can be made specific to spatially heterogeneous contexts (urban vs. rural, high-density vs. low-density neighborhoods) using geographically weighted or context-conditioned architectures. The paper's allele-selectivity result motivates a stronger version of my geographically weighted regression work: rather than assuming a smooth spatial variation of coefficients (MGWR), design a model that sharpens its predictions specifically in the spatial "allele" (context) where the intervention matters most — e.g., underserved census tracts with the largest healthcare access gap.
