Olympic legacies and the sustainability agenda

John R. Gold · Margaret M. Gold Published 2021-04-20 DOI 10.1038/s41893-021-00711-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Staging the Olympics now obligates host cities to deliver both sustainability commitments and tangible urban legacies — two agendas that often compete. The IOC's Olympic Agenda 2020 shifted from imposing strict conditions to allowing greater host-city flexibility, yet the real driver of Olympic bids remains the host city's own regeneration, infrastructure, and prestige goals. Only by working with the grain of the host city–IOC relationship — rather than against it — can mega-events and sustainability genuinely converge.

Why It Matters

  • Reframes the sustainability–legacy tension as a governance problem. The paper argues that sustainability and legacy are not inherently opposed but become so when the power dynamics of the host city–IOC relationship are ignored — shifting the problem from a planning question to a political-economy question.
  • Documents the declining pool of host cities. By 2017–2020 only two or three cities were competing for each Games, signaling that the financial and reputational burden was deterring otherwise willing hosts — a direct threat to the Olympic project's continuity.
  • Traces why legacy "wins" over sustainability in practice. London 2012 is used to show that tangible physical legacies (housing, parks, transport) are easier to sell politically than the more conceptual claims of sustainability, which tends to be instrumentalized as a technical delivery mechanism.
  • Provides a roadmap for reform. The analysis of Olympic Agenda 2020 and its successive revisions shows how temporary venues, reuse of existing facilities, flexible village design, and improved public transport can align host-city incentives with sustainability — potentially saving up to US$959 million per Summer Games on a conceptual basis.

Core Methods

  • Historical-comparative analysis: traces the evolution of IOC governance documents from Agenda 21 (2006) through Olympic Agenda 2020 (2014) to The New Norm (2018) and 2021 updates, examining how the language, ambitions, and operational requirements of "sustainability" and "legacy" have shifted over time.
  • Case-study analysis (London 2012): uses the London Games as the canonical post-legacy-framework Summer Olympics to illustrate how the two agendas play out in practice — examining the Chobham Manor housing development as a physical artefact of legacy vs. sustainability tensions.
  • Political-economy framing: interprets host city behavior through the lens of urban incentives (regeneration, infrastructure, national branding), arguing that sustainability commitments are only viable when they align with, rather than contradict, host-city motivations.
  • Documentary evidence synthesis: cross-references IOC-published cost figures, bidding histories, and strategic recommendations to evaluate the realistic scope of reform.

① Quantify the accessibility dividend (or deficit) of post-Olympic urban transformation

Extending this paperThe paper argues that Olympic legacy housing like Chobham Manor is "substantially sold at market value" and fails broader social sustainability standards, but it never measures the actual accessibility or service equity implications for new residents. A direct extension: apply a 15-minute-city or 2SFCA accessibility framework across Olympic legacy zones for London 2012, Rio 2016, Tokyo 2020, and Paris 2024 — using OpenStreetMap POIs and street networks — to test whether post-Games neighbourhood transformation systematically improves or degrades proximity to essential services compared to pre-Games brownfield baselines. This would move the paper's qualitative critique of legacy housing from assertion to quantified evidence.

The temporal dimension (pre-Games to post-Games) aligns with my spatiotemporal kernel density methods, and the multi-city scope (London, Rio, Tokyo, Paris) provides a comparative panel for GeoAI cross-city transfer learning.

② Map the social sustainability gap in Olympic legacy housing using remote sensing and mobility data

Extending this paperThe paper flags that London 2012 legacy housing was "substantially sold at market value," failing broader social sustainability, but offers no spatial or demographic evidence of displacement or exclusion. Extending it: fuse high-resolution remote-sensing data (building footprints, land-use change) with mobile-phone GPS trajectories and social-media check-in data to reconstruct pre- vs. post-Games population mobility patterns around the Olympic Park. This would provide empirical grounding for the paper's gentrification claim and reveal whether the Games accelerated displacement of lower-income residents from East London — a question the paper raises but cannot answer from documentary evidence alone.

Extending this paperThe paper reports that the New Norm framework could save up to US$959 million per Summer Games by shifting to temporary venues and flexible Athletes' Villages. Extending it: combine the paper's cost-saving estimates with quasi-experimental evidence from affordable public housing programs to quantify whether redirecting Games savings toward affordable post-Games housing has measurable intergenerational mobility effects for lower-income families living in legacy zones.
