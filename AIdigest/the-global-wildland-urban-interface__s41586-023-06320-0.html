The global wildland–urban interface

Franz Schug · Avi Bar-Massada · Amanda R. Carlson · Heather Cox · Todd J. Hawbaker · David Helmers · Patrick Hostert · Dominik Kaim · Neda K. Kasraee · Sebastián Martinuzzi · Miranda H. Mockrin · Kira A. Pfoch · Volker C. Radeloff Published 2023-07-19 DOI 10.1038/s41586-023-06320-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The wildland–urban interface (WUI) — where buildings meet wildland vegetation — is a global phenomenon covering 4.7% of land area yet home to 3.5 billion people (nearly half of humanity). Using a globally consistent 10 m resolution remote sensing approach for 2020, the study reveals widespread previously undocumented WUI hotspots across East Africa, eastern Asia and South America, and shows that two thirds of all people affected by wildfires (2003–2020) live in the WUI. As wildfire activity intensifies under climate change, understanding WUI distribution is critical for global hazard preparedness.

★

Why It Matters

  • First global map of the WUI at 10 m resolution. No previous global land cover or land-use map had included the WUI as a class; this fills a fundamental data gap for hazard research, urban planning and conservation globally.
  • Reveals hidden hotspots of human–environmental risk. Large WUI areas in East Africa, Southeast Asia and Brazil — where wildfire regimes, settlement patterns and wildfire risk had not been assessed — are now documented and quantified.
  • Quantifies the wildfire exposure of billions. Of the 0.4 billion people who experienced wildfire from 2003 to 2020, two thirds live in the WUI; in Africa alone, 150 million WUI residents were affected — actionable intelligence for disaster preparedness.
  • Open, validated, reusable data infrastructure. All raster maps are freely available via Zenodo and an interactive viewer, enabling any researcher or planner to access globally consistent WUI data for their region.
  • Grounds future climate-change impact projections. By showing the biomes and world regions where WUI is largest and wildfire hazard is growing, the study provides the spatial baseline for projecting how expanding WUI under climate change will affect people.

Core Methods

  • Raster-based WUI classification: building density (GHS-BUILT-S, 10 m) and land cover (ESA WorldCover, 10 m) are combined using circular moving-window kernels (500 m radius) to classify intermix WUI (≥50% wildland vegetation + buildings) and interface WUI (buildings within 2,400 m of a large wildland patch ≥5 km²) at global scale.
  • EQUI7 tiling and FORCE processing: all data are organized in an equidistant projection grid (seven world regions, 100 km tiles) using the FORCE software to enable consistent mass processing while avoiding projection distortion.
  • Land-cover stratification: WUI classes are further stratified by dominant vegetation type (forest/shrubland/wetland vs. grassland) to support regionally appropriate fire-risk interpretation.
  • Elevation and water masking: building density is zeroed where slope >25° or intra-annual water occurrence >20% to reduce false detections on steep bare rock and river beds.
  • Area-weighted summary statistics: population, biomass and wildfire exposure computed as pixel-based area-weighted aggregations by country, biome and world region, with pixel-area correction factors applied for the equidistant projection.
  • Stratified accuracy assessment: 9,024 validation sites drawn by stratified random sampling across six world regions and five classes; overall area-adjusted accuracies: 79.6% (all classes) / 82.0% (WUI vs. non-WUI); reference labels from expert Google Earth interpretation.
  • MODIS/VIIRS wildfire analysis: fire presence derived at 1 km grid; only vegetation fires included (excluding volcanoes, gas flares, agricultural fires); cross-validated with VIIRS 375 m data (2013–2020).

① From static WUI map to dynamic wildfire-risk scoring at parcel level

Extending this paperThe paper delivers a 10 m raster layer classifying WUI presence but does not quantify the probability or severity of wildfire damage to individual structures. A direct extension would feed the paper's WUI layer as a covariate into a structure-level wildfire-risk model — combining WUI class, dominant biome, slope, proximity to fire ignition points, and defensible space — to produce probabilistic risk scores rather than binary WUI membership. The corpus paper on Wildfire risk for global WUI areas (Nature Sustainability) has already begun this integration; the present paper's 10 m resolution baseline could sharpen its spatial granularity globally.

Added value to my researchMy work on spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and spatial accessibility transfers directly here: modelling ignition density as a spatiotemporal KDE surface layered over the WUI map creates a time-varying wildfire-exposure accessibility index analogous to 2SFCA service areas but for hazard proximity — a novel application of healthcare accessibility methodology to disaster-risk assessment.

② Integrating WUI governance zones with socio-ecological complexity

Extending this paperThe paper characterizes WUI by physical attributes (vegetation type, building density, biome) but does not incorporate governance capacity or socio-economic vulnerability — variables that determine whether WUI populations can actually respond to hazard. Extending it with the Pyro-Socio-Ecological Zones framework (a corpus paper in Nature Communications) would layer institutional capacity, income inequality and land tenure onto the WUI map, producing a multi-dimensional governance-vulnerability atlas rather than a purely physical interface map. This would operationalize the paper's call for "different management responses" across the diverse WUI it has catalogued.

Added value to my researchMy research on health geography and digital divides shows that physical access and social vulnerability interact non-linearly. Applying explainable ML (SHAP-based GBM) to predict governance deficits from the WUI's physical attributes — biome, building density gradient, income class — would quantify which spatial features most strongly signal a community's inability to self-protect, directly extending my methods to environmental justice in WUI contexts.

Cross-ref (corpus): A tridimensional framework for governance in the wildland-urban interface using Pyro-Socio-Ecological Zones — 10.1038/s41467-025-66452-x

③ Coupling urban structural growth trajectories with WUI expansion forecasting

Extending this paperThe paper maps the WUI as of 2020 but explicitly notes that WUI expansion is driven by housing growth — a process it does not model. A temporal extension would pair the paper's WUI definition with global urban structural growth data (the Nature Cities paper tracking 1,550+ cities over three decades from spaceborne scatterometers) to project where the WUI perimeter will shift by 2050 under different urban growth scenarios. The paper's validated 10 m WUI algorithm applied to a time series of GHS-BUILT-S layers would yield the first multi-decadal global WUI change trajectory.

Added value to my researchI work on urban agglomeration, regional collaboration and job-housing networks. A WUI expansion forecast model built on the urban-structural-growth corpus — combined with my spatiotemporal analysis methods — could identify which metropolitan fringe zones are on course to enter high-risk WUI status within a policy-relevant horizon, directly informing land-use planning and housing-price externality models.

Cross-ref (corpus): Global urban structural growth shows a profound shift from spreading out to building up — 10.1038/s44284-024-00100-1

④ Quantifying air pollution exposure of WUI populations from structure fires

Extending this paperThe paper identifies where WUI residents are exposed to wildfire but treats fire as a binary hazard (presence/absence). Pairing the WUI map with a structure-fire emissions inventory (the Nature Communications paper quantifying WUI structure-fire pollutant emissions across the US) would translate WUI membership into a continuous health-exposure surface — concentrations of PM2.5, VOCs and heavy metals — disaggregated by WUI type (intermix vs. interface) and biome. The paper's open 10 m layers are the ideal spatial backbone for this compound exposure assessment.

Added value to my researchMy health-geography work on spatial accessibility of primary care and digital health divides focuses on supply-side access. Adding a WUI-derived air-quality burden layer creates a demand-weighted accessibility model: communities with simultaneous high wildfire-smoke exposure and low primary-care access face compounded health disadvantage — a hypothesis I could test using my R2SFCA framework with the GHS-POP denominator from this paper.

Cross-ref (corpus): Emissions from burned structures in wildfires as significant yet unaccounted sources of US air pollution — 10.1038/s41467-025-66292-9

⑤ A GeoAI foundation model for global WUI monitoring from multi-temporal satellite streams

Extending this paperThe paper's algorithm is a hand-crafted rule-based classifier (density threshold + proximity rules) applied to two static rasters. It cannot update automatically as new Sentinel imagery arrives nor learn from the validation labels it collected. A GeoAI extension would fine-tune a geospatial foundation model on the paper's 9,024 expertly labelled validation sites — using the ESA WorldCover and GHS-BUILT-S inputs as context — to produce a continuous, self-updating WUI probability map that improves as new Sentinel acquisitions land, replacing static snapshots with a living global WUI monitor.

Added value to my researchFine-tuning geospatial foundation models is a core direction in my research agenda. Using the paper's validated WUI labels as a geographically stratified, expert-annotated benchmark dataset would serve as a high-quality downstream task for evaluating how well foundation models generalize across different biomes, population densities and continent-scale domain shifts — a transferable contribution to the broader GeoAI evaluation ecosystem.
