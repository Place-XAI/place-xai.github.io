Disproportionate flood exposure for slum populations of the Global South

Dan Li · Laixiang Sun · Kuishuang Feng · Ning Zhang · Yang Yu · Dandan Zhao · Yuyu Zhou Published 2025-07-04 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00273-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Combining machine learning with publicly available satellite imagery to map slum populations across the Global South, the study finds that roughly one in three slum dwellers lives in a floodplain. Slum residents are about 32% more likely to settle in floodplains than people in adequate housing, and their concentration is highest precisely in areas that have already suffered severe floods. The result is a data-driven demonstration of disproportionate, systematic flood exposure for the most vulnerable urban communities — and a call for just, equitable flood-adaptation planning.

Why It Matters

  • Quantifies an invisible equity gap. Slums are chronically under-mapped in official statistics; the paper builds a satellite-based map of slum populations and shows that flood risk is not random but concentrated on the poorest — turning anecdote into a measurable, Global-South-wide pattern.
  • Couples hazard with vulnerability. By overlaying slum locations on floodplains and historical flood records, it links where hazard is to who can least cope, the core of climate-justice analysis.
  • Targets adaptation resources. Identifying hotspots where slum populations cluster in high-hazard zones tells planners and donors where equitable flood adaptation is most urgent.
  • Scales without ground surveys. The method works from open satellite imagery and household survey anchors, making it transferable to regions where conventional slum censuses are missing or outdated.

Core Methods

  • Satellite-based slum identification: machine learning (gradient-boosting / deep-learning models referenced in Methods) trained on multi-source features — Landsat surface reflectance, Copernicus land cover, NPP-VIIRS nighttime lights — to detect and map slum populations where official maps are absent.
  • Survey anchoring: DHS household data used to define housing adequacy and to calibrate/validate the slum-vs-adequate-housing distinction.
  • Floodplain overlay: intersect mapped slum populations with floodplain extents and GHS population/settlement grids to compute exposure.
  • Settlement-bias metric: compare the propensity of slum dwellers vs. nonslum residents to occupy floodplains, yielding the ~32% bias and its regional divergence (Figs. 2–3).
  • Hazard-concentration analysis: relate slum-population concentration to flood-hazard level / historical flood severity (EM-DAT/GDIS), showing clustering in the worst-hit areas (Fig. 4).
  • Hotspot mapping: Getis-Ord–style spatial hotspot detection of slum dwellers in floodplains across the Global South (Fig. 1).

① From static floodplain overlay to dynamic, climate-projected exposure

Extending this paperThe paper measures exposure against a present-day floodplain and historical flood records (EM-DAT/GDIS). But slum footprints and flood extents both move. A direct extension: couple its satellite-derived slum map with future flood-hazard and population projections so the ~32% settlement bias is propagated forward under climate and urbanization scenarios — turning a snapshot into a forward-looking risk surface for adaptation planning.

Added value to my researchThis maps onto my spatiotemporal-analysis line: the same projection machinery lets me forecast future gaps in healthcare/telehealth access for the same vulnerable populations as climate stress reshapes where people can live, fusing hazard and service-accessibility horizons.

② Make the slum-detection model explainable and uncertainty-aware

Extending this paperThe paper's slum map is the load-bearing input, yet ML slum detection from Landsat + nighttime lights can be biased by region, season, and built form. Extending it: wrap the classifier in explainable-ML attribution (which spectral / texture / light features drive a "slum" call) and propagate per-pixel uncertainty into the exposure statistics, so the 32% bias carries honest confidence bounds across regions.

Added value to my researchThis is exactly my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction agenda: SHAP/feature-attribution over a fused remote-sensing stack, with calibrated uncertainty — directly reusable for my deprivation- and access-mapping models.

Cross-ref (corpus): Socioeconomic predictors of vulnerability to flood-induced displacement — 10.1038/s41467-025-64015-8

③ Shift from place-based exposure to people-and-activity exposure

Extending this paperThe paper counts where people live relative to floodplains. But slum dwellers' daily exposure also depends on where they work, travel, and seek care. Extending it with mobility data (mobile-phone, GPS trajectories) would convert residential exposure into life-activity flood exposure, revealing whether commuting routes and informal workplaces amplify or buffer the 1-in-3 residential figure.

Added value to my researchThis connects to my mobility-and-accessibility work: a spatiotemporal KDE / trajectory pipeline over GPS and mobile-phone records, turning a static vulnerability map into a dynamic exposure-during-daily-life metric I can reuse for service-area delineation.

④ Couple exposure to equitable adaptation-resource allocation

Extending this paperThe paper identifies where disproportionate exposure concentrates but stops short of where to invest. Extending it: feed the hotspot map into a facility-location / efficiency-vs-equity optimization for flood defences, drainage, early-warning, and emergency shelters, explicitly weighting the most-exposed slum clusters to compute an equitable adaptation plan and its cost.

⑤ Embed exposure in a graph of slum settlement and disaster dynamics

Extending this paperThe paper treats slum cells largely independently. Extending it: build a spatial graph linking neighboring settlements, drainage networks, and flood pathways, and learn a physics-informed graph operator (hydrology-aware) that predicts how slum expansion and flood propagation co-evolve — making the "slums cluster where floods hit hardest" finding a mechanistic, transferable model rather than a correlation.
