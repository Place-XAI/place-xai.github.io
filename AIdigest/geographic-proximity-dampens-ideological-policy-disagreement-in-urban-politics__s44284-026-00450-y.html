Geographic proximity dampens ideological policy disagreement in urban politics

Jack Lucas · Martin Horak · Shanaya Vanhooren · David A. Armstrong II Published 2026-06-05 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00450-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Left–right ideology does structure how residents think about municipal policy — but its grip weakens markedly when the same issue is framed at the geographically proximate scale of one's own neighborhood. Using a topic-sampling experiment spanning 40 municipal policy issues, >40,000 policy preferences and 4,000 Canadian respondents, the authors reconcile two opposing schools of urban scholarship: ideological conflict is real, yet the "localness" of a question systematically dampens it. Proximity, not just partisanship, shapes urban policy attitudes.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reconciles a long-standing debate. One camp says urban politics is structured by left–right ideology; another says cities run on non-ideological, place-based coalitions. The paper shows both are right — the answer depends on the geographic scale at which a policy is considered.
  • Identifies a causal mechanism, not just a correlation. A randomized framing manipulation (city-wide vs. own-neighborhood) isolates proximity as the lever that compresses ideological disagreement.
  • Breadth over a single case. Topic sampling across 40 policy issues lets the finding generalize across the policy space rather than hinging on one hot-button topic.
  • Practical for local governance. Suggests that re-scaling deliberation to the neighborhood — participatory budgeting, ward-level consultation — may defuse ideological gridlock on otherwise polarizing municipal issues.

Core Methods

  • Topic-sampling experiment: randomly sample policy issues per respondent so treatment effects generalize across the issue universe, not just hand-picked items.
  • Proximity framing treatment: randomize whether an issue is posed at the city scale or the respondent's own-neighborhood scale; the contrast identifies the dampening effect.
  • Ideology as predictor: measure left–right self-placement and relate it to policy preferences, estimating the slope of disagreement under each framing.
  • Interaction modeling: test ideology × proximity interactions (Fig. 2) to quantify how much geographic proximity compresses the ideological gap.
  • Theoretical grounding: motivated by construal-level theory / psychological distance — proximate, concrete framings reduce abstract ideological reasoning.

Expanded Ideas — this paper's extensibility & added value to my research

① Geo-reference the "proximity" treatment with real accessibility, not just a verbal frame

Extending this paperThe proximity manipulation here is verbal/cognitive ("imagine this in your own neighborhood"). A natural extension is to make proximity measured: link each respondent to their actual neighborhood's built environment (POIs, network distance, service accessibility) and test whether the dampening effect scales with objective proximity to the policy's object — e.g., distance to the nearest transit stop or shelter. This converts a framing effect into a place-conditioned dose–response, and would let the authors map where ideology recedes.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct hook for my accessibility work: a respondent's 2SFCA/R2SFCA access score becomes a covariate predicting attitude formation, joining health-geography access metrics to public-opinion outcomes — useful for telehealth and primary-care siting debates.

Cross-ref (corpus): The 15-minute city quantified using human mobility data — 10.1038/s41562-023-01770-y

② Test whether residential sorting predicts the size of the dampening effect

Extending this paperThe paper holds geography as a framing scale, but doesn't ask how partisan/economic sorting of the respondent's neighborhood moderates the effect. Extension: merge the survey with neighborhood segregation/sorting indices and test whether proximity dampens ideology more in mixed neighborhoods (where the proximate frame surfaces shared local interests) and less in homogeneous ones. This sharpens the mechanism from "localness" to "local social composition."

Added value to my researchSupplies a template for coupling spatial-segregation indices (entropy, network community detection) with individual outcomes — exactly the multi-source fusion I use for neighborhood social-determinant indices in urban-health prediction.

Cross-ref (corpus): The measurement of partisan sorting for 180 million voters — 10.1038/s41562-021-01066-z

③ Connect the proximity effect to the experience of inequality

Extending this paperSeveral of the 40 issues are redistributive (housing, services). The paper could ask whether the proximate frame changes attitudes differently for residents who personally experience local inequality. Pairing the experiment with measures of experienced economic segregation would test if neighborhood framing makes inequality concrete enough to override ideology — or, conversely, to harden it.

Added value to my researchReinforces my interest in how experienced (not just residential) segregation shapes outcomes; the mobility-weighted exposure logic transfers directly to how I weight access in R2SFCA service-area delineation.

Cross-ref (corpus): Economic segregation is associated with reduced concerns about economic inequality — 10.1038/s41467-024-49778-w

④ Model the ideology × proximity interaction with explainable / spatially-varying methods

Extending this paperThe dampening is estimated as a global interaction. Extending it spatially: fit MGWR or an explainable-ML interaction model so the proximity coefficient is allowed to vary across cities/regions, revealing whether the effect is uniform (as the "universal" framing implies) or geographically heterogeneous — and which contextual features drive that variation.

Added value to my researchSquarely my methods stack: MGWR + SHAP-style attribution on a public-opinion target, giving me a worked example of treating an experimental interaction as a spatially non-stationary surface rather than a single number.

Cross-ref (corpus): US cities are defined by rings and pockets with limited socioeconomic mixing — 10.1038/s44284-025-00350-7

⑤ Embed the experiment in an agent-based / network model of local deliberation

Extending this paperThe finding is individual-level; its civic payoff depends on aggregation. Extension: feed the measured ideology×proximity response curve into an agent-based model on a residential network to simulate whether re-scaling deliberation to the neighborhood actually reduces aggregate polarization, given segregation and urban topology. This turns a static experimental estimate into a dynamic policy simulator.
