Care networks as unanticipated gains of local public policy

María José Álvarez-Rivadulla · Sebastián Orlando Espejo Fandiño · Friederike Fleischer · María José Gómez · Adriana Hurtado-Tarazona · Anyela Lizeth Moreno Palma · Vanesa Estefanía Ospina Ramírez · Daniel Sanchez Vega Published 2026-02-25 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00389-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Bogotá's District Care System (SIDICU) and its neighborhood Care Blocks (Manzanas del Cuidado) measurably improve recognition of care work and give marginalized low-income women first-time access to public institutions. Beyond those intended goals, the policy produces an unanticipated gain: peer care networks of mutual support form among caregivers who meet at the Care Blocks. The program does not resolve the structural burden of unremunerated care, but it is an important step toward more equal and caring cities.

  • Names a side-effect that standard evaluation would miss. The headline finding is an unintended benefit: place-based care services seed durable peer support networks. Metrics built only around the program's stated outputs (classes, services delivered) would never capture this social-capital dividend.
  • Centers women's unpaid care burden as an urban-infrastructure problem. It reframes care not as a private household matter but as something cities can provision in space — a "third place" for caregivers.

Core Methods

  • Ethnographic fieldwork: participant observation at Care Blocks to capture how care services are used and how women interact in situ.
  • Semi-structured interviews: with low-income women caregivers to elicit lived experience of the unremunerated care burden and of the program.
  • Focus groups: group discussions surfacing shared experiences, peer ties, and collective perceptions of care work's value.
  • Thematic / interpretive analysis: coding of transcripts and field notes around three findings — recognition of care work, access to the state, and emergent peer support networks.
  • Theory-driven framing: reads the empirical material through care theory and social-capital concepts (bonding/bridging ties, "third places"), positioning Care Blocks as care infrastructure.

Extending this paperThe paper treats Care Blocks as physically reachable "third places," yet never analyzes who can actually reach them given Bogotá's steep peripheral geography and time-poor caregivers. Extending it with a 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility model of Care Block supply against caregiver demand would reveal coverage gaps and whether the network-formation benefit is confined to women who already live close enough to attend.
