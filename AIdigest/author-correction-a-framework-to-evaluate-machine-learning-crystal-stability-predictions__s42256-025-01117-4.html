Core Conclusion

This is an Author Correction to the Matbench Discovery paper (s42256-025-01055-1, published 23 June 2025). The correction updates Figs. 1–3, Table 1, and the Supplementary Information, which in the initially published version showed more models than were present in the accepted text. The original paper establishes Matbench Discovery as a prospective benchmarking framework for ML crystal stability prediction and demonstrates that universal interatomic potentials (UIPs such as EquiformerV2+DeNS, MACE, CHGNet, SevenNet) outperform all other ML methodologies in high-throughput materials discovery, achieving discovery acceleration factors up to 6× over random selection. The core scientific finding stands; this correction aligns figures and tables with the accepted model set.

Why It Matters

  • First prospective benchmark for ML-guided crystal discovery. Matbench Discovery moves beyond retrospective property-prediction benchmarks to simulate a real high-throughput DFT screening campaign, testing 13 ML models on 215,488 hypothetical crystals against the Materials Project convex hull.
  • Identifies a key metric misalignment. Accurate regressors (low MAE/RMSE) can produce very high false-positive rates when predictions cluster near the 0 eV/atom stability threshold — the “triangle of peril.” The framework redirects evaluation toward F1 and Discovery Acceleration Factor (DAF) metrics that better reflect practical utility.

Core Methods

Methods below are those of the original Matbench Discovery paper; this correction does not alter the methodology.

  • Prospective benchmarking (IS2RE task): models receive only the unrelaxed structure as input and must predict the relaxed convex hull distance, mimicking a real discovery workflow and avoiding circular dependence on DFT relaxation.
  • Convex hull distance as target: thermodynamic stability (Ehull ≤ 0 eV/atom vs. MP convex hull) is used instead of formation energy, because formation-energy MAE does not predict classification accuracy near the stability boundary.
  • Classification over regression metrics: F1 score and Discovery Acceleration Factor (DAF = precision / prevalence) are introduced as primary metrics, exposing “triangle of peril” false-positive failure modes invisible to MAE/RMSE.
  • 13 models compared across four families: (a) random forests (Voronoi RF); (b) energy-only GNNs (CGCNN, CGCNN+P, MEGNet, ALIGNN, Wrenformer); (c) Bayesian optimizer (BOWSR); (d) universal interatomic potentials trained on forces and stresses (M3GNet, CHGNet, MACE, SevenNet, Orb, EquiformerV2+DeNS).
  • Cumulative precision–recall simulation: models rank materials by predicted stability and cumulative metrics are computed as a function of campaign length, guiding resource-constrained screening decisions.
  • Protostructure deduplication: WBM test set cleaned by matching wyckoff-position protostructures against the MP training set, removing 11,175 leaking examples and 524 pathological entries.
  • Correction mechanism: extra model rows present in the initially published version but absent from the accepted manuscript were removed from Figs. 1–3, Table 1, and Supplementary Information.

① Transfer the prospective-benchmarking design to geospatial ML evaluation

Extending this paperMatbench Discovery’s most important methodological advance is the prospective test set: unlike random splits, the WBM test set is generated by the same discovery workflow, creating a realistic covariate shift from the MP training distribution. The framework’s four principles — prospective benchmarking, relevant targets, informative metrics, scalability — are domain-agnostic. A direct extension would apply these principles to a geospatial ML benchmark for urban health or accessibility prediction: training on historical facility configurations, then evaluating on newly deployed facilities as the “ground truth,” with DAF-analogue scoring measuring how much better than random a model identifies underserved locations.

Added value to my researchMy accessibility and facility-location papers are almost always evaluated on MAE/R2 with random splits — the same disconnect Matbench Discovery diagnoses. Adopting a DAF-like metric (“facility discovery acceleration factor”: how many more underserved census tracts a model correctly identifies vs. random placement) and building a geographic holdout benchmark would substantially sharpen evaluation of my R2SFCA, MGWR, and GeoAI pipelines for healthcare or EV charging facility siting.

② Adapt the “triangle of peril” diagnostic to spatial policy-threshold classifiers

Extending this paperThe “triangle of peril” — the zone where rolling MAE exceeds the distance to the stability decision boundary — is a domain-agnostic diagnostic for classification-near-threshold failure. It exposes not just average model quality but where in label space errors flip decisions. A direct extension: formalize “boundary risk zone width” as a standard benchmark metric alongside F1 and AUROC, computed for any ML-predicted continuous score that feeds a binary policy classification — applying to drug, catalyst, and geospatial screening alike.

Added value to my researchIn my explainable ML for spatial health outcomes (classifying neighborhoods as healthcare-accessible vs. not, or identifying EV charging deserts), predictions near the policy threshold are exactly where decisions are hardest. A geospatial “triangle of peril” — rolling prediction error as a function of true accessibility score around the binary cutoff — would identify which spatial contexts (peri-urban fringes, low-density corridors) are most misclassification-prone, directly informing where uncertainty quantification should be prioritized in my MGWR and GeoAI pipelines.

③ Exploit the model-disagreement structure for geospatial ML ensemble design

Extending this paperA striking result in the original paper is that the intersection of all UIP models’ correct predictions accounts for only 57% precision — despite similar overall F1 scores, models make meaningfully different errors. This complementarity suggests ensemble strategies could substantially push DAF beyond any single model. Extending the framework: characterize model disagreement as a function of chemical-space distance from the training distribution, then design disagreement-weighted ensemble protocols that maximize DAF by exploiting complementary error patterns across UIP families.

④ Formalize asymmetric false-positive/false-negative costs for equity-aware facility optimization

Extending this paperMatbench Discovery explicitly frames false positives (predicting unstable materials as stable) as more costly than false negatives: wasted DFT validations consume real experimental budgets. The DAF metric encodes this asymmetry. A direct extension: generalize the benchmark to accept domain-specific false-positive and false-negative cost weights as parameters, producing a cost-weighted DAF that reranks models based on the deployment economics of each scientific domain — whether materials, drug, catalyst, or urban screening.

Added value to my researchIn my EV charging and healthcare facility optimization, false positives (siting in an already well-served area) waste capital investment, while false negatives (missing an underserved area) carry equity costs affecting vulnerable populations. Formalizing this asymmetry analogously to the Matbench DAF would let me express the efficiency-equity trade-off as an explicit cost-weighted precision/recall objective — providing a principled, communicable evaluation narrative for policy audiences and funding bodies.
