Predicting drug responses of unseen cell types through transfer learning with foundation models

Wang, Yixuan · Liu, Xinyuan · Fan, Yimin · Xie, Binghui · Cheng, James · Wong, Kam Chung · Cheung, Peter · King, Irwin · Li, Yu Published 2025-10-03 DOI 10.1038/s43588-025-00887-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

CRISP (Cell-type-specific drug perturbatIon responses predictor) is a foundation-model-powered transfer learning framework that predicts drug-induced transcriptional responses in previously unseen cell types at single-cell resolution. By combining single-cell foundation models (scGPT, Geneformer, scFoundation) with cell-type-specific contrastive learning, CRISP achieves a 24.5% improvement in Pearson correlation over state-of-the-art baselines across unseen-cell-type benchmarks. Zero-shot application to chronic myeloid leukemia (K562 cells) accurately predicts sorafenib's anti-tumor mechanisms — including CXCR4 pathway inhibition — without any prior exposure to CML data, aligning with completed clinical trials and opening a cost-effective pathway for in silico drug repurposing.

Why It Matters

  • Addresses a fundamental gap in drug development. Predicting how drugs affect new, previously unmeasured cell types — including rare or disease-emergent populations — is critical but prohibitively expensive with wet-lab experiments. CRISP enables in silico screening at scale.
  • Bridges single-cell foundation models and perturbation prediction. Prior methods that naively plug scFMs into autoencoder pipelines yield negligible gains; CRISP's cell-type-specific architecture (contrastive learning + classifier) unlocks the latent biological information in these large pre-trained models.
  • Generalizes across platforms and patients without retraining. Zero-shot transfer from NeurIPS PBMC data to PBMC-bench (7 sequencing platforms) and cross-tissue transfer to cancer cell lines demonstrate cross-platform robustness that existing tools lack.
  • Validates independently discoverable biology. Sorafenib's predicted CXCR4 inhibition in CML — obtained from solid-tumor training data alone — is supported by a completed Phase II clinical trial and literature, providing a stringent, leak-free validation of the approach.
  • Substantially more computationally efficient. CRISP achieves state-of-the-art performance with 25x faster training than CellOT, enabling large-scale drug screening across hundreds of compounds (188 drugs in SciPlex3 against K562 cells).

Core Methods

  • scFM-assisted paired control encoder: uses frozen scGPT (or Geneformer / scFoundation) to embed control-state cells into dense pre-embeddings; a synthetic paired control embedding is constructed by averaging scFM embeddings of unperturbed cells sharing the same cell-type covariate as each perturbed sample, then encoded through a VAE encoder.
  • Drug treatment encoding: drug SMILES strings are encoded via RDKit (201-dim molecular fingerprint); a dosage-specific encoder scales the drug embedding; both are concatenated with the control latent vector for perturbation decoding.
  • Cell-type-specific prediction module: the decoder (MLP) is regularized by (a) cross-entropy cell-type classification loss and (b) cosine-distance contrastive learning — a negative cell of a different cell type is sampled per training step — forcing the latent space to be cell-type-discriminative without explicit one-hot labels.
  • Loss function: VAE evidence lower bound = DE-gene-focused MSE (top 50 DE genes by |log FC|, coefficient 0.75) + MMD distribution matching + KL divergence + cell-type regularization (classification + contrastive). MMD coefficient is 0.1 for NeurIPS, 1e-4 for SciPlex3/GBM.
  • Zero-shot inference: at test time, CRISP only requires control-state gene expression + treatment condition — no feature alignment, no ground-truth post-perturbation data, no cell-type label required for new platforms or patients.
  • Evaluation metrics: Pearson correlation of log(FC) on top-50 DE genes (PrΔ), R² score, Sinkhorn distance (single-cell resolution via GeomLoss), and directional accuracy of fold-change sign for top-50 DE genes.
  • Functional validation (GSEA): GSEApy using Hallmark and C2-Canonical Pathway gene sets; GSEA run with 1,000 phenotype-based permutations and two-sided signal-to-noise statistic; FDR q-values reported.
  • Baselines compared: scGen, CellOT, Biolord, chemCPA, CPA, and chemCPA-FM (chemCPA with same scFM as CRISP).

① Replace the frozen scFM backbone with a multimodal biological foundation model

Extending this paperCRISP currently uses frozen single-modality scFMs (scGPT, Geneformer) trained on unperturbed transcriptomics. A direct extension: replace the frozen encoder with a multimodal foundation model co-trained on scRNA-seq, chromatin accessibility (scATAC-seq), and protein interaction data. The corpus paper "Towards multimodal foundation models in molecular cell biology" (Nature, 2025) outlines exactly this paradigm — embedding chemical perturbations, epigenomics and transcriptomics in a shared latent space. Plugging such a model into CRISP's FM-PC encoder could substantially improve unseen-cell-type generalization by providing richer cross-modal cellular context beyond gene expression alone, particularly for rare or disease-emergent cell populations where scRNA coverage is sparse.

Added value to my researchThe multimodal embedding strategy directly parallels my work on multi-source data fusion (mobile phone, remote sensing, social media for urban analytics). Just as CRISP fuses scFM embeddings with molecular encodings, I can fuse spatiotemporal GPS trajectories with POI semantic embeddings and remote sensing indices in a shared latent space — yielding richer context representations for spatial prediction models that mirror CRISP's "control + treatment" architecture as "baseline mobility + intervention" in urban policy evaluation.

② Extend CRISP's cell-atlas transfer to a generalizable perturbation operator via optimal transport

Extending this paperCRISP's zero-shot predictions transfer NeurIPS PBMC knowledge to 7 sequencing platforms and cross-tissue cancer lines, but direction accuracy on unseen platforms (Smart-Seq2, CEL-Seq2) remains modest (~0.5). The recently published "Conditional Monge Gap" framework (NMI, 2026) in the corpus introduces a theoretically grounded optimal transport formulation that conditions transport maps on cell-type covariates — directly complementing CRISP's cell-type-aware training strategy. Replacing CRISP's MMD matching loss with a conditional Monge Gap objective could yield tighter distribution alignment across sequencing platforms while preserving cell-type specificity, potentially closing the cross-platform accuracy gap without additional data collection.

Added value to my researchThe conditional optimal transport framing translates to spatiotemporal distribution alignment in my geospatial ML work: aligning mobility data from different cities (different "platforms") while conditioning on local urban morphology ("cell type") mirrors CRISP's problem exactly. This opens a principled approach for my cross-city transferability challenge in 2SFCA accessibility models, where learned distance-decay functions must transfer across diverse spatial contexts.

Cross-ref (corpus): Conditional Monge Gap enables generalizable single-cell perturbation modelling — 10.1038/s42256-026-01242-8

③ Build a unified cell atlas foundation model as the CRISP backbone for genome-wide search

Extending this paperCRISP benchmarks three scFMs (scGPT, Geneformer, scFoundation) with modest differences in performance, suggesting the field has not yet converged on an optimal encoder. The corpus paper "A cell atlas foundation model for scalable search of similar human cells" (Nature, 2024) proposes a unified foundation model trained on hundreds of millions of cells for similarity-based retrieval — a qualitatively different paradigm that could power CRISP's paired control encoder. Incorporating such a retrieval-augmented encoder into CRISP's FM-PC module — using k-nearest-neighbor control cells from a full Human Cell Atlas index rather than within-dataset averaging — would make CRISP's synthetic paired control embedding richer and would naturally extend to zero-shot scenarios where zero within-dataset controls exist for the target cell type.

Added value to my researchThis retrieval-augmented encoding strategy maps onto my interest in fine-tuning geospatial foundation models: rather than training from scratch, retrieve spatially similar regions from a large urban atlas and use them as "paired controls" for predicting the effect of an urban intervention (new transit line, rezoning). The analogy is direct — both CRISP and a geospatial retrieval-augmented model transfer knowledge from observed contexts to unseen target contexts.

Cross-ref (corpus): A cell atlas foundation model for scalable search of similar human cells — 10.1038/s41586-024-08411-y

④ Encode drug molecular structure with a genomic language model for structural drug repurposing

Extending this paperCRISP currently encodes drug molecules via RDKit SMILES fingerprints (201-dimensional molecular descriptors), an approach the Discussion section explicitly identifies as a limitation — richer drug encoding is needed. The corpus paper "A foundation model of transcription across human cell types" (Nature, 2025) demonstrates that sequence-level pre-trained models capture gene-regulatory logic that cheminformatics tools miss. An extension of CRISP: replace the RDKit encoder with a molecular sequence language model pre-trained on drug-target interaction data (e.g., ChemBERTa or MolFormer), enabling CRISP to generalize to structurally novel compounds beyond the training distribution. Combined with drug-drug interaction information (currently absent), this would make CRISP's drug repurposing pipeline applicable to combination therapies — the next frontier the Discussion identifies.

The CRISP paradigm of using pre-trained encoders for both the "cell" (place) and the "drug" (policy) is directly applicable to predicting how a specific urban policy change will shift spatial health outcomes in a new city, using a spatial foundation model as the place encoder.

⑤ Apply CRISP's training-data-composition analysis to understand generalization failure modes

Extending this paperCRISP's performance degrades for T-regulatory cells (only 4.7% of training data) and on Smart-Seq2 (small sample size), but the paper does not systematically analyze how training data composition drives these failures. The corpus paper "Training data composition determines machine learning generalization and biological rule discovery" (NMI, 2025) provides a framework for analyzing how positive/negative class ratios and diversity of training examples determine OOD generalization. Applying this framework to CRISP's training splits — systematically varying cell-type class balance, drug diversity, and sequencing-platform representation — would turn CRISP's empirical observations into prescriptive guidelines for assembling scRNA-seq perturbation datasets that maximize zero-shot transfer, directly informing which future experimental data to prioritize for atlas expansion.

Added value to my researchThe training-data-composition framework resonates with my work on spatial data imbalance in health geography: rare populations (rural communities, minority neighborhoods) are systematically under-represented in health datasets, just as T-regulatory cells are under-represented in scRNA-seq atlases. Adopting CRISP's cell-type-specific contrastive re-weighting strategy for spatially imbalanced health accessibility models — up-weighting geographically marginal zones in R2SFCA training — is a concrete methodological transfer that could improve healthcare facility access predictions in data-sparse regions.
