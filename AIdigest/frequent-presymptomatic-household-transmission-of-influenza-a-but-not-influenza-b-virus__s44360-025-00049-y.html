Frequent presymptomatic household transmission of influenza A but not influenza B virus

Chengyao Zhang · Xiaotong Huang · Vicky J. Fang · Kwok-Hung Chan · Gabriel M. Leung · Dennis K. M. Ip · J. S. Malik Peiris · Benjamin J. Cowling · Simon Cauchemez · Tim K. Tsang Published 2026-01-28 DOI 10.1038/s44360-025-00049-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Influenza A and influenza B viruses differ sharply in their capacity for presymptomatic spread: approximately 9.6% of influenza A transmissions (95% CrI: 5.9%–14.7%) occur more than half a day before the infector's symptom onset, while influenza B shows no detectable presymptomatic transmission. Validation against a SARS-CoV-2 Omicron dataset—where presymptomatic transmission reached 65.5%—confirmed the framework's reliability. These findings suggest that symptom-based isolation remains a viable strategy for influenza control, though presymptomatic quarantine of contacts provides only marginal additional benefit.

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a long-standing uncertainty in influenza control policy. Whether influenza spreads before symptoms begin has been debated for decades; this is the first study to provide rigorous pathogen-specific estimates with quantified credible intervals derived from real household data.
  • Reveals a fundamental virology-driven difference. The contrast between influenza A (~10% presymptomatic) and influenza B (~0%) is striking and likely reflects biological differences in replication kinetics and innate immune suppression — challenging the assumption that all influenza strains behave similarly.
  • Directly informs quarantine policy. By quantifying precisely how much transmission occurs before symptom onset, the study gives public-health authorities a quantitative basis for deciding whether contact quarantine (beyond case isolation) is worth its social and economic cost.
  • Methodological advance with cross-pathogen validity. The individual-based Bayesian model marginalizes over unobserved infection times — a technically rigorous solution to a latent-variable problem — and generalizes across influenza and SARS-CoV-2, making it a reusable epidemiological tool.
  • Open data and code. The household datasets and full R/C++ implementation are deposited on GitHub, enabling reproducibility and future comparative studies across settings and pathogens.

Core Methods

  • Individual-based household transmission model: each household member is modelled explicitly, allowing the model to track who infected whom and when — at individual (not population-aggregate) resolution.
  • Marginalization over latent infection times: because exact infection times are unobserved, the model integrates them out analytically, correctly propagating uncertainty about when transmission occurred relative to symptom onset.
  • Presymptomatic transmission proportion: the key estimand is the fraction of secondary cases infected more than 0.5 days before the infector's symptom onset, estimated as a pathogen-specific parameter with full posterior uncertainty (95% credible intervals via MCMC).
  • Covariate modelling of susceptibility and infectiousness: age, viral load, and fever status are included as covariates predicting individual-level susceptibility and infectiousness, yielding risk ratios with credible intervals (Fig. 3–4).
  • Incubation period and infectiousness profile estimation: the model jointly estimates incubation period distributions and time-varying infectiousness profiles for each pathogen (Fig. 1–2).
  • Cross-pathogen validation: the same model is applied to a SARS-CoV-2 Omicron dataset and recovers presymptomatic transmission proportions consistent with prior literature (~65%), demonstrating methodological validity.
  • Implementation: Bayesian inference via MCMC in R 4.1.1, with computationally intensive steps parallelized via RcppParallel.
