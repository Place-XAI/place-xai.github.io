Planning sustainable urban lighting for biodiversity and society

Léa Tardieu · Chloé Beaudet · Sarah Potin · Julie Chaurand · Léa Mariton · Vincent Delbar · Maia David Published 2025-06-20 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00245-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

There is no one-size-fits-all way to dim a city. By coupling remote-sensing + ecological connectivity modeling (what nocturnal species need) with socioeconomic choice modeling (what residents will accept), the authors show that light-pollution-mitigation measures produce either trade-offs or synergies depending on the local spatial context. For Montpellier, France, they rank the best lighting compromise streetlight-by-streetlight and deliver it as an open RShiny "SustainLights" decision tool, arguing lighting policy must be tailored to each environmental and social context.

Why It Matters

  • Bridges two communities that rarely meet. Light-pollution research is usually either ecological or social; this paper puts species' connectivity needs and residents' acceptability on the same map, so dimming decisions optimize biodiversity and human comfort/safety jointly.
  • Makes "trade-off vs. synergy" spatially explicit. The same measure (e.g., switching streetlights off 11 p.m.–6 a.m.) is a win-win in some streets and a conflict in others — the work shows where, turning a political debate into a per-asset map.
  • Decision-ready at the streetlight scale. Outputs are not city averages but recommendations for each individual streetlight, the actual unit a municipality controls.
  • Ships as open infrastructure. The companion SustainLights RShiny app plus open QGIS plugins (LightPollutionToolbox, BioDispersal) let other cities replicate the workflow — research as a reusable planning tool.

Core Methods

  • Light-pollution mapping: derive spatial light-pollution indicators for the MMA from remote sensing / night-time light products (Fig. 2), using the LightPollutionToolbox QGIS plugin.
  • Ecological connectivity modeling: model landscape connectivity (least-cost / graph-based dispersal) for six nocturnal species groups and quantify baseline ecological stakes and the connectivity loss attributable to light pollution (Fig. 3, BioDispersal plugin).
  • Socioeconomic acceptability modeling: a discrete-choice / stated-preference experiment estimates residents' acceptance (and implicit willingness-to-pay) for measures such as night-time extinction windows and dimming, mapped across the metro area (Fig. 4).
  • Trade-off / synergy analysis: overlay ecological benefit and social acceptability into bivariate maps per measure (e.g., "extinction 11 p.m.–6 a.m.", Fig. 5) to reveal where dimming is win-win versus where it conflicts.
  • Decision support: integrate all layers into an RShiny "SustainLights" app that prioritizes the recommended action for each individual streetlight.

① From per-streetlight ranking to a constrained dimming-optimization solver

Extending this paperThe paper ranks each streetlight by overlaying ecology and acceptability, but it does not solve for the city-wide configuration that maximizes biodiversity benefit subject to a safety/energy budget and connectivity-corridor constraints. A direct extension: cast streetlight dimming/extinction as a combinatorial facility-state optimization (each lamp = a controllable node) and learn or search the Pareto-optimal switching pattern — turning the descriptive bivariate map into a prescriptive plan, optionally via deep reinforcement learning on the street graph.

Added value to my researchThis is structurally identical to my EV-charging efficiency-vs-equity optimization: nodes with on/off states, a benefit field, and a budget. The dimming formulation gives me a second domain to validate a transferable node-state allocation optimizer for urban infrastructure.

② Replace assumed acceptability surfaces with an explainable, transferable model

Extending this paperThe paper's acceptability maps come from a single-city choice experiment in Montpellier and may not transfer. Extending it: fit an explainable ML model (e.g., gradient boosting + SHAP, or MGWR for spatial non-stationarity) that predicts acceptability from local socioeconomic, land-use and crime/perceived-safety covariates, so the social layer can be extrapolated to cities without a bespoke survey — and so planners can see why a street resists dimming.

Added value to my researchAcceptability-as-accessibility maps directly onto my explainable spatial-prediction agenda: the same MGWR/SHAP pipeline I use for telehealth uptake and primary-care access transfers to "willingness to accept a service change," giving me a reusable spatial-attitude predictor.

③ Turn species connectivity loss into a physics-informed graph operator for dark corridors

Extending this paperThe ecological layer uses least-cost / graph connectivity per species group, recomputed for the static current network. Extending it: encode dispersal as a physics-informed graph learning problem where light acts as an edge-resistance field, so the model can predict connectivity loss under any candidate dimming scenario without re-running every species' least-cost path — enabling fast "what-if" dark-corridor design.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my physics-informed graph-learning line: light-as-resistance on a graph is the same operator family as flow/diffusion priors I want to bake into GNNs for accessibility, letting one trained operator serve both ecological corridors and human service catchments.

④ Add a temporal dimension: dynamic dimming and seasonal phenology

Extending this paperThe paper evaluates fixed measures (e.g., a single 11 p.m.–6 a.m. window). But both ecology and human activity are strongly seasonal and hourly; ALAN even reshapes growing seasons and migration. Extending it: model adaptive, time-varying dimming schedules driven by spatiotemporal demand (mobility) and species activity calendars, optimizing trade-offs hour-by-hour and season-by-season rather than with one static rule.

Added value to my researchDynamic dimming is a spatiotemporal kernel-density / mobility problem of exactly the kind I tackle with GPS-trajectory and mobile-phone data: night-time human presence becomes the demand surface that tunes when a street can safely go dark.

⑤ Fold dark-sky planning into the 15-minute / healthy-city accessibility frame

Extending this paperThe paper treats lighting as a standalone trade-off, separate from broader urban access. Extending it: integrate night-time biodiversity and dark-sky quality as an accessibility dimension — measuring residents' access to dark, biodiverse night-time green space alongside daytime services — so that dimming decisions are co-optimized with the proximity goals of a 15-minute, nature-rich city.
