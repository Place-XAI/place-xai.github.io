Measuring the knot of non-Hermitian degeneracies and non-commuting braids

Yogesh S. S. Patil · Judith Höller · Parker A. Henry · Chitres Guria · Yiming Zhang · Luyao Jiang · Nenad Kralj · Nicholas Read · Jack G. E. Harris Published 2022-07-13 DOI 10.1038/s41586-022-04796-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

In non-Hermitian coupled-oscillator systems, traversing a closed loop in control-parameter space generically produces a braid of eigenfrequencies rather than trivially returning each mode to its starting value. For systems with more than two oscillators (N > 2) these braids form a non-Abelian group — meaning the order in which loops are executed matters — and the group structure is governed by the topology of a trefoil knot formed by the degeneracy (exceptional-point) locus in parameter space. The paper confirms these predictions experimentally for N = 3 using a cavity optomechanical system, demonstrating for the first time that non-commuting spectral braids are a measurable, physical reality.

Why It Matters

  • Extends a fundamental framework from N = 2 to arbitrary N. The two-mode case (encircling a single exceptional point) has been studied for decades; this paper is the first to treat the general multi-mode case and reveal that the resulting braid group is non-Abelian — a qualitatively new phenomenon with no N = 2 analogue.
  • Links knot theory to physical observables. The degeneracy locus forms a trefoil knot in a three-sphere of control parameters; the paper shows how that knot's homotopy classes directly determine which braids are experimentally realizable, connecting abstract topology to precision measurements.
  • Non-commutation as a resource. Because the order of control loops matters (non-Abelian braiding), these systems could encode information in the sequence of parameter excursions — a potential platform for non-Abelian topological operations relevant to quantum information processing.
  • Sets the template for N-mode spectral engineering. Any application that tunes resonant systems (sensors, lasers, phononic devices, quantum circuits) must contend with exceptional-point geometry; this framework tells engineers exactly how the spectrum will respond to any control trajectory.

Core Methods

  • Braid-group theory for N-mode non-Hermitian systems: formal proof that control loops in parameter space generate elements of the Artin braid group B_N, and that for N > 2 this group is non-Abelian; the braid realized by a loop depends on how it encircles the knotted degeneracy locus.
  • Topological characterization of the degeneracy locus: showing that the EP₂ hypersurface S in the 3-sphere is a trefoil knot (for the generic N = 3 case), identified via four independent signatures: zeroes of the discriminant D, phase vortices of D, zeroes of the eigenvector indicator E, and phase vortices of E.
  • Hypersurface scanning: mapping the EP₂ locations experimentally by measuring the complex susceptibility on 61 two-dimensional sheets tiling the 3-sphere S, followed by an automated minima-finding algorithm to locate EP₂ from each signature.
  • EP₃ localization: iterative six-sheet scanning procedure around an estimated EP₃ location; the quantity d(Ψ) is defined to vanish exactly at the three-fold degeneracy, and its minimum is tracked across sheets.
  • Eigenvalue braid measurement: direct measurement of the complex eigenvalue spectrum λ(Ψ) as Ψ traverses closed control loops of different homotopy classes, demonstrating non-commutation experimentally (Fig. 4) by executing loops in two different orders and confirming the distinct final braids.
  • Optomechanical model fitting: global fit of bare resonance frequencies and coupling constants to standard optomechanical theory, enabling comparison of measured and calculated braids (Extended Data Fig. 6).

④ Spectral flow as a metaphor for health-service accessibility transitions

Extending this paperThe paper shows that when control parameters are varied continuously, eigenfrequencies can undergo non-trivial permutations — a mode that starts as "mode 1" at baseline may end as "mode 2" after a loop, even though the spectrum as a set is unchanged. This spectral flow phenomenon has a functional analogy in healthcare accessibility: when a facility closes or a population shifts, the "label" of which facility serves which neighborhood can permute discontinuously. Extending the paper: formalize accessibility reassignment events (e.g., hospital closures forcing patient redistribution) as braids in a parameter space of facility locations and population densities, and ask whether the resulting permutation group is non-Abelian — i.e., whether the order of closures matters for which patients end up unserved.

Added value to my researchThis directly reframes my 2SFCA/R2SFCA work: instead of asking for a static accessibility score, ask for the braid of service-catchment reassignments induced by a sequence of policy interventions. A non-Abelian result would mean that intervention order is a policy variable — closing rural clinic A before urban clinic B produces a different final state than the reverse — which is a practically significant and mathematically precise statement for health equity planning.

⑤ Accelerating knot-reconstruction via graph neural network layout learning

Extending this paperReconstructing the EP₂ knot requires projecting a high-dimensional cloud of measured degeneracy points onto 2D using stereographic projection, then fitting a best-fit trefoil. This pipeline — dimensionality reduction, graph layout, topological classification — is currently done by hand for each new system. Extending it with a graph neural network trained to optimize layout energy (analogous to force-directed graph drawing, but with topological constraints) would automate knot identification from raw data and generalize across different N-mode systems, vastly accelerating the experimental workflow described in the paper.
