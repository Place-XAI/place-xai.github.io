Banks, alternative institutions and the spatial–temporal ecology of racial inequality in US cities

Small, Mario L. · Akhavan, Armin · Torres, Mo · Wang, Qi Published 2021-07-05 DOI 10.1038/s41562-021-01153-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Based on more than 6 million routing queries across every block in 19 major US cities, this study finds that race is strikingly more important than class in determining access to conventional banks versus predatory alternative financial institutions (AFIs) like check cashers and payday lenders. Even affluent, college-educated, minority homeowner neighbourhoods have worse bank access than high-poverty white renter neighbourhoods. The disparity is driven not by the absence of banks but by the concentration of AFIs in minority areas — gaps too large to be explained by differences in demand preferences.

★

Why It Matters

  • Race beats class as an explanatory variable. The paper demonstrates that racial/ethnic composition predicts AFI proximity more powerfully than poverty level — a finding that cuts against economics-first explanations of financial exclusion.
  • Reframes "banking deserts" from absence to constrained choice. Disadvantaged neighbourhoods are not empty of financial institutions; they are saturated with predatory ones. The paper shows that the relevant comparison is relative proximity: nearby AFI vs. distant bank.
  • Methodological advance in travel-time accessibility. Moving from counts-per-area to multimodal travel time (walk, drive, public transport) from the centroid of every block provides a substantially more accurate picture of lived access than straight-line or area-based measures.
  • Policy relevance for the Community Reinvestment Act and AFI regulation. Results suggest both bank-incentive policies and AFI-location restrictions as potentially effective tools, and offer a template for evaluating their causal effect via natural experiments.
  • Scalable, reproducible infrastructure. Replication data (travel times aggregated to block group) and STATA/Python code are publicly available at GitHub, making the analytic framework reusable for other service types and time periods.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

19 of the largest US cities (18 cities for public transport due to missing Memphis data at extraction time). A total of 21,864 block groups across all cities; ~21,800–21,852 used per model after data quality filters.
Routing queries
More than 6 million routing queries computed from the centroid of every block, for travel by foot, car, and public transport, to the ten nearest banks and AFIs respectively.

Core Methods

  • Multimodal travel-time accessibility: for each block centroid, an algorithm calculates physical distance to the 10 nearest establishments, then computes actual travel time by foot, car, and public transport (using GraphHopper + OpenTripPlanner on OSM data + GTFS), extracting the fastest route to the nearest bank and nearest AFI separately. Results are aggregated to the block group.
  • Binary outcome logit models: the core outcome is a binary indicator of whether the nearest AFI is faster to reach than the nearest bank. Nested logistic regression models with an increasing set of covariates are estimated to test whether race effects survive demographic, economic, and built-environment controls.
  • City random-effects models with robust standard errors: given large between-city heterogeneity (New York vs. Detroit), city-level random effects are specified and standard errors are clustered within city to account for intra-city dependence.
  • Marginal prediction at specified covariate levels: adjusted probabilities are visualised at defined combinations of racial/ethnic composition (10%, 30%, 50%, 70%, 90%) and poverty levels (10% and 50%), with remaining racial groups split proportionally to produce interpretable comparisons.
  • Counterfactual demand analysis: an additional descriptive analysis contrasts affluent, educated minority homeowner neighbourhoods with poor, low-education white renter neighbourhoods to test whether demand-side preferences can plausibly account for observed spatial patterns.
  • Robustness checks: non-linear race-class interactions, income substitution for poverty, city subgroup analyses (Texas/California, AFI-restriction cities), and reliability-of-ACS-estimates sensitivity tests — all leaving core conclusions unchanged.

① Replace static routing queries with dynamic, time-of-day multimodal accessibility

Extending this paperThe paper's 6 million queries compute a single representative travel time per mode per block — presumably off-peak — and acknowledge that car and transit times are sensitive to traffic. A direct extension would incorporate time-of-day GTFS schedules and congestion-weighted road speeds (e.g., from OpenStreetMap traffic layers or historical GPS data) to produce temporal accessibility profiles: does the bank-vs-AFI gap widen at 7 pm when transit headways thin out, or on weekends when bank hours differ? This would transform the paper's snapshot into a dynamic ecology consistent with its "spatial–temporal" title. The corpus paper on experienced urban inequality using human mobility data (Xu et al., Nature Human Behaviour 2025) illustrates how mobility traces reveal temporal variations in access that static measures miss.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and 2SFCA/R2SFCA work typically assume static supply and fixed catchment sizes. Introducing time-varying transit impedance — derived from GTFS + real observed flows — into the supply-side catchment delineation would substantially improve the realism of spatial accessibility models for healthcare and financial services alike, especially for car-free low-income populations.

Cross-ref (corpus): Using human mobility data to quantify experienced urban inequalities — 10.1038/s41562-025-02282-7

② Extend the bank-vs-AFI ecology to healthcare: primary care vs. urgent-care chains

Extending this paperThe paper's comparative-proximity framework — measuring relative accessibility of a high-quality vs. lower-quality institution of the same functional type — is directly transferable to healthcare. The analogue is primary care clinic vs. urgent-care chain (or emergency room): minority neighbourhoods may be closer to expensive, fragmented urgent care while being farther from continuous primary care, perpetuating a "healthcare AFI" problem. Applying the same block-level multimodal routing across the same 19 cities would test whether healthcare mirrors banking inequality, and whether the same race-beats-class finding holds. The paper on visitation patterns and service access disparities for ageing populations (Han et al., Nature Human Behaviour 2025) confirms this kind of facility-type differential access persists at scale.

Added value to my researchThis is directly actionable for my health geography and accessibility research theme: my R2SFCA work on spatial accessibility of primary care and mental health services could adopt the same "relative proximity" framing — computing the adjusted probability that urgent care (or a mental health crisis centre) is closer than a primary care provider, decomposed by race/ethnicity at block-group resolution.

Cross-ref (corpus): Visitation patterns reveal service access disparities for ageing populations in the USA — Nature Human Behaviour (2025), cited in this paper · US cities are defined by rings and pockets with limited socioeconomic mixing — 10.1038/s44284-025-00350-7

③ Use explainable ML to unpack which neighbourhood features drive AFI concentration

Extending this paperThe paper's nested logit models identify race/ethnicity as the dominant predictor but are inherently linear with additive interactions — they cannot capture which combination of built-environment variables (commercial density, transit proximity, vacancy rate, historic redlining zone, zoning class) jointly produces AFI clustering in minority areas. A gradient-boosted model with SHAP attributions applied to the same block-group dataset would reveal the spatial feature interactions that sustain the AFI ecology, potentially identifying leverage points (e.g., specific zoning or lease-price thresholds) that logit cannot surface. The paper on historical redlining and biodiversity (Nature Human Behaviour 2023) already links legacy segregation maps to present-day spatial patterns — integrating redlining geography as a feature would directly test whether the banking disparity is a continuation of structural discrimination.

Added value to my researchThis is the core of my explainable machine learning for spatial prediction research line. A SHAP-decomposed GBM trained on block-group financial-access outcomes would produce spatially varying importance maps — something MGWR already does for linear effects. The combination (MGWR for linear spatial non-stationarity + XGBoost-SHAP for non-linear interactions) would give a richer mechanistic picture of urban financial inequality than either method alone.

Cross-ref (corpus): Historical redlining is associated with increasing geographical disparities in bird biodiversity sampling in the United States — 10.1038/s41562-023-01688-5

④ Model the supply-side location decisions of AFIs as a strategic placement game

Extending this paperThe paper deliberately stops short of causal explanation for why AFIs cluster in minority neighbourhoods. A natural extension is to model AFI entry and location decisions using a spatial competition framework: given block-group purchasing power (income × population), AFI operators maximize profit subject to regulatory constraints (distance buffers from schools, churches). Calibrating such a model on the paper's 19-city data would let researchers simulate whether tightening AFI zoning restrictions would materially improve bank proximity — translating the paper's descriptive finding into a policy-counterfactual engine. The corpus paper on urban inequality and the housing crisis (Nature Cities 2024) shows how longitudinal policy variation across US cities can be exploited as quasi-experiments for this type of causal inference.

Added value to my researchFor my facility-location optimization and EV charging equity work, the same two-sided spatial game structure applies: charging station operators target high-traffic, higher-income areas, leaving minority communities under-served. Borrowing this paper's regulatory-counterfactual framing — what happens to the efficiency-equity gap if placement is constrained by equity requirements? — would directly strengthen the normative component of my optimization studies.

Cross-ref (corpus): Urban inequality, the housing crisis and deteriorating water access in US cities — 10.1038/s44284-024-00180-z

⑤ Quantify experienced segregation in financial-service utilization using mobility traces

Extending this paperThe paper infers access from computed travel times but cannot observe actual visits. Linking anonymized GPS mobility data (e.g., SafeGraph, Spectus) to the paper's bank/AFI location layer would reveal revealed-preference utilization: do minority residents in high-access-inequality block groups actually visit banks at lower rates, or do they substitute with mobile banking? This would bridge the supply-side ecology the paper documents with demand-side behavioural responses — directly testing the paper's assertion that proximity drives use. The corpus paper on experienced income segregation and mobility patterns (Nature Communications 2021) demonstrates exactly this GPS-to-facility-visit linkage at scale across multiple US cities.

Added value to my researchMy multi-source data fusion work (mobile phone, GPS trajectories, POIs) is directly applicable here. A joint analysis of computed travel-time access (this paper's method) with actual visit rates from mobile-phone records — disaggregated by block-group racial composition — would provide the first behavioural validation of the bank-AFI ecological hypothesis, and would establish a reproducible template for merging routing-based access measures with observed mobility for health and financial equity research.

Cross-ref (corpus): Mobility patterns are associated with experienced income segregation in large US cities — 10.1038/s41467-021-24899-8
