Ubiquitous mobility data enable near-real-time emission estimates for urban traffic

Songhua Hu · Paolo Santi (MIT Senseable City Laboratory) Published 2026-04-14 DOI 10.1038/s41893-026-01798-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

By fusing footage from more than 300 traffic cameras with anonymized location data from 1.75 million mobile devices and coupling a dynamic traffic assignment model (DTALite) with a microscopic emission model (MOVES-Matrix), this framework generates near-real-time, road-level estimates of PM2.5, NOx, CO and CO2 across Manhattan without intrusive vehicle tracking. Applied to Manhattan's congestion pricing programme, the analysis finds a 16–22% reduction in traffic emissions within 8 weeks of implementation, and reveals that conventional emission inventories introduce systematic bias — for example, omitting traffic signals alone underestimates PM2.5 by 49%.

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the "long-term average" assumption in emission accounting. Conventional emission inventories use static traffic volumes and fleet averages; this framework reveals emission densities that vary from −100% to +319–513% of the average across road segments, exposing how much policy-relevant signal is hidden.
  • Enables rapid, spatially resolved policy evaluation. For the first time, decision-makers can observe how emissions respond to a major transport intervention — Manhattan's congestion pricing — within weeks rather than years, with full spatiotemporal detail.
  • Quantifies systematic biases in standard inventories. Omitting traffic signals underestimates PM2.5 by 49%; using citywide average fleet composition or speed shifts NOx estimates by −15% to +25%. These biases are not negligible for policy.
  • Scalable and non-intrusive by design. The framework uses existing urban data streams — public camera feeds and anonymized phone location data — rather than costly GPS tracking or instrumented vehicles, making it transferable to any city with adequate infrastructure.
  • Supports travel demand management simulation. Beyond measurement, the study simulates modal shift and peak-hour departure shifts, providing quantitative decarbonization targets directly usable in policy design.

Core Methods

  • Computer-vision traffic observation: AI-powered image processing of camera footage to extract road-level traffic flow rates, fleet composition (vehicle type mix), and traffic signal timing — replacing costly in-road sensors.
  • Origin–destination flow estimation: anonymized mobile phone location data processed to derive citywide OD matrices, capturing where trips originate and end across the full urban network.
  • Dynamic traffic assignment (DTALite): OD flows are calibrated against camera counts via mesoscopic simulation, producing second-by-second driving cycles (speed, acceleration profiles) for every road segment — the link between observed counts and emission-relevant behavior.
  • Microscopic emission modeling (MOVES-Matrix): driving cycles are fed into the MOVES-Matrix framework to generate per-pollutant, per-road-segment emission rates, enabling near-real-time spatial emission maps.
  • Bias analysis of conventional inventories: systematic comparison against long-term-average methods, isolating the contribution of traffic signal omission, fleet composition averaging, and speed averaging to total emission bias.
  • Counterfactual policy evaluation: pre/post comparison of emission maps to quantify the effect of Manhattan's congestion pricing, plus simulation of additional demand-management scenarios (modal shift, peak-hour departure spreading).

① Extend the framework from emission measurement to adaptive traffic signal control for decarbonization

Extending this paperThis paper reconstructs second-by-second driving cycles from camera and mobile data but uses them only for emission estimation. A direct extension is to close the loop: feed the near-real-time emission maps back into an adaptive traffic signal controller that minimizes emission exposure rather than just delay. The paper already shows that omitting signal timing underestimates PM2.5 by 49%, which implies that optimizing signal timing is among the highest-leverage interventions available — yet the paper stops short of testing this. The corpus paper on big-data-empowered traffic signal control provides the complementary intervention piece, estimating that adaptive signals could reduce urban CO2 by up to 17% in China's 100 most congested cities. Combined with this paper's emission-aware driving-cycle reconstruction, a closed-loop system becomes feasible.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal analysis work focuses on urban system coupling. A feedback loop between emission sensing and signal control is a canonical cyber-physical coupling problem. Framing it as a physics-informed graph learning task — where the graph encodes the road network, the physics prior is the emission-speed relationship from MOVES, and the learned policy adjusts signal phases — connects directly to my agenda of embedding domain knowledge into GNN architectures. This would generate a new class of health-geography outputs: not just where emissions are high, but which intersections, if signal-optimized, would produce the greatest reduction in population-weighted PM2.5 exposure.

② Transfer the framework to cities without dense camera networks using satellite-derived mobility proxies

Extending this paperThe paper's authors acknowledge that its reliance on traffic cameras and mobile phone data may limit transferability across jurisdictions with different privacy laws or data infrastructure. A direct extension is to replace or supplement camera-derived traffic counts with deep-learning inference from satellite imagery — predicting inter-regional origin–destination flows from visual urban features (road density, land use, built form) rather than from sensor data. The corpus paper on predicting human mobility flows in cities using deep learning on satellite imagery demonstrates exactly this substitution at scale, recovering OD matrices with reasonable accuracy where ground sensors are absent. Integrating that approach as a fallback data source into this paper's DTALite calibration step would extend the framework to the Global South and smaller cities.

Specifically, training a model on camera-rich cities (Manhattan, London) and transferring to camera-poor cities via satellite image embeddings is a direct application of foundation-model transfer in GeoAI. ③ Quantify the equity distribution of congestion pricing's emission benefits across neighborhoods

Extending this paperThe paper shows that the congestion pricing emission reductions are spatially uneven and evolve over time, but it does not link this spatial unevenness to the socioeconomic characteristics of the populations bearing or benefiting from those changes. A critical extension is to overlay the paper's road-level NOx/PM2.5 change maps with census-derived demographic data — income, race, transit access — to ask whether the emission improvements accrue disproportionately to wealthier areas, or whether the areas that see emission increases (darker red roads outside the congestion zone) are lower-income. The corpus paper on the co-benefits of compact urban development provides the analytical template: it combines emission reductions with health-benefit valuation and distributional analysis across Chinese cities.

④ Replace static fleet composition averages with multimodal latent OD flow reconstruction

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that using citywide average fleet composition introduces −15% to +25% NOx estimation error. The root cause is that fleet composition varies systematically by trip purpose, origin–destination pair, and time of day — correlations invisible to a simple spatial average. An extension is to replace the single-mode fleet assumption with a multimodal, time-dependent OD decomposition: separating truck, taxi, private car and ride-hail flows from the same mobile phone signal using latent variable methods, then assigning mode-specific emission factors. The corpus paper on uncovering latent urban mobility patterns via smart-card and survey data fusion shows exactly this multimodal TD-OD recovery from mixed data streams.

Added value to my researchMy data science and multi-source fusion agenda benefits directly here. The smart-card + survey fusion approach in the corpus paper maps cleanly onto mobile phone + camera fusion in this paper: both involve recovering a richer latent variable (mode, trip purpose) from sparse, aggregate observations. Methodologically, embedding this latent decomposition into a spatiotemporal kernel density estimation framework — one of my core methods — would allow continuous, mode-stratified emission density maps. For health geography, mode-specific emission maps matter because pedestrian exposure to truck emissions on a shared street segment differs substantially from car-dominated exposure.

Extending this paperThis paper and the 15-minute city literature have developed in near-parallel but have never been coupled: proximity-based city research asks whether people can meet daily needs within 15 minutes, while this paper tracks whether people actually choose to drive — and what emissions result.
