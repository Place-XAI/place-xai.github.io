Natural infrastructure in sustaining global urban freshwater ecosystem services

Min Gon Chung · Kenneth A. Frank · Yadu Pokhrel · Thomas Dietz · Jianguo Liu Published 2021-10-21 DOI 10.1038/s41893-021-00786-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 317 cities and 2,113 watersheds worldwide, protected wetlands sustain freshwater provision and protected forests improve dam performance for sediment reduction and hydropower — yet urban flood mitigation depends primarily on reducing impervious surfaces and expanding green spaces within cities, not on upstream protected areas. Natural infrastructure and built infrastructure are complementary, not substitutes: integrating them is the strategic path to sustainable urban water management under rapid urbanization and climate change.

★

Why It Matters

  • First global network analysis linking natural and built infrastructure for urban water. The egocentric network framework simultaneously models watershed-level (sending) and city-level (receiving) dynamics across four distinct freshwater ecosystem services.
  • Resolves a persistent knowledge gap. Natural infrastructure (protected areas, wetlands, green space) has been advocated rhetorically but rarely quantified in relation to dams and grey infrastructure at a worldwide scale — this study provides the multilevel evidence base.
  • Differentiates by ecosystem service type. Forest cover in PAs helps sediment reduction and hydropower; wetlands help freshwater provision; but flood mitigation is driven by intra-urban green space. This granularity translates into targeted, non-generic policy.
  • Identifies spatial priorities across geographies. From 2000 to 2016, China and India constructed large dams in 11–16% of relevant watersheds with no new protected area designations — quantifying a governance gap with immediate policy relevance.
  • Advances the sustainable development agenda. Results connect directly to SDGs 6 (clean water), 7 (clean energy), 11 (sustainable cities), and 15 (life on land), providing evidence for cross-goal co-benefits of natural infrastructure investment.

Study coverage
317 cities and 2,113 watersheds worldwide (1,156 freshwater source watersheds, 637 flood watersheds across 189 cities, 454 hydropower watersheds across 189 cities). Sediment regulation

Flood mitigation
Global Flood Hazard Maps (Dottori et al.) — 100-year return-period flood extent and depth at 30 arcsecond (~1 km) resolution, from the Global Flood Awareness System 2D hydrodynamic model.
Hydropower production
Global Power Plant Database — operational hydropower dams >1 MW; ~89% of global installed capacity; aggregated per watershed. Power lines from OpenStreetMap to identify dams within 100 km of cities.
Protected areas
World Database on Protected Areas (WDPA) — terrestrial PAs legally designated at national/subnational level; dissolved to avoid double-counting; intersected per watershed in ArcGIS 10.3.1.
Forest & wetland cover
Global land cover data at 1 km resolution (forest cover %); Global Lakes and Wetlands Database (GLWD) at 30 arcsecond (~1 km) resolution. Proportions of cover in PAs and non-PAs calculated per watershed.
Dams
Global Reservoir and Dam Database (GRanD) — dams >15 m height and >0.1 km³ reservoir; construction year, capacity, location. Dam density = dams per 100 km of river length (HydroSHEDS at 30 arcsecond resolution).
Watershed delineation
USGS HydroSHEDS at 30 arcsecond (~1 km); HydroBASINS levels 7 and 8 for flood and hydropower watershed sizing.
Urban extents
Global Administrative Database (GADM); where unavailable, global urban extent from Schneider et al. (MODIS-based); for the USA, USCB Cartographic Boundary Files.
Socioeconomics
Urban GDP-PPP (0.5° gridded dataset, 2010); annual precipitation and temperature from WorldClim at 1 km resolution; irrigation area from GFSAD (Global Food Security-Support Analysis Data) at 1 km; impervious surface from Global Man-made Impervious Surface (GMIS) dataset at 30 m resolution.
Time window
Freshwater ES: 2000–2010; dam and PA spatial changes tracked 2000–2016; socioeconomic covariates circa 2010.

Network model code at github.com/mingonchung/urbanfreshwaterES; data from corresponding author on request.

Core Methods

  • Metacoupling-based conceptual framework: Treats cities as receiving systems and watersheds as sending systems of freshwater ecosystem services, capturing both local/adjacent and distant telecoupled interactions (e.g., interbasin transfers and aqueducts).
  • Egocentric network analysis (multilevel modeling): Each city (ego) is nested with its multiple source watersheds (alters); Level 1 models watershed- and tie-level characteristics (forest cover in PAs, wetland cover, dam density, watershed size, distance, elevation, slope, irrigation); Level 2 models city-level characteristics (IWS programmes, urban population, GDP-PPP, temperature, precipitation).
  • Four ecosystem service indicators: Freshwater provision (surface runoff from ISIMIP multimodel ensemble), sediment regulation (suspended sediment flux from WBMsed), flood mitigation (flood extent ratio from Global Flood Hazard Maps), hydropower production (installed capacity from Global Power Plant Database).
  • Log-log (elasticity) transformation: All dependent and independent variables log-transformed; coefficients interpreted as elasticities. Restricted maximum likelihood (REML) estimation in R 4.0.3; variance inflation factors checked for multicollinearity.
  • Spatial change analysis (2000–2016): Overlay of new large dam construction and new PA designations per watershed; identifies watersheds gaining only dams, only PAs, both, or neither — used to map policy gaps across geographies.
  • Alternative model robustness checks: Three alternative models tested: (1) forest/wetland in non-PAs; (2) forest/wetland in both PAs and non-PAs; (3) reservoir storage capacity instead of dam density.

① Replace the cross-sectional multilevel model with a spatiotemporal panel to establish causality

Extending this paperThe paper's authors explicitly acknowledge that their cross-sectional multilevel model establishes associations, not causal relationships. A direct extension: reconstruct the same egocentric network annually for 2000–2020 as panel data, then apply a difference-in-differences or synthetic-control design using newly designated PAs as quasi-experimental events — exploiting the spatial change map already shown in Fig. 2 as an instrument. This would elevate the contribution from correlation to causal inference, and test whether the complementarity of natural and built infrastructure is robust to temporal confounders such as regional economic development and climate trends. The corpus paper on urban flooding across 306 Chinese cities demonstrates the feasibility of multi-year, multi-city panel designs at comparable scale.

Added value to my researchPanel egocentric network analysis is directly applicable to my spatiotemporal work on urban health and service accessibility: the city-as-ego and facility/service-catchments-as-alters structure mirrors R2SFCA delineation. A temporal panel design would let me attribute changes in healthcare or green-space accessibility to specific policy interventions (facility openings, transit expansions) using the same quasi-experimental logic — moving my GeoAI models from predictive to causal.

② Map equity of natural infrastructure benefits across income gradients within and across cities

Extending this paperThe paper's multilevel model controls for city-level GDP-PPP but does not decompose within-city distributional heterogeneity in freshwater ES access — who inside a city benefits from upstream PAs or urban green space depends heavily on neighborhood income. An extension: link the paper's watershed-level ES outputs to fine-scale (census-tract or 100 m grid) socioeconomic layers, then compute a freshwater-ES Gini coefficient per city. This would test whether wealthier urban districts systematically capture more of the flood mitigation co-benefits of urban green space, and whether PA-driven sediment reduction preferentially improves water quality for high-income water utilities. The corpus paper on green–blue adaptation and gentrification in African cities provides a theoretical frame for interpreting such inequities.

Added value to my researchThis equity decomposition connects directly to my work on spatial accessibility disparities and facility-location efficiency-vs-equity optimization. Extending it to ecosystem services: an equity-weighted ES accessibility score (analogous to 2SFCA supply-to-demand ratio) that captures not only proximity to green infrastructure but also the socioeconomic stratification of its benefits — a natural bridge between urban health geography and environmental justice.

Cross-ref (corpus): The gentrification paradox of green–blue adaptation in African cities — 10.1038/s44284-026-00432-0

③ Embed explainable ML to rank watershed characteristics driving each ecosystem service

Extending this paperThe paper's restricted maximum likelihood multilevel model yields linear log-log elasticity coefficients — useful for inference but structurally constrained. An extension: replace Level 1 with a gradient boosting or random forest layer and apply SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) to rank which watershed characteristics (forest in PA, dam density, elevation, slope, irrigation) most influence each of the four ES, while retaining the multilevel structure at Level 2 for city-scale effects. This would reveal nonlinearities (e.g., threshold effects in forest cover needed to meaningfully reduce sediment) that the log-log form cannot capture, and produce spatially resolved importance maps to guide PA investment priorities.

Added value to my researchExplainable ML for hierarchical spatial data is a core theme of my GeoAI agenda. Applying SHAP to a multilevel setting where Level 1 predictors operate at the watershed and Level 2 at the city mirrors my spatially-stratified SHAP analyses of health accessibility drivers — both involve interpreting feature importance in models where observations are spatially nested. A methods paper demonstrating spatially-stratified XAI in an ecosystem-services context would generalize my existing accessibility work into a new substantive domain.

④ Build a global nature-based solution mapping layer on top of this paper's network

Extending this paperThe paper focuses on PAs (a formal conservation instrument) and IWS programmes, but the broader landscape of urban nature-based solutions (NbS) — green roofs, bioswales, constructed wetlands, riparian buffer strips — is not systematically mapped in its network. The corpus paper on global mapping of urban NbS for climate adaptation provides exactly this inventory. An extension: overlay the NbS mapping layer onto this paper's city–watershed network to test whether informal or semi-engineered NbS provide incremental ES benefits on top of formal PAs, and whether they disproportionately substitute for PAs in cities where formal designation is absent (e.g., China and India as identified in Fig. 2).

Added value to my researchMulti-source data fusion — combining remote sensing (for impervious surface and green space), administrative boundaries (PAs), and point-based NbS inventory data — is a direct extension of my work on fusing mobile phone records, POIs, and remote sensing for urban health analysis. The NbS overlay task is a spatial data fusion problem: aligning heterogeneous vector and raster sources at multiple resolutions, exactly the type of problem my GeoAI pipeline is designed to handle.

⑤ Couple the ES network model with an urban growth projection to stress-test future freshwater security

Extending this paperThe paper's cross-sectional design does not project how the natural-vs-built infrastructure balance will shift as cities grow and as climate changes. An extension: couple this network model with SSP-consistent urban expansion scenarios and a projected PA expansion pathway, then simulate future freshwater ES flows under each scenario. The corpus paper on urban forest growth projections demonstrates that city-scale greenery changes are foreseeable and quantifiable. The question for this paper: if emerging megacities in South Asia and Sub-Saharan Africa follow a dam-heavy expansion path without complementary PA designation (as observed in China and India 2000–2016), by how much will freshwater provision and sediment regulation degrade by 2050?

Added value to my researchScenario-based spatiotemporal forecasting is integral to my work on urban agglomeration, regional collaboration, and facility planning under population growth. Adapting agent-based modeling (one of my core methods) to simulate city-watershed interaction dynamics — where urban growth agents generate demand shocks and PA expansion agents respond — would operationalize the metacoupling framework as a forward-looking simulation tool, directly informing strategic decision-making at the intersection of urban health geography and freshwater sustainability.
