Representation in science and trust in scientists in the USA

James N. Druckman · Katherine Ognyanova · Alauna Safarpour · Jonathan Schulman · Kristin Lunz Trujillo · Ata Aydin Uslu · Jon Green · Matthew A. Baum · Alexi Quintana-Mathé · Hong Qu · Roy H. Perlis · David M. J. Lazer Published 2025-12-08 DOI 10.1038/s41562-025-02358-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Trust gaps between underrepresented Americans (women, Black people, rural residents, religious people, less educated, lower economic status) and scientists have been stable for 50 years. These groups exhibit significantly greater trust in scientists who share their own demographic characteristics, driven by perceptions of greater benevolence and integrity — not competence. Overrepresented groups, by contrast, are largely indifferent to scientist demographics. Diversifying the scientific workforce is therefore a concrete, evidence-backed mechanism for closing these long-standing trust gaps.

★

Why It Matters

  • Pinpoints the structural roots of science distrust. The 50-year stability of trust gaps — across race, gender, rurality, religiosity, education, and class — shows this is a systemic problem, not a temporary polarization artifact.
  • Provides a causal mechanism, not just correlation. Two pre-registered conjoint experiments isolate demographic concordance as a driver of trust decisions, distinguishing benevolence and integrity from competence as the operative dimensions.
  • Links distrust directly to life outcomes. Members of lower-trusting groups had higher COVID-19 mortality rates, illustrating the public health cost of unaddressed trust gaps.
  • Delivers an actionable policy implication. Expanding scientific representation — beyond gender/race to rurality and economic class — is identified as a structural remedy that does not appear to alienate overrepresented groups (with the exceptions of religiosity and educational prestige).
  • Cross-domain relevance. The concordance mechanism is shown to extend to trust in physicians, broadening the implications for healthcare access and patient–provider relationships.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Historical trend data
General Social Survey (GSS) — USA probability sample survey tracking confidence in the scientific community since 1973. Data used through 2024, covering 32 survey years. Annual unweighted sample sizes range from ~1,372 to ~4,510; total observations across years exceed 70,000. Groups tracked: gender, race (Black vs. white only), rural/urban residence, religiosity, education, and class.
Similarity-to-scientists survey
COVID States Project (opt-in non-probability online panel, 22 vendors). Collected 29 June – 1 August 2023. Measures: trust in scientists (4-point scale) and perceived similarity to 12 professions (0–100 scale). Effective sample sizes: N = 23,217 (trust), N = 22,688 (similarity), N = 22,635 (combined regression). Composition: 51% female, 48% male; age 18–65+. Not pre-registered.
Conjoint study 1
Bovitz Inc. Forthright panel — nationally representative quota-based USA non-probability sample. Collected 11–15 December 2023. N = 1,220 respondents; each completed 10 conjoint tasks (scientist or physician choice) = 12,220 observations. Outcome: general trust (which scientist's advice to follow). Pre-registered 8 December 2023.
Conjoint study 2
Bovitz Inc. Forthright panel. Collected 17–25 January 2025. N = 3,031 respondents; 10 tasks each; 5 randomly assigned outcome conditions (general trust, trustworthiness, integrity, benevolence, competence). Total observations per dimension: ~5,860–6,210. Composition: 54% female, 45% male; avg. age 45.79. Pre-registered 11 January 2025.
Conjoint design
Seven attributes per scientist profile: gender (male/female), race/ethnicity (white, Black, Hispanic/Latino, Asian American), experience (low/high), educational prestige (public / Ivy League), religiosity (signals religion / civic engagement), rural/urban upbringing, and class background (lower/middle/upper).

Core Methods

  • Longitudinal descriptive analysis: weighted time-series plots of trust by demographic group from GSS (1973–2024), establishing the 50-year stability of trust gaps.
  • Ordinary least-squares (OLS) regression: regressing trust in scientists and perceived similarity on demographic indicators, controlling for partisanship, ideology, and conspiratorial thinking (Fig. 2); delta-method standard errors for conjoint marginal effects (Figs. 3–4).
  • Conjoint survey experiment (study 1): forced-choice design (Scientist A vs. B) with 7 randomly varied attributes; outcome = probability of choosing a scientist; effects estimated for match (shared attribute) vs. non-match conditions. 10 tasks per respondent.
  • Conjoint survey experiment (study 2): replication with 5 randomly assigned outcome dimensions (general trust, trustworthiness, integrity, benevolence, competence); enables parsing which trustworthiness facets drive concordance preferences.
  • Trust-change analysis: examines whether accumulating matched scientist exposures during the conjoint shifts respondents' stated general trust in scientists — a priming/updating test.
  • Statistical software: Stata 16.1 and R 4.3.1; all tests two-tailed; pairwise deletion for missing values; pre-registration on AsPredicted for both conjoint studies.

① Spatialize the trust gap: map scientist-population demographic divergence at sub-national scale

Extending this paperThe paper treats geography as a binary rural/urban indicator and pools the entire USA. A direct extension: geolocate the scientific workforce at the county or metro level (using NSF/NIH grant data and institutional geocodes), compute a demographic divergence index between local scientist characteristics and local population (race, rurality, class), and map how this index co-varies with county-level trust in scientists. This would transform the paper's population-level finding into a spatially explicit one — revealing which regions face the largest representation-trust deficits and prioritizing where recruitment or science-communication interventions are most needed.

Added value to my researchThis is directly applicable to my health-geography accessibility work: the same divergence-index logic maps onto primary-care or mental-health facility catchment areas where provider demographics diverge from patient demographics — informing a "concordance-aware" 2SFCA that weights not just distance but demographic match between provider and population.

② Combine concordance effects with digital health literacy to model information-seeking behavior

Extending this paperThe paper shows that trust in scientists shapes willingness to follow advice, but the pathway from trust to actual health behavior also depends on digital health literacy — the capacity to find, evaluate, and use health information online. A natural extension: link the paper's concordance-trust mechanism to a multilevel model of health information-seeking, where scientist demographic match raises initial trust and health literacy then determines whether that trust translates into behavior change. This would require combining the paper's conjoint design with a health literacy module, potentially via a follow-on to the COVID States Project.

Added value to my researchThis connects to my work on telehealth and digital divides: rural, lower-SES, and minority populations — the same groups with lower scientist trust — are also those with lower digital literacy and telehealth adoption. A concordance-adjusted trust model could refine spatial accessibility estimates for digital health services, separating supply-side barriers (no broadband) from demand-side barriers (low trust in the information source).

Cross-ref (corpus): A global survey on trust, digital health literacy and health information quality — 10.1038/s44360-026-00102-4

③ Model trust-gap dynamics using an agent-based framework with feedback between diversification and trust

Extending this paperThe paper documents equilibrium trust gaps and identifies concordance as a lever, but treats the system as static — it cannot predict how trust gaps evolve as representation improves. An agent-based model (ABM) could simulate the co-evolution of scientific workforce demographics and population trust: as underrepresented scientists enter the workforce, concordance effects raise trust in target communities, which may in turn attract more students from those groups into science — a positive feedback loop (or its absence). The paper's conjoint estimates provide empirically calibrated parameters for the ABM's behavioral rules.

Added value to my researchABM is one of my core methods. Calibrating it to this paper's conjoint effect sizes gives a real empirical grounding for simulating community-level adoption of public health recommendations — directly relevant to SIR-style epidemic models where trust mediates compliance with health advice (vaccination, social distancing). The ABM outputs could be overlaid on my spatiotemporal KDE surfaces to identify where trust-driven non-compliance clusters geographically.

④ Extend concordance to patient–provider match in healthcare access and explain it with GeoAI

Extending this paperThe paper already includes physician choices alongside scientist choices (for stimulus sampling), but buries the physician results in supplementary sections. A direct extension: fully analyze and publish the physician concordance data, then link those results to revealed healthcare utilization — do patients in counties with higher demographic mismatch between providers and population show lower utilization rates, longer delays to care, or worse outcomes? Administrative claims data (HCUP, CMS) could answer this, bridging the paper's stated-preference design with observational behavior.

Added value to my researchThis is the core of my spatial accessibility of primary care and mental health agenda. If provider-patient demographic concordance acts as a trust multiplier on utilization, then my 2SFCA/R2SFCA models need a concordance weight in the demand function — a novel and defensible correction to the standard formulation. Explainable ML (SHAP) could then decompose which concordance dimensions (race, rurality, gender) drive utilization variation at the census-tract level.

⑤ Intersect the urban–rural trust gap with urban–rural health outcome disparities to identify high-priority intervention zones

Extending this paperRurality is one of the paper's five underrepresented dimensions — rural residents report less trust in scientists and less perceived similarity — but the paper cannot say whether this trust deficit is larger in regions where rural–urban health disparities are also most severe. Extending it: couple the paper's rurality trust coefficient with county-level health outcome data (life expectancy, chronic disease burden, unmet care need) to produce a combined trust-deficit × health-burden score. High-scoring counties would be the most impacted by science distrust and the highest-priority targets for representation-based interventions or concordance-oriented science communication.
