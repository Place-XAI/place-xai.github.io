Social disparities in neighborhood flood exposure in 44,698 urban neighborhoods in Latin America

Josiah L. Kephart · Usama Bilal · Nelson Gouveia · Olga Lucia Sarmiento · Emily Shingara · Karla Rangel Moreno · Maryia Bakhtsiyarava · Juan Pablo Rodriguez · Salvador Ayala · Gabriel Carrasco-Escobar · Ana V. Diez Roux Published 2025-02-10 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00203-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across 276 Latin American cities (44,698 neighborhoods, 228 million residents, 117 satellite-observed floods, 2000–2018), flood exposure follows a steep social gradient: roughly 1 in 4 residents of the lowest-education neighborhoods lived in a neighborhood that flooded, versus 1 in 20 in the highest-education neighborhoods — a 4.3× regional gap. Within cities the gap is even sharper (median slope index of inequality ≈ 8.3, present in 80% of cities). Neighborhoods that were lower-education, coastal, less dense, greener, peripheral and steeper flooded more. This is a descriptive finding (not causal), and authors urge that flood adaptation be targeted to lower-SES neighborhoods.

Why It Matters

  • First neighborhood-scale evidence for a whole region. Prior flood-equity work in Latin America was national or subnational; this is initial evidence of intra-urban social inequity in flooding across eight countries — exactly the scale where targeted adaptation happens.
  • Quantifies an environmental-justice gap, consistently. The lowest-education quintile is 4.3× more likely to be in a flooded neighborhood region-wide, and 80% of individual cities show the same direction — a robust, transferable signal rather than a single case study.
  • Maps the urban form behind flooding. Coastal location (OR ≈ 46), lower population/intersection density, greater greenness and peripheral position raise the odds — linking flood hazard to the geometry of segregated, under-serviced urban peripheries.
  • Directly policy-actionable. The neighborhood-level slope index of inequality tells city governments where to prioritize flood adaptation and recovery, framing flooding as a human (not only economic) impact.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Study scope
44,698 neighborhoods in 276 cities across 8 countries (Argentina, Brazil, Chile, Colombia, Costa Rica, Guatemala, Mexico, Panama), totaling ≈ 228 million residents. 50 cities with no recorded flood were excluded; El Salvador, Nicaragua, Peru dropped for lack of neighborhood-level data.
Flood data
Global Flood Database (GFD) — satellite (MODIS) flood extents at 250 m, daily, 2000–2018, derived from Dartmouth Flood Observatory (DFO) events. Of 273 DFO floods affecting SALURBAL cities, 117 (43%) were captured by GFD and used here.
SES proxy
Neighborhood educational attainment (% of adults 25+ who completed primary school), from national censuses (years 2002–2018). Neighborhoods = country-specific units analogous to US census tracts (median pop. 2,137; median area 0.36 km²).
Urban features
SALURBAL-harmonized population density, intersection density, NDVI greenness, coastal flag (<1 km from coast), distance-to-center, plus elevation/slope from a 30-m topography model; city-level GDP per capita and Köppen climate zone.
Time window
Floods 2000–2018; census 2002–2018; analysis cross-sectional/descriptive.
Availability
GFD public via Google Earth Engine; SALURBAL urban data at data.lacurbanhealth.org; census via national agencies (links at Drexel UHC); analysis code on GitHub. Some census data obtained directly from agencies (not all redistributable).

Core Methods

  • Exposure construction: overlay GFD flood rasters with SALURBAL neighborhood boundaries; a neighborhood is "flooded" if any flooding occurred within its boundary, cumulating distinct events 2000–2018.
  • Cross-city disparities: group neighborhoods into quintiles of educational attainment and report % of residents exposed by quintile and country (descriptive summaries).
  • Within-city disparities: a city-specific Poisson model with flooding (yes/no) on ordinal education rescaled 0–1, yielding a slope index of inequality (SII) / relative-risk comparing lowest vs highest education within each city.
  • Urban-form associations: multilevel logistic regression (random intercept per city, fixed effect for country) of neighborhood flooding on z-scored neighborhood and city features (density, intersection density, NDVI, coastal, distance-to-center, slope, altitude, GDP, climate).
  • Sensitivity analyses: restrict to 196 cities with ≥2 floods; swap NDVI for % greenspace; collinearity checked (max Spearman 0.57). Computed in R 4.4.1 with lme4.

① From satellite-place exposure to human-activity exposure

Extending this paperThe paper concedes its core limitation: it measures whether a neighborhood polygon intersects a flood, not whether people were actually in harm's way — exposure is place-based and static. A direct extension fuses the GFD flood extents with mobile-phone / GPS dwell-time data so that exposure is weighted by where lower-education residents actually spend time (home, work, transit), turning the binary "neighborhood flooded" into a person-time exposure that could either widen or narrow the measured 4.3× gap.

② Replace the SII descriptor with an explainable predictive model of where inequity concentrates

Extending this paperThe paper reports a city-specific slope index of inequality but never asks which urban features explain why some cities have SII ≈ 1.3 and others ≈ 100. Extending it: train an explainable ML ensemble (gradient boosting + SHAP) on the city-level SII using coastality, density gradient, GDP, climate zone and segregation, surfacing the configurations that produce extreme intra-urban flood inequity — converting four descriptive tables into a transferable, interrogable model.

③ Couple flood-exposure inequity with accessibility loss to essential services

Extending this paperThe paper stops at exposure; it never asks what flooding does to residents' access to clinics, pharmacies or shelters. Extending it: overlay flood extents on the street network to compute flood-induced accessibility degradation — how much 2SFCA/R2SFCA service access collapses for the lowest-education quintile when peripheral roads inundate — turning a hazard map into a health-system-resilience map.

④ Make the analysis longitudinal and causal-leaning

Extending this paperThe authors are explicit that the design is descriptive and cannot say whether floods depress SES or low-SES people sort into flood-prone land. Extending it: exploit the panel of 117 dated floods plus multi-census education to build a difference-in-differences / event-study at the neighborhood level, probing the bidirectional flood↔SES dynamic the paper flags but cannot test — and test whether protective infrastructure merely displaces risk to the periphery.

⑤ A physics-informed graph model that downscales the missing hyperlocal floods

Extending this paperGFD misses 47% of DFO events at 250 m — precisely the hyperlocal floods likely concentrated in poor neighborhoods, biasing the disparity downward. Extending it: train a physics-informed graph / GNN flow model over the drainage-and-street network that ingests slope, elevation, imperviousness and precipitation to downscale and infill sub-250 m flood probability, correcting the measurement bias the paper warns about and sharpening the equity estimate.
