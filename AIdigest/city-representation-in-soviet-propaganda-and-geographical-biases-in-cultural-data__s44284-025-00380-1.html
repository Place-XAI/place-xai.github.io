City representation in Soviet propaganda and geographical biases in cultural data

Mikhail V. Tamm · Mila Oiva · Ksenia D. Mukhina · Mark Mets · Maximilian Schich Published 2026-02-02 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00380-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

In a large corpus of Soviet Novosti Dnya ("News of the Day") newsreels, how often a city is mentioned grows superlinearly with city size — bigger cities get disproportionately more coverage than their population alone would predict. On top of this size effect, representation is systematically biased by economic specialization and geography: cities with major hydroelectric and steel works were overrepresented, while the industrial heartland and non-European socialist countries were underrepresented. The authors deliver this through an iterative quantitative-then-qualitative feedback loop, turning measurable representational bias into an interpretable, transferable method for cultural data.

Core Methods

  • Superlinear scaling fit: model number of city mentions as a power law of city population (mentions ∝ populationβ, β>1), with confidence intervals, computed separately for each census period (Extended Data Fig. 2).
  • Population-only vs. full model: compare a baseline that uses only population against a richer model adding economic specialization and geographical location as predictors, to isolate over/under-representation (Fig. 2).
  • Information-theoretic feature selection: classify hypothetical city parameters as relevant vs. irrelevant via a predetermined information-theoretic criterion (Extended Data Fig. 1 workflow).
  • Quantitative-qualitative feedback loop: obtain quantitative bias estimates, qualitatively interpret model outliers (close reading of newsreel outlines), then feed interpretations back to refine the hypothesis — an iterative refinement loop.
  • Optimization stack: all models fit with custom code in Wolfram Mathematica 13.1; model comparison and per-city outputs released as spreadsheets.

① Turn the mentions-vs-population scaling into a reusable "representation accessibility" audit

Extending this paperThe paper measures bias as deviation from a single superlinear mentions ∝ populationβ curve, fitted per census decade. A direct extension: replace the global power law with a locally varying scaling exponent (geographically weighted), so the over/under-representation residuals become a spatial surface rather than a list of outliers — revealing whether the bias regime itself shifts between Soviet regions and across the three periods.

Added value to my researchThis is structurally identical to my 2SFCA/R2SFCA work: "mentions per capita" is a supply-side accessibility ratio. I can borrow the paper's information-theoretic feature selection to decide which contextual covariates legitimately belong in a healthcare-access model, and adopt geographically weighted scaling as a principled alternative to fixed catchments.

④ Add geographical isolation and network position as explicit predictors

Extending this paperThe paper finds non-European socialist countries and the industrial heartland underrepresented, but treats geography largely as location/specialization labels. Extending it: encode each city's position in the transport/administrative network (centrality, isolation, distance-to-Moscow) and test whether network remoteness independently predicts under-representation beyond size and specialization.

⑤ Treat representation bias as a longitudinal, demographically coupled process

Extending this paperThe paper fits three static census snapshots (1959/1970/1979) separately. Extending it: model representation and population jointly over time, asking whether coverage leads or lags population/industrial growth — does a city get filmed because it grew, or grow in salience because it was filmed? A dynamic coupling would turn three snapshots into a causal-temporal story.
