A generative model for inorganic materials design

Claudio Zeni · Robert Pinsler · Daniel Zügner · Andrew Fowler · Matthew Horton · Xiang Fu · Zilong Wang · Aliaksandra Shysheya · Jonathan Crabbé · Shoko Ueda · Roberto Sordillo · Lixin Sun · Jake Smith · Bichlien Nguyen · Hannes Schulz · Sarah Lewis · Chin-Wei Huang · Ziheng Lu · Yichi Zhou · Han Yang · Hongxia Hao · Jielan Li · Chunlei Yang · Wenjie Li · Ryota Tomioka · Tian Xie Published 2025-01-16 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-08628-5 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

MatterGen is a diffusion-based generative model that produces stable, diverse inorganic crystal structures across the periodic table and can be fine-tuned to satisfy a broad range of property constraints — including chemistry, symmetry, and scalar properties such as magnetic density, bandgap, and bulk modulus. Compared with prior generative models, MatterGen generates structures more than twice as likely to be new and stable, and more than ten times closer to the local DFT energy minimum. As proof of concept, the team synthesized one of MatterGen's proposed materials and measured its bulk modulus to be within 20% of the design target, establishing MatterGen as a foundational generative model for inorganic materials discovery.

Why It Matters

  • Breaks through the candidate ceiling of screening-based approaches. Existing high-throughput methods are limited to ~106–107 known materials; MatterGen directly generates structurally novel candidates outside this set, unlocking a vastly larger design space.
  • Breadth of property conditioning is unprecedented. A single fine-tunable framework spans chemistry, symmetry, magnetic, electronic, and mechanical properties — previous models were constrained to formation energy alone or a narrow element subset.
  • Efficiency advantage over established search methods. For quinary chemical systems, MatterGen achieves comparable or superior hull coverage with only 10,240 generated samples versus ~70,000 for substitution and 600,000 for random structure search.
  • Multi-objective design demonstrated. The supply-chain-risk magnet task (jointly optimizing magnetic density and HHI score) shows that competing constraints can be satisfied simultaneously, relevant to real-world critical-materials challenges.
  • Experimental validation closes the loop. Synthesis of TaCr2O6 with measured bulk modulus within 20% of target confirms that generative outputs are genuinely synthesizable, not just computationally stable.

Core Methods

  • Joint diffusion process for crystalline materials: MatterGen simultaneously corrupts and denoises atom types (categorical masked diffusion), fractional coordinates (wrapped Normal distribution respecting periodic boundary), and periodic lattice (symmetric diffusion toward a cubic-mean reference). This three-component joint process is the key architectural innovation over prior work.
  • Equivariant score network: a graph neural network with SE(3)-equivariant outputs for coordinates/lattice and invariant outputs for atom types, encoding physical symmetry constraints directly into architecture without learning them from data.
  • Adapter-based fine-tuning with classifier-free guidance: small tunable adapter modules are injected into each layer of the pre-trained score network and conditioned on encoded property labels; classifier-free guidance steers generation toward target property values without modifying the base model weights.
  • Multi-objective conditional generation: adapter modules can be stacked for multiple simultaneous property constraints (demonstrated for joint magnetic density + HHI supply-chain-risk optimization).
  • MatterSim MLFF pre-relaxation: the machine-learning force field MatterSim is used to pre-relax and pre-filter candidates before expensive DFT calculations, dramatically reducing the DFT budget needed to evaluate stability.
  • DFT evaluation pipeline: stability defined as energy above hull < 0.1 eV/atom after DFT relaxation; novelty checked via ordered-disordered structure matcher against Alex-MP-ICSD.
  • Experimental synthesis and characterization: Rietveld refinement of X-ray diffraction spectra confirms crystal structure; nanoindentation measures Young's modulus, converted to bulk modulus via DFT-computed Poisson ratio.

① Retarget MatterGen's fine-tuning strategy for geospatial property prediction

Extending this paperMatterGen's adapter-based fine-tuning is designed for labelled property datasets that are small relative to the unlabelled pretraining corpus — exactly the regime encountered in most scientific domains. A direct extension: apply the same pretraining-then-fine-tuning paradigm to molecular property prediction on non-crystal data (e.g., drug-like molecules), using MatterGen's equivariant score network as a universal 3D molecular backbone. The paper's own ablations show that scaling training data from MP-20 to Alex-MP-20 drives a 70% SUN increase, suggesting the marginal value of more data is still high — an invitation to incorporate even larger open databases (OQMD, AFLOW).

Added value to my researchThe adapter fine-tuning philosophy maps directly onto my work on geospatial foundation model fine-tuning: a pretrained spatial encoder (e.g., on global remote sensing or GPS trajectories) can be frozen and then fine-tuned with small labelled sets of health outcomes or accessibility measurements. MatterGen's classifier-free guidance mechanism is an architectural template for conditioning spatial predictions on target quantities (e.g., desired accessibility score ranges) without retraining the entire encoder.

② Replace MatterGen's greedy MLFF pre-filter with a physics-informed GNN surrogate

Extending this paperMatterGen currently uses MatterSim (a separate MLFF) as a black-box stability pre-filter before expensive DFT relaxation. A tighter integration: embed a physics-informed graph neural network that simultaneously learns interatomic potentials and encodes conservation-law constraints (charge neutrality, valence balance) directly into the diffusion score network's loss. This would let the diffusion model itself avoid chemically implausible structures during generation rather than filtering them post-hoc — reducing DFT budget further and accelerating the rediscovery of synthesizable structures.

Added value to my researchThis is the materials-domain instance of physics-informed graph learning, one of my core methodological themes. The same idea — encoding conservation priors as soft constraints in graph-network loss functions — applies in my spatial modeling work: traffic flow conservation on road networks, population balance in region-partitioning models, and energy conservation in spatiotemporal diffusion of disease. MatterGen's experimental evidence that physics-alignment reduces RMSD by 10x is a compelling motivation to pursue the same strategy in geospatial GNNs.

The paper stops at two objectives and scalar properties; an extension would be to handle vector-valued properties (e.g., full band structure, phonon dispersion) or objectives that are themselves spatial distributions rather than scalar targets — requiring a new adapter architecture that encodes objective functions rather than target values.

Extending this paperMatterGen introduces a rigorous Stable-Unique-New (SUN) composite metric for evaluating generative quality across three independent dimensions, rather than collapsing them into a single score. A methodological extension: apply the SUN decomposition to evaluate generative models for non-material scientific domains — e.g., generated molecular dynamics trajectories, synthetic urban mobility traces, or procedurally generated street networks — where analogous triples (physically plausible / not a copy / not seen in training) define quality.
