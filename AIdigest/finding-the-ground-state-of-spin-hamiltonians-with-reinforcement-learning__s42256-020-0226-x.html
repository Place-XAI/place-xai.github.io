Finding the ground state of spin Hamiltonians with reinforcement learning

Kyle Mills · Pooya Ronagh · Isaac Tamblyn Published 2020-09-07 DOI 10.1038/s42256-020-0226-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A reinforcement learning agent trained via proximal policy optimization (PPO) learns dynamic temperature schedules for simulated annealing that surpass standard heuristics on two classes of spin Hamiltonians. The method — called COOL (Controlled Online Optimization Learning) — generalizes across an entire class of nearest-neighbour spin glasses and achieves almost two orders of magnitude better scaling with system size than classic linear annealing. Crucially, it operates without any explicit knowledge of the Hamiltonian and remains effective even in a “destructive observation” mode that mimics quantum measurement constraints.

Why It Matters

  • Automates the “black art” of annealing schedules. Since Kirkpatrick et al. (1983) the temperature schedule for simulated annealing has been set by heuristic trial and error; an RL agent replaces that empirical craft with a policy that adapts in real time to the observed spin state.
  • Demonstrates generalization across an entire problem class. Trained on random nearest-neighbour spin glasses, the learned policy transfers to any Hamiltonian of that class, enabling one training run to solve theoretically infinite problem instances.
  • Achieves polynomial scaling vs. near-exponential for classic SA. The convolutional + LSTM network scales linearly with system size, providing far more favorable n99 (time-to-solution) than linear annealing on L = 14² and beyond.
  • Bridges classical and quantum optimization. By simulating “destructive observation” (analogous to wavefunction collapse), the framework foreshadows RL-driven control of real quantum annealers and quantum computers.
  • Ships fully open and reproducible. Code and test datasets are archived at Zenodo (zenodo.org/record/3897413), enabling community benchmarking.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Problem instances
Two classes of Hamiltonians. (1) Weak–strong clusters (WSC) model: a fixed L = 16 bipartite graph with two fully connected clusters, inspired by D-Wave’s Chimera topology. (2) Nearest-neighbour square spin glasses: toroidal lattices of sizes L = 4² to 16² (16 to 256 spins), with Gaussian-distributed couplings and zero bias.
Test sets
For each of 7 lattice sizes (sqrt(L) = 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, 14, 16), Ntest = 100 unique instances; true ground-state energies obtained by exact branch-and-cut via the Spin Glass Server (University of Cologne).
Training protocol
Each RL agent trained for 25,000 episodes; Nsteps = 40 steps per episode; Nsweeps = 100 Metropolis sweeps per step; Nreps = 64 independent replicas per observation.
Evaluation metric
Time-to-solution n99: number of anneals required to be 99% certain of observing the ground state at least once, computed from 100 runs over each of 100 instances.
Software
PPO implemented via Stable Baselines; environment built as an OpenAI Gym interface; Wang–Landau sampling for density-of-states estimation (policy analysis only).
Data availability
Test datasets and full code archived at zenodo.org/record/3897413 (DOI 10.5281/zenodo.3897413).

Core Methods

  • Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO): the RL algorithm of choice for stable performance on continuous action spaces; the agent outputs a normal distribution over inverse-temperature changes Δβ, sampled to drive the SA process at each step.
  • Convolutional + LSTM policy network: two parallel 1D convolutional layers extract spin-wise and replica-wise statistics from the Nreps×L spin array; outputs feed into an LSTM module capturing temporal dynamics; architecture scales linearly with system size L.
  • Sparse reward engineering: reward of zero at every intermediate step; terminal reward equals the negative of the minimum energy across all replicas, encouraging the agent to minimize energy without constraining its path through phase space.
  • Metropolis–Hastings simulation: the underlying SA substrate; the environment returns the full spin state after every Nsweeps = 100 sweeps, providing the observation on which the RL policy acts.
  • Destructive observation mode: replicas are “consumed” on measurement (mimicking quantum wavefunction collapse); buffered action sequences are replayed on freshly initialized replicas, testing RL robustness to non-repeatable observations.
  • Wang–Landau sampling: used for policy analysis on the 16×16 ferromagnetic Ising model to compute the density of states g(M, E) and overlay the learned value function in energy–magnetization phase space.
  • Time-to-solution metric (n99): binomial-distribution-based metric counting the number of episodes needed to observe the ground state with 99% probability; enables hardware-agnostic comparison across SA schedules and system sizes.

① Extend COOL to spatial facility-location cast as a spin glass

Extending this paperThe paper frames combinatorial optimization (finding the ground state) as an annealing problem controllable by RL. Facility-location problems — choosing where to place clinics, EV chargers, or transit hubs to maximize coverage and minimize inequity — are themselves NP-hard discrete problems that can be cast as spin Hamiltonians: each candidate site is a binary spin (σ = ±1 for open/closed), and the Hamiltonian energy is the negative of the coverage objective. A direct extension would show that the COOL policy trained on abstract toroidal spin glasses can transfer to Hamiltonians derived from real urban street networks, exploiting the paper’s demonstrated generalization across problem classes and scaling this generalization from synthetic to geographically structured graphs.

Added value to my researchThis directly upgrades my EV-charging efficiency-vs-equity optimization and healthcare facility placement. Rather than exhaustive spatial search or greedy heuristics, the RL agent would explore the combinatorial configuration space guided by observed coverage states, scaling polynomially with candidate site count. The paper’s scaling result (near-two-orders-of-magnitude improvement at L = 14²) suggests that COOL could handle facility placement at realistic urban scale with far fewer evaluations than my current Monte Carlo approaches.

② Replace the convolutional observation encoder with a GNN for irregular spatial graphs

Extending this paperThe paper’s policy network uses parallel 1D convolutional layers that aggregate statistics over a regular L-spin array, implicitly assuming a grid topology. Real combinatorial optimization on transportation networks, road graphs, and irregular urban layouts requires a graph-structured observation space. A direct extension replaces the convolutional feature extractor with a graph neural network (GNN) encoder — leaving the LSTM temporal module intact — so the RL agent can observe spin states on arbitrary graphs such as the Chimera topology the paper already mentions or full city-scale road networks. The paper’s linear scaling claim would then generalize from regular lattices to graphs of arbitrary connectivity, substantially widening the applicability of the COOL framework.

Added value to my researchMy physics-informed graph learning agenda aims to encode conservation and scaling priors into GNNs for spatiotemporal prediction. Coupling a GNN observation encoder with the COOL temperature-scheduling agent creates a physics-informed graph RL system for large-scale network routing or resource allocation on real urban graphs — a direct convergence of my graph-learning and combinatorial optimization research lines that neither approach achieves alone.

③ Apply the “destructive observation” paradigm to privacy-constrained mobility data collection

Extending this paperThe paper’s destructive-observation mode abstracts a quantum constraint into a general framework: an agent must make decisions from a state that is consumed by the act of measurement, requiring robustness to partial and non-repeatable information. The same structure applies whenever data collection is physically or legally constrained: environmental sensors with limited battery, clinical sampling that alters patient state, or mobile-phone traces that can be retrieved only once per user per day for privacy reasons. A direct extension would systematically characterize the paper’s destructive-observation performance degradation as a function of observation frequency and consumption rate, and propose an augmented action set that lets the agent decide when to measure (controlled observation), minimizing unnecessary measurements as the authors themselves suggest in the conclusion.

Added value to my researchMy data fusion work with mobile-phone records and GPS trajectories faces privacy constraints limiting retrieval frequency and resolution. Treating each retrieval as a destructive measurement in the COOL sense enables me to design RL-based sampling strategies that choose when and where to collect observations, maximizing the accuracy of spatiotemporal density estimates and accessibility measurements within a fixed privacy budget — turning a legal constraint into a structured resource-allocation problem solvable by the paper’s framework.

④ Integrate free-energy machine priors into COOL for faster convergence on hard instances

Extending this paperThe paper’s RL agent learns a temperature schedule from scratch through 25,000 episodes of trial-and-error interaction with the Metropolis–Hastings environment. Recent work on free-energy machines (FEM) for combinatorial optimization encodes physics-based free-energy landscape information — effectively a prior over which spin configurations are energetically favorable — without requiring RL exploration. A direct extension would seed the COOL agent’s policy initialization with a FEM-derived value estimate, replacing the random initialization with a structured warm start that reduces the exploration burden on difficult instances. The paper already identifies difficult instances where the agent underperforms (Fig. 5); a FEM prior specifically for those hard cases could close the gap.

Added value to my researchFor spatial optimization problems (facility placement, routing), a warm-started COOL agent with a physics-based prior would require far fewer evaluations to find near-optimal solutions, directly reducing the computational cost of my multi-objective EV-charging and healthcare accessibility optimizations. The FEM prior can also encode domain knowledge (e.g., population density constraints) as soft Hamiltonian terms, providing a natural interface between my GeoAI data and the COOL control framework.

⑤ Adopt the value-function phase-plane visualization as an XAI tool for spatial RL policies

Extending this paperFig. 6 in the paper projects the learned value function V(st) onto the energy–magnetization phase plane, revealing that the agent assigns higher value to configurations that are closer to the global minimum in action space rather than identical in energy. This is a form of XAI for RL that goes beyond feature importance scores: it exposes the agent’s implicit representation of the problem landscape. A direct extension would systematize this visualization across problem classes (spin glasses of different sizes and topologies) and test whether the learned value function encodes universal geometric features of the energy landscape, or is problem-specific. The trajectory overlays in Fig. 6b already hint at how phase-plane plots could diagnose pathological cases where the agent enters and exits the wrong energy basin.
