School-based suicide prevention using the gatekeeper programme: a cluster-randomized trial

Diyang Qu · Bowen Liu · Xuan Zhang · Chengxi Cai · Dongyang Chen · Dongyu Liu · Xue Wen · Zhijun Wu · Shufang Sun · Shekhar Saxena · Jing An · Runsen Chen Published 2026-01-20 DOI 10.1038/s44360-025-00037-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A single-day, train-the-trainer Life Gatekeeper Training Program (LGTP) delivered across 84 Chinese schools produced significant and sustained improvements in all four primary outcomes — reduced stigma, higher suicide literacy, greater perceived competence, and stronger willingness to intervene — in a cluster-RCT of 4,140 teacher participants. Effect sizes remained significant at both 6- and 12-month follow-ups with 91.4% retention, and no adverse events were reported, establishing LGTP as a brief, scalable, and effective school-based suicide prevention strategy.

Why It Matters

  • First large-scale cluster-RCT of gatekeeper training in China. With 84 schools and over 4,000 participants, this is among the most rigorously powered trials of school-based suicide prevention in the Asia-Pacific region, where cultural stigma and low help-seeking rates make the challenge especially acute.
  • Demonstrates sustained effect at 12 months. Most mental health training studies report only immediate post-training gains; this trial shows effects persisting at 6 and 12 months across all four primary outcomes, establishing durability not just efficacy.
  • Train-the-trainer cascade enables system-level scale-up. By training teacher-trainers who then deliver LGTP to their peers, the model sidesteps the specialist bottleneck and offers a blueprint for low-resource replication at national scale.
  • Directly targets gatekeeper competencies, not just knowledge. The four primary outcomes — stigma, literacy, perceived competence, and willingness to intervene — collectively map the behavioral pipeline from awareness to action, providing an evidence base for policy mandates.
  • Zero adverse events in a sensitive population context. The safety profile answers a critical concern in suicide-prevention research about potential contagion or distress from explicit training content.

Core Methods

  • Cluster-randomized controlled trial (RCT): schools (not individuals) randomized 1:1 to LGTP intervention or waitlist control, controlling for within-cluster correlation in outcomes.
  • Train-the-trainer cascade: designated trainer teachers first received LGTP and then delivered it to participant teachers within their schools, creating a scalable dissemination model.
  • Linear mixed models (LMM) — intention-to-treat: primary analysis using LMM with random effects for school clusters; estimates reported as β coefficients with 95% confidence intervals across all four outcomes.
  • Generalized estimating equations (GEE): secondary analysis (Extended Data Tables 2 and 4) modeling population-averaged effects of group-by-time interactions over 12 months.
  • False discovery rate (FDR) correction: applied to control Type I error across multiple simultaneous outcome comparisons; all primary p-values < 2.0 × 10−16 after adjustment.
  • Sensitivity and subgroup analyses: separate LMMs for trainer teachers (n = 223), combined sample (n = 4,363), and exploratory subgroups (Extended Data Tables 6–8); behavioral gatekeeper outcomes assessed at 12-month follow-up only (Extended Data Tables 9–10).
  • Retention and safety monitoring: tracked at each time point; adverse and serious adverse events formally monitored and recorded.

① Map the geographic reach of the LGTP intervention with spatial accessibility analysis

Extending this paperThe trial demonstrates that LGTP works but treats all 84 schools as functionally equivalent in terms of access to mental health support resources. A direct extension would overlay the trial school locations with spatial accessibility surfaces for professional mental health services (psychiatrists, counselors, crisis hotlines) and test whether the LGTP effect size is moderated by the local mental health service desert each school sits in. Schools in areas with near-zero mental health specialist access may show larger and more durable gatekeeper effects because gatekeepers are the only realistic pathway to help — a finding with major implications for where to prioritize roll-out.

② Predict heterogeneous treatment effects using explainable ML on school-level spatial and socioeconomic features

Extending this paperThe paper's subgroup analyses (Extended Data Table 8) explore a handful of pre-specified moderators, but the 84-school clusters carry rich spatial context — urbanicity index, neighborhood income, distance to mental health services, school size — that a simple LMM cannot leverage. Extending the paper with a causal forest or gradient-boosted heterogeneous treatment effect estimator trained on cluster-level covariates would reveal which school contexts amplify or attenuate the LGTP benefit, enabling precision deployment of a nationally scalable program.

③ Extend the cascade model into a spatiotemporal diffusion simulation to optimize trainer deployment

Extending this paperThe train-the-trainer cascade is the paper's delivery mechanism but is treated as a fixed design rather than an optimization problem. Extending it: model the diffusion of gatekeeper competency across school networks using a spatially-explicit agent-based or network SIR-type model, where "infection" is acquisition of gatekeeper skill and trainers are seeds. Simulating alternative seeding strategies (which schools to train first, in what geographic order) could show how to minimize the time to national coverage subject to a fixed budget of initial trainer slots.

④ Quantify spatial inequity in LGTP access to build an equity-weighted rollout strategy

Extending this paperThe trial is silent on whether the recruited schools were representative of the geographic and socioeconomic diversity of Chinese schools. An equity extension: using census and school administrative data, compute a spatial Gini coefficient of LGTP coverage under different rollout strategies — random, urban-first, rural-first, mental-health-desert-first — and identify the strategy that most efficiently reduces the equity gap in gatekeeper availability. This speaks directly to the WHO equity agenda for suicide prevention.

⑤ Link the individual-level gatekeeper competency gains to city-level mental health outcomes using mobile data

Extending this paperThe paper measures outcomes at the teacher level but cannot yet show whether scaling LGTP to all schools in a city actually changes population-level suicide rates or help-seeking behavior. A longitudinal extension: in cities where LGTP is rolled out at scale, integrate mobile phone crisis-line call records, emergency department mental health visits, or social media distress signals as city-level ecological outcomes and test whether LGTP school penetration rate predicts improvements in these downstream indicators, creating a meso-scale bridge between individual competency and population health.
