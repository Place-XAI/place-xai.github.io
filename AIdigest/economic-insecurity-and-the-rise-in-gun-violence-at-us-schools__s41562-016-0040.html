Economic insecurity and the rise in gun violence at US schools

Pah, A. R. · Hagan, J. · Jennings, A. L. · Jain, A. · Albrecht, K. · Hockenberry, A. J. · Amaral, L. A. N. Published 2017-01-30 DOI 10.1038/s41562-016-0040 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A newly assembled consensus dataset of 381 US school shooting events (1990–2013) reveals two distinct periods of elevated gun violence, with the more recent surge (2007–2013) concentrated in post-secondary schools. Rates of school shootings are significantly correlated with unemployment at national, regional, and city levels, and with multiple broader economic distress indicators, strongly supporting the hypothesis that breakdown in the school-to-work transition — not random or culture-specific factors alone — is a structural driver of US school gun violence.

Why It Matters

  • First rigorous consensus dataset for US school shootings. Prior studies relied on incompatible, poorly curated sources (Shultz, SAVD, Brady Campaign, Slate, Wikipedia, Virginia Tech Report); this paper harmonizes them with 88–89% inter-coder agreement into a single 381-event reference dataset for 1990–2013.
  • Identifies a structural economic driver. By linking shooting rates to unemployment at three geographic scales (national, regional, city), the study moves beyond individual or cultural explanations toward a measurable, policy-tractable social mechanism.
  • Reveals a temporal and institutional shift. The elevated 2007–2013 period is disproportionately driven by events at post-secondary schools — consistent with a school-to-work transition hypothesis — while K12 shootings characterized the earlier 1992–1994 peak.
  • Demonstrates robustness across multiple economic indicators. Unemployment, foreclosure rate, consumer confidence, and a composite indicator all show significant correlations with gun violence at post-secondary and all schools, ruling out spurious association with a single metric.
  • Policy implication is direct: improving economic security and strengthening youth employment pathways should reduce, not just the abstract "risk," but the measurable frequency of school shooting events.

Core Methods

  • Dataset harmonization: multi-source review with three independent coders; events included by consensus (88–89% inter-coder agreement); original sources cross-checked against raw reports and news archives.
  • Poisson regression: monthly shooting count modeled as a Poisson process with unemployment rate and summer-month dummy as predictors; pseudo-R² and 95% CI reported (national model: P < 10⁻⁴).
  • Poisson change-point detection: fitted to monthly incident time series to identify distinct rate periods; best model identifies four periods, with a statistically elevated rate 2007–2013.
  • Kolmogorov–Smirnov two-sample test: compares distributions of normalized unemployment rates between months with and without shootings at national, regional, and city levels; confirms significant stochastic dominance of high-unemployment months in shooting months.
  • Multi-level geographic aggregation: analysis repeated at national, seven-region, and six-city levels to rule out ecological fallacy and test robustness of the unemployment–violence correlation.
  • Educational-attainment-stratified unemployment: "less than high school" unemployment matched to K12 events; "some college" unemployment matched to post-secondary events, testing specificity of the school-to-work transition hypothesis.
  • Composite economic indicator: equal-weighted linear combination of unemployment, inverse consumer confidence, and foreclosure rate, normalized by mean and standard deviation, to assess generalizability across economic distress metrics.

① Spatially disaggregate the unemployment–violence signal with fine-grained urban mobility data

Extending this paperThe paper's city-level analysis aggregates unemployment and shootings to six cities, masking intra-city heterogeneity. A direct extension would dissolve the city-level unit into census-tract or neighborhood-level unemployment gradients and test whether schools in spatially concentrated high-unemployment tracts have disproportionate shooting rates — using GPS mobility data and local unemployment estimates. This would sharpen the school-to-work hypothesis to a spatial mechanism: proximity to economic opportunity (or its absence) within the city.

The result would be an MGWR-based local effect estimator for socio-economic stress and public safety.

② Replace the Poisson regression baseline with urban-scaling-aware event-rate models

Extending this paperThe paper uses national monthly shooting counts in a Poisson regression without accounting for the fact that city-level violence follows urban scaling laws (superlinear in population). The corpus paper on urban scaling of firearm violence shows that gun violence is not randomly distributed across city sizes but systematically amplified in larger cities. Extending this paper to incorporate population-scaling correction would remove a confound — larger cities both have more shootings and more unemployment variance — producing cleaner causal estimates of the economic effect.

Added value to my researchThis informs my urban-analytics work on agglomeration and regional collaboration: the same scaling framework I would apply to healthcare facility utilization can be applied to public-safety events, allowing a unified spatiotemporal model where economic distress, city size, and service accessibility interact. It also provides a natural use case for my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation methods applied to violence event data.

③ Apply predictive AI to anticipate elevated-risk periods from leading economic indicators

④ Embed spatial access to employment and education as mediating variables

Extending this paperThe paper measures aggregate unemployment but never asks whether spatial access to jobs and post-secondary education mediates the unemployment–violence relationship. A city's unemployment rate captures demand–supply imbalance in the labor market, but spatial accessibility of employment centers and vocational programs introduces a further geographic inequality: students near transit-rich job corridors may experience less insecurity even in high-unemployment months. Extending the paper with 2SFCA-based measures of educational and employment accessibility alongside unemployment would separate structural economic exposure from spatial opportunity deprivation.

⑤ Extend the dataset forward in time and use mobile-phone mobility as a real-time economic distress sensor

Extending this paperThe paper's dataset ends in 2013; the US has experienced both further economic disruption (COVID-19, 2020–2021) and record school shooting frequencies in the years since. A direct extension would update the consensus dataset to 2024 and ask whether the economic–violence correlation held through the pandemic recession and recovery — a natural quasi-experiment on the causal mechanism. Crucially, mobile-phone mobility data offers a near-real-time proxy for economic activity (visit frequency to commercial areas, commute patterns) that could replace the lagged unemployment releases with a contemporaneous economic-distress sensor.
