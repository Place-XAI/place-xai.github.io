Promoting avian diversity in cities requires multiple parks with variable features

Frank A. La Sorte · Bertrand Fournier · Jeffrey A. G. Clark · Christopher A. Lepczyk · Myla F. J. Aronson Published 2026-02-20 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00385-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

No single "best" park design maximizes urban bird biodiversity. Across 935 parks in 186 US cities, the three facets of avian diversity respond to different and often conflicting park features: species richness rises with park area and the presence of water; phylogenetic diversity is higher in parks near the urban boundary; and functional diversity is higher where habitat heterogeneity is lower. Critically, the effect of tree canopy cover flips sign geographically — beneficial in the Intermountain West but negative in eastern cities for richness and phylogenetic diversity. Conserving all dimensions of bird diversity therefore demands a portfolio of multiple parks with variable features, not a one-size-fits-all template, with regional and seasonal tuning of tree canopy.

Why It Matters

  • Breaks the "more green = more biodiversity" myth. By splitting biodiversity into three orthogonal facets (taxonomic richness, phylogenetic, functional), the paper shows that optimizing one can suppress another — a direct challenge to single-metric green-space planning.
  • Gives planners a portfolio rule, not a recipe. The headline finding — multiple parks with variable features — converts park design from "build the perfect park" to "diversify the network," an actionable principle for citywide park systems.
  • Exposes a geography-dependent design lever. Tree canopy cover, usually treated as universally good, reverses sign between regions and seasons; blanket "plant more trees" mandates can backfire for some bird communities in eastern cities.
  • Continental scale with real occurrence data. Built on community-science eBird records across 186 cities, it moves urban-bird ecology beyond single-city studies toward transferable, cross-regional inference.

Core Methods

  • Three-facet biodiversity quantification: for each park and season, compute species richness (count), phylogenetic diversity (shared evolutionary history) and functional diversity (trait/ecological-role space) — treating biodiversity as multi-dimensional rather than a single number.
  • Feature–diversity modeling: relate the three facets to seven park features across seasons, using machine-learning regression (gradient-boosted / random-forest-style models per the reference list) to capture non-linear effects.
  • Relative-influence & partial-dependence analysis: rank feature importance (Fig. 2) and trace the shape of each feature's effect (Fig. 3), revealing where relationships are non-monotonic.
  • Geographic decomposition: summarize feature effects by longitude (Fig. 4) and map the spatially varying effect of tree canopy cover (Fig. 5), exposing the East–West sign reversal.
  • Seasonal stratification: repeat all analyses across seasons, separating resident from migratory community signals.

① Make the geographic sign-flip explainable with spatially varying attribution

Extending this paperThe paper documents that tree canopy cover reverses sign between East and West (Figs. 4–5) but explains the reversal only descriptively via longitude binning. A direct extension: replace the global gradient-boosted model with an explainable spatially varying model — e.g. geographically weighted SHAP or MGWR on the feature effects — so the canopy sign-flip becomes an estimated, mapped local coefficient surface with confidence, rather than a binned summary. This would identify the exact climate/urban thresholds where canopy stops helping birds.
