Urbanization signatures on climate and soils uncovered by crowd-sensed plants

Susanne Tautenhahn · Martin Jung · Michael Rzanny · Patrick Mäder · Markus Reichstein · Bernhard Ahrens · … · Jana Wäldchen Published 2026-01-15 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00378-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using more than 80 million crowd-sensed plant observations as living environmental sensors, the authors map fine-scale (~100 m) climate and soil conditions across 326 European cities. Beyond the familiar urban heat island, they find parallel built-up-vs-green contrasts for moisture, soil pH, salinity, light and soil disturbance. These within-city contrasts rival the differences between cities 1,500–3,000 km apart, yet built-up areas are more alike across cities than forests are — evidence that urbanization both diversifies environments locally and homogenizes them across cities, with urban forests acting as reservoirs of cooling, moisture and environmental diversity.

★

Why It Matters

  • Opens up urban soils — the long-standing "blind spot." In-situ soil and microclimate networks are infeasible at continental scale and remote sensing sees only a few variables; this work shows that opportunistic plant observations can quantify soil pH, salinity, fertility and disturbance alongside temperature, moisture and light.
  • Extends urban environmental signatures beyond heat. It demonstrates that the urban heat island is one facet of a broader, consistent built-up → forest environmental gradient repeated across European regions.
  • Reconciles two competing paradigms. Cities are shown to be simultaneously locally diverse and inter-city homogenized, resolving an apparent contradiction in urban ecology with a single observational lens.
  • Turns citizens into a continental sensor network. AI plant-ID apps plus bioindication become a cost- and time-effective monitoring infrastructure for rapidly evolving urban socio-ecosystems, directly serving SDG-aligned planning.
  • Quantifies the value of urban forests. Forests emerge as the principal source of within-city cooling, moisture retention and environmental heterogeneity — a concrete, evidence-based argument for nature-based solutions.

Core Methods

  • Mobile crowd sensing of environments (MCSE): for each grid cell, compute the mean ecological indicator value of co-occurring species (presence-only), turning plant lists into climate/soil estimates — ≥ 25 species per cell at 10 km, ≥ 5 species per cell at 100 m.
  • Taxonomic harmonization: all species names standardized to the GBIF backbone taxonomy via rgbif before merging with three indicator-value systems rescaled to 0–10.
  • Land-use signatures: mixed-effect ANOVA per environmental factor (fixed: region × land-use class; random intercept: city), with marginal means (emmeans/lme4) expressed as region and land-use deviations.
  • Within- vs between-city analysis: relative difference ΔErel = (ΔEbetween − ΔEwithin)/ΔEwithin, stratified by distance classes (0–1,000, 1,000–2,000, >2,000 km) to expose distance-decay of similarity and homogenization (flatter slope for built-up vs forests).
  • Robustness & validation: full-factorial sensitivity analysis over indicator system, presence vs abundance weighting, and species/observation thresholds; corroboration against WorldClim, SoilGrids and 40,000 sPlotOpen expert plots.

① Couple crowd-sensed environments with health-accessibility surfaces

Extending this paperThe paper produces fine-scale (~100 m) maps of heat, moisture and soil disturbance but stops at the environmental layer, treating cities as ecosystems rather than as places where people seek services. A direct extension: overlay the MCSE built-up→forest gradient onto recreational and green-space exposure to test whether the most environmentally degraded urban quarters are also the most under-served — turning bioindicated environments into an environmental-justice variable.

② Fuse living-sensor maps with remote sensing for soil where satellites are blind

Extending this paperThe authors validate MCSE soil pH against SoilGrids and even find MCSE more sensitive where the random-forest product saturates below pH 5. The natural extension is an explicit multi-source fusion: combine plant-bioindicated soil signals with satellite land-surface temperature and land cover so each compensates the other's blind spots, yielding a calibrated continental soil-disturbance / salinity product rather than two parallel maps.

Added value to my researchThis is exactly my multi-source data-fusion agenda (remote sensing + social media + opportunistic sensing). Treating crowd-sensed plant lists as a fourth fusion modality alongside RS/POI/mobile-phone data extends my fine-tuning of geospatial foundation models to a soil-property task that imagery alone cannot solve.

③ Quantify and de-bias the crowd-sensing sampling process explicitly

Extending this paperRobustness is demonstrated empirically (sPlotOpen, sensitivity tests), but the paper never models the observation process itself — who records where, in which season, favoring showy plants. Extending it with an explainable correction model (e.g. spatially varying coefficients on observer density, day-of-year, app market share) would convert qualitative robustness into a quantified uncertainty surface per grid cell, strengthening downstream policy use.

Added value to my researchThis maps onto my explainable-ML and MGWR/GWR work on opportunistic data: a spatially non-stationary bias model for citizen-science observation effort is directly transferable to my social-media and GPS-trajectory studies, where coverage bias is the central threat to validity.

④ Make the homogenization finding a physics-informed graph-learning problem

Extending this paperThe paper measures distance-decay of similarity (the "first law of geography") through stratified medians, but the 326 cities are analyzed largely independently. Extending it: treat cities as a spatial graph and learn a graph operator that predicts a city's land-use environmental profile from its neighbors and density field, with the observed distance-decay encoded as a conservation/smoothness prior — so the homogenization signal becomes a trainable, transferable law rather than a descriptive statistic.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my physics-informed graph-learning line: encoding spatial-autocorrelation and distance-decay priors into a GNN over urban units, transferable from my job-housing and facility-access networks to environmental prediction over the city meta-graph.

⑤ Scale MCSE from Europe to a FAIR, global living-sensor service

Extending this paperMCSE relies on three Europe-specific indicator systems and excludes Türkiye and parts of the east. Extending it toward a globally transferable framework — learning indicator values where expert systems are absent, and publishing versioned, queryable gridded products — would move it from a regional study to reusable open infrastructure, the way comparable urban-climate work has formalized scaling relations across boundary definitions.
