Functional brain imaging and population-level visits to urban spaces

Ardaman Kaur · André Leite Rodrigues · Sarah Hoogstraten · Diego Andrés Blanco-Mora · Bruno Miranda · Paulo Morgado · Dar Meshi Published 2024-11-01 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00158-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Brain activity in a small lab sample can forecast the behavior of a whole population in a city they have never visited. When US participants viewed geotagged Flickr photos of Lisbon during fMRI, activity in the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) — a core reward-valuation region — predicted the real density of photographs taken around those Lisbon locations, a proxy for population-level visits. This "neuroforecasting" result implies that reward-related neural responses to the built environment help shape where people actually go, offering a brain-based lever for designing attractive, sustainable cities.

★

Why It Matters

  • Brain-to-population bridge. It extends "neuroforecasting" (a few brains predicting many people's choices, previously shown for media, crowdfunding and markets) into the spatial / urban-design domain for the first time — neural signals predict where a population congregates.
  • A mechanism for place attractiveness. Linking visit density to vmPFC reward valuation proposes why some urban spaces draw crowds — they are intrinsically rewarding to look at — moving beyond correlational "nice places attract people."
  • Cheap behavioral proxy validated against the brain. It treats geotagged social-media photo density as a measurable stand-in for visitation and ties that proxy to neural valuation, useful for cities lacking mobility surveys.
  • Design relevance. If reward responses to streetscapes predict footfall, planners gain a pre-construction screening signal for whether a space will be used — supporting livable, sustainable city design.

Core Methods

  • Stimulus construction: mine and curate 160 geotagged Flickr photos of Lisbon and compute a photo-density field as the population-visit proxy.
  • Task fMRI: US participants who had never been to Lisbon view the images and rate preference while BOLD activity is recorded.
  • Neuroforecasting design: test whether individual/averaged neural responses predict out-of-sample, population-level photo density — the "brain-as-predictor" / neural-focus-group paradigm.
  • Region-of-interest valuation: focus on the vmPFC reward/valuation system; relate its activity to both subjective preference ratings and the external visit-density measure.
  • Robust statistics: robust regression / correlation methods (per the reference list) to guard against outliers in small-sample neuroimaging.

Extending this paperThe study links vmPFC activity to Flickr photo density in one city (Lisbon) with 160 images.
