Infrastructure deficits and informal settlements in sub-Saharan Africa

Luís M. A. Bettencourt · Nicholas Marchio Published 2025-09-03 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09465-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

By applying a topological block complexity metric (k — the number of building layers away from the nearest street) to a complete dataset of 415 million building footprints across 9.8 million blocks in 50 nations of sub-Saharan Africa, this study produces the first street-block-level continental map of infrastructure deficits. About 0.55 billion people live in blocks with severe street-access deficits (k > 5), of which 74% are rural — overturning the stereotype that informality is primarily an urban-slum problem. Critically, higher block complexity is systematically associated with worse outcomes across 67 human development indicators (health, education, wealth, sanitation), forming a single principal component that covaries predictably with k, enabling localized and modular Sustainable Development Goal tracking at the street-block level continent-wide.

Why It Matters

  • Reframes where informality lives. The study demonstrates that the majority of people in sub-Saharan Africa experiencing severe infrastructure deficits are rural and peri-urban, not concentrated in the urban slums that have dominated policy narratives — a fundamental reorientation of the development challenge.
  • Replaces slum/non-slum dichotomy with a continuous spectrum. Block complexity k provides an objective, fine-grained measure that avoids the binary classification problems of conventional slum delineation and remote-sensing approaches, revealing a full spectrum of access deprivation.
  • Links physical access directly to multidimensional deprivation. A single principal component explains 48% of variance across 67 DHS indicators, with each unit increase in k associated with 5.3% lower female literacy, 4.5% lower improved-sanitation access, 2.8% lower piped-water access, and 1.5% lower household wealth.
  • Enables tractable, modular urban planning. Block-level decomposition turns a continental-scale problem into millions of independent, locally solvable units — enabling community co-production of solutions and generation of cadastral maps and official addresses as co-benefits.
  • Provides a replicable open-science platform. All code and block-level data are publicly archived (Harvard Dataverse, GitHub/Zenodo), and an interactive visualization at millionneighborhoods.africa makes results queryable for every block on the continent.

Core Methods

  • Block graph construction: building footprints are abstracted to centroids; Voronoi decomposition creates land parcels; adjacency between parcels forms the block graph. Block complexity k is the topological shortest-path distance from the least-accessible building centroid to any street-boundary node — a measure of infrastructure access deprivation independent of block geometry.
  • Continental-scale parallel block processing: because each street block is topologically independent, the algorithm decomposes the continent into 9.8 million independent problems solvable in parallel on high-performance computing, then stitched into a coherent continental database.
  • Population downscaling: WorldPop (100 m) and LandScan (1 km) raster populations are allocated to each block proportionally to total building footprint area, creating block-level population estimates with uncertainty bounds from the two-source comparison.
  • Urban/peri-urban/rural classification: GHSL Urban Centre Database boundaries define urban cores; peri-urban is defined as a commutable 10-km spatial buffer; remaining territory is classified as rural.
  • Statistical characterization: block complexity distributions fit by log-normal across all settlement types (lnk = 1.74, σ = 0.81 for sub-Saharan Africa overall), providing a universal statistical signature of informality at any aggregation scale.
  • PCA of human development indicators: correlation and principal component analysis of 67 DHS variables across 219 subnational region-year observations identifies a single dominant PC (explaining 48% of variance) that covaries predictably and negatively with block complexity k, with country fixed effects and urban-share controls raising R² to 0.82.
  • Validation: block complexity validated against community self-declared slum surveys in 9 cities (know-your-city programme) and OpenStreetMap network completeness assessed via CIA World Factbook road-length ratios and internal OSM contribution-growth modelling.

① Use block complexity k as a spatial demand-weight in 2SFCA healthcare accessibility modelling for sub-Saharan Africa

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that block complexity k predicts multidimensional human deprivation (health, sanitation, wealth) through a single principal component — but it does not close the loop to actual service accessibility gaps. A direct extension: use k as a demand-side weight in a two-step floating catchment area (2SFCA) or enhanced R2SFCA model that maps healthcare facility accessibility at the block level across sub-Saharan Africa. Blocks with high k would have both elevated demand (sicker populations) and physically impeded access to facilities — capturing the double disadvantage that the paper identifies but does not quantify in service-flow terms. This would operationalize the paper's PCA finding into an actionable facility-gap map, aligned with the SDG 3 (health) and SDG 11.1 (slum) targets the authors invoke.

Added value to my researchThis is a natural next deployment of my R2SFCA framework in a Global South context where conventional catchment models assume continuous road-network access — an assumption the paper's block graph shows is massively violated. Integrating k into the supply-to-demand ratio of 2SFCA would let me produce k-adjusted accessibility scores that account for the last-meter physical barrier between a building and the street, adding a topological dimension to my spatial accessibility methods that currently treat all locations as street-accessible. The Harvard Dataverse data release means the block-level inputs are immediately available without new data collection.

② Map the trajectory of k over time using multi-temporal satellite imagery and geospatial foundation models

Extending this paperThe current study uses a single-epoch building footprint dataset (Ecopia 2022) and acknowledges that results must be updated as the built environment changes. A dynamic extension: apply multi-temporal satellite imagery (e.g., Planet Labs, Sentinel-2 annual composites) and fine-tuned geospatial foundation models for building-footprint extraction to compute k at multiple time points (e.g., 2015, 2018, 2021, 2024). Change in k over time would directly measure the rate of infrastructure formalization — a localized SDG progress tracker the paper calls for but cannot yet provide. The log-normal distribution of k offers a natural parametric summary of temporal change (shifts in ln(k) mean and variance).

Added value to my researchThis aligns directly with my agenda of fine-tuning geospatial foundation models and multi-source data fusion. Training a building-extraction head on top of a geospatial foundation model (e.g., SatLAS or Clay) with supervision from Ecopia's 2022 labels, then deploying it on historical Maxar/Planet imagery, would yield annual block complexity timeseries at the continental scale. The resulting delta-k maps would be a high-impact open dataset for tracking SDG 11.1 that could be maintained annually — a reproducibility and open-science contribution in line with my FAIR data agenda.

③ Build an explainable ML model to predict k from freely available satellite-derived features, bypassing the building-footprint bottleneck

Extending this paperThe paper's biggest data bottleneck is the Ecopia building footprint dataset — comprehensive for sub-Saharan Africa but requiring a data-usage agreement and high-performance computing. A democratizing extension: train an explainable ML model (e.g., gradient-boosted trees with SHAP attribution, or a lightweight CNN) to predict block complexity k and its log-normal parameters directly from freely available features: nighttime lights (VIIRS), Sentinel-2 spectral indices (NDVI, built-up index), OSM road density, and elevation. Validated against the paper's ground-truth k values, such a predictor could extend the paper's framework to Asia, Latin America, and other world regions where building-footprint data remain absent — a critical limitation the authors themselves flag.

Added value to my researchThis sits squarely in my explainable ML for spatial prediction research line. Using SHAP to decompose which satellite-derived features drive predicted k in each region would generate interpretable informality signatures — e.g., whether nighttime light intensity or road density is the dominant predictor varies by urban type. This would be a publishable companion to the Nature paper that extends its reach globally while contributing a methodological demonstration of GeoAI for development monitoring.

④ Couple block complexity to flood and climate-risk exposure to quantify compound vulnerability in informal settlements

Extending this paperThe paper shows that high-k blocks concentrate in peri-urban and rural areas — precisely where drainage infrastructure is absent and topographic flood-prone locations are more likely settled. A compound-risk extension: overlay the paper's block-level k values with global flood inundation maps (e.g., FATHOM, JRC Global Surface Water) and heat-island/extreme-temperature datasets to produce a compound vulnerability score per block. This would reveal whether the ~0.55 billion people with street-access deficits face compounded climate stress — a critical gap in current climate-adaptation policy that the paper's data uniquely enables to fill at continental scale.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and Monte Carlo methods could quantify the joint probability distribution of flood exposure and infrastructure deficit at the block level, integrating the paper's topological k metric with hydrological event frequencies. For urban health policy, this compound vulnerability surface would be a stronger predictor of health outcomes (diarrheal disease, vector-borne disease) than either dimension alone — extending my health geography agenda to climate-health linkages in the Global South.

⑤ Apply physics-informed graph learning to predict infrastructure upgrade priorities from the block graph structure

Extending this paperThe paper's block graph is a rich relational structure — buildings as nodes, spatial adjacency as edges, k as a node label — but it is currently used only to compute k, not to learn anything about the network's dynamics or optimal intervention targets. A graph-learning extension: train a physics-informed graph neural network (GNN) on the block graph where the physical constraint is that adding a new street edge to a node at topological distance d reduces k for all buildings it gates access to (a cascade rule derivable from the block graph's topology). The GNN would learn to rank candidate street insertions by their predicted k-reduction impact, prioritizing the interventions that formalize the most people with the least infrastructure investment — an optimization layer the paper's block-level decomposition makes tractable.

Added value to my researchThis is the most direct application of my physics-informed graph learning agenda to a development problem. Encoding the paper's block-graph cascade rule as a physics constraint in the GNN's message-passing loss would make the model extrapolate correctly to unseen block geometries — a transferability guarantee that a purely data-driven GNN lacks. The resulting optimal street-insertion ranker is also a novel facility-location optimization variant (edges rather than nodes), connecting to my EV-charging and public-facility-accessibility optimization work by demonstrating that the same framework applies to linear infrastructure (streets) as well as point facilities.
