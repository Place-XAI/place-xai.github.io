Nature Deep Learning Biomedical Imaging Obesity Foundation Model

A deep-learning framework reveals whole-body perturbations at cell level

Doris Kaltenecker · Izabela Horvath · Rami Al-Maskari · Ying Chen · Zeynep Ilgin Kolabas · Luciano Hoeher · Mihail Todorov · David-Paul Minde · Saketh Kapoor · Sena Gül Turhan · Louis B. Kuemmerle · et al. · Ali Ertürk Published 2026-05-20 DOI 10.1038/s41586-026-10535-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

MouseMapper, a foundation-model-based deep-learning suite, enables the first automated, whole-body quantification of peripheral nerves, immune cells, and 31 organs and tissues in the entire mouse body. Applied to diet-induced obesity, it revealed a previously unknown structural impairment of the infraorbital trigeminal nerve — reducing its network complexity by ~58% — linked to functional sensory deficits, and confirmed by conserved proteomic signatures in obese human trigeminal ganglia. The framework's zero-shot generalizability across different imaging resolutions, labelling strategies, and species establishes a scalable blueprint for systemic disease mapping from animal models to clinical translation.

Why It Matters

  • First whole-body AI atlas of nerves and immune cells. MouseMapper segments elongated peripheral nerve fibres over centimetre lengths and single immune-cell clusters simultaneously across the entire mouse body — a capability that did not exist before.
  • Closes a major bottleneck in systems disease biology. Advanced tissue-clearing (vDISCO) can image entire mouse bodies, but analysis has been done manually or organ by organ; MouseMapper automates the pipeline end-to-end for any organ.
  • Uncovers a new obesity-linked neuropathy. Diet-induced obesity reduces infraorbital nerve endings, edges, and vertices by ~58–61%, and this structural loss translates into measurable sensory deficits — a finding invisible without whole-body context.
  • Bridges mouse to human pathology. Proteomic pathways dysregulated in the trigeminal ganglion of obese mice (axon remodelling, complement, actin cytoskeleton) are conserved in post-mortem human tissue from individuals with obesity — providing direct translational relevance.
  • Open science by design. Whole-body scan maps are browsable online (discotechnologies.org/MouseMapper), code is on GitHub, and mass-spectrometry data are deposited to PRIDE/ProteomeXchange — enabling broad community reuse.

Core Methods

  • vDISCO whole-body tissue clearing and light-sheet fluorescence microscopy (LSFM): Uchl1-eGFP and Cd68-eGFP reporter mice undergo chemical clearing followed by LSFM at 1.1× (5.9 µm/voxel, whole-body) and 4× (1.62 µm/voxel, high-resolution) objectives, generating multiterabyte 3D volumes.
  • Foundation model fine-tuning (VesselFM → Nerve/Immune Modules): VesselFM (pre-trained for 3D blood vessel segmentation) is adapted to nerve segmentation via learning without forgetting (LwF) and to immune cell detection by freezing the encoder and fine-tuning the decoder. Nerve-Module achieves voxel Dice 0.7494; Immune-Module 0.7878, outperforming all competing baselines.
  • Whole-body sliding-window inference: ZARR file format and DASK parallel computing framework enable lazy loading and multiprocessing across multiterabyte whole-body scans; true 3D sliding-window inference (not 2D projections or pre-cropped volumes).
  • 3D UNet Tissue-Module: 27 organs annotated in 12 downsampled whole-mouse scans; ensemble of 5 networks (3D UNet selected as best) segments organs, then full-resolution tissue model handles fat, muscle, bone. Final Tissue-Module covers 31 anatomical units — the largest reported for LSFM.
  • Graph extraction and quantification: Nerve segmentation binary masks are skeletonized and converted to graphs; node (vertex), edge, and leaf-node (nerve ending) counts and radii extracted for comparative morphometry between chow and HFD conditions.
  • Spatial proteomics (DIA-MS/timsTOF SCP): Trigeminal ganglion needle-punch biopsies from mice and humans analysed by data-independent acquisition mass spectrometry; >6,000 proteins identified per sample; pathway enrichment via KEGG/Reactome.
  • Multiplex immunofluorescence (MACSima): Five immune-cell markers on ViscAT sections; pixel-level segmentation using a 30×30 self-organizing map + Leiden clustering; spatial proximity analysis of macrophage co-localization patterns.
  • Virtual Reality (VR) annotation pipeline: syGlass software used to manually annotate ground-truth training data in 3D for all three modules — critical for enabling supervised training at whole-body scale.

① Extend MouseMapper's foundation model from anatomy to spatial omics integration

Extending this paperMouseMapper segments anatomical structures (nerves, immune cells, organs) but treats molecular data (proteomics) as a separate downstream analysis. A direct extension of the paper is to co-register the spatial proteomics needle-punch locations with the 3D LSFM segmentation maps, creating a multi-modal spatial atlas where each voxel has both an anatomical label and a molecular profile. The paper already identifies "hotspots" (e.g., trigeminal ganglia) by structural change; integrating spatial omics would let the framework automatically discover molecular hotspots without pre-defining regions of interest. The SWITCH framework for spatial multi-omics integration from the corpus provides a methodological blueprint for this fusion.

④ Replace per-scan VR annotation with physics-informed active learning for training data generation

Extending this paperMouseMapper's training required 72 GB of manually VR-annotated nerve data — a massive labelling effort that is the main scalability bottleneck if the framework is extended to new diseases, mouse lines, or organs. The paper uses iterative annotation expansion for the Tissue-Module (inference on unannotated patches + manual correction), but this remains human-in-the-loop intensive. Extending it: integrate physics-based constraints (nerve topology continuity, biological plausibility of branching angles) as physics-informed uncertainty estimates to prioritize which patches most urgently need human annotation — an active learning loop grounded in the biology. The corpus's physics-based ML for subcellular segmentation demonstrates this approach in the fluorescence microscopy domain with limited ground truth.

Added value to my researchThe challenge of annotation scarcity in large spatial datasets is directly relevant to my work on spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and agent-based modeling calibrated from sparse GPS trajectories or social media data. MouseMapper's iterative annotation strategy — train, infer, correct high-uncertainty patches — is a reusable template for semi-supervised calibration of spatial models from sparse ground-truth observations, applicable to any GeoAI pipeline where labeled data is expensive.

⑤ Adapt MouseMapper's modular multi-system architecture to multi-domain urban health risk mapping

Extending this paperMouseMapper's power lies in its modular tri-module architecture: Nerve-Module (structural connectivity), Immune-Module (cellular distribution), and Tissue-Module (anatomical context). These three modules produce outputs that are meaningfully combined — nerve density is interpreted in its organ/tissue context; immune cluster composition is mapped onto specific anatomical regions. Extending the paper: design a fourth Dynamics-Module that tracks temporal changes in nerve and immune distributions over disease progression (e.g., obesity developing over 4, 8, 16, 18 weeks), moving from a cross-sectional to a longitudinal whole-body atlas. This would require a temporal registration framework aligning 3D mouse volumes across timepoints, enabling trajectory-based analysis of systemic disease.
