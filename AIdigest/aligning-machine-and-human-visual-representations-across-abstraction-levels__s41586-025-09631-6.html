Aligning machine and human visual representations across abstraction levels

Lukas Muttenthaler · Klaus Greff · Frieda Born · Bernhard Spitzer · Simon Kornblith · Michael C. Mozer · Klaus-Robert Müller · Thomas Unterthiner · Andrew K. Lampinen Published 2025-11-12 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09631-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

State-of-the-art vision foundation models fail to capture the full multi-level conceptual hierarchy of human knowledge — performing poorly on global coarse-grained similarity judgements even when fine-grained performance is adequate. The authors introduce AligNet, a synthetic dataset of 10 million human-aligned triplet judgements distilled from a surrogate teacher model, and show that fine-tuning diverse vision models on AligNet simultaneously improves human alignment, generalization, and out-of-distribution robustness — yielding a best-of-both-worlds representation without sacrificing downstream ML performance.

Why It Matters

  • Identifies a concrete, measurable gap in foundation models. Rather than a vague claim that models "differ from humans," the paper precisely locates the deficit at the coarse-grained semantic level — where standard models score below human-to-human reliability (36–57% vs. human ceiling 61.92%) — and the AligNet fix brings them above the ceiling (65–68%).
  • Scalable alignment without expensive human data. The AligNet pipeline bootstraps 10 million synthetic triplets from just 4.7 million real human responses via teacher–student distillation, demonstrating a data-efficient path to human-aligned AI that avoids large-scale behavioural collection.
  • Generalization and robustness improvements come for free. Alignment with human similarity judgements — not designed for distribution-shift robustness — significantly boosts BREEDS benchmarks and ImageNet-A adversarial robustness, challenging the assumption that human-like representations sacrifice engineering utility.
  • Introduces Levels: a new multi-abstraction cognitive benchmark. Collected from 473 human participants (Prolific Academic), Levels stratifies triplet odd-one-out judgements across coarse-grained, fine-grained, and class-boundary levels, filling a gap in existing cognitive datasets for evaluating foundation models.
  • Broad applicability beyond vision. The paper argues the same coarse-grained hierarchy misalignment likely exists in language models, making AligNet's distillation paradigm a template for alignment across modalities.

Core Methods

  • Affine uncertainty distillation (teacher model): an affine transform W, b maps a pretrained SigLIP-So400m representation into the THINGS human similarity space by minimizing a soft-alignment KL-divergence loss that incorporates human uncertainty (modelled via VICE Bayesian embeddings) rather than just discrete majority choices.
  • Cluster-boundary triplet sampling: ImageNet representations are k-means clustered (c = 500 via the Elbow criterion) in the teacher’s aligned space; triplets contain two images from the same cluster and one from a different cluster — injecting coarse semantic structure without requiring category labels.
  • KL-divergence student fine-tuning (AligNet objective): student vision models are fine-tuned by minimizing KL divergence between teacher soft-label distributions and student pairwise similarity distributions, with an ℓ₂ weight-decay-to-initialization regularizer to prevent catastrophic forgetting of pretrained features. n′ = 10 million triplets.
  • Triplet odd-one-out evaluation: accuracy = fraction of triplets where the model’s argmax pairwise similarity matches the human majority choice; supplemented by Spearman rank correlations on Likert-scale and multi-arrangement tasks from prior cognitive science datasets.
  • Representational similarity analysis (RSA): Pearson-correlation-based representational similarity matrices (RSMs) are compared between model and human representations to quantify geometric convergence after alignment.
  • Mixed-effects linear regression: tests whether representation distances change in the expected direction (closer within subordinate/basic/superordinate categories, farther across them) with statistical control for cluster non-independence.
  • Linear probing: frozen aligned and base features evaluated on downstream tasks via a linear read-out head (one-shot, few-shot, distribution shift, adversarial robustness benchmarks).

① Apply AligNet-style distillation to align geospatial vision encoders on human geographic similarity judgements

Extending this paperThe paper’s teacher–student pipeline is architecture-agnostic: any domain where humans organize knowledge into a conceptual hierarchy is a candidate. A direct extension is to collect human similarity judgements over urban scene images (“which two of these three satellite patches are most similar in land use?”) and apply the same uncertainty-distillation + cluster-boundary sampling to fine-tune geospatial encoders such as SatCLIP or GFM. The paper shows that 4.7 million base human responses are sufficient to synthesize 10 million triplets for meaningful alignment — feasible at geospatial scale via crowd annotation. The expected benefit mirrors the object-recognition case: coarse-grained land-use hierarchy (residential, commercial, industrial as superordinates) is likely the domain where current geospatial models are most miscalibrated relative to human expert judgement.

Added value to my researchMy agenda includes fine-tuning geospatial foundation models for domain-specific tasks. This paper provides a principled recipe: rather than task-specific supervised labels (hard to collect for remote sensing at scale), I can bootstrap a surrogate teacher from a small set of human land-use similarity judgements and use cluster-boundary sampling to generate synthetic triplets. The resulting geospatially aligned encoder would better capture abstract land-use hierarchy, directly improving spatial classification, change-detection, and POI-type prediction tasks that underpin my accessibility and urban health analyses.

② Use the Levels benchmark design to audit and calibrate spatial perception in GeoAI models

Extending this paperLevels provides a reusable diagnostic template stratified across abstraction levels, but the paper applies it only to object recognition. An extension: construct “GeoLevels” — coarse-grained triplets contrast land-use types (park vs. highway vs. residential), fine-grained triplets contrast subclasses (row houses vs. apartment blocks vs. mixed-use), and class-boundary triplets probe edge cases at land-use transitions. Applying AligNet alignment to remote sensing encoders and evaluating on GeoLevels would establish whether geospatial models show the same coarse-grained hierarchy deficit as object-recognition models, and whether the fix transfers. The paper’s human-to-human reliability score methodology provides a ready-made calibration ceiling.

Added value to my researchThis connects directly to my explainable ML agenda: a well-calibrated GeoLevels benchmark provides an interpretable proxy for whether a spatial model “understands” land-use semantics the way a human planner would. Models that score above the human noise ceiling on GeoLevels coarse-grained items can be trusted to generalize across density gradients — a concrete reliability criterion for deploying GeoAI in urban health contexts where distributional shift (new cities, different morphologies) is routine.

③ Leverage representational convergence to build an ensemble-free multi-model spatial predictor

Extending this paperFigure 3a reveals that AligNet fine-tuning converges previously diverse model representations (trained with radically different objectives) toward a shared geometry. An extension: treat this converged representation space as a “consensus latent space” that aggregates information from heterogeneous architectures without explicit prediction averaging. Apply this to geospatial models — fine-tune a set of differently pre-trained encoders (supervised, self-supervised, image–text) on the same geographic similarity objective, then concatenate or average their aligned representations as a universal spatial feature. The paper’s results suggest this consensus should be more robust than any single model’s features to distributional shift in unseen cities.

Added value to my researchI routinely ensemble heterogeneous ML models (GBT, RF, neural nets, GWR) for spatiotemporal prediction of health outcomes, mobility, and housing prices. The AligNet result suggests a principled shared-representation upgrade: rather than averaging predictions at the output layer, fine-tune all encoders on a human-aligned geographic similarity objective over spatial units (census tracts, grid cells), then use the converged embedding as a universal feature. This would give my ensemble a semantically grounded shared feature space rather than an ad-hoc stacking procedure, likely improving generalization to underrepresented regions.

④ Transfer the uncertainty-distillation mechanism to calibrate spatial accessibility predictions

Extending this paperThe paper’s soft-alignment loss distils calibrated human uncertainty (from VICE Bayesian embeddings) into the model — and AligNet models’ entropy reliably tracks human response-time latency (a proxy for uncertainty) across all abstraction levels. This is a general principle independent of the visual domain: anywhere humans express graded confidence in judgements, the VICE + soft-label pipeline can transform sparse survey data into a large calibrated pseudolabel dataset for model fine-tuning. Extension: apply this to healthcare accessibility perception ratings (e.g., Likert-scale surveys of perceived proximity to care) or neighborhood quality assessments, producing a calibrated surrogate that generates aligned labels at city scale.

Added value to my researchMy 2SFCA/R2SFCA models produce point accessibility scores without uncertainty estimates — a limitation when health equity decisions hinge on whether a score is reliably above or below a threshold. The uncertainty-distillation approach offers a concrete path: collect a small set of perceived-accessibility Likert ratings from community members, use VICE to extract their uncertainty distribution, and distil this into the R2SFCA scoring function. The result would be an accessibility score that flags high-uncertainty catchment areas — exactly where policy intervention and field validation are most needed in under-resourced communities.

⑤ Exploit AligNet’s out-of-distribution robustness gains to improve cross-city transfer in urban AI

Extending this paperAligNet alignment consistently improves BREEDS distribution-shift benchmarks and ImageNet-A adversarial robustness — without being designed for this purpose. The mechanism is that human-aligned representations organize features by semantic meaning rather than superficial texture or background, making them less brittle to distributional shifts. An extension: systematically test whether AligNet-aligned geospatial encoders show analogous robustness improvements when applied cross-city (training in data-rich cities, testing in cities with different urban morphology, density, or climate zone) — where the semantic land-use categories remain comparable but visual appearance differs substantially.

Added value to my researchCross-city transfer is the central challenge in applied GeoAI: models trained on data-rich cities (New York, Beijing) degrade when deployed in data-scarce cities with different morphologies. The paper’s result suggests that aligning a geospatial model to human geographic similarity judgements would give it a semantically grounded representation that generalizes across urban morphological distributions — the spatial analogue of ImageNet-A robustness. This directly supports my goal of building transferable GeoAI models for urban health and accessibility policy, where the regions most needing analysis are typically the most data-scarce and morphologically dissimilar from training cities.
