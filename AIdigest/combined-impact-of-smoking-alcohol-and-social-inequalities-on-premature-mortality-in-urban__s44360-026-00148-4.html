Combined impact of smoking, alcohol and social inequalities on premature mortality in urban India

Vendhan Gajalakshmi · Fiona Bragg · Ben Lacey · Vendhan Kanimozhi · Manigandan Chandrasekaran · Ramu Ashok Kumar · Paul Sherliker · Richard Peto · Sarah Lewington Published 2026-06-19 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00148-4 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

In a 20-year prospective cohort of 458,609 adults in Chennai, India, educational disadvantage alone triples premature mortality (RR ~2.9) across all major disease categories, even among non-smokers and non-drinkers. In men, smoking and alcohol use each independently amplify this risk, and their combination nearly doubles mortality (RR 1.89) — with low-education men three times more likely to smoke and drink than highly educated peers, meaning social inequalities compound behavioral risk. Alcohol showed a dose–response relationship with no evidence of a protective effect at moderate intake. These findings underscore that social and behavioral determinants of premature death operate multiplicatively in a middle-income urban setting, with implications for health equity and public health strategy across India.

Why It Matters

  • Largest prospective cohort in India to date. With 500,810 recruits followed for up to 20 years and 52,127 deaths, this is uniquely powered to disentangle the independent and combined effects of education, smoking, and alcohol with narrow confidence intervals — enabling reliable sub-group estimation that prior Indian studies could not achieve.
  • Quantifies the education–mortality gradient across nine cause-of-death categories. Even absent any behavioral risk factors, mortality from stroke, heart disease, TB, cancer, renal disease, and other causes all showed steep inverse associations with schooling (RR up to 4.2 for TB), pointing to healthcare access disparities and broader social determinants.
  • Challenges the protective-drinking hypothesis in an LMI context. No protective effect of moderate alcohol was found for cardiac mortality — a finding that diverges from many high-income studies and has direct relevance for alcohol policy in India and similar middle-income settings.
  • Provides a realistic behavioral change baseline. The resurvey showed that >97% of smokers and drinkers had not quit over 14 years despite global cessation pressures, framing taxation and structural healthcare reform as more realistic levers than behavior-change campaigns.
  • Transferable insights to other low- and middle-income settings. Chennai's educational and behavioral risk distributions are not exceptional among India's major cities, making the qualitative findings relevant across South Asia and comparable middle-income urban populations.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Study population
500,810 adults aged ≥35 years recruited from Chennai city (two of ten administrative zones fully canvassed; five more zones at 30–50% street sampling) during 1 January 1998 – 31 December 2001. After excluding those aged ≥70 or with chronic disease at baseline, the analytic cohort was 458,609.
Follow-up
Mortality monitored to 2020 (intervened by COVID-19); mean follow-up 16.4 ± 5.4 person-years at risk. Active follow-up with annual visits to 2005, then biennial. Passive follow-up via probabilistic linkage to Chennai municipal death records.
Deaths recorded
52,127 deaths at ages 35–69 years (31,337 men; 20,790 women); 91% identified by active follow-up. Cause of death assessed by verbal autopsy using open-narrative fieldworker interviews; each narrative independently coded by two study doctors with ICD-10 three-character codes.
Resurvey
A random subsample of 10,161 participants resurveyed in 2013–2014 (~14 years post-baseline) to assess reproducibility of self-reported smoking/drinking and to correct for regression-dilution bias.
Mortality reference data
Registrar General of India age- and sex-specific Chennai mortality rates; urban Tamil Nadu official statistics for 2010 (the study's median year of death).
Socioeconomic data
Self-reported educational level (years of formal schooling; four categories), composite household-asset-based SES score (housing type, cooking fuel, ownership of bicycle/motorcycle/car/refrigerator/TV/electric grain grinder). No income or caste data collected.
Analysis software
SAS v9.4 and R v4.1.2. Cox proportional hazards regression stratified by 5-year age groups. Analytical code available at GitHub (Cox-Macro) and GitHub (Chennai-Study).
Data availability
Primary data not publicly available (consent limitation); deidentified data available on reasonable request to the corresponding author (S.L., sarah.lewington@ndph.ox.ac.uk) following institutional ethics approval.

Core Methods

  • Prospective cohort design: household-based recruitment of all adults aged ≥35 years in sampled streets of Chennai; 5% refusal rate. Exclusion of those with pre-existing chronic disease at baseline to limit reverse causality.
  • Exposure assessment: self-reported educational level (four categories), composite household-asset SES score (quartiles), smoking status (never/ex/current, type: cigarette vs. bidi, quantity), and alcohol drinking (never/ex/current, frequency, quantity in 10-g units). Main analyses used ever vs. never classification given high behavioral stability.
  • Verbal autopsy mortality coding: trained fieldworkers interviewed family informants using an open narrative plus checklist; each narrative independently ICD-10-coded by two study doctors. Passive follow-up via probabilistic probabilistic linkage (name, age, sex, address) to city records.
  • Cox proportional hazards regression: multivariable models stratified by 5-year age groups (35–69 years), adjusted for age, sex, education, SES, and smoking/drinking as appropriate. Floating absolute risk method (Easton et al.) used to estimate variances for each category.
  • Regression-dilution correction: resurvey data used to estimate usual (rather than baseline) smoking and alcohol consumption within each baseline-assigned category, adjusting dose–response RRs for within-person measurement variability over follow-up.
  • Absolute risk estimation: age-standardized mortality RRs combined with official Chennai/Tamil Nadu age- and sex-specific mortality rates to yield the probability of a 35-year-old dying before age 70 by education and behavioral group.
  • Quality control: 5% random re-interview of recent participants by supervisors throughout the baseline survey for fieldworker training and data validation.

① Spatialize the education–mortality gradient: from city-level to neighborhood-level health mapping

Extending this paperThe Chennai cohort has precise household recruitment addresses but the main analyses collapse all spatial variation into city-wide Cox regression. A direct extension would geocode participant households and model the spatial non-stationarity of the education–mortality RR using geographically weighted regression (GWR/MGWR), asking whether low-education mortality penalties cluster in specific urban zones, slums, or areas of poor healthcare access — linking the paper's quantified social gradient to its spatial roots. ② Link spatial healthcare accessibility to the paper's unexplained education–mortality mechanism

Extending this paperThe paper explicitly acknowledges that the inverse association of education with mortality from every major disease category "might well reflect major disparities in access to effective healthcare" but states "we cannot quantify these." A natural extension: overlay the cohort's geocoded neighborhood data with spatial measures of healthcare facility proximity (primary care, hospital, TB clinic) and fit a mediation model — asking what fraction of the education–mortality RR is mediated by measurable spatial access deficits. This would transform the paper's open question into a quantified mechanism. The US-cities healthcare diversity paper in the corpus (trade-off between diversity and specialized healthcare) provides a comparable methodological framework.

Added value to my researchThis is the core of my healthcare accessibility agenda: applying R2SFCA or 2SFCA to geocode healthcare facilities in Chennai neighborhoods, delineate supply-demand catchments, and test whether low-access areas statistically explain residual education–mortality gradients — a direct pipeline from health geography methods to public health policy evidence.

Cross-ref (corpus): Trade-off between diversity and provision of specialized healthcare in US cities — 10.1038/s44284-025-00326-7

③ Apply explainable ML to predict mortality risk strata and identify non-linear interaction effects

Extending this paperThe paper uses Cox proportional hazards regression — a linear, additive model in the log-hazard scale. While powerful for overall RRs, it may miss non-linear and higher-order interaction effects between education, SES, smoking quantity, alcohol frequency, age, and sex. A direct extension: train a survival gradient boosting model (XGBoost-Cox) on the same covariates, apply SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) to decompose feature importance by subgroup, and compare with the Cox estimates to reveal any effect heterogeneity the linear model smoothed over — particularly for bidi vs. cigarette mortality differences after education adjustment.

Added value to my researchExplainable ML for spatial health prediction is one of my core research directions. This use case grounds it in a major mortality dataset: SHAP-based decomposition of mortality risk provides a template for attributing spatial health disparities to education, access, and behavioral covariates simultaneously — a paradigm applicable to my work on digital health divides and telehealth access.

Cross-ref (corpus): Socio-spatial inequality and the effects of density on COVID-19 transmission in US cities — 10.1038/s44284-023-00008-2

④ Extend from individual risk to population-attributable spatial burden mapping under climate scenarios

Extending this paperThe paper quantifies individual-level RRs but does not translate these into geographically distributed population-attributable mortality burden across Chennai's neighborhoods. Extending it: combine the paper's education- and behavior-stratified RRs with fine-resolution spatial population data (WorldPop, census) and neighborhood-level education distributions to produce a map of preventable deaths per km², identifying where interventions (healthcare, education, taxation) would yield the greatest absolute mortality reductions. Cross-referencing with the PM2.5–India-mortality corpus paper provides a multi-risk-factor spatial burden framing.

⑤ Build a transferable mortality-risk digital twin from the Chennai cohort for other Indian cities

Extending this paperThe paper argues its findings are qualitatively transferable across India's major cities yet does not operationalize this claim. An extension: use the paper's fitted Cox parameters (education, smoking, drinking RRs) as Bayesian prior distributions and update them with smaller local cohort data or sample surveys in other Indian cities (Mumbai, Delhi, Kolkata) to produce city-specific calibrated risk surfaces. This would formally instantiate the paper's transferability claim as a multi-city mortality digital twin.
