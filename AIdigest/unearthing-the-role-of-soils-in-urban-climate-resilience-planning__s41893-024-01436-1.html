Unearthing the role of soils in urban climate resilience planning

Monika Shankar · Melody Ng · Morgan Rogers · Elizabeth M. Cook · Dustin L. Herrmann · Kirsten Schwarz Published 2024-10-28 DOI 10.1038/s41893-024-01436-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Urban soils are systematically overlooked in climate resilience planning, yet they deliver essential services — stormwater regulation, heat mitigation, carbon storage, and biodiversity support. This paper advocates reframing urban soils from a managed hazard to essential infrastructure within an equity-centred social-ecological framework, and calls for sustained investment to democratize access to healthy soils and their climate benefits across all communities.

★

Why It Matters

  • Closes a critical planning blind spot. Soils are the substrate of virtually every urban green intervention — yet climate adaptation plans treat them as a contaminant risk rather than an asset, creating policies that undermine the very nature-based solutions they champion.
  • Links soil health to environmental justice. Historically disinvested communities bear the worst legacy soil contamination while gaining the least from soil-dependent ecosystem services (urban forests, gardens, parks), making soil equity a tangible dimension of climate justice.
  • Provides an actionable reframing. By positioning soils as essential infrastructure — analogous to roads or pipes — the paper opens a path to sustained public investment budgets, maintenance regimes, and regulatory protection that discretionary "green" programmes lack.
  • Bridges disciplines. The social-ecological framework integrates soil science, urban ecology, climate adaptation, and environmental justice into a single governance lens — directly enabling cross-sectoral collaboration that isolated disciplinary policies cannot achieve.

① Map the equity gap in urban soil ecosystem-service access using spatial accessibility methods

Extending this paperThe paper asserts that historically disinvested communities have inequitable access to healthy-soil-dependent services (parks, gardens, urban forests) but offers no quantitative spatial evidence. A direct extension: apply a 2SFCA or R2SFCA accessibility model to measure each neighbourhood's access to quality urban greenspace as a proxy for soil-based ecosystem services, overlay it with legacy soil contamination maps and socioeconomic vulnerability indices, and produce an urban soil equity score at the census-tract level. This would turn the paper's framework from assertion into a reproducible, city-comparable metric — and identify which communities need the most investment, precisely the actionable output the paper calls for.

Added value to my researchThis is a natural extension of my 2SFCA/R2SFCA work into a new service domain: instead of healthcare facility access, the supply side becomes urban greenspace quality weighted by soil health indicators (contamination, organic matter, permeability), and the demand side is residential population weighted by heat-vulnerability and income. The spatial non-stationarity of both soil quality and population vulnerability makes this an ideal candidate for MGWR, revealing where the soil-equity relationship differs across intraurban gradients — directly publishable as a methods paper in urban environmental justice.

② Use explainable ML to disentangle drivers of urban soil quality across cities

Extending this paperThe paper's framework is city-agnostic, but urban soil quality is the product of interacting historical, demographic, and land-use processes that vary enormously across cities. Extending it: compile multi-city soil health observations (contamination levels, organic carbon, compaction, microbial richness) from existing monitoring databases and city-led soil surveys, then train an ensemble ML model (gradient boosting + spatial cross-validation) with SHAP explanations to quantify how much variance in soil quality is explained by: race/income history, imperviousness, distance to industrial legacy sites, tree canopy, and age of urban development. The SHAP importance maps would translate directly into the prioritization logic needed to operationalize the paper's "invest in equity" recommendation.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my explainable ML for spatial prediction pipeline to a new environmental justice outcome variable. The spatial cross-validation protocol I use for GeoAI benchmarking applies cleanly here; SHAP force plots become a communication tool for planners and community advocates — bridging technical methods to the governance audience the paper targets. The microbial homogenization findings from urban greenspace soil studies could provide a ready-made feature set.

③ Integrate urban soils into a multi-objective nature-based solution siting model

Extending this paperThe paper argues for treating soils as essential infrastructure, but current nature-based solution (NbS) planning models (green roofs, urban forests, bioswales) site interventions without modelling the soil substrate. A concrete extension: build a multi-objective facility-location model that simultaneously maximizes: (a) climate service delivery (heat mitigation, stormwater, carbon), (b) equity of access across income and race strata, and (c) soil-health improvement potential — constrained by legacy contamination, soil permeability, and budget. This is the algorithmic operationalization of the paper's "investment framework," turning Fig. 1's conceptual matrix into an optimization problem.

Added value to my researchThis generalizes my EV-charging efficiency-vs-equity siting work into urban green infrastructure, where the three-way objective (climate service / equity / soil-health co-benefit) mirrors the charging-station efficiency/equity trade-off I have published on. The soil permeability and contamination layers add a new physical constraint dimension that makes the problem richer than pure location optimization — and the model output (an investment priority map) is a direct policy deliverable aligned with my urban analytics agenda.

④ Build a GeoAI soil-health index from multi-source remote sensing and crowd-sensed data

This directly addresses the data scarcity barrier the paper acknowledges, providing the monitoring substrate that its investment framework requires.

⑤ Test whether green gentrification displaces the soil-equity gains from urban soil investment

Extending this paperThe paper advocates investment in soil health for underserved communities, but urban greening investments are well-documented drivers of green gentrification — displacing the very residents they aim to benefit. A critical extension: use difference-in-differences or synthetic-control methods to evaluate whether past urban soil remediation and urban-greening projects in low-income neighbourhoods subsequently triggered neighbourhood demographic change (rising rents, population displacement), and quantify the net equity outcome. This grounds the paper's "equity-centred investment" recommendation in causal evidence of whether such investments actually deliver equitable outcomes or reproduce displacement.

Added value to my researchThis links my urban analytics and health geography lines to the gentrification literature via spatial causal inference. The study design (treatment = soil remediation / greening investment; outcome = neighbourhood demographic shift + greenspace accessibility) is a natural application of my spatiotemporal kernel density and spatial econometric methods, and the equity evaluation framing mirrors my EV-charging and healthcare-access equity analyses. Findings would directly inform whether the paper's policy recommendations are sufficient or need anti-displacement safeguards.
