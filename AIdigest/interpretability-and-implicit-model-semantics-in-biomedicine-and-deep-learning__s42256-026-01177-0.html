Interpretability and implicit model semantics in biomedicine and deep learning

Jonathan Warrell · Michael Gancz · Hussein Mohsen · Prashant Emani · Mark Gerstein Published 2026-03-23 DOI 10.1038/s42256-026-01177-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Interpretability is only one facet of a deep-learning model's full semantics. By drawing on the philosophy of science, this Brief Communication introduces an "implicit model semantics" framework that distinguishes the minimal semantics (the learned input–output mapping) from richer implicit semantics (structured features embedded in the network's internal representations). Applied to AlphaFold, computational genomics, and medical diagnosis, the framework reveals that progressively removing explicit physical or biological constraints from architectures does not impoverish — and often enriches — what the model implicitly encodes, with consequences for how biomedical AI systems should be evaluated, trusted, and interpreted.

Core Methods

  • Implicit semantics framework: introduces a two-level taxonomy — minimal semantics (the learned input–output function, probed via in silico simulation) vs. implicit semantics (structured, recoverable knowledge in the network's internal representations, probed via layer inspection or latent-factor decomposition).
  • Philosophy-of-science grounding: connects the framework to existing philosophy-of-science theories of model semantics (French & Ladyman 1999, French 2000), placing DL interpretability within the broader epistemology of scientific modeling.
  • Solomonoff induction as unifying principle: formalizes the compression view of science — theories are programs; the credence placed in a model is a Bayesian distribution over programs weighted by their Kolmogorov complexity — providing a rigorous definition of why interpretability serves scientific induction.
  • Case-study analysis: applies the framework to AlphaFold2/3 (explicit → implicit physical semantics), computational genomics (in silico mutagenesis vs. weight decomposition), and medical imaging DL (accuracy–interpretability trade-off in clinical deployment).
  • Adversarial training perspective: discusses adversarial training and adversarial examples as tools to audit implicit semantics and mitigate unknown confounders in medical imaging models.

② Extend the AlphaFold semantics progression to foundation models in medical imaging

Extending this paperThe paper traces how AlphaFold removed explicit physical priors yet retained implicit physical semantics. The same trajectory is now underway in medical imaging: gigapixel pathology models (e.g., whole-slide foundation models) remove hand-crafted histological features yet learn clinically meaningful representations. Extending the paper: apply its minimal vs. implicit semantics framework quantitatively to a pathology foundation model — measuring how much biological information (cell type, grade, prognosis) is recoverable via linear probes on frozen embeddings without explicit annotation, then trace how that implicit richness scales with pretraining data volume.

Added value to my researchFoundation-model probing has a direct analogue in geospatial foundation models: I can probe whether a fine-tuned geo-FM's frozen embeddings implicitly encode accessibility gradients, demographic structure, or health outcomes without explicit labeling — using this paper's framework to justify and structure the probing experiments as semantic recovery, not just transfer learning.

③ Ground the implicit-semantics framework in causal structure to handle confounding

Extending this paperThe paper acknowledges that implicit semantics may encode confounders (e.g., hospital-site artefacts in medical imaging) rather than true biology, and cites adversarial training as one mitigation. A deeper extension: replace the paper's Bayesian compression view with an explicit causal graphical model in which semantics are defined relative to a causal DAG — distinguishing spurious correlations (confounders) from genuine implicit biological structure. This grounds the framework's "is implicit semantics real?" question in the causal inference literature and provides principled tools (do-calculus, invariant risk minimization) to separate them.

Added value to my researchSpatial predictive models for health outcomes routinely conflate environmental determinism with socioeconomic confounding. Adopting the paper's implicit-semantics language plus a causal DAG would let me articulate — and audit — whether my GWR coefficient maps recover place effects or merely unmeasured confounders, directly strengthening causal claims in my health-geography work.

⑤ Use the explicit-to-implicit semantics trajectory to design interpretable GeoAI for health geography

Extending this paperThe paper frames the AlphaFold2→3 shift as an insight about how DL can progressively internalize domain knowledge. The same logic applies to biomedical explainability more broadly: the paper proposes that when interpretability incurs negligible accuracy loss it should be preferred, but it does not provide a principled mechanism for detecting this crossover. An extension: develop a sensitivity-of-accuracy-to-interpretability curve (sweeping regularization strength on interpretable constraints) across multiple tasks to empirically locate where the accuracy cost of interpretability becomes negligible — making the paper's pragmatic recommendation operational.
