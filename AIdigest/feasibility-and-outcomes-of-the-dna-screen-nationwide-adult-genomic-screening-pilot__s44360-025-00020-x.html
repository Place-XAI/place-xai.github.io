Core Conclusion

Australia's DNA Screen prospective pilot enrolled 10,263 young adults (18–40 years) nationwide and detected pathogenic or likely pathogenic variants (PLPVs) in 2.0% of participants across ten high-risk genes linked to hereditary breast/ovarian cancer, Lynch syndrome, and familial hypercholesterolaemia. Of those referred for clinical care, 97.9% accepted and 87.3% attended specialist appointments — critically, 74.5% of attendees would have been ineligible for any government-funded criteria-based genomic testing, demonstrating that population-wide screening reaches a largely hidden at-risk population that current criteria-driven pathways miss entirely.

★

Why It Matters

  • Proves real-world feasibility at national scale. Unlike prior retrospective or biobank-return studies, this is a prospective, de-novo population screening programme embedded within a national public healthcare system — setting the template for replication internationally.
  • Exposes the gap in criteria-based testing. Three-quarters of high-risk individuals identified would have been completely missed by the existing referral criteria that underpin government-funded genetic testing in Australia — a structural blind spot with direct policy consequences.
  • Demonstrates high public engagement and clinical uptake. More than 30,000 people registered within days of a single media campaign; clinical follow-up rates (97.9% referral acceptance) rival those of symptomatic clinical pathways, countering assumptions that population genomic screening yields poor engagement.
  • Achieves demographic diversity by design. Stratified recruitment ensured geographic, socioeconomic, Indigenous Australian and culturally/linguistically diverse (CALD, 30.0%) representation — a deliberate contrast with US studies dominated by white, older, female cohorts.
  • Provides a cost-effectiveness bridge. Coupled with prior health-economic modelling, the findings position population genomic screening as a scalable, cost-justifiable preventive health strategy for countries with national public healthcare systems.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Study design
Prospective nationwide pilot; Australia; August 2022 – July 2024. Target base population: 8,324,242 Australian adults aged 18–40 years.
Registrants
30,017 individuals registered to participate (15,899 in first 48 hours); spanning all Australian states and territories including remote areas. 42.3% CALD, 36.3% born outside Australia.
Final cohort
10,263 participants completed genomic screening. Median age 31.9 years; 45.5% male; 30.0% CALD; 2.2% Indigenous Australian; geographically representative of national population distribution.
Genomic assay
Custom next-generation sequencing panel (Agilent SureSelect XTHS2); 10 high-risk medically actionable genes (BRCA1, BRCA2, PALB2, ATM for HBOC; MLH1, MSH2, MSH6 for Lynch syndrome; LDLR, APOB, PCSK9 for FH); target coverage 200 reads/base; sequenced on Illumina NextSeq550. Aligned to GRCh38 reference genome.
Variant curation
Modified ACMG/AMP guidelines (Richards et al. 2015; Tavtigian et al. 2020). PLPVs authorized by clinical geneticist, laboratory director and principal investigator. Benign/VUS not returned.
Clinical data
Standardized forms from 11 specialist partner sites; collected and managed via REDCap (Monash University Helix platform). Personal/family history, eligibility assessment for government-funded testing recorded at each appointment.
Population reference
Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) census definition used for CALD classification; SEIFA (Socio-Economic Indexes for Areas) by postcode for SES; ARIA+ remoteness classification for geographic remoteness. Analysis in R (versions 4.2.1–4.4.2).
Data availability
Primary data not publicly available (consent restrictions); de-identified data may be requested from Prof. Lacaze (paul.lacaze@monash.edu) with ethics approval. Code available on request.

Core Methods

  • Stratified random sampling for recruitment: registrants were invited in batches of 500–1,000 using stratified random sampling in R, allocating targets across sex, age, state/territory, CALD status and remoteness to achieve demographic representativeness; under-represented groups (men, remote, CALD, Indigenous) were preferentially invited.
  • Custom next-generation sequencing (NGS) panel: saliva-derived DNA extracted and enriched using Agilent SureSelect XTHS2 indexed libraries; sequenced to 200× coverage on Illumina NextSeq550; aligned to GRCh38 with exon-flanking region analysis; validated at 100% concordance against known PLPV carriers.
  • ACMG/AMP variant curation: modified classification pipeline to identify pathogenic (P) and likely pathogenic (LP) variants only; VUS and benign variants explicitly excluded from results return to minimize over-diagnosis.
  • Clinical eligibility assessment: at partner sites, clinicians retrospectively assessed each PLPV carrier's eligibility for Australian Government-funded index-case or cascade genetic testing based on personal and family history, using condition-specific funded criteria (e.g. DLCNS ≥6 for FH; ≥10% PLPV probability for HBOC/LS).
  • Participant satisfaction survey: 10,658 post-consent surveys (86.7% response rate) measuring convenience, information sufficiency, satisfaction and acceptability of genomic testing.
  • Descriptive statistical analysis in R (v4.2.1–4.4.2): cohort demographics, PLPV yield, referral uptake and clinical characteristics reported with frequencies and proportions; insufficient statistical power to test subgroup differences in follow-up outcomes.

① Spatial accessibility analysis of the genomic screening pathway

Extending this paperThe DNA Screen cohort includes geographic remoteness and SES-by-postcode data (Fig. 2), but the paper does not quantify spatial access to the 11 partner clinical genetics sites as a predictor of referral uptake or appointment attendance. A direct extension: apply 2SFCA / R2SFCA accessibility modelling to the national postcode-level cohort, using travel time to the nearest partner lipid clinic or genetics service as the supply measure and PLPV carrier home postcodes as demand points. This would identify geographic cold spots where clinical follow-up lags — informing where telehealth or satellite clinics are most needed to close the 87.3% → 100% attendance gap.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my 2SFCA / R2SFCA healthcare accessibility toolkit to a precision-medicine setting. The per-condition service-area structure (lipid clinics for FH, clinical genetics for HBOC/LS) maps cleanly onto my multi-type facility accessibility framework, and the postcode-level SES data provides an equity lens consistent with my health geography work. The result is a publishable spatial accessibility profile of a real national genomic screening programme.

② Integrating mobile-phone-derived mobility flows to predict clinical follow-up uptake

Extending this paperThe paper reports that 13 referred individuals "declined to attend or could not be contacted" while 3 have pending appointments — but has no behavioural model of who fails to engage with clinical follow-up. Extending the work: link participant postcode-level socioeconomic and remoteness data to mobile phone mobility traces to estimate revealed willingness-to-travel and likely healthcare-seeking behaviour. This would build a predictive model of referral drop-off risk for each geographic zone, enabling proactive outreach to likely non-attenders before appointment booking — converting the study's 87.3% attendance rate into a near-complete one.

Added value to my researchMobile phone records are a core data source in my research; using them to infer spatially resolved healthcare engagement propensity (rather than actual hospital visits) is a novel extension. This aligns directly with papers in my corpus showing mobile phone data capturing hospital bypass behaviour and SES-driven access inequities, and gives my spatiotemporal mobility-analysis tools a genomic screening application.

③ Human–AI collaboration to triage and optimize gene panel selection at scale

Extending this paperThe DNA Screen used a fixed expert-consensus 10-gene panel, deliberately excluding moderate-risk genes (e.g. PMS2, CHEK2) to reduce clinical burden. However, the right panel composition for a national programme is a dynamic health-economic optimization problem — not a one-time expert decision. Extending the paper: apply human–AI collaboration frameworks from cost-effectiveness analysis of disease screening to model how different gene panel configurations (adding or removing genes) alter the yield–burden trade-off across Australian demographic subgroups, feeding into a panel calibration process anchored in real DNA Screen yield data.

Added value to my researchMy explainable machine learning research focuses on making spatial prediction models interpretable for policymakers. Here, XAI applied to genomic yield prediction (given patient demographics, SES, geographic context) would identify which subpopulations benefit most from expanded gene panels — a GeoAI-for-genomics application that is both methodologically novel and policy-relevant for precision public health.

④ Gene-by-environment interactions: linking PLPV carriers to urban environmental exposures

Extending this paperThe DNA Screen identifies who has PLPVs but treats environment as background. However, penetrance of HBOC and FH variants is known to be modulated by lifestyle and environmental factors. Extending this paper: link the geographic postcode locations of identified PLPV carriers to ambient environmental exposure layers (air pollution, built-environment walkability, green-space access, food environment) and test whether environment modulates the clinical expression of PLPVs — for instance, whether FH PLPV carriers in high-pollution or low-walkability postcodes have higher LDL-C than carriers in healthier environments. This adds a preventive geography dimension to the study's clinical genetics findings.

Added value to my researchThis is a natural extension of my urban health geography work — coupling genomic risk with spatial exposure data is a new frontier for GeoAI. The DNA Screen's Australian postcode-level cohort provides a unique spatial anchor for gene-environment interaction analysis, connecting my remote sensing and multi-source data fusion methods to precision public health genomics.

⑤ Using health literacy and trust data to model differential screening uptake across CALD communities

Extending this paper30.0% of the DNA Screen cohort were CALD (culturally or linguistically diverse) and the programme required English-language proficiency — explicitly excluding non-English speakers from participation. This is an acknowledged equity gap: the paper's diverse cohort still does not capture the most linguistically isolated communities. Extending it: integrate digital health literacy and trust measures (surveyed across diverse populations) into the DNA Screen's recruitment model to simulate which CALD subgroups have the lowest probability of self-registering and would benefit most from community-health-worker-mediated outreach, multilingual materials, or telehealth genetic counselling — turning the current English-only pilot into a truly inclusive programme.

Added value to my researchHealth literacy, trust, and digital access are intertwined with the telehealth and digital divide themes in my research. Mapping digital health literacy scores onto the spatial distribution of CALD communities (using ABS census data + postcode-level literacy proxies) enables a spatial targeting model for equity-focused genomic screening outreach — directly combining my spatial accessibility and data-fusion methods with the public health equity agenda of this paper.
