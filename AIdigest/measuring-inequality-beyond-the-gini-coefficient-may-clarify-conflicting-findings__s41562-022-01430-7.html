Measuring inequality beyond the Gini coefficient may clarify conflicting findings

Kristin Blesch · Oliver P. Hauser · Jon M. Jachimowicz Published 2022-08-29 DOI 10.1038/s41562-022-01430-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The Gini coefficient — the field's near-universal inequality summary — systematically loses information by condensing an entire income distribution into one number. Across N = 3,056 US counties, a two-parameter Ortega model consistently outperforms all 17 competing single-parameter models. Its two parameters distinguish bottom-concentrated versus top-concentrated inequality, and when correlated with 100 policy outcomes (health, crime, mobility), the Ortega parameters reveal 33 significant associations that the Gini coefficient misses entirely — including opposing effects of bottom- vs. top-inequality on obesity and educational attainment. Moving to multi-parameter models and data-driven model selection can resolve longstanding contradictions in the inequality literature.

Why It Matters

  • Identifies a measurement root cause of conflicting findings. Many contradictory results in the inequality literature — on obesity, well-being, prosociality — coexist because they all use the Gini coefficient. This paper shows the contradiction may stem from where inequality is concentrated (top vs. bottom), not from disagreement about the facts.
  • Reframes inequality measurement as a model-selection problem. By treating income distribution summarization as a data reduction task and using AICc-based Borda voting across 17 competing Lorenz-curve models, it gives researchers a replicable, criteria-driven procedure — not an ad-hoc choice of statistic.
  • Reveals directionally opposite effects masked by the Gini. For obesity, greater bottom-concentrated inequality is positively correlated while top-concentrated inequality is negatively correlated. A Gini-only analysis finds no significant association — hiding two real, opposing signals.
  • Ships an open, interactive platform. Ortega parameter estimates for all 3,056 counties plus an interactive R Shiny tool are freely available at measuringinequality.com, enabling immediate reuse by researchers and policymakers.
  • Calls for data infrastructure reform. The method requires fine-grained income distribution data (at least 15 data points per Lorenz curve, including top-income shares); the paper explicitly urges statistics bureaus worldwide to publish such data rather than only Gini values.

Core Methods

  • Data reduction framing: income distribution measurement is recast as a model-selection task — which parametric Lorenz-curve model best compresses the shape of real distributions while retaining maximum information?
  • Maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) of Lorenz curve parameters: following Chotikapanich & Griffiths (2002), parameters are estimated by maximizing a log-likelihood derived from a Dirichlet distribution representation of the Lorenz curve.
  • AICc model evaluation: the bias-corrected Akaike Information Criterion is computed per county per model (19–23 data points makes small-sample correction necessary); models are ranked by AICc within each county.
  • Borda count voting: model ranks are aggregated across all 3,056 counties via a Borda count, providing a democratic model-selection criterion that identifies the winner across all counties (rather than in any single one).
  • AICc difference analysis: pairwise AICc differences (Δi,j) quantify the strength of evidence for the winning model over single-parameter competitors; thresholds >10 = decisive, 4–10 = some, <4 = inconclusive.
  • Simulation study of Ortega parameters: systematic variation of α and γ while holding the other constant reveals that α captures bottom-concentrated inequality (stretches the Lorenz curve at lower percentiles) and γ captures top-concentrated inequality (90th+ percentile).
  • Robustness checks: nonlinear least squares (NLS) as alternative estimation; BIC as alternative criterion; validation that 98.5% of true-model detection rate is maintained at 19 data points.
  • Correlational study with policy outcomes: Pearson correlations (Gini) and partial Pearson correlations (Ortega α and γ jointly) with 100 county-level variables; Bonferroni correction (α = 0.0005) for multiple testing across 100 variables.

① Spatialize the Ortega parameters — a county-level GeoAI model of bottom vs. top inequality

Extending this paperThe paper's Fig. 4 maps Gini, α, and γ across US counties but treats them as static attributes to be visualized, not as spatially structured outcomes to be explained. A direct extension: fit a geographically weighted regression (GWR/MGWR) of Ortega α and γ on urban structural covariates (density, land use, commuting patterns) to ask whether the spatial heterogeneity of bottom- versus top-concentrated inequality follows different drivers. This would move the paper from a mapping exercise to a mechanistic spatial model of where and why different kinds of inequality concentrate. The corpus paper on intra-urban inequality scaling provides the theoretical backbone — examining how α and γ scale with city size and density would test whether bottom- and top-inequality follow different urban scaling laws.

Fitting spatially varying coefficients of Ortega α vs. γ on accessibility-related predictors (POI density, transit access, job-housing balance) would let me ask whether places with high bottom-inequality are the same places that score poorly on my 2SFCA accessibility metrics — creating a bridge between distributional income inequality measurement and spatial accessibility measurement for urban health policy.

② Apply the paper's model-selection framework to spatial inequality metrics beyond income

Extending this paperThe paper argues compellingly that the Gini coefficient is the wrong single-parameter summary for income — but the same critique applies to any domain where a Lorenz-curve-style summary is used: land access, hospital utilization, green-space distribution. Extending it: adapt the paper's AICc-Borda procedure to spatial accessibility distributions (replacing income percentiles with accessibility scores across census tracts) and test whether a two-parameter model again outperforms the Gini of accessibility. The corpus paper on hospital utilization disparities via mobile-phone data provides a ready-made case study where an accessibility Lorenz curve could be estimated across socioeconomic strata.

Added value to my researchThis directly enriches my work on 2SFCA accessibility measurement. If the distribution of accessibility scores across a study area is itself best described by a two-parameter Lorenz model, then reporting "the Gini of accessibility" loses information in exactly the same way the income Gini does — meaning my accessibility equity analyses should adopt a multi-parameter framework parallel to this paper's.

③ Link the Ortega parameters to experienced income segregation via mobility data

Extending this paperThe paper correlates Ortega α and γ with 100 static policy outcomes but never tests whether they predict dynamic behavioral outcomes — specifically, the degree to which residents of a county actually encounter people from different income groups in daily life. Extending it: join the county-level Ortega estimates with mobility-derived income-encounter measures to test whether bottom-concentrated inequality (α) and top-concentrated inequality (γ) have different effects on the degree of socioeconomic mixing in daily movement. The corpus paper on mobility patterns and experienced income segregation in US cities is exactly the dataset needed for this extension.

④ Use the Ortega framework to disentangle inequality effects on intergenerational mobility

Extending this paperThe paper applies the Ortega parameters to 100 policy outcomes but treats social mobility as one variable among many, without exploiting the paper's own theoretical insight: that bottom-concentrated inequality (α) may mechanistically constrain upward mobility for low-income children, while top-concentrated inequality (γ) may relate to elite entrenchment at the top. Extending it: apply the Ortega decomposition to a dedicated intergenerational mobility study, testing whether α and γ carry opposite signed effects on upward mobility probability — exactly as they do for obesity. The corpus paper on childhood wealth inequality and intergenerational income mobility in the US provides the mobility outcome variable needed.

Added value to my researchMy health-geography research asks how neighborhood conditions (including inequality) shape health outcomes across the life course. A finding that bottom- and top-concentrated inequality have opposite effects on children's long-term mobility — parallel to the obesity finding — would sharpen hypotheses in my accessibility-and-health work: it is not "inequality per se" but the spatial concentration of deprivation that shapes outcomes, which my R2SFCA service-area models directly capture.

Cross-ref (corpus): The association between childhood exposure to local wealth inequality and intergenerational income mobility in the United States — 10.1038/s41467-025-64222-3

⑤ Build a multi-parameter inequality dashboard for urban policy analytics

Extending this paperThe paper's interactive tool at measuringinequality.com shows county-level Ortega estimates for the US but is limited to income distributions and relies on relatively dated ACS data. Extending it: create a city-level multi-indicator inequality dashboard that computes Ortega-style two-parameter fits for multiple distributional outcomes simultaneously (income, housing costs, green-space access, hospital proximity) across cities globally, updated as new data become available. The corpus paper on urban inequality, the housing crisis, and water access in US cities demonstrates the policy need — different resource distributions in the same city may show very different α/γ profiles, enabling targeted policy intervention rather than a single "inequality score."
