Impacts of transportation network companies on urban mobility

Mi Diao · Hui Kong · Jinhua Zhao Published 2021-02-01 DOI 10.1038/s41893-020-00678-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using fixed-effect panel models across US metropolitan statistical areas (MSAs), this study finds that transportation network companies (TNCs) such as Uber and Lyft have intensified urban transport challenges rather than relieving them: they increased road congestion intensity by 0.9% and duration by 4.5%, reduced public transit ridership by 8.9%, and had no significant effect on vehicle ownership in general. Despite their promise as a sharing-economy solution, TNCs largely substitute for public transit and induce additional road traffic through low-occupancy deadheading miles, forming a vicious cycle of congestion and transit decline.

★

Why It Matters

  • First comprehensive US-wide causal assessment of TNCs. Moving beyond city-specific surveys, this is the first panel study to simultaneously quantify TNC effects on all three key mobility dimensions — congestion, transit ridership, and vehicle ownership — across 174 MSAs.
  • Punctures the "sharing economy = sustainability" narrative. TNCs are widely marketed as a path to sustainable transport; this study provides systematic causal evidence that their net effect is negative for congestion and transit, challenging the prevailing optimism.
  • Temporal dynamics matter. The TNC effect on transit ridership grows for three years post-entry before stabilizing at ~16% decline; the congestion duration effect continues growing. Policy responses must account for this escalation, not just entry impacts.
  • Actionable policy design. The finding that TNCs only reduce vehicle ownership in high-transit MSAs, and that a second entrant (Lyft) amplifies negative effects through price competition, provides specific levers for regulators — e.g., restricting market entry in transit-poor areas or taxing deadheading miles.
  • Addresses endogeneity rigorously. The IV approach (distance to TNC hub cities as instrument) confirms OLS results are directionally correct and likely conservative, adding credibility to the causal claims.

Core Methods

  • Fixed-effect panel models (OLS): two-way fixed effects (MSA fixed effects + time fixed effects) absorb unobservable MSA characteristics and common temporal trends; the treatment dummy TNCit captures average entry effect.
  • Temporal evolution specification: dummy variables for each year-after-entry (TNC_Year1, TNC_Year2, … TNC_YearN) trace the growing effect over time; a separate dummy captures the abrupt impact of the second TNC operator's entry.
  • Instrumental variable (IV) / 2SLS regression: instrument = MSA's distance to the nearest TNC hub city (Uber HQ or regional offices), addressing endogeneity in TNC entry timing. First stage: logit model predicting TNC presence; second stage: regress mobility outcomes on predicted TNC presence.
  • Difference-in-differences expansion: robustness check including non-TNC MSAs as a control group, comparing mobility changes before and after TNC entry in TNC MSAs relative to never-treated MSAs (2005 vs. 2016).
  • Outcome variables in log form: TTI, Congested Hours, monthly PT ridership, and household vehicle ownership are all log-transformed; coefficients are interpreted as approximate percentage changes.
  • Sample: 174 MSAs for transit/ownership models; 44 MSAs for congestion models (limited by NPMRDS coverage); all with TNC entry between 2010 and 2016.

① Replace MSA-level aggregation with fine-grained spatiotemporal mobility flows

Extending this paperThe paper's fixed-effect panel operates at the coarse MSA level, masking enormous within-city heterogeneity in TNC penetration, transit coverage, and congestion. A direct extension: disaggregate the analysis to the census-tract or grid-cell level by fusing smart-card transit data, mobile-phone origin-destination flows, and TNC activity traces. This would reveal which neighborhoods lose transit ridership (presumably low-income areas relying on buses) versus which gain an alternative, converting the paper's blunt MSA coefficient into a spatially heterogeneous treatment surface.

Added value to my researchThis aligns directly with my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and multi-source data fusion agenda: mobile-phone records and transit smart-card data can jointly delineate TNC-transit substitution zones at fine resolution, feeding a spatially-weighted R2SFCA model where the effective supply of public transit shrinks in high-TNC-penetration zones — creating a dynamic, data-driven accessibility estimator instead of the paper's static count.

② Quantify the spatial equity dimension: do TNCs worsen transit access for vulnerable populations?

Extending this paperThe paper reports an average 8.9% ridership decline but does not decompose effects by neighborhood income, car ownership, or demographic vulnerability. Extending it: interact the TNC treatment with census-derived socioeconomic variables to test whether transit loss falls disproportionately on transit-dependent, low-income communities — and whether the vehicle-ownership reduction in top-10 transit MSAs benefits affluent car owners while harming low-income transit users. This would sharpen the paper's sustainability message with an equity lens.

③ Extend causal identification to bidirectional TNC–congestion feedback

Extending this paperThe paper tests how TNC entry causes congestion but explicitly flags the untested arrow in Fig. 1: that worsening congestion may in turn reduce transit ridership further (the vicious-cycle hypothesis). A natural extension: adopt a structural vector autoregression (SVAR) or Granger-causal framework at the MSA-month level, jointly modeling congestion, transit ridership, and TNC activity to identify the feedback loop and its strength over time — quantifying whether the indirect congestion-to-transit channel amplifies the direct TNC-to-transit effect.

Added value to my researchThis resonates with my interest in physics-informed graph learning and network causality: treating MSAs as nodes in a causal graph where TNC penetration, congestion, and transit are dynamically coupled state variables is a template for my spatiotemporal GNN work, where edge weights encode directional Granger causality rather than spatial adjacency — applicable to any urban system with feedback dynamics.

④ Link the paper's congestion effect to emissions and climate externalities

Extending this paperThe paper quantifies congestion intensity and duration but stops short of monetizing the externality. Extending it: map the paper's MSA-level congestion increases to excess vehicle-miles-traveled (VMT) and tailpipe emissions using fleet composition data, then project forward under TNC growth scenarios. The 4.5% increase in Congested Hours, compounded by TNCs' low occupancy (~0.5 passengers) and rising deadheading miles, likely translates to measurable CO2 impacts that would strengthen the sustainability case for regulation.

⑤ Build a multimodal network resilience model incorporating TNC as a dynamic layer

Extending this paperThe paper treats TNC entry as a permanent one-time shock, but TNCs operate as a dynamic, demand-responsive layer atop the fixed transit and road networks. Extending it: embed the paper's quantified substitution coefficient (8.9% ridership decline per TNC entry) into a multimodal network model where TNC supply fluctuates with surge pricing and can either complement or cannibalize transit depending on service frequency — testing whether transit-friendly MSAs are structurally more resilient to TNC disruption because their dense network limits the substitution opportunity.
