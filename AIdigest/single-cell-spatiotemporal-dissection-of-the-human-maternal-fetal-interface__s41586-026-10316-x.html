Single-cell spatiotemporal dissection of the human maternal–fetal interface

Wang, Cheng · Zhou, Yan · Wang, Yuejun · Guha, Tuhin Kumar · Luo, Zhida · Mustafaraj, Anxhela · McIntyre, Tara I. · Schwab, Marisa E. · Davidson, Brittany R. · Reeder, Gabriella C. · Wong, Ronald J. · England, Sarah K. · Gonzalez, Juan M. · Blelloch, Robert · Combes, Alexis J. · Giudice, Linda C. · Erlebacher, Adrian · MacKenzie, Tippi C. · Stevenson, David K. · Shaw, Gary M. · Snyder, Michael P. · Sun, Xiaofei · Winn, Virginia D. · Fisher, Susan J. · Li, Jingjing Published 2026-04-08 DOI 10.1038/s41586-026-10316-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

By integrating large-scale paired single-nucleus multiomics (221,380 snRNA-seq and 210,191 snATAC-seq nuclei), submicrometre-resolution spatial transcriptomics (~1.1 million cells across 16 tissue sections at 0.5-µm pixel resolution), and CODEX multiplex protein imaging, this study produces the most comprehensive spatiotemporally resolved atlas of the human maternal–fetal interface across gestational weeks 5–39. It discovers previously unknown arterial endothelial state transitions during spiral artery remodelling, identifies a novel decidual stromal subtype (DSC4) that suppresses trophoblast invasion via endocannabinoid signalling, develops a LASSO-based machine learning model of EVT invasiveness, and pinpoints the specific cell populations most genetically vulnerable to pre-eclampsia, preterm birth, and miscarriage.

Why It Matters

  • First gestational-span, spatially resolved single-cell multiomic reference. Prior studies were confined to narrow gestational windows; this atlas spans GW5–39 and integrates transcriptomics, chromatin accessibility, and submicrometre spatial mapping in one framework, substantially boosting resolution over all prior work.
  • Mechanistic discovery: the DSC4–endocannabinoid axis. The newly identified DSC4 subtype suppresses EVT invasion through CB1/endocannabinoid signalling — a finding with direct public health implications for prenatal cannabis use and adverse birth outcomes including preterm birth and stillbirth.
  • Transferable ML model of trophoblast invasiveness. A LASSO sparse learning model predicts EVT invasiveness from transcriptomes, validated across independent cohorts (smooth chorion, placenta accreta spectrum), opening a route to non-invasive assessment of trophoblast dysfunction from biopsy or liquid data.
  • Cell-type-resolved genetic risk maps for pregnancy complications. Integrating snATAC-seq open chromatin with GWAS datasets for pre-eclampsia, preterm birth, and miscarriage identifies fetal EVTs and a shared POU5F1+LGR5+ endometrial epithelial population as convergent vulnerability points — reframing these conditions as failures of a shared endometrial programme.
  • Open data infrastructure. The COSMOS explorer (cell.ucsf.edu/snPlacenta), raw data in dbGaP (phs004305.v1), and analysis code on GitHub enable broad community reuse of this reference resource.

Core Methods

  • Paired single-nucleus multiomics (snRNA-seq + snATAC-seq): 10x Genomics Chromium Next GEM Multiome; Cell Ranger ARC v2.0.0 alignment to GRCh38; Seurat v4 (RNA) and Signac (ATAC) integration via reciprocal PCA/LSI projection; Louvain clustering; UMAP visualization; doublet removal by Scrublet.
  • Cell-of-origin assignment: Souporcell with 1000 Genomes Project reference variants; 94.3% accuracy validated by UTY expression in male-fetus samples; maternal/fetal separation for >95% of nuclei (182,773/191,735).
  • Gene regulatory network reconstruction: CellOracle integrating snATAC-seq peak-to-gene links (GimmeMotifs + Cicero co-accessibility scaffolding) with snRNA-seq via Ridge regression; chromVAR for transcription factor motif enrichment; ATAC-seq footprinting at ~20 bp resolution.
  • Submicrometre spatial transcriptomics: STOmics Stereo-seq on 0.5-µm DNB arrays; deep-learning-based cell segmentation in SAW v8.1 pipeline; CellBin aggregation per cell boundary; Harmony batch correction; spatial cell community analysis identifying 6 recurrent anatomical niches via Stereopy v1.6.1.
  • CODEX multiplex protein imaging: 7-cycle Akoya CODEX microfluidic system; 4 fluorescence channels; 9-antibody barcoded panel; protein-level validation of transcriptomically defined endothelial state transitions.
  • Pseudotime trajectory inference: CellRank v1.5.1 for initial and terminal state identification; Palantir v1.1.0 for developmental trajectory reconstruction in trophoblasts, endothelial cells, and DSCs; fate probability estimation to terminal states.
  • Spatially resolved cell–cell communication: CellChat v2 with CellChatDB ligand–receptor database; spatial coordinate constraints (max 200 µm Euclidean distance); permutation significance testing (n = 1,000 permutations).
  • Machine learning — EVT invasiveness prediction (iScore): LASSO (L1-regularized) sparse regression on 3,192 EVT-enriched genes from spatial pseudobulk profiles (pseudobulk depth windows of 10 cells); 1:1 train/test split; 54 genes selected with non-zero coefficients; performance by Spearman correlation; validated in 2 independent GEO cohorts.
  • GWAS integration (SCAVENGE): per-cell trait relevance scores by mapping GWAS signal onto snATAC-seq open-chromatin peak matrix; MACS2 v2.2.7 peak calling; Fisher's exact test with Benjamini–Hochberg correction for cell-type enrichment; negative controls in fetal and adult brain datasets.

① Transfer the paper's spatial tissue-community topology to a graph-based spatial anatomy framework

Extending this paperThe paper identifies six recurrent spatial communities (D1, D2, MFI junction, maternal artery, floating villi, villous core vessel) via cell co-occurrence analysis on the 0.5-µm Stereo-seq grid. However, the topology of how these communities connect — which niches are characteristically adjacent and how that wiring changes across gestation or in disease — is never formalized into a transferable structural model. A direct extension: represent each tissue section as a spatial neighbourhood graph where nodes are segmented cells and edges encode proximity within a defined radius, then apply topological graph analysis (Laplacian spectra, persistent homology of niche boundaries) to define structural invariants of the MFI that hold across donors and gestational ages. Such invariants would identify which spatial configurations predict disease-relevant transitions — for example, whether the loss of DSC4 proximity to anchoring villi precedes EVT invasion failure.

Added value to my researchTissue spatial communities are biological analogues of my urban network community detection. The same Louvain clustering and spatial entropy methods I apply to job-housing networks or regional city collaboration graphs could be adapted here — treating cells as nodes and proximity as edges. This would let me extend GeoAI community-detection algorithms bidirectionally between urban and biomedical spatial data, demonstrating their generality across scale, and would open a new application domain (spatial pathology) for methods I already develop and publish on.

② Upgrade the paper's LASSO iScore to a spatially-contextualized, explainable ML predictor

Extending this paperThe paper's sparse LASSO model predicts EVT invasiveness (iScore) from transcriptomics of 54 genes selected from 3,192 candidates, using decidual depth as the supervised signal. While validated in independent datasets, the model treats each cell's transcriptome as independent and ignores microenvironmental context explicitly. A direct extension: embed the LASSO-selected gene scores into a spatially-contextualized model that also uses the local cell-type composition in a neighbourhood window (directly available from the Stereo-seq spatial coordinates) as additional features, then apply SHAP attribution to decompose which spatial microenvironmental factors — proximity to DSC4, local endothelial state, distance from vessel wall — amplify or suppress the transcriptional invasive programme independently of cell-autonomous genes.

Added value to my researchThis is the precise biological equivalent of my explainable GeoAI for spatial prediction: SHAP attribution of spatially heterogeneous features to an outcome (here, invasiveness; in my work, health access disparities or disease rates). My experience with MGWR, geographically weighted regression, and spatially-varying coefficient models directly translates to interpreting spatially heterogeneous gene-expression effects on invasion — treating the tissue as a continuous spatial field with locally varying coefficients, exactly as I model urban accessibility.

③ Apply deep multi-modal fusion to learn a joint embedding of spatial transcriptomics and protein imaging

Extending this paperThe paper validates transcriptomic cell states with CODEX protein imaging, but the two modalities are processed separately on different (though related) tissue sections. A natural extension: apply a deep learning framework that learns a joint low-dimensional embedding of matched spatial transcriptomics and protein images — training on paired Stereo-seq and CODEX data from the basal plate sections in this study — to predict protein abundance maps directly from whole-slide transcriptomics. This would make the expensive CODEX assay redundant for new samples, enable imputation of protein states where only transcriptomics is available, and reveal where transcript–protein decoupling occurs spatially (for example, PDE3A mRNA versus protein in transitioning endothelial cells).

Added value to my researchThis multi-modal fusion challenge is structurally identical to my work on fusing heterogeneous geospatial data streams (mobile phone, social media, remote sensing, POIs) for urban sensing and health outcome modelling. The same encoder–decoder and cross-attention architectures I study for predicting one geospatial modality from another (e.g., land use from GPS trajectories) apply directly here. Engaging with this biological fusion problem would sharpen the methodological toolkit and provide a new cross-domain publication angle for multi-source data fusion research.

④ Pre-train a MFI-specific foundation model to enable zero-shot annotation and perturbation prediction

Extending this paperThe paper produces a manually curated 19-cell-type atlas requiring iterative annotation across 191,735 nuclei in RNA and ATAC modalities — a labour-intensive, brittle procedure when applied to new disease samples. A direct extension: use the full atlas as pre-training corpus for a cell foundation model that learns a universal embedding of MFI transcriptomes, enabling (1) zero-shot annotation of new pregnancy samples including pathological ones (preeclampsia biopsies), (2) in silico perturbation prediction (what happens transcriptomically if DSC4 is ablated?), and (3) cross-tissue transfer (endometrium, decidua from other species). The foundation model of transcription across human cell types in our corpus provides the methodological backbone.

Added value to my researchFoundation models for biological atlases are methodologically parallel to geospatial foundation models that I work on fine-tuning for urban health sensing. The pre-train-then-fine-tune paradigm, handling multi-scale and multi-modal inputs, and transfer to label-scarce domains are challenges identical in both settings. Engaging with cell foundation models would sharpen my understanding of fine-tuning strategies for domain-specific geospatial foundation models and open a collaborative bridge between GeoAI and computational biology — both fields converging on foundation model adaptation.

⑤ Extend the GWAS-to-cell risk mapping to a spatial tissue risk-geography framework

Extending this paperThe paper's SCAVENGE pipeline maps population-level GWAS signals onto per-cell open-chromatin profiles to generate cell-type-resolved genetic risk scores — a novel bridge from epidemiology to cell biology. The current output is a per-cell score collapsed by cell type; it does not produce a spatial map of where in the tissue high-risk cells cluster. A direct extension: integrate the SCAVENGE per-cell risk scores with the Stereo-seq spatial coordinates to generate continuous spatial risk-density maps of the decidual tissue for each pregnancy complication — showing not only which cell types but which anatomical zones (superficial decidua near anchoring villi vs. perivascular regions) concentrate genetic vulnerability, and testing whether disease-relevant spatial zones can be predicted from normal-pregnancy atlases.

Just as I use 2SFCA/R2SFCA to delineate service catchments that concentrate accessibility disparities, SCAVENGE delineates tissue catchments that concentrate genetic disease risk.
