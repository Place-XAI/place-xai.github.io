Long-term effects of redlining on climate risk exposure

Arianna Salazar-Miranda · Claire Conzelmann · Toàn Phan · Jeremy Hoffman Published 2024-05-17 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00076-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Neighborhoods that the Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC) graded as less desirable for investment in the 1930s–1940s — the practice known as "redlining" — face disproportionately higher current and projected exposure to flooding and extreme heat today. Using a boundary-discontinuity design across 202 US cities, the study shows this gap is driven partly by diminished environmental capital in once-redlined areas: less tree canopy, lower ground-surface permeability, and lower building-foundation height. Historical policy still shapes who is most vulnerable to climate hazards nearly a century later.

Why It Matters

  • Connects historical injustice to present-day climate vulnerability with a credible causal design. By comparing parcels straddling HOLC grade boundaries, the work moves beyond correlation toward a quasi-experimental estimate of redlining's persistent footprint on climate risk.
  • Identifies the physical mechanisms, not just the disparity. It pinpoints tree canopy, surface permeability, and foundation height as the environmental-capital channels through which a 1930s map still translates into 2020s heat and flood exposure.
  • Speaks to both current and projected risk. Using forward-looking flood projections, it shows the inequity is not only historical baggage but will widen under climate change absent intervention.
  • Hands planners concrete, place-based levers. The mechanism set — canopy, permeability, elevation — maps directly onto greening, stormwater, and retrofit policies that can target the most-affected neighborhoods.

Core Methods

  • Boundary-discontinuity (regression-discontinuity-style) design: compares parcels immediately on either side of HOLC grade boundaries, so that areas are similar in everything except their historical grade — isolating the causal effect of redlining from confounders.
  • Multi-city pooling: the design is applied uniformly across 202 US cities, yielding a generalizable rather than city-specific estimate.
  • Climate-risk outcome modeling: estimates effects of historical grade on current and projected flood risk and on extreme-heat exposure.
  • Mechanism decomposition: tests how much of the exposure gap is explained by environmental capital — tree canopy, surface permeability, and foundation height — linking outcomes back to physical neighborhood attributes.
  • Robustness: Supplementary material reports additional discussion and Tables 1–11 supporting the boundary-design assumptions and sensitivity checks.

① Disentangle the legacy effect from contemporary inequality

Extending this paperThe boundary design cleanly identifies that once-redlined areas are hotter and more flood-prone today, but a sharp open question — raised directly by a companion corpus study — is how much of that gap is the historical HOLC grade versus present-day income and demographic sorting. Extending the paper: add a mediation/decomposition layer at the same boundaries that holds current income constant, quantifying the share of climate exposure attributable to the 1930s policy net of contemporary inequality, city by city.

Added value to my researchThis is a template for my explainable spatial models of health access: separate durable structural causes (zoning, historical disinvestment) from current socio-economic confounders, so MGWR/SHAP attributions reflect mechanism rather than collinear proxies.

② Turn the static legacy map into a person-centered exposure model

Extending this paperThe paper measures exposure at the place (parcel) level, but residents move through the day, and the most-exposed people are not always those who sleep in the most-exposed parcels. Extending it: fuse the HOLC-boundary outcomes with mobility/activity trajectories to estimate realized daily heat-and-flood exposure of people whose homes are in once-redlined areas — converting a place-based legacy into a population-based risk metric.

Added value to my researchThis directly feeds my spatiotemporal KDE + GPS-trajectory work: a redlining-conditioned, time-resolved exposure surface becomes a covariate in telehealth/primary-care access models for heat-vulnerable populations.
