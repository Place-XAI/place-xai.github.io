Global disparities in urban green space use during the COVID-19 pandemic from a systematic review

Fritz Kleinschroth · Sini Savilaakso · Ingo Kowarik · Paloma Julia Martinez · Yuyang Chang · Kristen Jakstis · Jessica Schneider · Leonie K. Fischer Published 2024-01-18 DOI 10.1038/s44284-023-00020-6 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Urban green space (UGS) use during COVID-19 changed in opposite directions across the world, not uniformly. Synthesizing 177 articles covering 237 cities in 60 countries, the review finds that use generally decreased where lockdown policies were stricter and GDP per capita was lower — including most of the few studied parts of the Global South — while it increased where wealth was higher. By green-space type, all studies of private gardens and most studies of forests/natural areas reported increases, whereas 77% of public-park studies reported decreased use. The pandemic thus exacerbated pre-existing global disparities in green-space access, signaling the need for green infrastructure that extends well beyond public parks.

★

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a contested literature. Prior local studies disagreed on whether UGS use rose or fell during lockdowns; this review shows the disagreement is real geographic divergence, not noise — and pins it to policy stringency, economic wealth and green-space type.
  • Quantifies an equity gap, globally. By linking decreased use to lower GDP per capita and the Global South, it gives an evidence base for the claim that crises widen environmental inequity rather than distribute hardship evenly.
  • Re-prioritizes the green-infrastructure portfolio. The type breakdown (gardens and forests up, parks down) argues that resilient cities need diverse, distributed, near-home greenery — not just flagship public parks.
  • Exposes a research blind spot. Evidence is overwhelmingly concentrated in the Global North (Africa represented by a single survey study), making the unequal evidence base itself a finding and a call to action.

Core Methods

  • Pre-registered systematic review: CEE-compliant, PRISMA-reported, with an a priori protocol externally peer-reviewed; PECO question framing (population, exposure, comparators, outcomes).
  • Multilingual, multi-source search: Boolean string over settlement × green-space × COVID × use terms, refined via litsearchr text-mining and validated against 20 known key papers; gray literature included.
  • Dual screening & critical appraisal: two independent reviewers (3% / 5% disagreement at title-abstract / full-text), with a 0–5 validity score capturing sampling, statistics, pre-COVID controls and confounder handling (e.g. weather).
  • Structured data extraction: per city and per UGS type — location, reference period, UGS category (7 classes), method (6 classes), sample size, study period and percent change; graph values digitized with graphreader.com.
  • Weighted statistical analysis: validity-weighted logistic regression (GLM, binomial) of increase vs. decrease against stringency and GDP; sensitivity runs on high-validity subset; weighted χ² tests for UGS-type differences, visualized as mosaic plots; means with 95% CIs.
  • Qualitative coding: equity issues (income, ethnicity, gender, migration, age) and avoidance/adaptation/compensation behaviors coded from study quotes (basis for Fig. 5).

Expanded Ideas — this paper's extensibility & added value to my research

Each idea stays anchored to this paper: first how the review itself could be extended (drawing on our corpus), then the added value for my research lines (migis.net — GeoAI, health geography & 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility, explainable & physics-informed spatial learning, multi-source data fusion).

① Replace narrative "use change" with a measured supply-side accessibility layer

Extending this paperThe review reports self-reported / mobility-proxy use change per city but never measures the underlying supply of UGS that conditions those changes. A direct extension: for the 237 studied cities, compute a harmonized green-space accessibility surface (network distance to nearest park / garden / forest) so each reported increase or decrease can be re-read against actual supply — converting a qualitative synthesis into a supply-controlled meta-analysis that isolates demand shifts from provision gaps.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct 2SFCA/R2SFCA application: green-space catchments become the "supply," population the "demand," and pandemic-era use the "realized utilization" — giving me a real-world stress test of whether R2SFCA service-area delineation predicts where use collapsed.

Cross-ref (corpus): Unequal access to social, environmental and health amenities in US urban parks — 10.1038/s44284-024-00153-2

② Fix the Global-South evidence gap with remote sensing + mobility data fusion

Extending this paperThe review's most honest limitation is its Global-North bias — Africa appears in a single online survey, Latin America largely via one Google-Mobility study. Rather than wait for new primary surveys, extend the paper by fusing satellite greenness (NDVI / canopy), gridded population and anonymized mobility to estimate UGS-use change directly for under-studied cities, attaching uncertainty so the global map fills in honestly instead of staying blank.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely my multi-source data fusion agenda (remote sensing + mobile-phone + social media), and a chance to fine-tune a geospatial foundation model to transfer green-space-use inference into data-sparse regions.

Cross-ref (corpus): Potential drivers of urban green space availability in Latin American cities — 10.1038/s44284-024-00162-1

③ Explain the divergence with interpretable spatial ML instead of binary logistic regression

Extending this paperThe paper's mechanism (stringency + GDP) rests on a weighted binary logistic regression that collapses rich heterogeneity and, in high-validity subsets, loses significance. Extending it: refit increase/decrease with an explainable ML ensemble (gradient boosting + SHAP) or MGWR over the city panel, letting interaction effects and spatial non-stationarity in the stringency–GDP–UGS relationship surface — turning "stricter+poorer → decrease" into a spatially varying, attributable map.

Added value to my researchA clean testbed for my explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction and MGWR work: a globally distributed outcome with known confounders, ideal for demonstrating that local coefficients beat one global odds ratio.

Cross-ref (corpus): A generalized relationship between dose of greenness and mental health response — 10.1038/s44284-025-00285-z

④ Couple use-change to health outcomes via dose-response and wearable evidence

Extending this paperThe review documents changed use but stops short of the health consequence it repeatedly invokes. Extending it: link the city-level use-change signal to physical-activity and mental-health dose-response curves, estimating the well-being deficit incurred where park use fell hardest — and crucially where falls coincided with vulnerable, low-GDP populations.

Added value to my researchThis bridges to my spatial accessibility of mental health / primary care line: green-space access becomes a modifiable upstream determinant in the same accessibility framework I use for telehealth and clinic catchments, enabling a combined health-amenity accessibility index.

Cross-ref (corpus): Wearable data link urban green space to physical activity — 10.1038/s44360-025-00011-y

⑤ Turn the static review into a longitudinal equity-monitoring system

Extending this paperThe authors flag that they cannot say whether elevated use persists — their evidence stops mid-pandemic. Extending it: stand up a continuously updated equity dashboard that tracks green-space-exposure inequality over time using gridded greenness + population, so "the pandemic widened the gap" becomes a testable, ongoing trajectory rather than a 2020 snapshot — directly answering the review's open longitudinal question.

Added value to my researchAligns with my open-science / FAIR and spatiotemporal analysis work: a reproducible, multi-source pipeline (with spatiotemporal KDE / spatial-entropy equity metrics) for monitoring the coupling of space–human systems for urban-health policy.
