Multidecadal legacy of uneven urbanization on divergent prospects for bird biodiversity

Xinghao Lu · Yuwei Guo · Jiake Shen · Jiaxiang Liu · Gaoyue Ling · Xinyu Tian · Yifei Jia · Yue Che · Hui Wang · Yuncai Wang Published 2026-02-20 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00381-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Today's urban bird communities are not yet in equilibrium with today's cities — they still carry a 31-year legacy of past urbanization. By combining equilibrium and non-equilibrium models, the study shows that China's uneven urbanization has produced widespread extinction debts (losses still to come) and colonization credits (gains still to arrive) across the taxonomic, functional and phylogenetic dimensions of bird assemblages. Different urban drivers act with different lags — historical vegetation cover and anthropogenic activity leave the longest imprint — and each species' lag is tied to its urban tolerance. The upshot: conservation must be forward-looking, because the bird communities we see now reflect the city we built decades ago.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reveals a hidden time bomb (and a hidden windfall). Snapshot biodiversity maps understate future losses where extinction debt is unpaid, and understate future gains where colonization credit is unrealized — so today's "healthy" or "degraded" urban ecosystems may be mislabeled.
  • Quantifies lag, not just impact. Rather than asking whether urbanization harms birds, the work measures how long different drivers take to play out, turning a vague "legacy effect" into datable response times planners can act on.
  • Connects driver to trait. Linking each species' lag to its urban tolerance explains which birds will be winners or losers, enabling targeted, species-specific conservation rather than blanket green-space rules.
  • Reframes urban governance as a long-horizon problem. Because the largest imprints come from historical vegetation cover and anthropogenic activity, the paper argues biodiversity outcomes are decided by decisions made decades earlier — a direct call for forward-looking, biodiversity-friendly urban planning.

Core Methods

  • Equilibrium vs. non-equilibrium coupling: the central innovation — comparing an equilibrium model (assuming communities track current conditions) against a non-equilibrium species distribution model (allowing delayed responses) to detect and size extinction debts and colonization credits.
  • Three-axis biodiversity accounting: debts/credits are quantified separately for taxonomic, functional and phylogenetic diversity, so structural, ecological-role and evolutionary losses are distinguished.
  • Lag-duration estimation: for each urban driver (landscape structure, climate, socioeconomic), the time lag at which historical conditions best explain current bird distributions is identified — revealing that vegetation and anthropogenic activity have the longest lags.
  • Trait modulation analysis: species-specific lagged responses are regressed against life-history traits and urban tolerance to explain divergent prospects across species.
  • Urbanization-pattern stratification: debts and credits are contrasted across the country's three urbanization patterns, linking the form of urban growth to its ecological legacy.
  • Multi-source spatiotemporal fusion: remote-sensing (NDVI, land cover, nighttime light), climate reanalysis, gridded population and trait/phylogenetic databases are integrated over 31 years.
