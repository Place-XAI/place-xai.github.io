Inequities in spatial access to childbirth care in the Grand Conakry conurbation, Guinea

Fassou Mathias Grovogui · Nafissatou Dioubate · Hawa Manet · Pépé Kpogomou · Tamba Mina Millimouno · Aline Semaan · Lenka Beňová · Alexandre Delamou · Peter M. Macharia Published 2025-04-23 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00220-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

In Grand Conakry (a conurbation of ~2.6 million), short geographic distance hides long travel times: under average traffic nearly all women of childbearing age (WoCBA) reach any childbirth facility within 30 minutes, but coverage falls to 82% for public hospitals — and under heavy congestion travel time to a public hospital can exceed 2 hours in peri-urban communes. Access is consistently pro-rich (richest quintile ~22 min vs poorest ~52 min to the nearest public facility under minimum speed), with the peri-urban Dubréka and Coyah districts forming near "medical deserts." The fix is spatial: build roads, add and strengthen public hospitals, and improve transport on the urban periphery.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reframes urban access as a traffic problem, not a distance problem. Guinea has one of the world's highest maternal mortality ratios (553 / 100,000 livebirths). The study shows that in a dense capital, congestion — not raw kilometers — is what severs timely access to lifesaving emergency obstetric care.
  • Uses real, locally geotraced speeds instead of generic assumptions. Earlier continental models assumed major-road speeds up to 100 km/h; here measured speeds were 14–28 km/h, meaning prior studies likely underestimated travel time across African cities.
  • Couples accessibility to wealth. By linking modeled travel time to Meta's Relative Wealth Index, it quantifies pro-rich inequity and surfaces a paradox: in older central communes the poor are sometimes closer to public hospitals than the rich who moved to the periphery.
  • Directly actionable for planners. Commune-level coverage tables tell the Ministry of Health exactly where to add facilities, upgrade roads, and improve public transport (Kagbélen, Sanoyah, Manéah).

Core Methods

  • Least-cost path / cost-distance modeling: hybrid walk-then-motorized travel computed in AccessMod v5.8.0 (WHO) on a merged 10 m surface (roads + land cover + barriers), Tobler-adjusted walking speeds via DEM slope, anisotropic "knight's move" 16-neighbor routing.
  • Five speed scenarios × four destinations = 20 models: average, minimum, maximum, 25th and 75th percentile speeds, capturing best/worst traffic conditions and uncertainty bounds.
  • Geographic coverage metrics: percentage of WoCBA and pregnant women within 15, 30 and 60 minutes of the nearest facility, computed for Grand Conakry and each of the 14 communes.
  • Equity analysis: travel-time raster linked to Meta RWI, summarized by wealth quintile (Q1 poorest–Q5 richest) via equiplots in R (ggplot2) for the conurbation and three districts.
  • Realistic-speed calibration: GPS trajectories aggregated by OSM road type to replace generic continental speed assumptions — the methodological core of the contribution.

① From nearest-facility proximity to competition-aware R2SFCA

Extending this paperThe paper measures travel time to the nearest facility but explicitly flags that women may bypass it and that hospitals differ in capacity. Replacing the nearest-facility metric with an R2SFCA that divides 86 supply points by the surrounding WoCBA/pregnancy demand would expose crowding and competition the current static map misses — turning "reachable" into "actually obtainable" care.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my R2SFCA service-area delineation: the geotraced 14–28 km/h speeds and 240 m RWI grid give a real LMIC testbed to calibrate distance-decay against measured congestion rather than assumed Gaussian kernels.

② Model facility bypass behavior with mobile-phone trajectories

Extending this paperA stated limitation is that the model assumes everyone uses the nearest facility and cannot capture waiting/transport time. Fusing mobile-phone or GPS mobility data would let the authors observe realized hospital-seeking flows, validate the least-cost routes, and detect the bypass of low-quality nearby facilities for distant trusted hospitals.

③ Explain the pro-rich inequity with spatially explicit ML

Extending this paperThe paper documents pro-rich inequity and a central-poor paradox but attributes them narratively (historical siting, peripheral migration). An MGWR or explainable-ML attribution over commune covariates (facility density, road class, slope, RWI, urbanization age) would quantify which spatial drivers produce the gap and where they invert — making the policy levers measurable.

Casting this as a facility-location optimization over the same cost surface — maximizing WoCBA within 30 min subject to budget, with an equity constraint across RWI quintiles — would output the specific number and placement of new public hospitals, especially in Kagbélen, Sanoyah and Manéah.

⑤ Densify incomplete peri-urban road graphs with a physics-informed graph model

Extending this paperA key uncertainty is that OSM road coverage is sparse in peri-urban communes, possibly overestimating travel times there. Learning a physics-informed graph model that infers missing road segments and propagates measured speeds from observed to unmapped areas (constrained by land cover, slope, settlement density) would shrink that bias and sharpen the medical-desert verdict.
