Mapping urban gullies in the Democratic Republic of the Congo

Guy Ilombe Mawe · Eric Lutete Landu · Elise Dujardin · Fils Makanzu Imwangana · Charles Bielders · Aurélia Hubert · Caroline Michellier · Charles Nzolang · Jean Poesen · Olivier Dewitte · Matthias Vanmaercke Published 2025-08-27 DOI 10.1038/s41586-025-09371-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Systematic satellite mapping across 26 Congolese cities reveals 2,922 urban gullies that have displaced approximately 118,600 people between 2004 and 2023. Displacement rates have accelerated sharply — from ~4,650 to ~12,200 persons per year after 2020 — while the population living inside potential expansion zones doubled from 1.6 to 3.2 million between 2010 and 2023. Urban sprawl, road construction and climate-intensified rainfall are driving a largely overlooked geo-hydrological hazard that requires urgent planning and mitigation strategies.

★

Why It Matters

  • First country-scale quantification of a neglected hazard. Urban gullies have been documented only in local case studies; this study establishes order-of-magnitude displacement figures (118,600 people) comparable to major natural hazards in Africa.
  • Displacing more people than landslides in the DRC. At ~50 persons per 100,000 urban inhabitants per year, gully-induced displacement substantially exceeds landslide fatality rates (~6 per 100,000), yet gullies receive far less policy attention.
  • Compound and growing risk. Projected increases in rainfall intensity (+10–15%) under climate change, combined with tripling of the African urban population by 2050, could double gully expansion rates — turning a current emergency into a future crisis.
  • Roadmap for prevention. Because 98% of gullies connect to road networks and are triggered by human landscape modification, the paper shows prevention via adapted infrastructure and spatial planning is achievable in principle — providing a science basis for policy.
  • Open, reproducible baseline. The full gully inventory and susceptibility-model code are publicly archived (doi:10.48804/HTEZR0), enabling monitoring, updating, and cross-country comparisons.

Core Methods

  • Systematic satellite-image mapping: manual digitization of gully polygons from <1 m Google Earth imagery for all DRC cities (≥80,000 inhabitants or officially designated); gully thalweg ≥30 m threshold enforced. Field validation of 434 gullies in Kinshasa, Kikwit and Bukavu confirmed classification accuracy.
  • Historical baseline via 1950s aerial photographs: cross-checked with pre-urban RMCA aerial photographs to distinguish urban-induced gullies from natural predecessors; 46 gullies (1.6%) pre-existed urbanization.
  • Gully expansion tracking: each gully digitized 1–5 times on different image dates (2002–2023); expansion events (≥10 m² change) subdivided into gully head retreat (GH), sidewall widening (SW) and new gully formation (New). A total of 8,161 expansion events quantified.
  • Logistic regression susceptibility model: bivariate Mann–Whitney U-tests followed by backward stepwise logistic regression on five retained predictors (slope, soil type, tree cover, built-up area, road density) at 1 km resolution; 10-fold 70:30 cross-validation with AUC performance metric.
  • Population displacement estimation: JRC GHS-POP data at ~100 m combined with digitized expansion polygons; population densities linearly interpolated between 5-year GHS epochs; uncertainty range of ±40% estimated by comparison against WorldPop and local census data.
  • Exposure zone delineation: three nested buffer zones per gully — 100 m proximity buffer, potential expansion zone (95th-percentile width + 10-year head-retreat projection, stratified by sandy vs. non-sandy substrate), and expected expansion zone (mean width + mean head-retreat projection).
  • Uncertainty assessment: cross-validation against WorldPop and Bukavu census (2018) showed JRC GHS estimates to be 22–39% lower than direct census, suggesting reported figures are conservative underestimates; overall ±40% error assumed.

① From logistic susceptibility to an explainable machine-learning hazard model

Extending this paperThe paper's susceptibility model is a five-predictor logistic regression — interpretable but limited in capturing non-linear interactions between road density, slope, soil type and built-up fraction. A direct extension: replace the logistic baseline with an XGBoost or random-forest ensemble with SHAP explanations, trained on the same 754 gully vs. 1,467 non-gully cells. SHAP force plots would reveal city-specific interaction effects (for example, whether steep roads in Kinshasa differ from sandy slopes in Kikwit), and a spatial-leave-one-city-out cross-validation would test whether the model transfers to unseen DRC cities — or to neighbouring countries such as Cameroon or Angola where data are emerging. The corpus paper on socioeconomic predictors of flood-induced displacement provides a directly analogous pipeline using explainable ML on gridded hazard and vulnerability data.

Added value to my researchThis is my core explainable-ML-for-spatial-prediction theme applied to a new domain. The SHAP-based decomposition of gully susceptibility into road, slope, and land-cover contributions directly parallels my work on healthcare access predictors using MGWR/ML hybrids — the method is transferable with minimal adaptation, and a geo-hazard application would diversify the portfolio of domains where explainable GeoAI has demonstrated value.

② Coupling urban-growth projections with gully susceptibility to forecast future exposure

Extending this paperThe paper documents that 52–54% of rising exposure between 2010 and 2023 was attributable to population growth into existing hazard zones, and projects exposure may triple by 2050 given African urban growth rates. Yet it does not simulate where new urban land will form. Extending the paper: couple its logistic susceptibility map with urban-growth projections from the African urban-evolution framework in the corpus — which traces Zipf's law and city-rank dynamics across the continent 1950–2020 — to produce spatially explicit 2040–2060 exposure scenarios under different planning intervention levels. The susceptibility model's built-up area term creates a feedback: each projected new built-up cell can itself shift adjacent susceptibility values.

Added value to my researchSpatiotemporal simulation of urban hazard exposure ties directly into my urban agglomeration and regional collaboration research. Forecasting exposure as a function of where cities grow connects my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation methods to scenario-planning, and the coupled susceptibility–growth model could be generalized to any urban environmental hazard — air quality, heat islands, or flood risk — expanding the scope of my urban analytics work.

③ Incorporating socioeconomic vulnerability layers into the gully exposure index

Extending this paperThe paper quantifies physical exposure (number of people within buffer zones) but does not weight by vulnerability. Many expansion zones contain informal settlements where residents have no capacity to relocate or rebuild. A direct extension: overlay the paper's three nested hazard zones with socioeconomic vulnerability proxies — income proxies from night-light data, building quality from open building footprints, or green infrastructure deficits as mapped for African informal settlements in the corpus — and compute a compound risk index. This would shift the paper's contribution from hazard mapping to disaster-risk reduction intelligence and align with Sendai Framework targets.

Added value to my researchMulti-source data fusion combining remote sensing (gully hazard), administrative data (population grids) and built-environment proxies (night lights, building footprints) is exactly my data science and reproducibility strength. Constructing a compound index for a previously unmeasured hazard type is a methodologically clean contribution that would complement my existing spatial-accessibility and health-geography work — both domains grapple with the challenge of combining physical proximity measures with socioeconomic vulnerability.

④ Applying the paper's gully framework to slum flood-exposure research across the Global South

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that 98% of DRC gullies connect to road networks, making them a drainage-failure hazard directly linked to urban infrastructure deficits. In other Global South cities, the same infrastructure deficit — inadequate road drainage and unplanned paving — may be a primary driver of compound gully-flood events. Extending the paper: systematically test whether areas identified as disproportionately flood-exposed slum neighbourhoods (as mapped across the Global South in the corpus flood-exposure study) show elevated gully-susceptibility scores when the DRC model is applied. This would test cross-country model transferability and potentially establish gullies as an untracked component of compound geo-hydrological hazard in slum areas globally.

Added value to my researchCross-hazard compound analysis using multi-source data fusion — satellite imagery plus gridded population plus flood-extent data — is a natural extension of my remote sensing and spatiotemporal analysis methods. The compound gully-flood framing would create a novel research thread bridging geo-hydrological hazard mapping with the health and housing access disparities I study in urban systems, since residents displaced by gullies lose their housing and access to primary care in the same event.

⑤ Evaluating whether green–blue infrastructure interventions can reduce gully risk without displacement

Extending this paperThe paper recommends integrated approaches combining structural measures, vegetation and upstream runoff capture to stabilize gullies. However, it notes that gully stabilization can cost more than 1 million USD per gully, and that vegetation establishment is frequently disrupted by new expansion events. Green–blue adaptation in African cities — parks, wetland restoration, integrated drainage — could in principle address root causes (excess runoff) while co-delivering ecosystem services. But the corpus paper on Africa's gentrification paradox shows such interventions also risk accelerating displacement of the very populations they aim to protect. Extending this paper: combine its gully-susceptibility model with proposed green–blue intervention zones across Kinshasa to simulate whether a managed drainage intervention could reduce susceptibility without generating gentrification pressure.

Added value to my researchThe tension between hazard mitigation and displacement risk is a precise analogue of my efficiency-versus-equity optimization work on EV charging and healthcare facility location. Casting gully mitigation as an optimization problem — minimize exposure subject to gentrification cost constraints — would let me apply my multi-objective facility-location methods to an environmental-justice context, enriching both domains. The green–blue infrastructure angle also connects to my healthy-cities and public health research, since runoff reduction and vegetation restoration carry co-benefits for air quality and urban heat.
