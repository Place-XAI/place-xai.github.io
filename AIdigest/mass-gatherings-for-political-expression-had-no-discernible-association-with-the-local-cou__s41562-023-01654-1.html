Mass gatherings for political expression had no discernible association with the local course of the COVID-19 pandemic in the USA in 2020 and 2021

Eric Feltham · Laura Forastiere · Marcus Alexander · Nicholas A. Christakis Published 2023-07-31 DOI 10.1038/s41562-023-01654-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Across five types of political mass gathering — US primary elections, the Georgia Senate run-off, gubernatorial elections in New Jersey and Virginia, Trump campaign rallies, and Black Lives Matter protests — no statistically significant increase in COVID-19 deaths, cases, or effective reproduction number (Rt) was detected in the 40-day period following events. Using a novel non-parametric, generalized difference-in-differences estimator with county-level matching across 3,108 US counties, the average estimated effect was −0.057 deaths per day (95% CI: −0.319, 0.162) for counties that held gatherings compared to matched controls. Political activities, as typically conducted, appear not to have constituted super-spreader events during the first two years of the pandemic — a finding that extends across pre- and post-vaccine periods and multiple dominant variants.

Why It Matters

  • Resolves a high-stakes empirical controversy with rigorous causal machinery. Public health officials, politicians, and media debated whether elections and protests drove COVID-19 surges; this is the most comprehensive causal analysis to date, covering over 700 events across two years and multiple pandemic phases.
  • Methodological advance applicable far beyond COVID. The non-parametric generalized difference-in-differences estimator with rolling matching windows can handle any nonlinear contagion process — a reusable tool for quasi-experimental epidemiology of future outbreaks.
  • Disentangles public health from partisan rhetoric. By including both politically charged events (Trump rallies, BLM protests, elections) and controlling rigorously for mobility and demographic confounders, the study provides an evidence base that is politically neutral, directly relevant to pandemic governance worldwide.
  • Addresses a global policy dilemma. During ongoing outbreaks (COVID-19 and future epidemics), banning elections and protests involves fundamental civil rights trade-offs; this evidence suggests those bans may not have provided the epidemiological benefit often assumed.
  • Open, reproducible, and complete. Data sources are publicly available (JHU, US Census, SafeGraph, ACLED US Crisis Monitor, NYT GitHub). Replication code is released as the COVIDPoliticalEvents.jl Julia package, enabling independent verification and extension.

Core Methods

  • Non-parametric generalized difference-in-differences (DiD): a novel extension of the Callaway & Sant'Anna (2021) framework, adapted for nonlinear epidemic curves and time-varying treatments — avoids parametric assumptions about the epidemic trajectory that would be violated by SARS-CoV-2 dynamics.
  • Covariate-based matching with calipers: each treated county (holding a political event) is matched to up to five control counties on the basis of 30-day pre-treatment histories of cumulative death rate, mobility metrics, population density, and socio-demographic characteristics. Calipers ensure standardized covariate imbalance stays below 0.1 on average.
  • Sliding crossover window: control counties must be treatment-free in a dynamic window around each outcome day (10–40 days post-event), preventing contamination from concurrent gatherings — a key innovation over standard DiD.
  • Average Treatment Effect on the Treated (ATT): estimated daily for each day 10–40 post-event (the epidemiologically motivated window from infection to death, based on 5th–95th percentiles of observed infection-to-death times).
  • Geographic spillover model for Trump rallies: exposure defined at four degrees of county adjacency (direct, 1st-, 2nd-, 3rd-degree neighbors), accounting for attendees travelling from neighboring counties.
  • Multiple outcomes: county-level death rate (primary, more reliable), case rate, effective reproduction number Rt, and mobility at three location types (restaurants, grocers, fitness/recreation).
  • Stratified robustness checks: extensive subgroup analyses by voter turnout, population density, census region, partisan vote share, time since first case, cumulative case/death rate at event date, mask-wearing propensity, and protest size.
  • Bayes factors: reported alongside frequentist p-values to quantify evidence for the null hypothesis, not just failure to reject it.
  • Implementation: data cleaning in R; analysis in Julia using the TSCSMethods.jl and COVIDPoliticalEvents.jl packages (open source, GitHub).

① Embed the paper's county matching into a mobility-network epidemic model to recover the spatial transmission mechanism

Extending this paperThe paper establishes a compelling null — gatherings did not measurably increase county-level deaths — but cannot explain why not. One powerful extension: couple the paper's DiD matching logic with a mobility-network metapopulation model in which SafeGraph visit flows define inter-county transmission edges. Running counterfactual simulations (inject realistic gathering-sized contact pulses into calibrated network parameters) could quantify exactly how large a gathering would need to be, and under what mobility baseline, to produce a detectable county-level effect — turning the null into a threshold estimate rather than an absence.

Added value to my researchThe mobility-network SEIR framework introduced in Kang et al. (2020 Nature) is directly relevant to my spatiotemporal analysis work: integrating SafeGraph POI flows as weighted graph edges is the same data infrastructure I use for point-of-interest accessibility. Calibrating the transmission threshold question gives me a transferable, evidence-grounded mobility-epidemic coupling model applicable to future public-health event analysis.

② Extend the paper's causal estimator to measure fine-grained mobility changes induced by gatherings

Extending this paperFig. 8 shows gatherings did not shift aggregate mobility at restaurants, grocers, and gyms — but the SafeGraph data have much finer resolution: census block group × POI-type × hour-of-day. An extension of the paper: apply the same sliding-window DiD to hyperlocal mobility trajectories around each event venue (within 1 km vs. 5 km) to test whether gatherings induced compensatory avoidance in surrounding neighborhoods — a displacement effect invisible at the county level. Such fine-grain analysis might also explain the null: if attendees reduced other contacts in the days around an event, net exposure could cancel out.

Added value to my researchThis directly advances my work on spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and mobile-phone record fusion: the displacement hypothesis requires a spatially explicit, time-resolved mobility surface — exactly the GeoAI pipeline I develop. Demonstrating that gathering-induced avoidance is hyperlocal would also strengthen the case for fine-grained mobility data over county-level aggregates in health geography studies.

③ Apply the paper's DiD framework to vaccine-uptake events and public health campaigns

Extending this paperThe paper covers pre-vaccine gatherings (elections, rallies, BLM protests) and two post-vaccine elections (GA, NJ/VA) but never examines mass vaccination events themselves. An important extension: apply the same estimator to mass vaccination drives (large community vaccine clinics), asking whether the in-person gathering required to administer vaccines itself produced any measurable local spike — the precise inverse of the gatherings studied. Null results here would be a strong argument for high-density vaccination campaigns; positive results would inform logistics of future rapid-vaccination roll-outs.

Added value to my researchVaccine accessibility is a spatial problem: 2SFCA-style catchment analysis of vaccination sites and their coverage of under-resourced communities is directly in my health geography toolkit. Integrating the causal estimator from this paper with spatial accessibility models would let me jointly estimate where vaccine sites should be placed (optimization) and whether placement decisions have measurable population-health effects (causal evaluation) — a clean efficiency-equity framework.

④ Use the paper's null finding to calibrate explainable ML models for epidemic risk scoring of public events

Extending this paperThe paper provides high-quality ATT estimates and extensive covariate data for 700+ events — an underutilized training signal for predictive models of gathering risk. A direct extension: build an XGBoost or gradient-boosted ensemble that predicts county-level ATT magnitude from event-level features (indoor/outdoor, crowd density, vaccination rate, prevalent variant, masking, baseline Rt). The paper's own robustness strata (turnout, vote share, masks, density, time since first case) define natural feature candidates. SHAP attribution would reveal which conditions push gatherings from safe to risky — converting the null into actionable thresholds for future pandemic event permitting.

Added value to my researchExplainable ML for spatial prediction is a core method in my GeoAI agenda. The paper supplies a rare labeled dataset of quasi-experimental effect sizes at the county level — a ground truth that is much harder to obtain for urban accessibility or health-facility studies. Applying SHAP-based attribution here would generate a validated spatial risk-scoring pipeline transferable to other public-health event assessment problems.

⑤ Cross-validate the political-gathering null with protest-driven behavioral change using spatial cell-phone data and the Fridays for Future corpus

Extending this paperThe paper is restricted to the USA, 2020–2021. An important cross-national replication: apply the same generalized DiD estimator to the Fridays for Future (FFF) climate protests in Germany, where cell-phone based mobility data and precise protest records also exist. Germany had different pandemic timing, non-pharmaceutical interventions, and protest dynamics than the USA. If the null holds there too — across a very different political context and epidemic trajectory — it substantially strengthens the claim that political gatherings as typically conducted are not super-spreader events. Differences in masking norms, outdoor-event prevalence, and pre-existing immunity across waves could also be directly tested.

Added value to my researchMulti-source data fusion across GPS trajectories, social media, and administrative records is central to my research.
