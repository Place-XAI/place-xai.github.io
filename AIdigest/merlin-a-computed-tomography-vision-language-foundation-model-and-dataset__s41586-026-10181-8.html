Core Conclusion

Merlin is a 3D vision–language foundation model for abdominal CT interpretation trained on over 6 million images, 1.8 million diagnosis codes, and 6 million text tokens from 15,331 CT scans — without additional manual annotation. Evaluated on 752 tasks across 6 task types, Merlin outperforms 2D VLMs, CT-specific foundation models, and off-the-shelf radiology models on diagnostic, prognostic, and quality tasks. External validation on 44,098 CT scans across 3 independent clinical sites confirms strong generalization. The authors publicly release trained models, code, and a dataset of 25,494 CT–report pairs, creating an open-science resource for future biomarker discovery and disease risk stratification.

Core Methods

  • 3D image encoder (I3D inflation): 2D ImageNet-pretrained convolutional weights are inflated to 3D using the I3D strategy, enabling volumetric CT processing without training 3D encoders from scratch.
  • Multistage pretraining framework: Stage 1 aligns CT volumes with EHR diagnosis codes (contrastive learning); Stage 2 aligns CT volumes with free-text radiology reports (CLIP-style image–text contrastive learning using Clinical Longformer for text encoding). No additional manual annotation required.
  • Vision–language alignment: OpenCLIP-style contrastive learning extended to 3D; embeddings from the CT encoder and Clinical Longformer text encoder are aligned in joint latent space.
  • Task adaptation: Zero-shot tasks use off-the-shelf cosine similarity (no fine-tuning); adapted tasks (disease prediction, report generation, 3D segmentation) add lightweight heads or decoders trained on downstream data.
  • Report generation: Merlin encoder embeddings are projected and fed into a language model via a projection layer; reports are generated section-by-section with anatomical-section prompts.
  • 3D semantic segmentation: Merlin encoder is extended with a decoder and skip connections within the nnUNet framework; evaluated across 20 abdominal organs in both full-data and 10%-data (low-label) regimes.
  • Scaling laws and ablation: Systematic scaling-law experiments vary pretraining dataset size; ablations study I3D initialization, multi-task vs. staged training, EHR vs. report-only pretraining, and text-splitting strategies.
