Cost-effective case-finding strategies for cognitive impairment with digital twin simulations

Tau Ming Liew · Way Inn Koay Published 2026-03-03 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00076-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Without active case finding, 79.5% of cognitive impairment cases in Singapore remain undiagnosed. A digital twin of the national older adult population (n = 753,905) — built using a deep learning generative model trained on 1,856 real-world individuals — shows that screening adults aged ≥75 years who report cognitive concerns achieves an optimal reach-to-cost balance (undiagnosed rate reduced to 48.3%; SGD$25.3 per new diagnosis). Willingness-to-pay thresholds govern the optimal strategy: highly targeted approaches suit resource-limited settings, while broader strategies (e.g., known hypertension) become preferable as budgets grow. Critically, low screening-test specificity sharply inflates downstream health system costs, a hidden fiscal risk ignored by most current screening guidelines.

Core Methods

  • Conditional Tabular GAN (CTGAN) for digital twin generation: a deep learning generative model trained on the 1,856-person seed cohort to synthesize a national-scale older adult population (753,905) that preserves multivariate clinical and demographic correlations while matching national demographic structures.
  • Case-finding strategy simulation: seventeen distinct case-finding strategies (varying by age threshold, risk factor, and subjective cognitive complaint) are applied to the twin to estimate the number of true positives, false positives, and undiagnosed cases under each strategy.
  • Cost-effectiveness analysis (CEA): costs calculated from two perspectives — the screening program perspective and the health system perspective — including screening test costs and downstream care costs triggered by false positives.
  • Cost-Effectiveness Acceptability Curves (CEACs): probabilistic sensitivity analysis using bootstrap resampling to generate CEACs, which show the probability of each strategy being cost-effective across a range of willingness-to-pay (WTP) thresholds (in SGD$).
  • Expected Net Loss (ENL) Curves: complementary to CEACs, ENL curves quantify the opportunity cost (in SGD$ millions) of choosing a suboptimal strategy, allowing identification of the strategy with minimum expected loss at each WTP threshold.
  • Sensitivity analyses across screening test accuracy: the full CEAC and ENL analyses are repeated for nine combinations of screening sensitivity (80–95%) and specificity (80–95%), mapping how diagnostic accuracy affects cost-effectiveness rankings.
  • Tiered policy framework synthesis: findings are synthesized into a resource-stratified decision framework — highly targeted strategies for low WTP/resource-limited settings, progressively broader strategies as WTP increases.

① Spatialize the digital twin: map cognitive-impairment case-finding burden across Singapore's neighborhoods

Extending this paperThe paper's digital twin produces a national aggregate count but treats the 753,905 individuals as a spatially homogeneous pool. A direct extension: assign each synthetic individual to a residential planning area (using SingStat sub-zone population distributions) and simulate case-finding coverage geographically. This would reveal spatial hot-spots of undiagnosed cognitive impairment — districts where the 79.5% baseline un-diagnosis rate is even higher — and enable clinic-level resource allocation decisions that the current aggregate framework cannot support.

② Build a transferable digital twin framework calibrated from air-pollution and built-environment risk factors

Extending this paperThe paper's CTGAN twin uses individual clinical features (age, hypertension, BMI, frailty, subjective cognitive complaints) as predictors but does not incorporate place-level risk factors such as long-term air pollution exposure, neighborhood walkability, or green space — all established dementia risk modifiers. Extending the generative model to condition on spatial covariates would allow the twin to vary by residential environment, generating city-specific prevalence gradients rather than a single national rate. The same CTGAN framework, re-seeded with a different country's cohort plus its environmental data, could be made globally transferable.

③ Replace the fixed willingness-to-pay threshold with a spatial equity-weighted cost-effectiveness surface

Extending this paperThe paper's CEACs assume a single WTP threshold applied uniformly to the population — implicitly treating every undiagnosed case as equally costly regardless of the patient's socioeconomic or geographic position. Extending it: weight each case by spatial equity (distance to nearest memory clinic, area-level deprivation index) so that the cost-effectiveness denominator is not merely "new diagnoses" but "new diagnoses per equity-adjusted need unit." This would shift the optimal strategy recommendation in under-served zones even at the same aggregate WTP — giving planners a socially differentiated prioritization tool.

④ Extend the digital twin from case-finding to facility-location optimization for memory clinics

Extending this paperThe paper ends when a diagnosis is made — it does not ask where new diagnostic capacity should be physically sited to maximize cost-effective reach. A direct next step: feed the twin's per-strategy detection map into a facility-location optimization that minimizes total travel cost (or maximizes 2SFCA accessibility) for the newly diagnosed population, jointly optimizing the screening strategy and the clinic network expansion. This closes the loop from "who to screen" to "where to screen."
