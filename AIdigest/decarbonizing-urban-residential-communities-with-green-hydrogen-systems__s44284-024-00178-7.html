Decarbonizing urban residential communities with green hydrogen systems

You Wu · Lexuan Zhong Published 2025-01-02 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00178-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

For community green hydrogen systems (rooftop PV + hybrid hydrogen–battery storage) in North American residential communities, the choice of system-deployment pathway dominates cost-effectiveness: under identical community conditions, the cost gap between the best and worst pathway reaches up to 60%. The household centralized program (HCP) is the preferred pathway for most communities, and deliberately enlarging energy-storage demand in a pathway triggers a hydrogen-storage scale effect that can cut system design costs by up to fourfold.

★

Why It Matters

  • Makes an "emerging" technology economically actionable. Hydrogen storage for urban communities was technically promising but financially unclear; this work quantifies which deployment pathway is cost-effective rather than just whether the technology works.
  • A pathway choice, not a hardware choice, is the lever. The same households and climate can yield a 60% cost spread purely from how households are coordinated (distributed vs. centralized), reframing decarbonization as an organizational/planning decision.
  • Identifies a counter-intuitive design rule. Increasing energy-storage demand reduces system cost (up to fourfold) because cheap hydrogen tanks displace batteries via a scale effect — guidance planners can apply immediately.
  • Handles real uncertainty honestly. By layering climate, human-behavior, and community-design uncertainties into a bottom-up model, it gives planners robust, probability-weighted cost estimates instead of single deterministic numbers.

Core Methods

  • Bottom-up energy model (3 phases): link climate–human uncertainties → community archetypes → energy-system optimization, aggregating from household to community level.
  • Multi-source uncertainty quantification: climate uncertainty via synthesized representative weather sets (probability-weighted); human uncertainty via Latin Hypercube Sampling over seven behavioral features; community-design uncertainty via a household-scale × prosumer-scale characteristic matrix.
  • Three deployment pathways: Household Distributed Program (HDP, prosumer-owned, 3-layer trade), Household Centralized Program (HCP, prosumer-grouped, 2-layer trade), Community Centralized Program (CCP, community-owned, 1-layer trade).
  • Green hydrogen system design: rooftop PV + lithium-ion battery (short-term) + liquid-organic-hydrogen-carrier storage (long/cross-seasonal); 12-state intra-household energy-flow logic plus dynamic internal pricing (feed-in-tariff based).
  • Stochastic multi-objective optimization: multi-objective particle swarm optimization sizing battery/hydrogen-tank capacity and fuel-cell/electrolyzer power to minimize annual life-cycle cost (LCC) and grid-interaction level (GIL); Pareto-front + Euclidean-distance compromise selection; results reported as levelized cost of electricity (LCOE).
