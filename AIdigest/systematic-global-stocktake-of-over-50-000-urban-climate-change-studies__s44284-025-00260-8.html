Systematic global stocktake of over 50,000 urban climate change studies

Simon Montfort · Max Callaghan · Felix Creutzig · William F. Lamb · Chenxi Lu · Tim Repke · Ke Ge · Jan Minx Published 2025-06-24 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00260-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using supervised and unsupervised machine learning, the authors mine 53,295 urban climate-change studies from OpenAlex — including 19,733 spatially specific city case studies covering 23% of cities and 64% of the urban population — into a living, searchable evidence database. The literature is growing exponentially (4.5× faster than the whole climate literature) yet is heavily biased toward large, wealthy, high-emitting cities of the Global North, while small, fast-growing cities in Africa and Asia remain severely underrepresented. The latest IPCC AR6 covered only 4.6% of this evidence and amplified the bias toward big cities and practically oriented, interdisciplinary topics, omitting technological solutions (radiative cooling, smart grids, transport tech).

Core Methods

  • Supervised relevance classification: a replicable OpenAlex keyword query is filtered by an XGBoost classifier trained on 1,020 manually annotated abstracts, tuned via nested 5×5 cross-validation with minority upsampling (out-of-sample F1 = 0.952).
  • Spatial attribution (geoparsing): the Mordecai transformer NER geoparser is combined with a GHSL-UCDB city-name keyword match; studies are assigned to cities whose centroids intersect city polygons, with probabilistic disambiguation of duplicate names.
  • Unsupervised topic discovery: structural topic modeling (STM) sweeping 60–240 topics; the 220-topic model is grouped by hand into impacts, adaptation, mitigation and cross-cutting themes aligned with IPCC Working Groups.
  • IPCC coverage analysis: fuzzy matching (DOI + title + author/year) of the database against all AR6 references and the urban chapters, plus first- and second-order (review-mediated) coverage estimates.
  • Mixed-methods bias audit: quantitative comparison of included vs. omitted studies, complemented by a qualitative read of the 100 most-cited post-2013 articles to surface omitted technological solutions (Box 1).

① Geoparse the case-study database into a city-level explainable model of evidence gaps

Extending this paperThe paper describes underresearched cities as small, fast-growing, noncoastal, low-GDP, low-emission, but stops at group means with confidence intervals (Fig. 2c). A direct extension: turn its 19,733-study, GHSL-UCDB-linked table into a supervised target — studies-per-city — and fit an explainable ML / MGWR model on city covariates to map where and why evidence is missing, producing a continuous, spatially varying "evidence-gap surface" rather than three discrete bins.

Added value to my researchThis is the same explainable-spatial-prediction machinery I use for primary-care and mental-health access: swap "service supply per tract" for "studies per city" and SHAP/GWR coefficients become a diagnostic of structural under-service — directly transferable to my health-geography pipeline.

② From geographic gaps to a cross-city transfer-learning "evidence accessibility" metric

Extending this paperThe authors call for data-driven city typologies so that evidence can transfer from well-studied to understudied peers, but never build one. Extending the paper: cluster all GHSL-UCDB cities on size, morphology, growth, governance and climate, then define each city's "borrowable evidence" as a typology-weighted aggregate of its peers' case studies — operationalizing cross-city learning as a measurable quantity.

Added value to my researchThis is structurally my 2SFCA/R2SFCA logic applied to knowledge: cities are "demand," studies are "supply," and typology similarity is the distance-decay kernel — yielding a "knowledge-accessibility" score that reuses my service-area delineation methods in a brand-new domain.

③ Replace keyword + XGBoost screening with a fine-tuned geospatial/LLM screening loop

Extending this paperThe pipeline relies on a 2023 keyword query, an XGBoost classifier and the Mordecai geoparser — strong but English-only, with weaker recall in Africa/South America (F1 0.79) and a static snapshot. Extending it: fine-tune a multilingual LLM screener with a human-in-the-loop active-learning stage and an LLM-based geoparser, so the "living database" truly updates continuously and closes the spatial-attribution gap the authors flag as a limitation.

⑤ Fuse the evidence map with remote-sensing city signals to forecast where gaps will hurt most

Extending this paperThe discussion proposes using global remote-sensing initiatives to track city development (CO₂, growth) and pair it with AI screening, but the present study uses only bibliographic + GHSL covariates. Extending it: fuse the studies-per-city layer with satellite-derived built-up growth, urban-heat and emission products to build a forward-looking "evidence-risk" index — fast-growing, high-exposure cities with thin evidence — to pre-position synthesis effort before lock-in.
