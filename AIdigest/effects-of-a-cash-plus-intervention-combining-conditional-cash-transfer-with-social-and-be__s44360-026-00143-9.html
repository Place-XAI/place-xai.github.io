Effects of a cash-plus intervention combining conditional cash transfer with social and behaviour-change communication on pregnancy weight and birth weight in India

Tanya Marchant · Christian Bottomley · Rupinder Sahota · Akash Porwal · Namita Wadhwa · Srei Chanda · Raghwesh Ranjan · Divya Santhanam · Mahendra Soni · Arindam Das Published 2026-06-02 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00143-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A government-led cash-plus intervention in Rajasthan, India — combining conditional cash transfers with social and behaviour-change communication — was associated with a 7.4 percentage-point absolute reduction in low birth weight (21% relative reduction) and a 3.4 kg increase in mean third-trimester pregnancy weight. The evaluation covered 90,732 newborns and 60,899 mothers over 44 months (January 2021 – August 2024) using an interrupted time-series design, establishing that integrated social-protection programmes, embedded within existing government systems and targeting vulnerable tribal populations, can materially shift maternal and neonatal nutrition outcomes.

★

Why It Matters

  • Directly attacks India's disproportionate low-birth-weight burden. India accounts for 24% of newborns classified as low birth weight globally; this is one of the few rigorous evaluations of a cash-plus programme specifically targeting maternal nutrition in a South Asian setting, where the evidence base was previously sparse.
  • Validates the "cash-plus" model at scale within routine government systems. Rather than a bespoke research trial, the evaluation shows that a state-run programme (Mukhya Mantri Matritva Poshan Yojana + SBCC) can be assessed with linked administrative data, lowering the cost of evidence generation for similar programmes worldwide.
  • Demonstrates dose-response: optimal exposure doubles the benefit. Among women who received at least three counselling visits and two cash payments, the low-birth-weight reduction grew to 12.3 percentage points (36% relative), providing a clear target for implementation quality improvement.
  • Interrupts an intergenerational cycle. Girls born with low birth weight are more likely to give birth to low-birth-weight infants themselves; a 21% relative reduction in prevalence therefore carries compounding long-term public-health significance.
  • Contributes to the sparse evidence on pregnancy weight gain interventions. While cash transfers' effects on child stunting/wasting are studied, their effects on maternal third-trimester weight — a proximate determinant of birth weight — had barely been quantified, especially in South Asia.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Geographic scope
Four districts of Rajasthan, India (Banswara, Dungarpur, Pratapgarh, Udaipur) — a combined population of approximately 7 million people; approximately 14% of Rajasthan's population are from Scheduled Tribe communities. The fifth MMMPY pilot district (Baran) was excluded due to later deployment of digital weighing scales.
Study population
90,732 parity-two newborns (birth weight 500–5,000 g) and 60,899 mothers with third-trimester pregnancy weight records, drawn from 225,391 registered parity-two pregnancies between January 2021 and August 2024 (44 months: 8 pre-intervention + 4 scale-up + 32 full-intervention).
Data stream 1
Government mobile counselling app — frontline community volunteers registered parity-two women and recorded home-visit dates, household socio-demographic data, and third-trimester pregnancy weights. Used for exposure quantification (counselling) and pregnancy weight outcome.
Data stream 2
Government conditional cash-payment portal — eligibility verification and payment dates for the Mukhya Mantri Matritva Poshan Yojana (MMMPY) scheme. Used to quantify cash-transfer exposure (number of payments received during pregnancy).
Data stream 3
Digital hospital weighing scales installed in 123 maternity hospitals in the four study districts — automatically uploaded newborn birth weight measurements to a server. Primary outcome source.
Linkage key
Pregnancy, Child Tracking and Health Services Management System (PCTHSMS) national ID number, assigned by the government to each woman and used consistently across all three digital platforms.
Analysis data
Monthly aggregate summaries (not individual-level records) are publicly available via Figshare at 10.6084/m9.figshare.29967913. Individual-level data are the property of the Government of India and are not available for public release. Code (Stata/SE 18.0, R 4.4.0, package prais) is deposited alongside the data.

January 2021 – August 2024; intervention (MMMPY + SBCC) initiated September 2021.

Core Methods

  • Interrupted time-series (ITS) design: monthly outcome data (low-birth-weight prevalence, mean birth weight, mean third-trimester pregnancy weight) are modelled before and after the September 2021 intervention start, with a 4-month scale-up transition period. The ITS uses no parallel control group; instead it compares the same districts before vs. after intervention.
  • Linear regression on monthly aggregates: the ITS model includes indicator variables for the scale-up period and full-intervention period, a time-trend term for changing intervention impact, and sine/cosine harmonics to account for seasonal variation in birth weight.
  • Prais-Winsten autocorrelation correction: monthly time-series data exhibit serial autocorrelation; the Prais–Winsten method (R package prais) is applied to correct coefficient estimates, with robust standard errors for heteroskedasticity.
  • Direct standardization of low-birth-weight proportions: to control for temporal shifts in population composition (caste, maternal age, toilet type, water source, district), a logistic regression model generates counterfactual monthly LBW proportions standardized to the January 2021 reference population. This eliminates composition bias without requiring individual-level covariates in the ITS model.
  • Optimal exposure (per-protocol) analysis: a secondary analysis restricts to mothers with at least 3 counselling visits and 2 cash payments during pregnancy — removing pre-intervention exposed and post-intervention unexposed births — to quantify the maximum achievable effect. Two outlier months (January–February 2022) are removed via indicator variables.
  • Sensitivity analysis: the scale-up period duration is varied from 0 to 6 months (primary assumption: 4 months) to verify robustness of the main effect estimate; standardized LBW reduction remains stable at 7.3–7.5%.
  • Software: Stata/SE 18.0 and R 4.4.0; analysis code and data available at Figshare (10.6084/m9.figshare.29967913).

① Spatialise the ITS: map sub-district heterogeneity in intervention uptake and birth-weight response

Extending this paperThe current ITS collapses four districts into a single monthly time series, averaging over substantial spatial heterogeneity. The mobile app and hospital datasets contain sub-district location identifiers, enabling a spatially disaggregated ITS where each village cluster or primary health centre forms its own unit. A geographically weighted regression applied to the post-intervention panel could decompose the 7.4-point average effect into a spatially varying surface — identifying which sub-populations drove the gain, where the programme is underperforming, and whether access to the 123 maternity hospitals moderates the birth-weight response. This would transform a before-after average into an actionable equity map for resource reallocation.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly onto my MGWR/GWR toolkit and 2SFCA accessibility framework: the distance from a woman's village to a MMMPY-enrolled hospital enters as the supply-side accessibility denominator, while the pregnancy weight at each home visit is the demand indicator. ② Model the behavioural mechanism: disentangle cash anticipation from SBCC using causal machine learning

Extending this paperThe authors acknowledge uncertainty about the "active ingredient": only one-third of women received a cash payment during pregnancy, yet the full-population effect was still substantial. They hypothesise that anticipated cash and social-diffusion effects from SBCC community engagement drive part of the gain. A direct extension would use causal inference with counterfactual prediction on the linked administrative data: fit separate treatment arms (home-visit-only, payment-only, both) and estimate individual-level treatment effects using doubly robust estimation or targeted maximum likelihood, then use explainable ML (SHAP) to attribute the fraction of LBW reduction to each component conditional on household socio-economic characteristics.

Added value to my researchMy agenda on explainable machine learning for spatial prediction extends naturally here: the SHAP-decomposed mechanism map shows which households in which locations respond to cash vs. counselling, directly informing targeting rules. This mirrors my work on causal feature attribution in health access models and deepens my understanding of non-spatial behavioural determinants that confound spatial accessibility metrics.

③ Extend from a single-state pilot to a national natural experiment using mobile health data

Extending this paperThe first-parity national scheme (PMMVY, 33 districts) ran alongside the second-parity Rajasthan pilot (MMMPY, 5 districts) on the same digital registration system. This creates a natural difference-in-differences structure: PMMVY districts provide a geographically matched comparison, while staggered rollout of MMMPY across districts generates variation in exposure timing. A multi-site ITS with a comparative ITS arm — borrowing the PMMVY districts as imperfect controls — would considerably strengthen the causal identification beyond the current single-arm design and would allow testing whether a behaviour-change component (absent from PMMVY) is necessary for the birth-weight effect.

④ Quantify geographic access to MMMPY enrolment as a mediating variable in programme uptake

Extending this paperThe paper reports that by the end of the full-intervention period, only one-third of women had received a cash payment during pregnancy; by contrast, 69% received two or more home visits. A geospatial mediation analysis could test whether distance to an MMMPY-enrolled hospital (or to a frontline worker's base) mediates the gap between registration and cash receipt, and in turn whether that proximity gap explains residual low-birth-weight prevalence. Adding a network-routing accessibility score (time from each village centroid to the nearest 123 maternity hospital) to the logistic standardization model would reveal whether lower-access clusters are driving the remaining 27.4% LBW burden after the intervention.

⑤ From reactive cash transfers to predictive targeting: an agent-based simulation of SBCC diffusion on the village social network

Extending this paperThe authors invoke social diffusion effects — women adopting healthy behaviours through peer influence and community-level information sharing — as a partial explanation for effects that exceed the proportion receiving formal programme contact. The paper currently cannot quantify this pathway because it lacks network data. An extension would couple the mobile app's village-level registration geography with an agent-based SIR-style diffusion model on a proxy social network (e.g., shared frontline worker or antenatal group membership) to simulate how SBCC "spreads" and estimate the community multiplier on cash receipts. Identifying high-betweenness "seed" communities for counselling rollout would improve programme efficiency.

Coupling diffusion velocity with my spatiotemporal kernel density estimation of counselling coverage density yields a measurable proxy for social-diffusion intensity — a novel bridge between my network community-detection methods and maternal health program evaluation.
