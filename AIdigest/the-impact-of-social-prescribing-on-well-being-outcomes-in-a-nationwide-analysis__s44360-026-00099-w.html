The impact of social prescribing on well-being outcomes in a nationwide analysis

Feifei Bu · Daniel Hayes · Luke Munford · Daisy Fancourt Published 2026-03-24 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00099-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A large-scale UK national analysis of 14,657–19,627 patients across over 300 social prescribing (SP) sites demonstrates consistent and sizeable well-being improvements within 1–6 months of referral: SWEMWBS rose by 3.31 points, life satisfaction by 1.57 points, happiness by 1.59 points, and anxiety fell by 1.45 points. The life-satisfaction gain translates to a return on investment of £9 per £1 invested (conservative estimate, 2019 prices). Crucially, well-being gains were broadly consistent across sociodemographic groups, indicating SP is a widely applicable non-clinical health intervention.

Why It Matters

  • First large-scale national quantification of SP's well-being impact. Prior quantitative studies were small (most n < 150), geographically limited, or restricted to narrow referral types (e.g., arts or green activities). This study covers the full heterogeneity of UK SP delivery across all countries and referral routes.
  • Robust economic valuation. By mapping life-satisfaction gains to the HM Treasury WELLBY framework, the study estimates a £9 return-on-investment per £1 spent — providing the commissioning-ready cost-benefit evidence the NHS requires under the Green Book framework.
  • Equity finding: SP is broadly applicable. Little evidence of differential well-being improvement by deprivation, urbanicity, or referral route challenges the assumption that community-based interventions benefit only certain sociodemographic groups.
  • Demonstrates the value of digital administrative platforms as research infrastructure. Access Elemental's longitudinal SP records enable pre–post outcome analysis at national scale, showing how routine data collection platforms can substitute for expensive trials in health policy evaluation.
  • Bayesian framework with sensitivity robustness. Multiple sensitivity analyses (intervention subsample, COVID exclusion, entropy-balancing weights) all yield consistent results, strengthening causal plausibility despite the uncontrolled design.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Primary data source
Access Elemental — a UK-wide cloud-based digital social prescribing platform used by health and social care professionals. As of data access (25 April 2025), the platform holds records for over 600,000 SP cases from 482,575 patients across 482 sites. Data are third-party restricted and not publicly available; researchers may request the minimal dataset from the corresponding author.
Analytical sample
Restricted to patients with repeated well-being measures at two timepoints within 1–6 months, 2017–2025, UK-wide. Analytical n = 19,627 (SWEMWBS) and 14,657 (ONS4), from over 300 SP sites covering all countries and regions of the UK.
Geographical coverage
England (45% of integrated care boards), Wales (57% of university health boards), Northern Ireland (80% of health and social care trusts), Scotland (13% of health and social care partnerships).
Well-being instruments
SWEMWBS (7-item short Warwick–Edinburgh Mental Wellbeing Scale, scored 7–35, metric-transformed) and ONS4 (Office for National Statistics 4-item well-being scale: happiness, anxiety, life satisfaction, worthwhileness; each 0–10 Likert).
Covariates
Self-reported gender, age (four bands), Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMD quintiles), urbanicity (urban/rural via postcode linkage to lower-layer super output area), referral route (medical vs. non-medical). Ethnicity excluded due to >83% missingness.
Time window
Records spanning 2017–2025; majority (>60%) collected 2021–2024. Mean follow-up interval: 2.5 months (s.d. 1.2 months).
Code availability
Example analysis code publicly available at github.com/FFBU/ESRC-SP.

Core Methods

  • Bayesian growth curve modelling (unconditional): fitted linear growth models estimating average pre–post well-being trajectories and their 95% highest-density intervals (HDIs) for each outcome, using JAGS (Markov Chain Monte Carlo, 2,000 iterations, burn-in 1,000, thinning 5) in R 4.4.1.
  • Conditional growth models (subgroup heterogeneity): extended models including all sociodemographic covariates and their interaction with time, estimating whether improvement rates differ by age, sex, deprivation, urbanicity, or referral route.
  • WELLBY monetization: mapped the 1.57-point life-satisfaction increase over the 2.5-month mean follow-up to the HM Treasury Green Book monetary value (£13,000 per point per year, 2019 prices) to derive £4,252 per person and an RoI of £9 per £1 invested (using PSSRU cost of £466/referral).
  • Sensitivity analyses: (1) intervention subsample (only patients with a recorded SP referral between pre/post measures); (2) 2023–2025 subsample (excluding COVID-era data); (3) entropy-balancing weights (reweighting sample to match full-cohort demographics on gender, age, deprivation, urbanicity, country, referral route).
  • Non-informative Bayesian priors used throughout, with full propagation of uncertainty via posterior sampling rather than point-estimate confidence intervals.

① Geospatially stratify the paper's national well-being estimates

Extending this paperThe paper analyses 300+ SP sites nationwide yet reports aggregate UK-level growth curves — a spatially "flat" output. A direct extension: link each patient's postcode to fine-grained geospatial predictors (green-space access, transport connectivity, distance to nearest SP site, healthcare facility density) and re-estimate the Bayesian growth curves within a geographically weighted or spatially random-effects framework. This would reveal whether SP's well-being ROI is uniform or whether place-level factors (e.g., rural isolation, urban deprivation clusters) moderate the magnitude of gains — a question the paper explicitly leaves open.

Added value to my researchThis is precisely my 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility domain applied to SP itself: quantifying how the spatial accessibility of SP sites moderates the outcome — analogous to how healthcare facility access shapes utilization in my existing work on telemedicine and hospital catchments. The postcode-level IMD linkage already used in this paper provides the infrastructure; adding a spatial accessibility layer (driving/walking time to the nearest SP link worker) would produce a supply-weighted SP effectiveness model fully compatible with my GWR/MGWR toolkit.

② Build a counterfactual causal model using mobile-phone mobility as a proxy control

Extending this paperThe paper's central limitation is the absence of a control group, making regression-to-the-mean and temporal confounding unresolvable within the current design. A direct extension: construct synthetic controls from the non-monitored 580,000+ patients in the Access Elemental cohort by entropy-balancing on demographics, or complement the dataset with mobile-phone activity traces as a proxy for population-level well-being trends. The corpus paper on hospital utilization inequality demonstrates how mobile-phone records can operationalize healthcare behaviour at scale across hundreds of millions of users.

③ Extend the WELLBY-RoI framework to spatial equity accounting

Extending this paperThe paper's RoI of £9 per £1 invested is a national mean that masks distributional variation: an SP site in a deprived urban neighbourhood may generate different social value than one in a prosperous rural commuter town, even if SWEMWBS scores move identically. Extending it: weight the WELLBY computation by area-level social deprivation (IMD quintile) and baseline well-being deficit, producing a spatially disaggregated RoI surface — analogous to equity-weighted cost-effectiveness analysis in health economics. This would identify where SP investment generates the greatest social return and inform geographically targeted commissioning.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly onto my efficiency-vs-equity optimization agenda (EV charging, public facility location). The approach would formalize a spatially equity-weighted healthcare investment metric using IMD and WELLBY jointly — directly applicable to primary-care access and mental health service allocation problems where I use 2SFCA to measure spatial need gaps. A deprivation-stratified RoI model would also complement my explainable ML toolkit (SHAP-based feature attribution of what drives high-RoI SP delivery).

Cross-linking patient postcodes to urban green-space indices (tree canopy, park proximity, NDVI) would test whether the effect of nature-based SP referrals is amplified by local green infrastructure — directly validating the corpus work on parks and well-being.

⑤ Apply causal machine learning to disentangle SP pathways and generalize findings

Extending this paperThe paper uses Bayesian growth curve modelling to estimate average treatment effects, but acknowledges it cannot identify which SP pathway (general-practitioner link worker, secondary care, self-referral, voluntary sector) or which activity type generates the effect. Extending it with causal ML — specifically double/debiased ML or causal forests applied to the 600,000-patient full cohort — would estimate heterogeneous treatment effects conditional on referral pathway, activity category, sociodemographic profile, and geography simultaneously, without the sample-size constraints of the current analytical subsample (only 1-in-7 patients have outcome data).

Added value to my researchThe corpus paper on causal inference for actionable healthcare directly operationalizes this approach. For my research, applying counterfactual prediction methods to SP data would demonstrate how administrative health records + causal ML can substitute for randomized trials in health policy evaluation — a methodological contribution to my data science and reproducibility agenda. It also seeds a transferable pipeline: the same causal framework could evaluate spatial interventions (e.g., new GP surgeries, telehealth expansion) across my existing health accessibility datasets.
