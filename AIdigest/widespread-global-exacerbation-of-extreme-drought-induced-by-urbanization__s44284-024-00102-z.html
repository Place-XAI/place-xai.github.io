Widespread global exacerbation of extreme drought induced by urbanization

Shuzhe Huang · Siqi Wang · Yuan Gan · Chao Wang · Daniel E. Horton · Chuxuan Li · Xiang Zhang · Dev Niyogi · Jun Xia · Nengcheng Chen Published 2024-08-07 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00102-z Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using global weather-station observations, the study shows that urbanization intensifies local drought: drought severity has increased at ~36% of global city sites and the extreme SPEI (lowest fifth) at ~43%. Physics-based WRF simulations attribute this to urbanization producing warmer, drier environments that suppress light rainfall and aggravate extreme local drought. Mid-21st-century CMIP6 projections indicate that ~57% and ~70% of urban regions would face worsened drought severity and extreme SPEI, respectively, from continued urban expansion.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reframes cities as drought drivers, not just heat islands. Most urban-climate work focuses on the urban heat island; this is among the first global, observation-based demonstrations that urbanization makes extreme local drought worse.
  • Pairs detection with mechanism. Station observations establish the signal, while WRF simulations supply a causal chain (warmer/drier surface → suppressed light rainfall → aggravated drought), so the result is attribution, not mere correlation.
  • Projects the problem forward. CMIP6-driven projections show the share of urban regions facing exacerbated drought rising toward mid-century, linking present-day land conversion to future hydroclimate risk.
  • Direct policy hook. The findings argue for explicit urban drought preparedness — water supply, green infrastructure, and stormwater planning — under joint pressure from urbanization, population growth, and climate change.

Core Methods

  • Observational detection: compute SPEI (and its extreme lowest-fifth tail) at global city stations and quantify the fraction of sites where drought severity / extreme SPEI has increased.
  • Urban vs. non-urban contrast: use impervious-surface (GISA 2.0), GUB and MODIS land cover to separate urban from surrounding sites and isolate the urbanization signal.
  • Process-based attribution: run paired WRF simulations (with vs. without urban land) to trace the mechanism — warmer/drier urban surface suppressing light rainfall and aggravating local drought.
  • Mechanistic synthesis: assemble a schematic of the physical pathway from urban surface energy/moisture changes to rainfall suppression (Fig. 3).
  • Future projection: drive the analysis with CMIP6 mid-century scenarios plus projected urban expansion, summarizing exacerbation across IPCC-AR6 regions.

① Learn the urban→drought signal with explainable ML instead of station thresholds

Extending this paperThe paper detects exacerbation by counting stations where SPEI/extreme-SPEI rises, then attributes the cause with paired WRF runs. A complementary extension: train an explainable ML model (gradient-boosted trees / GNN) on station-level SPEI change against urban-form covariates (imperviousness, GUB density, NDVI, elevation, Köppen class) and use SHAP to rank which morphological features most amplify drought — turning the binary "increased/not" map into a continuous, attributable risk surface that complements the expensive WRF physics.
