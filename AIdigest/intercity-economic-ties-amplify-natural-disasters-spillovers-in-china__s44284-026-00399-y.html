Intercity economic ties amplify natural disasters spillovers in China

Hantian Sheng · Canfei He · Wenbo Hu Published 2026-03-03 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00399-y Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Disaster damage does not stay where the disaster strikes — it travels along intercity economic linkages. By combining geocoded disaster data with firm-level cross-city investment records and feeding economic-distance spatial weight matrices into spatial Durbin models, the authors show that ignoring economic ties underestimates total economic losses by 52.5%–57.5%. Cities with high closeness centrality or clustering coefficients amplify negative spillovers, and as the intercity investment network grows more complex over time, the amplification intensifies — so disaster-resilience policy must look beyond geographic proximity to economic interdependence.

★

Why It Matters

  • Quantifies a hidden channel of disaster loss. Conventional impact assessments are spatially myopic — they count what burns or floods locally plus the immediate geographic neighbors. This work shows that the economic-network channel is comparable in size to the geographic one, with losses underestimated by more than half when ties are ignored.
  • Reframes resilience as a network property. Where a city sits in the investment network — its centrality and how tightly its partners cluster — determines how much shock it transmits and receives, turning resilience from a local attribute into a structural, relational one.
  • Tracks a moving target. Because intercity investment networks are densifying over time, the amplification effect is growing, implying that yesterday's risk maps systematically under-price tomorrow's cascades.
  • Speaks directly to policy. It motivates cross-city, coordinated disaster-resilience planning and risk-sharing across economically linked cities rather than jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction defenses.

Core Methods

  • Spatial Durbin model (SDM): spatial econometric estimation of disaster impacts that explicitly separates direct, indirect (spillover), and total effects across cities (Fig. 3).
  • Modified spatial weight matrices: beyond the standard geographic-proximity weights, the authors build an economic-linkage weight matrix from intercity investment flows, plus a combined specification (Fig. 6), to capture non-contiguous transmission.
  • Complex-network analysis: the intercity investment network is characterized by node-level metrics — closeness centrality and clustering coefficient — which condition the magnitude of negative spillovers (Fig. 4).
  • Conditioning / moderation analysis: tests how network position amplifies spillovers, and how growing network complexity over time intensifies the effect (Fig. 5).
  • Loss decomposition: compares total economic losses with vs. without the economic channel, yielding the headline 52.5%–57.5% underestimation.

① Couple the economic-spillover channel with a supply-chain cascade model

Extending this paperThe paper measures spillovers as a reduced-form spatial Durbin effect on an investment network, but never simulates the actual production/supply-chain cascade that carries a shock from one city to another. A direct extension: feed the paper's economic spatial weight matrix into a risk-extended multiregional input–output (MRIO) model, so the 52.5%–57.5% underestimation gains an explicit mechanism and return-period stress tests — converting an econometric correlation into a process-based cascade.

② Replace fixed economic weights with a learned, physics-informed graph operator

Extending this paperThe SDM imposes a linear, exogenously specified spatial weight matrix; transmission strength is assumed proportional to an investment-flow weight. Extending it: learn the spillover propagator as a graph neural operator over the intercity network, with the paper's spatial-lag structure encoded as a soft physical constraint, so the model captures nonlinear, asymmetric, and threshold transmission the SDM cannot.
