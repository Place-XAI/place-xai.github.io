Passive cooling in an urban setting

Ronggui Yang · Xiaobo Yin Published 2019-08-09 DOI 10.1038/s41893-019-0358-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Radiative cooling — a passive, electricity-free process that emits thermal radiation to the cold sky — is substantially hindered in urban settings because tall nearby buildings block sky access, reducing both the sky-view factor and the net cooling flux. A new thermal beam-shaping design by Zhou et al. (companion paper, Nat. Sustain.) redirects infrared emission toward the open sky through a tapering transformation-optics structure, demonstrating ~10 °C sub-ambient cooling even with a high-solar-absorptance material, opening a path toward practical urban passive cooling.

★

Why It Matters

  • Bridges fundamental photonics and urban sustainability. Passive radiative cooling — long demonstrated in deserts and rooftops — is now shown to be viable in the densely built environments where air-conditioning demand is highest.
  • Quantifies the urban penalty. The sky-view factor reduction from surrounding high-rises sharply reduces cooling flux and adds warming from adjacent building surfaces — a double penalty that prior work did not address in combination.
  • A proof-of-concept for architecture-integrated photonics. The beam-shaping structure shows that optical engineering (tapering transformation optics) can rescue cooling performance in constrained urban geometries, motivating a new design paradigm.
  • Direct relevance to decarbonizing cities. Reducing air-conditioning electricity demand in metropolitan areas is a major lever for urban carbon neutrality; passive solutions that require no grid power are especially attractive.

Core Methods

  • Sky-view factor framing: introduces the sky-view factor (ratio of visible sky to hemisphere centered over a location) as the central physical quantity governing radiative cooling effectiveness in urban environments.
  • Thermal beam-shaping via transformation optics: the companion experiment uses a V-shaped tapering structure derived from transformation-optics principles to redirect infrared emission into a narrow angular cone aimed at the open sky, while blocking perpendicular solar irradiance.
  • Conceptual energy-balance analysis: the commentary explains qualitatively how nearby building surfaces (warmer than the upper atmosphere) reduce the net cooling flux, layering the sky-obstruction effect on top of the standard atmospheric-window radiative budget.
  • Material benchmark: discusses a material with ~10% solar absorptance — higher than ideal spectrally-selective emitters — to show that angular (beam-shaping) engineering can compensate for imperfect spectral selectivity.
  • Design generalization: closes with open problems including integration of inexpensive spectrally-selective materials, planar optics for angular/spectral co-engineering, and techno-economic analyses under realistic building structures and local climate data.

④ Use machine learning to co-optimize spectral emissivity and angular beam-shaping for heterogeneous urban morphologies

Extending this paperThe paper highlights two distinct engineering levers — spectral selectivity (material design) and angular beam-shaping (transformation optics) — but treats them sequentially rather than jointly. The companion experiment uses a high-solar-absorptance material precisely because beam-shaping compensates for it; an inverse problem would jointly optimize emissivity spectrum and geometric taper angle for a given local sky-view factor. Extending it: train a surrogate ML model (e.g., physics-informed neural network) on finite-difference time-domain or radiosity simulations across a parameter space of sky-view factors, emissivity spectra, and building heights to find Pareto-optimal material–geometry pairings for each urban morphology class.
