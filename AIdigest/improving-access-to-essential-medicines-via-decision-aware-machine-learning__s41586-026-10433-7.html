Improving access to essential medicines via decision-aware machine learning

Angel Tsai-Hsuan Chung · Jatu Abdulai · Patrick Bayoh · Lawrence Sandi · Francis Smart · Hamsa Bastani · Osbert Bastani Published 2026-04-29 DOI 10.1038/s41586-026-10433-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

A decision-aware machine learning framework — combining multi-task learning for sample efficiency and catalytic priors for equitable allocation — was deployed nationwide across Sierra Leone as a decision support tool for distributing essential medicines. A staggered econometric evaluation found an estimated 19% increase in consumption of allocated products in treated districts, with coverage subsequently scaled to an estimated two million women and children under 5. The work demonstrates that ML can deliver meaningful efficiency and equity gains in resource-constrained global health settings at very low cost.

Why It Matters

  • First large-scale, real-world ML deployment for essential medicine allocation in an LMIC. Unlike simulation-only or pilot studies, the system was deployed staggered nationwide across Sierra Leone and evaluated econometrically — a rare end-to-end proof of impact.
  • Bridges the "data-scarce" gap. Traditional data-driven supply-chain methods require high-quality historical records that LMICs rarely possess. Multi-task learning and catalytic priors allow the framework to work under severe data limitations, making the approach broadly transferable.
  • Quantified equity gains alongside efficiency gains. The catalytic-prior mechanism explicitly encodes an equity objective, ensuring allocation favors underserved populations — not just high-demand districts.
  • Demonstrates policy scalability at minimal marginal cost. Covering ~2 million beneficiaries from a single ML tool shows that algorithmic decision support can scale in resource-constrained settings where adding human capacity is prohibitively expensive.
  • Sets a reproducible template for decision-aware AI in public health. Anonymized evaluation data, ML code, and econometric evaluation code are all open access, enabling replication in other LMICs and health commodities.

Core Methods

  • Decision-aware machine learning: the ML model is trained not to minimize prediction error alone but to directly optimize the downstream allocation decision quality — bridging the predict-then-optimize gap via end-to-end differentiable optimization layers.
  • Multi-task learning: jointly learns demand forecasts across multiple medicine products and districts, sharing statistical strength across tasks to achieve sample efficiency when historical records are sparse or noisy.
  • Catalytic priors: Bayesian priors informed by population-level equity targets (e.g., per-capita baselines) are incorporated to ensure that the allocation favors underserved districts, preventing the model from over-concentrating resources in high-data, high-demand areas.
  • Staggered nationwide deployment: the system was rolled out sequentially across districts of Sierra Leone, creating a natural treatment-control structure exploitable by econometric methods.
  • Synthetic difference-in-differences (SDiD) evaluation: following Clarke et al. (2023), the causal effect of the ML decision-support tool was estimated via SDiD, comparing consumption trends in treated vs. untreated districts while accounting for pre-treatment heterogeneity.
  • Pre-deployment simulation: algorithmic performance was validated in simulation before live deployment, using historical facility-level data and population covariates from WorldPop and the friction surface.

① Spatialize the allocation model with travel-time catchment areas

Extending this paperThe paper's allocation model operates at the district level, using population-level covariates and a travel-time friction surface as inputs, but it does not explicitly model which sub-district populations can realistically reach which facilities. Extending it with a spatial accessibility layer — computing facility-specific catchment populations from the Oxford friction surface and WorldPop at the facility level rather than the district level — would let the catalytic priors be anchored to realized access rather than administrative boundaries. This would surface intra-district inequities hidden by district aggregation, especially in high-friction rural areas of Sierra Leone.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct application of my 2SFCA/R2SFCA framework: the friction surface already used in this paper can feed a two-step floating catchment area model that delineates facility service areas before the allocation optimization runs — yielding a spatially explicit demand denominator for each facility. The combined pipeline (R2SFCA catchment delineation → decision-aware allocation) would extend my healthcare accessibility work into the LMIC supply-chain domain.

② Incorporate causal counterfactual prediction to preempt allocation failure

Extending this paperThe paper evaluates its system with synthetic difference-in-differences after the fact, but the allocation decisions are made without an explicit causal model of how district-level interventions propagate through the supply chain. Extending it with a causal counterfactual layer — modeling what consumption would have been under alternative allocations — would allow the ML system to actively learn which districts respond most to supply increases versus which have demand-side bottlenecks (stock-outs vs. utilization barriers). This would sharpen the catalytic priors from static equity weights into dynamic, causally informed uncertainty quantification.

Added value to my researchCounterfactual prediction connects directly to my interest in explainable ML for spatial prediction: understanding not just "where is access low" but "what would change if we added a facility or increased stock" is the operational form of SHAP/LIME explanations applied to supply-chain interventions. This framing would let me transfer my XAI toolkit from prediction tasks to policy evaluation tasks in health geography.

Cross-ref (corpus): Causal inference and counterfactual prediction in machine learning for actionable healthcare — 10.1038/s42256-020-0197-y

③ Replace the district-level demand forecast with an active-learning sequential design

Extending this paperThe paper's multi-task learning framework requires historical consumption records to initialize the demand forecast for each district-product pair. In contexts where even initial records are near-zero (new programs, new geographies), the catalytic priors carry too much weight and may not reflect actual local conditions. Extending the paper with an active-learning sequential experimental design — choosing which districts to supply first specifically to reduce uncertainty in the demand model — would make the staggered rollout information-optimal rather than administratively determined, accelerating the learning curve demonstrated in Fig. 3.

Added value to my researchSequential experimental design maps onto my work in agent-based modeling and Monte Carlo simulation: instead of post-hoc econometric evaluation, the deployment itself becomes a controlled adaptive experiment. For my urban analytics agenda this suggests a transferable "adaptive rollout" template for public facility interventions (EV charging stations, telehealth kiosks) where demand is initially uncertain.

Cross-ref (corpus): Active learning for optimal intervention design in causal models — 10.1038/s42256-023-00719-0

④ Jointly optimize efficiency and equity across multiple health commodities

Extending this paperThe paper frames equity through the catalytic-prior mechanism applied to a single allocation objective. In reality, a national medical supply agency manages dozens of commodities simultaneously, with equity trade-offs that differ by product (maternal health vs. malaria vs. nutrition). Extending the framework to a multi-objective Pareto optimization — tracing the efficiency–equity frontier across commodity portfolios — would tell policymakers the true cost of equity mandates and identify commodity bundles where efficiency and equity are complementary rather than competing. The SDiD evaluation infrastructure already in place makes this a tractable extension.

Added value to my researchThis is a direct parallel to my EV charging-station efficiency-versus-equity optimization: the same Pareto-frontier methodology I use for siting public infrastructure applies here, with the medicine portfolio replacing spatial facility configurations. The catalytic-prior mechanism is essentially a Lagrangian equity constraint — a formulation I can adopt in my facility-location work to encode equity mandates in spatial optimization.

⑤ Generalize the framework to healthcare operations beyond Sierra Leone via transfer learning

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates impact in Sierra Leone but treats the trained model as country-specific. The multi-task learning architecture already shares information across districts within one country; a natural extension is cross-country transfer — pre-training on higher-data LMICs and fine-tuning on new countries with near-zero records, using the WorldPop friction surface and facility-density covariates (available globally) as the transferable feature backbone. This would operationalize the paper's claim that "ML can improve efficiency at very low cost" into a truly transferable global health tool.
