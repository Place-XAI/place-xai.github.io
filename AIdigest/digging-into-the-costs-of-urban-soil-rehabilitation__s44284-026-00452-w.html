Digging into the costs of urban soil rehabilitation

Mathilde Salin · Charles Claron · Elodie Nguyen--Rabot · Nicolas Mondolfo · Harold Levrel Published 2026-06-10 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00452-w Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

For the first time in France, this study estimates the full cost spectrum of urban soil rehabilitation: €50–310 m−2 for compacted, sealed, or built-up soils, rising to over €800 m−2 for polluted soils. Rehabilitation follows a sequence of up to ten technical steps, with preliminary diagnostics being cheapest and building deconstruction plus pollution remediation most costly. These price benchmarks are designed to guide spatial prioritization of rehabilitation zones and underpin economic instruments to incentivize urban soil recovery — while simultaneously underlining the far greater value of preventing soil degradation in the first place.

Why It Matters

  • First quantitative cost baseline for urban soil rehabilitation. Despite wide recognition that urban soils underpin flood mitigation, carbon storage, and biodiversity, the economics of repairing them were entirely unexplored. This study fills that gap with empirically grounded cost estimates across four soil degradation types.
  • Enables cost–benefit analysis for urban greening and climate adaptation. Soil rehabilitation is a precondition for urban nature-based solutions — parks, permeable pavements, urban forests — yet planners had no cost data. The estimates provided here make investment cases and benefit-transfer analyses tractable for the first time.
  • Reframes "Zero Net Artificialization" policy in economic terms. France's landmark law (Loi Climat et Résilience, 2021) mandates soil preservation; this paper translates that goal into a liability calculus — the cost of reversing past damage — strengthening the economic argument for not sealing soils in the first place.
  • Reveals hidden cost structure. By decomposing rehabilitation into up to ten sequential steps, the study identifies the largest cost drivers (building deconstruction, pollution remediation) versus cheap entry points (diagnostics), enabling targeted subsidy or regulation design.
  • Open dataset supports replication and scaling. All survey data are deposited on Zenodo (doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.20303439), making regional or European cost extrapolations feasible.

Core Methods

  • Expert elicitation: structured solicitation of cost estimates from practitioners and specialists, following established expert-elicitation protocols (Verdolini et al., 2018). Captures medians, minima, maxima, and individual observations across rehabilitation steps.
  • Rehabilitation pathway design: defined a typology of urban soil states (built-up B, sealed S, compacted C, polluted P) × final ecological targets (vegetation V, porous paving PP) × ecological engineering intensity (low l, moderate m, high h), yielding a matrix of distinct pathways named with the convention InitialState_FinalState_EcologicalEngineering.
  • Step-by-step cost decomposition: each pathway is decomposed into up to ten sequential steps; costs are collected and reported per step, enabling identification of cost-driving versus low-cost entry points.
  • Pathway cost aggregation: total pathway costs are assembled from step-level estimates and visualized as cost distributions (Fig. 1) and pathway-specific totals (Fig. 3).
  • Driver analysis: qualitative and quantitative analysis of cost determinants — soil type, degree of pollution, ecological engineering intensity, land-use context — yielding the driver taxonomy in Fig. 2.
  • Secondary data triangulation: expert estimates are cross-validated against ADEME program data, Cerema guides, and the SOILval project to assess consistency.

① Spatially explicit cost mapping: from France-wide estimates to city-scale rehabilitation priority maps

Extending this paperThe paper's cost estimates are aggregate for France but do not produce a spatial map of rehabilitation cost or priority. A direct extension: overlay the cost typology (compacted / sealed / built-up / polluted) with remote-sensing-derived impervious surface maps and urban soil contamination registers to produce a per-parcel cost surface across French cities. Combining this with ecosystem-service benefit estimates (flood mitigation value, urban cooling value) yields a spatial net-benefit map that policy-makers could use to rank intervention zones — transforming the paper's tabular benchmarks into a spatially actionable planning tool. The urbanization-climate-soil dataset from crowd-sensed plants (Urbanization signatures on climate and soils, Nature Cities 2026) provides a complementary signal layer for detecting soil degradation signatures at scale.

② Couple rehabilitation costs with flood-damage avoided: building an urban soil benefit-transfer framework

Extending this paperThe paper estimates costs but not benefits. Its own motivation (flood mitigation, climate regulation) implicitly defines the benefit side of a cost–benefit analysis. Extending it: pair the cost-per-m2 estimates with quantified flood-damage reduction per rehabilitated m2 — drawing on inundation models and flood-exposure datasets — to compute net-benefit ratios across city typologies. This would transform the paper from a cost accounting exercise into a full economic justification for soil rehabilitation investment, providing the missing link for policy makers applying Europe's Nature Restoration Law. The social disparity dimension of flood exposure (44,698 Latin American neighborhoods paper) underscores that rehabilitation benefits are unevenly distributed, adding an equity dimension the paper currently lacks.

④ Use explainable ML to identify the strongest predictors of cost heterogeneity and build a cost-prediction tool

Extending this paperThe paper identifies several qualitative cost drivers (Fig. 2: soil type, pollution level, ecological engineering intensity, site context) but uses descriptive statistics rather than a predictive model. A gradient-boosting or random-forest model trained on the Zenodo survey dataset, with SHAP attribution, would quantify which driver combinations most predict cost variance — and produce a cost-prediction tool for practitioners entering project-specific parameters. This directly addresses the paper's acknowledged limitation that "substantial cost variation" remains only partially explained, and would make the Zenodo dataset far more actionable for municipal planners.

⑤ Design equity-sensitive rehabilitation investment strategies: who bears the costs and who gets the benefits

Extending this paperThe paper frames rehabilitation primarily as an efficiency and cost question. Yet soil degradation is spatially concentrated in industrial brownfields and dense urban cores where low-income and minority communities disproportionately live — making rehabilitation a social-justice issue. Extending it: couple the per-m2 cost estimates with neighborhood-level socioeconomic data to quantify the inequality of the rehabilitation burden and the distribution of ecosystem-service benefits. The gentrification paradox of green-blue adaptation (Nature Cities 2026) directly warns that green infrastructure investment can displace vulnerable residents — the same risk attaches to urban soil rehabilitation if not designed with equity safeguards. A spatial optimization approach that maximizes ecosystem-service access gains for the most underserved neighborhoods while remaining within a rehabilitation budget would make this an equity-first planning tool.
