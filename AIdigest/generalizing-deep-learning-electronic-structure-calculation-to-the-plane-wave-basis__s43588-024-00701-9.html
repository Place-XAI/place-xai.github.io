Generalizing deep learning electronic structure calculation to the plane-wave basis

Xiaoxun Gong · Steven G. Louie · Wenhui Duan · Yong Xu Published 2024-10-03 DOI 10.1038/s43588-024-00701-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Deep learning electronic structure methods have been limited to the atomic-orbital (AO) basis, excluding the more accurate and widely used plane-wave (PW) basis. This work proposes a real-space reconstruction method that converts PW DFT results to AO Hamiltonian matrices with linear scaling and orders-of-magnitude speedup over projection-based alternatives. The reconstructed Hamiltonians faithfully reproduce PW electronic structure — demonstrated on magic-angle twisted bilayer graphene (11,164 atoms) and bilayer MoS₂ (546 atoms) — enabling deep learning models trained on small PW DFT datasets to generalize to large, complex Moiré systems with millielectronvolt accuracy.

★

Why It Matters

  • Closes a critical compatibility gap. Prior deep learning DFT Hamiltonian methods required the AO basis exclusively; the PW basis — used by the majority of solid-state DFT calculations — was inaccessible. This method bridges the two, unlocking the entire universe of PW materials databases for deep learning training.
  • Orders-of-magnitude computational speedup. The real-space reconstruction method achieves linear O(N) scaling versus the O(N³) scaling of traditional projection methods, making it faster than even a full PW self-consistent field calculation at large system sizes.
  • Enables large-scale, high-fidelity training datasets. Because PW DFT is more complete and easier to converge than AO DFT, interfacing it with deep learning produces more accurate neural network models. This opens a path to universal, broadly applicable electronic structure models.
  • Opens the door to advanced quantum methods. The real-space reconstruction philosophy extends beyond DFT to density functional perturbation theory (electron–phonon interactions), GW many-body perturbation, and time-dependent DFT — vastly expanding the future scope of deep learning in quantum physics.

Core Methods

  • Real-space reconstruction (H(r) method): the AO Hamiltonian is computed directly in real space from the PW effective potential Veff(r) and non-local pseudopotential projectors, exploiting the locality of AOs so that the computational cost scales linearly O(N) with the number of atoms — vs. O(N³) for the two projection-based alternatives.
  • Two-center integral technique: kinetic energy and non-local pseudopotential matrix elements are computed analytically via spherical Bessel transforms and Gaunt coefficients, making the reconstruction applicable to any separable AO basis without re-parameterization.
  • Basis-set design: double-zeta plus polarization (DZP) numerical AO basis sets generated by SIESTA, compatible with the norm-conserving Vanderbilt pseudopotentials used in the PW calculations, to maximize fidelity of the reconstructed Hamiltonian.
  • Neural network training: deep learning DFT Hamiltonian model (building on DeePH framework, ref. 34) trained on reconstructed AO Hamiltonians from small, non-twisted training structures; generalized to large twisted Moiré supercells at inference time with no additional DFT calculations.
  • Benchmarking: band structures from diagonalization of reconstructed/predicted AO Hamiltonians are compared directly with PW DFT references; energy errors reported in millielectronvolts along high-symmetry k-paths.
  • Extension to PAW formalism: the reconstruction method is analytically extended to the projector augmented-wave (PAW) formalism, broadening compatibility to virtually all PW DFT codes (VASP, Abinit, etc.).

① Build a universal Hamiltonian model by exploiting PW-accessible materials databases

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates reconstruction for two specific 2D material families (graphene, MoS₂). The Discussion explicitly targets a universal deep learning electronic structure model trained across diverse material families. A natural extension: use the HPRO pipeline to batch-process large open PW databases (e.g., Materials Project, AFLOW, OQMD — all PW-DFT-based), reconstruct AO Hamiltonians for tens of thousands of structures, and train a single equivariant neural network that generalizes across chemical species. The linear O(N) scaling of the reconstruction method is precisely what makes this tractable where projection methods would not be.

Just as HPRO reconstructs a common AO representation from PW outputs, my work on spatiotemporal foundation models needs a "reconstruction" layer that normalizes heterogeneous geospatial sensor formats into a common local patch representation for fine-tuning. ② Couple reconstruction with electron–phonon coupling for finite-temperature electronic properties

Extending this paperThe paper reconstructs static ground-state Hamiltonians. A direct extension is to interface the reconstruction method with density functional perturbation theory (DFPT) outputs — which are also PW-based — to capture electron–phonon coupling matrices. This is explicitly mentioned as a future direction in the Discussion. Training a neural network on reconstructed DFPT-derived quantities would enable deep-learning prediction of phonon-mediated superconductivity, carrier mobility, and thermal transport in large twisted Moiré systems — currently inaccessible by direct DFT.

③ Extend the linear-scaling reconstruction to excited-state and many-body perturbation methods (GW, BSE)

Extending this paperThe paper restricts itself to Kohn–Sham DFT (ground state). The reconstruction framework is architecturally compatible with GW and GW-BSE quasiparticle methods, which compute the self-energy and screened Coulomb interaction on top of PW DFT — also explicitly flagged in the Discussion. Applying the H(r) reconstruction philosophy to the self-energy operator Σ(r,r') would require a non-local generalization, since GW self-energy is range-separated rather than strictly local. Benchmarking reconstruction accuracy of optical excitation energies and exciton binding in Moiré systems would be a landmark follow-up.

④ Apply active learning to select the most informative PW DFT structures for training

Extending this paperThe paper constructs training sets with fixed-size random perturbations (300 graphene supercells, 256 MoS₂ supercells). This is agnostic to which structures are actually most informative for the neural network's generalization to Moiré twists. An extension using uncertainty-driven active learning — query structures where the neural network is most uncertain, run PW DFT + reconstruction only on those, iterate — would reduce the required training set size by potentially an order of magnitude, making the approach feasible for heavier, more complex materials (e.g., transition metal oxides, heavy-fermion compounds) where PW DFT is expensive.

The corpus paper on uncertainty-driven dynamics for interatomic potentials provides the methodological template.

⑤ Replace the fixed AO basis set with a learned, material-adaptive basis optimized jointly with the neural network

Extending this paperThe paper uses a fixed DZP numerical AO basis generated by SIESTA, which must be chosen by the user and tuned per pseudopotential. The paper notes that the reconstruction method works for any AO basis that has separable radial-angular form. An extension: make the radial functions themselves learnable parameters that are optimized jointly with the neural-network Hamiltonian model via automatic differentiation through the reconstruction integral (Eq. 5). This would automatically discover the minimal, maximally expressive basis for each material family — analogous to how NeuralODE learns basis functions for dynamical systems — and remove the most significant remaining user-tuning burden.

Added value to my researchThis maps precisely to my fine-tuning of geospatial foundation models: rather than fixing a pre-defined spatial feature basis (e.g., fixed POI categories, fixed grid resolution), a learnable spatial basis jointly optimized with the downstream prediction task would automatically adapt to the spatial scale and feature mix that matters for each health or urban outcome. The technical analogy is tight: optimize the spatial basis functions (analogous to AO radial functions) end-to-end so the downstream model (analogous to the neural-network Hamiltonian) achieves minimum prediction error — a shared paradigm for both computational physics and GeoAI.
