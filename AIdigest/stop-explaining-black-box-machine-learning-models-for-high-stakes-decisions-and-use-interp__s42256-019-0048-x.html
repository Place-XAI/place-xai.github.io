Stop explaining black box machine learning models for high stakes decisions and use interpretable models instead

Cynthia Rudin Published 2019-05-13 DOI 10.1038/s42256-019-0048-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Post hoc "explainability" of black box models is not a substitute for inherent interpretability: explanations are always unfaithful to the original model, often misleading, and can perpetuate serious harm in criminal justice, healthcare and other high-stakes domains. The accuracy–interpretability trade-off is a myth for structured data: interpretable models such as rule lists, scoring systems and prototype networks routinely match black box accuracy. Society should demand inherently interpretable models — not post hoc explanations — for high-stakes decisions.

Why It Matters

  • Reframes the dominant XAI paradigm. The paper argues the entire field of "explainable ML" is solving the wrong problem — adding a second model to approximate a black box — when the goal should be building transparent models from the start.
  • Documents real-world harms of black boxes. The COMPAS recidivism tool (130+ factors, widely used in US courts) is shown to be no more accurate than a 3-rule interpretable model, yet it introduces typographical-error unfairness, proprietary opacity and racial misattribution risks.
  • Establishes the Rashomon-set argument. A large set of near-optimal models nearly always contains at least one interpretable member — providing a theoretical guarantee that interpretable accuracy is achievable, not exceptional.
  • Proposes concrete governance mandates. Rudin advocates that regulators require organizations to demonstrate no interpretable model exists before deploying a black box — turning an ethical claim into an auditable burden of proof.
  • Demonstrates interpretability in computer vision. Prototype networks ("This Looks Like That") achieve accuracy parity with black box deep nets while making every prediction a transparent comparison to learned prototypes.

Core Methods

  • Conceptual / argumentation framework: the paper is a structured perspective — no new empirical methods, but it systematically decomposes the XAI field into two categories (black-box explainability vs. inherent interpretability) and enumerates failure modes of post hoc explanations (unfaithfulness, incompleteness, complexity, gaming risk).
  • CORELS (Certifiably Optimal Rule Lists): branch-and-bound search over rule lists with bit-vector acceleration and symmetry pruning — finds a globally optimal sparse if-then rule list (Table 1, 3-rule model) matching COMPAS accuracy on Broward County data.
  • RiskSLIM (Risk-Supersparse-Linear-Integer-Models): specialized cutting-plane method for mixed-integer nonlinear programs — produces sparse integer-coefficient scoring systems (Table 3) optimally balancing logistic loss and model complexity.
  • Prototype networks ("This Looks Like That"): deep learning architecture with an appended prototype layer that learns class-representative image patches during training; predictions are weighted sums of similarity to prototypes — inherently interpretable without post hoc explanation.
  • Rashomon-set theory: formal argument that when a data set admits many near-optimal models (large Rashomon set), at least one is likely interpretable — providing a theoretical basis for the central thesis.
  • Comparative analysis: qualitative and empirical comparison of COMPAS vs. CORELS (Table 2), and of accuracy–interpretability trade-off claims using the DARPA XAI figure (Fig. 1) as a foil.

① Apply the Rashomon-set argument to spatial prediction models in GeoAI

Extending this paperRudin's Rashomon-set argument — that large sets of near-equally-accurate models typically contain at least one interpretable member — is stated for tabular data but never tested spatially. A direct extension would be to empirically measure the Rashomon set for geospatial regression tasks (e.g., predicting healthcare accessibility or housing prices across census tracts) and ask whether it consistently contains sparse spatial models such as MGWR or geographically-weighted logistic regression. If so, the paper's thesis extends to spatial science: we should stop explaining black-box gradient-boosted spatial models and instead insist on interpretable GWR/MGWR solutions.

⑤ Operationalize Rudin's governance mandate as an interpretability compliance layer in spatiotemporal public-health ML pipelines

Extending this paperRudin's strongest policy proposal is: require organizations to prove no interpretable model achieves equivalent accuracy before deploying a black box. This mandate has no standard operationalization. A concrete extension of the paper: define a formal "interpretability compliance test" — run CORELS/RiskSLIM/MGWR as baselines, measure their accuracy within the Rashomon bound of the best complex model, and only approve the black box if the gap exceeds a pre-specified threshold (e.g., >2% AUC). Codifying this as a reusable audit pipeline would translate the perspective into a practical tool for AI governance.
