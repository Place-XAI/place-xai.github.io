Air pollution as a determinant of food delivery and related plastic waste

Junhong Chu · Haoming Liu · Alberto Salvo Published 2020-10-19 DOI 10.1038/s41562-020-00961-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Severe ambient air pollution (PM2.5 haze) is a causal behavioral driver of food delivery consumption in urban China: a 100 µg m⁻³ rise in PM2.5 increased food delivery ordering by 43% of the sample mean among office workers and 7.2% in a broad platform user base. Because delivered meals generate an estimated 54 g of disposable plastic per order versus 6.6 g for walk-to-restaurant meals, air pollution control carries a substantial plastic waste co-benefit. The link operates through technology-enabled avoidance of outdoor exposure, and likely generalizes to other routinely polluted developing-world cities.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reveals a hidden environmental feedback loop. Air pollution drives more food delivery, which generates more plastic waste, which worsens environmental quality — a reinforcing cycle that standard pollution-control analyses ignore.
  • Quantifies the plastic waste co-benefit of clean-air policy. If China's PM2.5 fell by 100 µg m⁻³ nationally, an estimated 2.6 million fewer meals per day would be delivered, saving 2.6 million plastic bags and containers daily.
  • Documents a novel pollution-avoidance channel. Smartphone-enabled food delivery platforms are shown to function as a technological shield against bad air — a behavioral mechanism absent from earlier literature.
  • Uses two complementary, quasi-experimental datasets. A causal 2SLS design with atmospheric thermal gradients as instruments provides convincing evidence across both a panel survey of 251 office workers and 23.6 million user-day choices from a commercial platform.
  • Generalizable to other environmental stressors. The authors note that extreme heat, viral outbreaks, and other shocks inducing indoor-staying behavior may similarly raise packaging waste — making the mechanism broadly relevant for urban sustainability.

Core Methods

  • Linear probability model with individual fixed effects: binary food-delivery choice regressed on city-day PM2.5 (linear and quadratic), weather controls (temperature bins, humidity, rain/snow dummies), week-of-sample × city and day-of-week × city fixed effects, and individual fixed effects — removing any time-invariant personal propensity.
  • Instrumental variables (2SLS): atmospheric vertical thermal gradients (surface to 1,000, 925, 850, 700, 500 mb) and surface wind direction from NOAA radiosonde, same-day and previous-day readings, instrument PM2.5 to isolate plausibly exogenous variation in haze from atmospheric regime shifts (temperature inversions vs. ventilation).
  • Ordinary least squares (OLS) as comparison: estimated alongside 2SLS; 2SLS generally produces larger estimates, consistent with either attenuation bias from PM2.5 measurement error or upward omitted-variable bias in OLS.
  • Distributed lag models: include up to four preceding days' PM2.5 to test intertemporal substitution effects; same-day PM2.5 remains the dominant and stable predictor.
  • Plastic quantification from photographs: trained coders assigned single-use plastic mass (grams) to each uploaded meal photograph, distinguishing food delivery (mean 54 g, 2.8 items) from walk-to-restaurant (mean 6.6 g, 0.5 items), enabling regression of plastic use on PM2.5.
  • Heterogeneity and robustness checks: subgroup regressions by gender, age, education, city; sensitivity to canteen access, IP-address mismatch, platform enrolment timing, dinner-time effects, coupon share, and aggregation level.

③ Integrate the delivery-platform order book with spatial accessibility of food options to test the substitution hypothesis

Extending this paperThe paper treats food delivery as one binary choice against "walk to restaurant," but ignores the spatial distribution of nearby dining options. In dense urban areas with abundant nearby restaurants, the threshold PM2.5 needed to tip someone toward delivery should be higher than in areas where the nearest eatery is a 20-minute walk. Extending it: merge the Beijing order-book with OpenStreetMap restaurant POI density and network-based walk time to test whether the PM2.5 elasticity varies with local food-access proximity — a testable spatial heterogeneity prediction. The corpus paper on Disparities in access to sustainable dining options provides an integrated accessibility framework for exactly this extension.

④ Quantify the environmental burden of last-mile delivery logistics triggered by pollution-driven demand surges

Extending this paperThis paper stops at the consumer side — it shows more orders on hazy days but does not trace what happens to last-mile logistics (more delivery riders on the road, more vehicle emissions) on exactly those high-PM2.5 days. On a hazy day in Beijing, 120,000 extra delivery orders means more scooter trips, generating additional tailpipe emissions that may partially offset the personal exposure reduction the consumer sought. Extending it: using the corpus paper on Decarbonizing emissions from last-mile deliveries in Chinese cities, estimate the net environmental balance of the pollution-avoidance substitution — consumer PM2.5 exposure avoided versus logistics emissions induced.

Added value to my researchThis net-balance framing is a natural application of my urban agglomeration and regional collaboration research: I study how city-scale decisions create externalities for neighboring areas, and here the question is whether individual pollution-avoidance behavior collectively worsens aggregate urban air quality through logistics emissions — an urban-scale feedback amenable to my agent-based modeling approach. Coupling an ABM of delivery rider behavior with the paper's elasticity estimates would quantify how much of the consumer-side benefit is offset by supply-side emissions.
