Three types of incremental learning

Gido M. van de Ven · Tinne Tuytelaars · Andreas S. Tolias Published 2022-12-05 DOI 10.1038/s42256-022-00568-3 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Continual learning is not one problem but three: task-incremental, domain-incremental, and class-incremental learning differ fundamentally in what information the algorithm receives at test time and in their difficulty. A comprehensive empirical comparison on Split MNIST and Split CIFAR-100 shows replay-based strategies are the only approach that performs well across all three scenarios, while parameter-regularization methods fail almost completely in the hardest setting (class-incremental). This taxonomy gives the field a shared framework for designing unambiguous benchmarks and comparing algorithms on equal footing.

★

Why It Matters

  • Ends the apples-to-oranges problem. Dozens of continual-learning papers claimed state-of-the-art on the same datasets but were actually solving different problems. The three-scenario taxonomy makes those comparisons honest.
  • Reveals hidden difficulty gradients. Task-IL is not "solved" — using separate networks per task is actually a strong baseline; class-IL is dramatically harder than commonly acknowledged.
  • Explains why popular methods fail. The paper shows mathematically why parameter-regularization cannot bridge class-incremental learning: retaining past weights is not enough when the algorithm must also distinguish classes never seen together.
  • Generalizes beyond the academic setting. The formal framework extends to task-free streams, gradual context shifts, and mixed scenarios — covering realistic non-stationary conditions, not just split-dataset toy protocols.
  • Open, reproducible benchmark. MIT-licensed code at github.com/GMvandeVen/continual-learning allows any algorithm to be slotted into all three scenarios and compared on equal footing.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

MNIST dataset
MNIST (LeCun et al., 1998) — 70,000 greyscale 28×28 images of handwritten digits 0–9. Standard split: 60,000 training / 10,000 test. Used as Split MNIST: divided into 5 contexts of 2 digits each (~6,000 train / ~1,000 test images per digit). Freely available at yann.lecun.com/exdb/mnist/.
CIFAR-10 & CIFAR-100
CIFAR-10 / CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky & Hinton, 2009) — 32×32 RGB colour images. CIFAR-10 (10 classes, 60,000 images) used to pre-train the convolutional backbone; CIFAR-100 (100 classes, 50,000 train / 10,000 test at 500/100 per class) used as Split CIFAR-100: divided into 10 contexts of 10 classes each. Freely available at cs.toronto.edu/~kriz/cifar.html.

Core Methods

  • Formal scenario taxonomy: three scenarios defined by how the mapping to be learned relates to the context space — task-IL (f: X×C→Y), domain-IL (f: X→Y), class-IL (f: X→C×Y) — and by whether context identity is provided or must be inferred at test time.
  • Five continual-learning strategy families compared: (1) context-specific components (XdG, Separate Networks); (2) parameter regularization (EWC, SI); (3) functional regularization (LwF, FROMP); (4) replay from buffer or generative model (DGR, BI-R, ER, A-GEM); (5) template-based classification (iCaRL, Generative Classifier).
  • Controlled architecture protocol: same base network (2-layer FC for Split MNIST; pre-trained conv + 2-layer FC for Split CIFAR-100) and same training hyper-parameters (ADAM, identical iterations) across all methods — isolating strategy differences from architectural advantages.
  • Output-layer treatment: multi-headed (per-context output units) for task-IL; single-headed for domain-IL and class-IL — ensuring the output space correctly matches each scenario's definition.
  • Baselines: "None" (sequential training without any anti-forgetting mechanism) as lower bound; "Joint" (simultaneous training on all data) as upper bound.
  • Generalization of the framework: extension to task-free streams (no sharp context boundaries), gradual context transitions, and mixed/hybrid scenarios via a probabilistic formulation of the data stream.

① Apply the three-scenario framework to geospatial AI models trained on non-stationary urban data

Extending this paperThe paper's three scenarios were validated only on image classification benchmarks (MNIST, CIFAR). A direct extension is to instantiate all three scenarios on non-stationary geospatial data streams: task-IL (learn to predict accessibility in a new city while retaining knowledge of previous cities), domain-IL (adapt to a new year's POI distribution without forgetting the spatial structure), class-IL (add new facility types incrementally to a healthcare accessibility model). This would test which continual-learning strategies transfer from computer vision to spatial prediction tasks — a gap the paper explicitly acknowledges but does not fill.

Added value to my researchMy geospatial foundation model fine-tuning work sits squarely in domain-IL and task-IL territory: when I fine-tune a pre-trained GeoAI model on a new city's health access data, I am doing domain-incremental learning. This paper's benchmark protocol tells me exactly how to evaluate whether the fine-tuned model catastrophically forgets spatial patterns from earlier cities — and replay with a spatial memory buffer is the evidence-backed strategy to try first.

② Class-incremental learning as a model for expanding healthcare facility classification

Extending this paperThe paper shows that class-IL is the hardest scenario and that only replay and template-based methods survive it. An extension: design a class-IL benchmark for medical service types where the algorithm incrementally learns to classify primary care, mental health, emergency, and telehealth facilities as new service categories are introduced over time. This would test whether the paper's conclusions (replay dominance, regularization failure) hold when the "classes" are spatial facility types rather than pixel categories.

Added value to my researchIn my spatial accessibility work with 2SFCA/R2SFCA, the set of healthcare facility types that must be modeled is not fixed: telehealth clinics, urgent care centers, and mobile health units are added over time. The paper prescribes replay or template-based classification (e.g., a prototype per facility type in feature space) as the correct strategy for this incremental facility typing — directly applicable to keeping my accessibility models current without periodic full retraining.

③ Integrate the paper's scenario taxonomy with transfer learning on distribution-shifted spatial data

Extending this paperThe paper's domain-IL scenario (same task, shifting input distribution) maps directly onto distribution shift in spatial prediction — a model trained on urban areas tested on suburban or rural geographies. Extending the paper: apply the five strategy families to domain-IL with real distribution shift (e.g., training on high-density census tracts, then incrementally learning low-density rural tracts), measuring which continual-learning approach best preserves the mapping f: X→Y under spatial distribution shift. The paper's formal generalization section already hints at this but provides no empirical evidence.

Added value to my researchMy MGWR/GWR models are explicitly designed for spatial non-stationarity, but they are re-estimated from scratch for each new area or time period. Treating spatial non-stationarity as domain-IL means I can ask: which of the paper's five strategy families best preserves MGWR coefficient surfaces when data from new regions arrive? This reframes my "retrain vs. transfer" dilemma as a benchmark with a known answer — functional regularization or replay — rather than ad hoc practice.

④ Build a mixed-scenario benchmark for urban change monitoring with temporal and spatial context shifts

Extending this paperThe paper introduces but does not empirically evaluate mixed/hybrid scenarios where multiple context dimensions shift simultaneously. A key extension: construct a benchmark where both the spatial context (city) and the temporal context (year) change, requiring a model to solve a combination of domain-IL and class-IL at once. The paper's probabilistic formulation (multidimensional context space C) already supports this, but no experiments are provided. This would establish whether the strategy rankings (replay first, regularization last) hold for multi-dimensional non-stationarity.

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and mobile-phone trajectory analyses deal with simultaneous shifts in space and time — exactly the mixed-scenario setting. The paper's framework gives me a principled way to decompose that non-stationarity: which axis is domain-IL (same spatial outcome, changing input distribution over time) and which is task-IL (different city, different task)? This decomposition guides model design and replay strategy selection for my urban change monitoring pipelines.

⑤ Use the paper's "Separate Networks" baseline as the upper-bound design for privacy-preserving federated GeoAI

Extending this paperThe paper's strongest baseline for task-IL is Separate Networks — one sub-network per context — which outperforms almost all anti-forgetting methods at equivalent parameter budgets. This is structurally identical to federated learning with non-IID client data, where each client trains on its own context. Extending the paper: recast Separate Networks as a federated baseline and measure how much knowledge sharing (via aggregated parameters or a shared backbone) is needed before a federated algorithm beats the isolated-network lower bound in all three scenarios.

Added value to my researchMulti-source data fusion (mobile phone, social media, remote sensing) across cities is the core data challenge in my research. If separate city models are already competitive (as this paper implies), then my federated GeoAI approach must demonstrate concrete positive transfer — not just privacy preservation — to justify the communication overhead. The paper provides the exact benchmark protocol (per-scenario accuracy vs. Separate Networks) to make that case rigorously.
