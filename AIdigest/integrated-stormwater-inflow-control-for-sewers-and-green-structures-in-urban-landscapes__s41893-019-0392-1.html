Integrated stormwater inflow control for sewers and green structures in urban landscapes

Nadia Schou Vorndran Lund · Morten Borup · Henrik Madsen · Ole Mark · Karsten Arnbjerg-Nielsen · Peter Steen Mikkelsen Published 2019-10-14 DOI 10.1038/s41893-019-0392-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

By introducing integrated stormwater inflow control — dynamically deciding whether runoff enters the underground sewer or is routed across cloudburst roads and stored in green retention spaces — the paper shows that combined sewer overflows (CSOs) can be eliminated for small events and reduced by an average of 12.4% across all events in a Copenhagen case study (using only 7% of the catchment as controllable area). Synergy between sewers, green infrastructure, and the urban landscape — orchestrated by model predictive control (MPC) — outperforms isolated control approaches and offers a more environmentally sustainable and cost-effective path toward resilient, liveable cities than traditional grey-infrastructure expansion.

Why It Matters

  • Unlocks hidden capacity in existing infrastructure. Most cities cannot afford full sewer separation or new underground basins; this framework exploits already-planned cloudburst roads and parks as dynamically controllable storage, achieving large CSO reductions with marginal additional cost.
  • Bridges the smart-city and urban-water worlds. By applying MPC — standard in smart energy and transport — to the jointly controlled urban landscape + sewer system, the paper opens a new class of cyber–physical water management that goes beyond single-element green infrastructure control.
  • Quantifies trade-offs transparently. The control framework forces explicit stakeholder negotiation over competing uses of streets and parks (traffic vs. stormwater storage vs. recreation), turning a previously ignored conflict into a negotiable, algorithmically encoded trade-off.
  • Scales to city-wide ambition. A proof-of-concept controlling only 7% of a Copenhagen catchment achieves 98.4% of the theoretical maximum CSO reduction for that sub-area; extrapolating to full city coverage is the direct next step for Copenhagen's 300-project cloudburst programme.
  • Advances climate-change adaptation globally. CSO frequency is expected to increase under climate change; this framework offers a practically deployable, lower-cost alternative to the grey infrastructure expansions currently absorbing 11–18% of megacity adaptation budgets.

Core Methods

  • Model Predictive Control (MPC): real-time optimization over a 2-hour prediction horizon with a 5-minute receding-horizon re-optimization cycle; objective function minimizes CSO volume. Actuators include stormwater inlets (open/close) and a pump from the retention space back to the sewer.
  • 1D hydrodynamic simulation: detailed sewer + terrain model used to validate MPC set-points and emulate a real operational system; initialized from MPC model state at each step.
  • Proof-of-concept scenario comparison: three scenarios — (1) no control / baseline, (2) integrated stormwater inflow control via MPC, (3) maximum CSO reduction (all controllable runoff kept on terrain) — tested across 32 rain events from 2016.
  • Performance indicator: CSO volume per event; average CSO reduction and fraction of maximum achievable reduction used as summary metrics.
  • Conceptual scaling analysis: theoretical curves (Fig. 2) show how CSO reduction scales with controllable area fraction and rain event size, framing the generalization of the result beyond the Copenhagen case.

① Replace the paper's fixed MPC model with a data-driven surrogate learned from sensor streams

Extending this paperThe paper's MPC relies on a hand-calibrated 1D hydrodynamic internal model. In cities without detailed sewer models, or when rapid model degradation occurs after pipe renovations, this is a hard barrier to deployment. A direct extension: replace the internal MPC model with a spatiotemporal graph neural network surrogate trained on in-situ sensor readings (flow meters, water levels, rain gauges), so the MPC can self-update from streaming observations. The paper already acknowledges that suboptimal MPC performance in 1/32 events stems from model mismatch; a learned surrogate would close that gap and generalize across cities without individual calibration.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly onto my physics-informed graph learning agenda: the sewer network is a natural directed graph, flow conservation at nodes is a hard physical constraint, and real-time sensor feeds are the training signal. Building a GNN surrogate for urban drainage would be a transferable GeoAI module — analogous to the spatiotemporal kernel density and network community detection I already apply to mobility data — but now operating on water-flow graphs.

② Extend from CSO minimization to multi-objective optimization: water quality + flood risk + urban heat

Extending this paperThe paper's MPC objective function contains only CSO volume minimization. Yet the retention space (Damhusengen park) simultaneously affects urban heat island intensity and ecological habitat; retaining water longer enhances cooling but risks mosquito breeding. A direct paper extension: embed a multi-objective MPC that jointly optimizes CSO reduction, surface-water temperature, urban cooling benefit, and public safety (water depth/velocity on streets), and map the Pareto frontier across these objectives. The paper calls this out as a challenge ("balancing contrasting usages") but leaves it unsolved.

Added value to my researchThis echoes my work on EV-charging efficiency–equity optimization: a multi-objective spatial allocation problem where infrastructure serves multiple constituencies simultaneously. The same Pareto-surface and explainable ML approach (SHAP decomposition of which spatial features drive objective trade-offs) could be ported here — turning the paper's qualitative "stakeholder negotiation" into a quantifiable, spatially mapped trade-off surface.

③ Scale from one Copenhagen sub-catchment to city-wide spatial allocation of controllable inlets

Extending this paperThe proof-of-concept controls 7% of a single 1.5 km² catchment. Copenhagen has 300 planned cloudburst projects; the paper implies city-wide integration is the logical next step but does not model it. Extending it: formulate the problem of which inlets to instrument and control, and in what sequence, as a spatial facility-location problem — given a fixed sensor/actuator budget, find the set of controllable sub-catchments that maximizes city-wide CSO reduction. This would turn the paper's single case study into a city-scale spatial optimization framework.

Added value to my researchThis is squarely a spatial accessibility and facility-location problem: the "facilities" are controllable stormwater inlets, and the "demand" is CSO risk distributed across the city. My 2SFCA/R2SFCA service-area delineation and supply–demand accessibility metrics can be adapted here to measure hydraulic service coverage — with each inlet serving a catchment polygon rather than a population centroid, but the spatial optimization logic is identical.

④ Incorporate equity: who bears the burden of deliberately flooded streets and parks?

Extending this paperThe paper acknowledges that deliberately routing stormwater across streets and parks creates winners (water quality, downstream flooding relief) and losers (pedestrians, cyclists, low-mobility residents disrupted by temporary inundation). However, no spatial analysis of who is most affected is provided. Extending it: overlay the controllable-area map with socio-demographic data (income, age, disability) and compute a burden–benefit spatial equity index — ensuring that the MPC does not systematically impose flooding costs on vulnerable neighbourhoods while channeling environmental benefits elsewhere. This is particularly relevant as the paper proposes scaling to all 300 Copenhagen projects.

⑤ Couple stormwater inflow control with urban heat mitigation using green-roof data

Extending this paperThe paper notes that green infrastructure reduces urban heat island effect as a co-benefit, but the control framework does not exploit this link. If green roofs and permeable surfaces are included as MPC-controlled elements — their moisture state monitored and infiltration capacity updated in real time — the same optimization horizon that prevents CSO could also pre-wet surfaces before heat events for evaporative cooling. This would merge the paper's stormwater inflow control with green-roof energy-saving dynamics, creating a dual-purpose climate adaptation controller.
