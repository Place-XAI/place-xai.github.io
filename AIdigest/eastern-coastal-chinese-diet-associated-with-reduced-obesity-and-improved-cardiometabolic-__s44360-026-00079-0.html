Eastern coastal Chinese diet associated with reduced obesity and improved cardiometabolic health

Yuwei Shi · Juntao Kan · Yuji Yu · Xiaoguang Yang · Wei He · Changzheng Yuan · Xinmei Li · Qiaoyu Wu · Yufan Hao · Ying Jiang · Ningling Wang · Wenjie Wang · Emma Yunzhi Huang · Fei Yang · Joyce Wu · April Myers · Ann W. Hsing · Jun Du · Yuexin Yang · Shankuan Zhu Published 2026-03-02 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00079-0 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using unsupervised k-means clustering on dietary data from 8,931 participants in the WELL-China cohort, this study identifies the "EastDiet" — a culturally embedded healthful dietary pattern adopted by 46.8% of eastern coastal Chinese. Adherence to the EastDiet is associated with 17% lower odds of central obesity (OR 0.83) and a 22% lower risk of major adverse cardiovascular events (HR 0.78), with multiomics profiling revealing favourable metabolomic and gut microbial signatures that may mediate these protective effects. The pattern was replicated in an independent cohort of 1,851 participants, establishing it as an empirically grounded, culturally relevant dietary reference for China.

Why It Matters

  • First data-driven identification of a Chinese regional dietary pattern. Prior work relied on prescriptive guideline indices; this is the first unsupervised, population-based derivation of the eastern coastal Chinese diet, capturing its authentic food combinations rather than assumptions.
  • Provides the Chinese population with a culturally grounded dietary framework. Unlike the Mediterranean or DASH diets derived from Western populations, the EastDiet integrates freshwater fish, soy products, edible fungi, and starchy tubers — foods central to Chinese culinary traditions and widely accessible in the region.
  • Multiomics evidence links diet to mechanism. By integrating metabolomics (793 metabolites, 8,844 participants) and 16S rRNA gut microbiota profiling (6,260 participants), the study goes beyond association to suggest plausible biological pathways — particularly the gut microbiota–metabolite network and indole-3-propionic acid (IPA) as a key mediator.
  • Addresses a high-burden public health gap. Dietary risks are the leading contributor to cardiovascular disease burden in China; an evidence-based, locally relevant dietary pattern has immediate policy relevance for one of the world's largest populations.
  • Cross-cohort replication strengthens validity. Results are replicated in the ABO cohort (1,851 participants from Shanghai and Hangzhou), an independent nationwide multicentre study, providing a degree of external validity rare in single-cohort dietary studies.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Discovery cohort
WELL-China cohort — 10,268 recruited, 8,931 eligible participants aged 18–80 years from three districts in Hangzhou, Zhejiang Province, China (Shangcheng, Xihu, Gongshu Districts). Recruited 2016–2019. Prospective follow-up to 1 July 2024 (median 6.3 years); 456 incident MACE cases recorded.
Validation cohort
Achieving Better Omics (ABO) cohort — nationwide multicentre (8 Chinese cities), recruited March 2024–April 2025. Subset of 2,749 from eastern coastal cities (Shanghai and Hangzhou); 1,851 eligible after exclusions. Cross-sectional.
Dietary assessment
Validated semiquantitative Food Frequency Questionnaire (FFQ) comprising 26 commonly consumed food items, consolidated into 22 food categories for clustering. Validated against a 146-FFQ and 3-day dietary records in the same population. 12-month recall window.
Metabolomics
Plasma untargeted metabolomics in 8,844 WELL-China participants. Platform: Vanquish Flex UPLC system + Orbitrap Exploris 120 mass spectrometer; ACQUITY UPLC T3 column. 794 metabolites retained after QC filtering; 793 analyzed. Positive and negative ion modes. Processed with XCMS in R.
Gut microbiota
16S rRNA V3–V4 region sequencing in 6,260 WELL-China participants. Platform: Illumina HiSeq PE-250. Taxonomic profiling via QIIME2 + DADA2 pipeline; annotated against Silva_132 99% OTUs. 134 genera retained after filtering.
Body composition
Dual energy X-ray absorptiometry (DXA) for fat distribution (trunk, android, gynoid, leg fat percentages) in 8,780 discovery cohort participants and 821 validation participants.
Genomics data access
16S rRNA gene sequencing data deposited in Genome Sequence Archive (GSA) at ngdc.cncb.ac.cn/gsa/, accession number CRA037609.

Analysis code publicly available at GitHub.

Core Methods

  • Unsupervised k-means clustering: applied to 22 standardized food group energy-contribution variables. Optimal cluster number (k=2) determined by silhouette coefficient and Calinski–Harabasz index via the NbClust R package. Applied independently to both discovery and validation cohorts.
  • Prospective Cox proportional hazards models: multivariable-adjusted (age, sex, residential area, BMI, education, smoking, physical activity, total energy, nutritional supplement use, history of diabetes/hypertension, family history of CVD) to estimate HR of incident MACE by dietary cluster. Proportional hazard assumption verified by Schoenfeld residuals. Median follow-up 6.3 years.
  • Logistic and linear regression: cross-sectional associations of dietary cluster with obesity (BMI ≥28 kg/m²), central obesity (WC ≥90 cm men / ≥85 cm women), and fat distribution indices (waist-to-hip ratio, waist-to-height ratio, DXA regional fat percentages).
  • Metabolome-wide association study (MWAS): multivariable linear regression per metabolite; Bonferroni correction for 793 tests; 96 significant metabolites identified. Partial Spearman correlation of metabolites with individual food groups (BH-corrected).
  • Gut microbiota analysis: Bray–Curtis dissimilarity + PERMANOVA (999 permutations) for beta-diversity; MaAsLin2 for multivariable-adjusted genus-level associations (BH-corrected); partial Spearman correlation of genera with food groups.
  • Gut microbiota–metabolite network: partial Spearman correlation network (|r|>0.3, BH-adjusted P<0.05) with 106 nodes and 831 edges. EastDiet-associated nodes traced to obesity and MACE associations via Cox and correlation models.
  • Stratification and sensitivity analyses: sex, age, education stratification; interaction term tests; MACE redefined excluding all-cause mortality; exclusion of early MACE cases (1- and 2-year lag).

① Map food environments spatially to explain EastDiet adherence geography

Extending this paperThe EastDiet was identified in Hangzhou and validated in Shanghai — both in the eastern coastal region — but the paper does not interrogate why adherence clusters spatially, nor whether within-city food environments drive differential access to EastDiet-compatible foods. A direct extension would overlay participants' residential locations with POI data (fresh markets, seafood stalls, tofu vendors, supermarkets) to model food-environment accessibility as a predictor of EastDiet adherence. This would test whether the EastDiet is itself a dietary consequence of a distinctive food access landscape — a unique causal pathway the paper's cohort design could support but does not yet exploit. The selenium-soil geography paper (s44360-026-00075-4) shows how geographic heterogeneity of nutritional exposures shapes metabolic disease risk across Chinese regions; the same spatial logic applies here to food availability.

Added value to my researchThis is a natural extension of my 2SFCA/R2SFCA accessibility framework beyond healthcare to food-system access: food store catchments, provider-to-population ratios, and spatial decay of fresh food availability. Integrating FFQ-derived adherence scores as the outcome variable in a geographically weighted regression (GWR/MGWR) model would yield spatially non-stationary estimates of how food environment accessibility drives dietary quality — connecting my spatial accessibility methods directly to public-health nutrition at the neighborhood scale.

Cross-ref (corpus): Dietary selenium from soil micronutrients and risk of incident type 2 diabetes — 10.1038/s44360-026-00075-4

② Use the multiomics network as a biological prior for explainable prediction of cardiometabolic risk

Extending this paperThe paper constructs a gut microbiota–metabolite correlation network (106 nodes, 831 edges) and identifies IPA as a key mediator linking the EastDiet to cardiometabolic protection. This network is currently used descriptively. Extending it: encode the network as a graph structure and train a graph neural network in which node features are metabolite/taxon abundances and edge weights are the partial Spearman correlations from the paper. Predicting MACE risk or central obesity as graph-level outputs would (1) test whether the biological network adds predictive power over diet alone, and (2) provide node-level attention scores that constitute mechanistic explainability. The metabolomics signatures paper (s44360-025-00023-8) shows that metabolomic indices of dietary carbohydrates predict type-2 diabetes independently, validating the principle of using omics signatures as predictive features.

Added value to my researchThis meshes with my physics-informed graph learning agenda: the EastDiet microbiota–metabolite network provides a biological-law constraint (dietary inputs → microbial community → metabolite outputs → health outcomes) that could be encoded as soft physics constraints in a GNN. The resulting model would be an explainable, biologically grounded cardiometabolic risk predictor — demonstrating how GeoAI interpretability methods transfer to biomedical graph settings, expanding my explainable ML portfolio beyond spatial to biological networks.

Cross-ref (corpus): Metabolomic signatures of dietary carbohydrates and differential association with type 2 diabetes — 10.1038/s44360-025-00023-8

③ Test whether urbanization erodes EastDiet adherence via microbiome Westernization

Extending this paperThe paper notes that the eastern coastal region's diet is influenced by internal migration, suggesting urban dietary change dynamics. But the study is cross-sectional on cohorts already urbanized. Extending it: compare EastDiet adherence and its gut microbiota signatures between rural-to-urban migrants and long-term urban residents in the same city — a natural experiment embedded in the ABO cohort's 8-city design (which the paper only partially exploits). The South Africa urbanization paper (s41467-024-46265-0) demonstrates precisely this design — diet transition from traditional high-fiber patterns to Western diets producing measurable microbiome and metabolome shifts linked to disease risk. Applying the same logic to internal Chinese migration would test whether the EastDiet's protective microbiome profile degrades with urbanization exposure time.

Added value to my researchUrban–rural health divides and the health consequences of urbanization trajectories are central to my urban health research. Mapping EastDiet erosion as a spatial process — using GPS trajectories or mobile-phone activity data to proxy urban integration — would connect my spatiotemporal data fusion methods (mobile phone records, POI data) to dietary health outcomes, extending the health geography dimension of my work from service accessibility to behavioral-dietary exposure.

Cross-ref (corpus): Diet changes due to urbanization in South Africa are linked to microbiome and metabolome signatures of Westernization and colorectal cancer — 10.1038/s41467-024-46265-0

④ Extend the diet–omics design to a multi-disease framework capturing Crohn's disease alongside cardiovascular risk

Extending this paperThe paper focuses on two outcomes (obesity and MACE) but constructs an extensive microbiota–metabolite network that is not interrogated for gastrointestinal disease. The EastDiet's high fiber, soy, and fungal content — and its enriched Lachnospiraceae genera — are mechanistically plausible protectors against inflammatory bowel disease. Extending it: add Crohn's disease or inflammatory bowel disease status as an outcome layer in the WELL-China cohort (ascertained via ICD-10 registry linkage, already in place for MACE), and cross-map the EastDiet–microbiota network against the diet-omics patterns identified in the SOURCE Crohn's cohort (s41467-024-48106-6). This would test whether the EastDiet's microbiome profile generalizes protective benefits across cardiometabolic and inflammatory-bowel axes.

Added value to my researchMulti-disease, multi-outcome spatial health analysis is an under-explored dimension of health geography. From a GeoAI perspective, building a multi-label spatial risk model — where a single dietary exposure (EastDiet adherence, spatialized by residence) simultaneously predicts cardiovascular and gastrointestinal disease risk across geographic strata — would demonstrate how spatiotemporal kernel density estimation of exposure surfaces generalizes to multi-endpoint health outcome modeling, a methodological contribution to my health analytics toolkit.

Cross-ref (corpus): Diet-omics in the Study of Urban and Rural Crohn disease Evolution (SOURCE) cohort — 10.1038/s41467-024-48106-6

⑤ Embed the EastDiet score as a modifiable-diet equity indicator in a healthy-city dashboard

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that EastDiet adherence is patterned by sex, age, and education — suggesting it is not uniformly accessible across social strata. A policy-facing extension would compute a community-level EastDiet adherence score aggregated from cohort or surveillance data, overlay it on the spatial distribution of food retailers and socioeconomic indicators, and generate maps of dietary equity gaps. This transforms the paper's individual-level dietary pattern into a neighborhood-level public health indicator with a spatial dimension the paper itself does not address. The gut microbiome–urbanization work (s41467-021-25213-2) similarly used a population-aggregate lens to connect dietary and lifestyle factors to immune-metabolic profiles across urban–rural gradients in African populations.

Added value to my researchThis connects directly to my healthy-cities and 15-minute-neighborhood research: access to EastDiet-compatible foods (fresh seafood, vegetables, soy, fungi) can be operationalized as a food-accessibility dimension of neighborhood healthiness, analogous to how I operationalize access to healthcare facilities or green space. Spatial entropy measures could quantify the diversity of food-store types within walkable catchments, while MGWR could estimate how the association between food-environment accessibility and dietary quality varies across the urban–rural gradient — producing spatially explicit, actionable diet-equity policy targets for Chinese cities.

Cross-ref (corpus): Gut microbiome-mediated metabolism effects on immunity in rural and urban African populations — 10.1038/s41467-021-25213-2
