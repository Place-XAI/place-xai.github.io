A strong but uneven increase in urban tree cover in China over the recent decade

Xiaoxin Zhang · Martin Brandt · Xiaoye Tong · Xiaowei Tong · Wenmin Zhang · Florian Reiner · Sizhuo Li · Feng Tian · Yuemin Yue · Weiqi Zhou · Bin Chen · Xiangming Xiao · Rasmus Fensholt Published 2025-04-22 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00227-9 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Using high-resolution nanosatellite imagery and a deep-learning canopy detector, the study maps urban tree cover across all major Chinese cities: 11.47% of urban area was tree-covered in 2019, and 76% of cities gained tree cover since 2010. The increase was strong but uneven — mega-cities (Shanghai, Beijing, Shenzhen, Guangzhou, +6.64%) and public-service (+3.09%) and residential (+1.79%) land uses benefited most, so national greening policy works overall but does not benefit all cities or land uses equally.

Why It Matters

  • Tree-level monitoring at national scale. National urban-tree dynamics were previously hard to track; mapping at sub-meter-to-meter detail with PlanetScope/RapidEye turns vague greening rhetoric into a quantified, city-by-city ledger of canopy change.
  • Connects policy to measurable outcome. China's decade of urban-greening campaigns now has an independent, data-driven verdict — most cities did green, validating that public investment moved canopy.
  • Surfaces a green-equity problem. Gains concentrate in mega-cities and in public-service/residential land, while other cities and land-use types lag — directly relevant to environmental-justice and ecosystem-service distribution debates.
  • A transferable measurement template. The U-Net canopy framework plus open land-use, nighttime-light and population layers is a reusable recipe for auditing urban greening anywhere with commercial high-resolution imagery.

Core Methods

  • Deep-learning canopy detection: a U-Net semantic-segmentation framework predicts tree-canopy cover from NIR/G/B nanosatellite imagery, applied consistently to 2010 (RapidEye) and 2019 (PlanetScope).
  • Change detection: canopy is differenced on 1-ha grids to map increases/decreases between 2010 and 2019, with unchanged areas excluded for clarity.
  • Validation: predictions compared against 185 manually labeled 1-ha patches and against independent products (MOD44B, ESA WorldCover, Esri land cover) for cross-consistency.
  • Land-use attribution: canopy change intersected with EULUC-China categories to attribute greening to public-service, residential, and other land uses.
  • Driver analysis: canopy and its change related to population density, GDP, nighttime lights, climate (temperature/precipitation) and city size to explain the uneven pattern.
