Outsourced carbon mitigation efforts of Chinese cities from 2012 to 2017

Chengqi Xia · Heran Zheng · Jing Meng · Yuli Shan · Xi Liang · Jin Li · Zihua Yin · Minggu Chen · Pengfei Du · Can Wang Published 2024-06-27 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00088-8 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Many Chinese cities are "outsourced mitigation beneficiaries": they cut their carbon footprints mostly through the mitigation efforts of upstream supply-chain cities rather than their own. Using a city-level multiregional input–output (MRIO) model for 309 cities, the share of outsourced emissions rose from 78.6% (2012) to 81.9% (2017), and 240 cities (77.7%) were beneficiaries — 65 "strong" (local emissions still grew) and 175 "weak". This free-riding masks true mitigation contributions and calls for supply-chain-aware, differentiated city policies and carbon-compensation mechanisms.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reframes who deserves climate credit. Conventional production- or even consumption-based accounting hides that many cities' apparent decarbonization is borrowed from upstream cities — distorting mitigation responsibility and recognition.
  • Operationalizes "free riding" at city scale. It gives the first systematic city-level definition and metric of outsourced mitigation, classifying every city into one of five roles (bad performers, strong/weak beneficiaries, role models, hard workers).
  • Targets policy by city type. Strong beneficiaries (industrializing agriculture/light-industry cities) vs. weak beneficiaries (high-tech, service-oriented downstream cities) need fundamentally different interventions — subsidies and capacity-transfer vs. innovation investment.
  • Speaks directly to China's carbon-neutrality pathway. With 80%+ of city emissions now embodied in cross-city supply chains, coordinated regional collaboration — not isolated city targets — becomes the lever for equitable decarbonization.

Core Methods

  • Environmentally extended MRIO + Leontief inverse: carbon footprint CF = ELY with L = (I − A)⁻¹, tracing CO₂ embodied in goods/services for each city's final demand across the supply chain.
  • City-level MRIO construction: nonsurvey method linking city SRIO tables via maximum-entropy (self/external supply split) and cross-entropy estimation of intercity trade flows, nested into provincial MRIO.
  • Structural decomposition analysis (SDA): change in footprint split into carbon intensity, production structure and final demand using the average of two polar decompositions, each further split into local vs. outsourced contributions.
  • Mitigation-effort normalization: technological-progress effect (intensity + structure) divided by average final demand (emissions per final demand) to remove the demand "amplifier" and fairly compare local vs. outsourced effort.
  • Five-role quadrant typology: cities classified by local vs. outsourced mitigation effort into bad performers, strong/weak beneficiaries, role models and hard workers.
  • K-means city typing: cities grouped into agriculture, light-industry, heavy-industry, energy and high-tech types from 2017 sectoral value-added shares.

① Turn the outsourced-mitigation flows into an explicit city interaction network

Extending this paperThe paper computes upstream→downstream mitigation contributions but reports them mostly as city-type averages and a static quadrant. The next step is to materialize the full intercity outsourced-mitigation graph (weighted, directed edges = Mt of borrowed mitigation) and apply network community detection to discover supply-chain "mitigation basins," then track how free-riding clusters reorganize over time and which hub cities anchor them.

② Model strategic interactions: who free-rides on whom, and why

Extending this paperThe paper labels cities as beneficiaries but treats their behavior as an accounting outcome, not a choice. Extending it with agent-based modeling / evolutionary game dynamics — where cities decide between local abatement and trading for cleaner upstream goods under peer and policy pressure — would let the static 2012→2017 typology become a forward-simulatable system for testing carbon-compensation rules.

③ Explainable ML for the drivers of beneficiary status

Extending this paperThe paper attributes city roles qualitatively (industrial stage, GDP, sector mix). ④ Fuse multi-source/remote-sensing data to push past the 2017 data wall

Extending this paperThe authors explicitly stop at 2017 because city MRIO and yearbooks are unavailable afterward. The framework could be extended by nowcasting carbon footprints from openly available proxies — nighttime lights, satellite-observed industrial activity, mobile-phone/POI activity — to interpolate intercity flows and update beneficiary roles for recent years without waiting for official IO tables.

Extending it into a spatial optimization that allocates compensation/transfer budgets to maximize total mitigation subject to inter-city fairness constraints — and stress-tests the result against supply-chain disruption — would turn the diagnostic into an actionable policy tool.
