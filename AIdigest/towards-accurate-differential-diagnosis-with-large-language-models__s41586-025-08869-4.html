Towards accurate differential diagnosis with large language models

Daniel McDuff · Mike Schaekermann · Tao Tu · Anil Palepu · Amy Wang · Jake Garrison · Karan Singhal · Yash Sharma · Shekoofeh Azizi · Kavita Kulkarni · Le Hou · Yong Cheng · Yun Liu · S. Core Conclusion

AMIE, a large language model fine-tuned for clinical diagnostic reasoning, outperformed unassisted board-certified physicians on 302 challenging NEJM case reports (top-10 accuracy 59.1% vs. 33.6%, P = 0.04). When used as an assistive tool, AMIE significantly improved the quality, comprehensiveness, and accuracy of clinicians' differential diagnosis lists compared to standard search tools (top-10 accuracy 51.7% vs. 44.4%, P = 0.03), without adding task time — suggesting strong potential to upskill clinicians and widen access to specialist-level reasoning in resource-limited settings.

★

Why It Matters

  • First rigorous randomized evaluation of an LLM as a DDx assistant. Prior work measured standalone LLM diagnostic accuracy; this study uses a two-stage, counterbalanced reader study to isolate the assistive benefit on real clinicians working real cases.
  • Clinically meaningful accuracy gains without efficiency costs. AMIE-assisted clinicians spent the same time (~7.3 min) as search-assisted clinicians yet produced substantially more accurate and comprehensive differential lists — no productivity penalty.
  • Upskilling potential for low-resource and training settings. The model raised the performance of generalist internists on rare-disease puzzle cases, pointing toward use in under-resourced healthcare systems and medical education.
  • Quantifies the human-AI gap honestly. The study shows that standalone AMIE still outperforms AMIE-assisted clinicians, illuminating the roles of anchoring bias, trust calibration, and LLM suggestibility that must be addressed before full clinical deployment.
  • Establishes NEJM CPCs as a reproducible LLM benchmark. The automated evaluation metric (Cohen's κ = 0.631 vs. human raters) and comparison against GPT-4 seed a scalable benchmarking infrastructure for future clinical LLMs.

Core Methods

  • Model architecture: AMIE builds on PaLM 2 Large — a transformer LLM — fine-tuned with long-context capacity on a multi-task mixture of medical QA, dialogue, and EHR summarization; contamination experiments verify no CPC data leakage from pre-training.
  • Zero-shot prompting: AMIE is given standardized prompts requesting top-10 differential diagnoses with a minimum diversity constraint; clinicians interacted via a pre-populated chat interface (mean 2.92 questions per case, mean response 237.6 words).
  • Two-stage randomized reader study: Stage 1 — 20 generalist physicians generate unassisted DDx then re-do with either Search (Condition I) or AMIE (Condition II), cases assigned via 10 experience-matched clinician pairs. Stage 2 — 19 specialists blind-evaluate all DDx lists with full case access.
  • Multi-dimensional DDx evaluation: (1) top-n accuracy (n = 1–10); (2) Bond et al. quality score (5-point); (3) appropriateness score (5-point Likert); (4) comprehensiveness score (4-point). All scored by blinded specialists.
  • Automated metric: Med-PaLM 2 judges diagnosis-level correctness; Cohen's κ = 0.631 vs. human raters (substantial agreement), enabling 10-trial repeated evaluation and cross-model comparison.
  • Statistical tests: McNemar's test for paired proportions (quality/comprehensiveness); paired t-test and Wilcoxon signed-rank test for continuous scores (appropriateness).
  • Qualitative component: semi-structured interviews with 5 clinicians; inductive thematic analysis with peer debriefing on anchoring bias, LLM suggestibility, and trust calibration themes.

① Spatial equity of LLM-assisted diagnosis: mapping access to AI expertise

Extending this paperAMIE is evaluated on a homogeneous pool of 20 U.S. board-certified internists with mean 11.5 years of experience. The paper itself speculates that assistive benefits may be largest for less-trained providers in resource-limited or LMIC settings, but this is never measured. A direct extension: recruit or simulate clinicians from rural, low-resource, or lower-middle-income contexts and map where the diagnostic accuracy gain from AMIE is largest — producing a geographic equity profile of assistive LLM benefit, using the same 302-case framework as the scaffold.

Added value to my researchThis maps directly onto my health geography and accessibility agenda. AMIE can be conceptualized as a virtual specialist: the accessibility question becomes whether a clinician in a remote setting gains specialist-level DDx capability via an LLM interface — extending my 2SFCA/R2SFCA frameworks into a novel digital supply-of-expertise dimension. Combining physical clinic access metrics with LLM performance data would yield a two-layer health equity map linking spatial proximity to facilities and AI-assisted diagnostic quality.

② Hallucination detection as a clinical safety layer for AMIE's DDx outputs

Extending this paperThe paper's most critical open problem — explicitly named by participating clinicians — is LLM confabulation: convincingly wrong suggestions that could mislead rather than assist. Human specialist review is the current safety backstop, which is not scalable. A direct extension: integrate a semantic entropy-based hallucination detector applied per diagnosis item in the DDx list, automatically flagging high-uncertainty suggestions before the clinician sees them. This would convert the paper's qualitative safety concern into a quantitative, deployable signal and also tighten the automated evaluation metric beyond its current Cohen's κ = 0.631.

Added value to my researchIn my data-science and reproducibility work, uncertainty quantification is central to trustworthy explainable ML. Semantic entropy applied to geospatial LLM outputs — place-name disambiguation, POI categorization, spatial query answering — provides the same safety layer for GeoAI pipelines that ingest noisy open-data sources. A shared uncertainty-aware inference module would serve both the clinical DDx setting and geospatial foundation model fine-tuning, and would directly address FAIR data quality in multi-source data fusion pipelines.

Cross-ref (corpus): Detecting hallucinations in large language models using semantic entropy — 10.1038/s41586-024-07421-0

③ From assistive chat to agentic diagnostic pathways: multi-step autonomous reasoning

Extending this paperAMIE is deployed in a few-turn chat interface: the clinician asks a question, AMIE answers, and the DDx is updated. The paper acknowledges that real clinical DDx formation is a multi-step, goal-directed process — history taking, targeted test ordering, iterative hypothesis revision. The natural next step for this paper: an autonomous medical AI agent that plans its own information-acquisition trajectory, decides which lab values or imaging findings to "order," and updates its DDx list accordingly — transforming the paper's static evaluation into a sequential decision-making benchmark with a full agentic loop.

Added value to my researchAgentic planning architectures directly inform my physics-informed graph learning work: the clinical diagnostic agent's multi-step query strategy is structurally analogous to an agent navigating a spatiotemporal graph to resolve spatial uncertainty (e.g., iteratively querying mobile-phone traces to localize an epidemic source under a SIR model). Borrowing AMIE's human-AI interaction design patterns — pre-populated context, suggested initial prompts, free-form follow-up — would accelerate the interface design of my own multi-agent spatiotemporal GeoAI frameworks.

Cross-ref (corpus): Towards autonomous medical artificial intelligence agents — 10.1038/s41586-026-10675-5

④ Reliability and calibration of medical LLMs as a function of case rarity

Extending this paperThe NEJM CPCs are by design rare, "puzzle" cases — the paper deliberately targets hard cases where assistive LLMs have the most room to add value. A critical extension: systematically measure how AMIE's accuracy and confidence calibration degrade as cases become more common (low rarity) or radically out-of-distribution relative to the fine-tuning data — mapping performance vs. case rarity using ICD prevalence as an axis. The paper's per-specialty Table 2 is the entry point; adding a disease-rarity dimension reveals where foundation model reliability breaks down and where human oversight must remain primary.

Added value to my researchThis reliability-vs-rarity framework transfers directly to my explainable ML for spatial prediction work: spatial ensemble models suffer performance degradation in low-data-density regions (analogous to rare diseases). A shared evaluation framework — calibration error as a function of local training-data scarcity — would unify evaluation across clinical LLMs and geospatial ML models, informing where and how human oversight or Monte Carlo uncertainty estimates should be prioritized in GeoAI pipelines.

⑤ LLM-powered chatbots for primary-care triage as a scalable digital front door

Extending this paperAMIE is evaluated exclusively with trained physicians as end users. The paper speculates that its assistive benefit may extend to less-trained providers and education, but does not test this. A direct extension: adapt AMIE's interface and DDx generation pipeline for patient-facing or community health worker triage — where the "clinician" is a nurse practitioner or community health worker — and rigorously evaluate whether AMIE-generated DDx suggestions reduce unnecessary emergency escalation, improve triage accuracy, and are safe for non-specialist use at the primary-care front door.

Added value to my researchMy telehealth and digital-divide research examines who cannot access primary care due to geographic or socio-demographic barriers. An LLM-powered primary-care chatbot is precisely the digital supply side of the healthcare accessibility equation. Combining my 2SFCA spatial-access metrics with uptake and accuracy data from a community-codesigned LLM chatbot would produce a hybrid physical-digital accessibility model — quantifying whether AI tools genuinely close or instead reproduce and spatially re-encode existing health inequities.
