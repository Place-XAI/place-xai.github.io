Foundation model of neural activity predicts response to new stimulus types

Eric Y. Wang · Paul G. Fahey · Zhuokun Ding · Stelios Papadopoulos · Kayla Ponder · Marissa A. Weis · Andersen Chang · Taliah Muhammad · Saumil Patel · Zhiwei Ding · Dat Tran · Jiakun Fu · Casey M. Core Conclusion

A foundation model trained on ~135,000 neurons across 14 mice and 6 visual cortical areas accurately predicts visual cortex responses to arbitrary natural videos and generalizes, with minimal fine-tuning, to new mice and entirely new stimulus domains (coherent motion, noise patterns, static images, Gabor patches). The model's readout weights also serve as a functional barcode that predicts anatomical cell types, dendritic morphology and synaptic connectivity in the MICrONS connectomics dataset — demonstrating that a single foundation model can bridge neural function and structure across biological scales.

★

Why It Matters

  • First foundation model of the brain's sensory cortex. The paradigm shift — from per-animal models trained from scratch to a shared core fine-tuned for each subject — mirrors what GPT-style pretraining did for NLP, now applied to neural circuits.
  • Radical gain in data efficiency. Foundation models require <30 min of natural video from a new mouse to exceed the accuracy of individual models that need >60 min, directly lowering the cost of in vivo neuroscience experiments.
  • Out-of-distribution generalization. Although trained only on natural videos, the model accurately predicts responses to Gabor patches, noise and dot kinematograms — stimuli from entirely different perceptual categories — without any domain-specific training data.
  • Structure–function integration. Readout feature weights predict anatomical cell types (balanced accuracy 32% vs. 9% chance) and visual areas (68% vs. 25% chance), unifying electrophysiology, two-photon imaging and nanoscale electron microscopy into one model.
  • Enables in silico experimentation at scale. Once trained, the model replaces costly in vivo experiments for parametric tuning analysis, freeing experimental time and enabling hypotheses impossible to test in live animals.

Core Data — Sources & Volume

Neural recordings
Two-photon (2P) calcium imaging of ~135,000 excitatory neurons across 14 awake, behaving mice (6 female, 8 male; age 2.2–4 months), expressing GCaMP6s. 6 visual cortical areas covered: V1, LM, AL, RL, AM and PM. Foundation cohort = 8 mice; 4 new mice for out-of-distribution testing; MICrONS mouse = 1 mouse (14 sequential scans).
Foundation training data
More than 900 minutes of natural video responses pooled from 8 mice and ~66,000 neurons spanning multiple cortical layers (L2/3, L4, L5) and areas. Recording sessions ranged from 33–76 min each.
Visual stimuli
Natural videos (Sports-1M dataset); natural images; drifting Gabor filters; flashing Gaussian dots; directional pink noise (Monet); random dot kinematograms. Out-of-distribution stimuli used for testing only — zero domain-specific training data.
MICrONS dataset
Functional recordings of >70,000 excitatory neurons in a 1 mm3 cortical volume (V1, LM, AL, RL); paired with serial electron microscopy of ~60,000 excitatory neurons and 500 million synapses. Available at bossdb.org/project/microns-minnie.
Anatomical labels
11 morphologically defined excitatory cell types (L2a–L5ET) from 16,561 unique EM-coregistered neurons (Schneider-Mizell et al., MICrONS consortium).
Code & model weights
Fully open: github.com/cajal/fnn (model weights); github.com/cajal/foundation (training pipeline); github.com/cajal/pipeline (recording pipeline).

Core Methods

  • Four-module ANN architecture: Perspective (ray-tracing eye-tracking correction), Modulation (LSTM over locomotion + pupil dilation), Core (3D Conv + ConvLSTM or CvT-LSTM feedforward-recurrent), and Readout (per-neuron linear combination at learned spatial position).
  • Foundation pretraining: a single shared core trained end-to-end on pooled data from 8 mice; then frozen and transferred to new subjects — only the perspective, modulation and readout modules are re-fitted per new mouse.
  • Transfer learning & data efficiency: foundation models with frozen core achieve median CCnorm > 0.65 on new mice with <30 min training data; individual models need >60 min to match.
  • Out-of-distribution generalization: model tested on 5 new stimulus domains (natural images, drifting Gabors, flashing Gaussian dots, directional pink noise, random dot kinematograms) with no domain-specific training data.
  • Parametric tuning in silico: OSI, DSI and SSI estimated from synthetic stimuli presented to the model; median in vivo vs. in silico preferred orientation error = 4° for neurons with OSI > 0.5.
  • Anatomical prediction: logistic regression on 512-dimensional readout feature weights predicts visual area (68% balanced accuracy) and cell type (32%) in MICrONS EM data.
  • Training objective: Poisson negative log-likelihood (outperforms MSE by 9.6%); 4-head ensemble; 200 epochs with cosine LR schedule and warm restart.

① Adapt the multi-subject foundation-core paradigm to geospatial sensor fusion across cities

Extending this paperThe paper's key architectural move is pooling data from 8 mice into a single frozen core that transfers to new subjects with minimal adaptation. The identical problem structure arises in geospatial AI: satellite imagery, mobile-phone signals and street-network graphs vary across cities (subjects) but share underlying spatial regularities. A direct extension: replace "mice" with "cities" and "neurons" with "spatial grid cells," training a foundation spatial core on diverse cities and freezing it before city-specific readout layers are fitted — mirroring the paper's foundation → readout paradigm for urban sensing.

Added value to my researchThis is a blueprint for fine-tuning geospatial foundation models in my research agenda. My work on multi-source data fusion (mobile phone, remote sensing, POIs) could adopt the frozen-core + lightweight-readout structure: pretrain on dense data cities (like New York or London) and transfer to data-sparse cities with only a small fine-tuning set, directly addressing the reproducibility gap in GeoAI applications to health geography.

② Leverage functional barcodes as a template for spatiotemporal embedding of health outcomes

Extending this paperThe paper produces a 512-dimensional readout feature vector per neuron — a "functional barcode" that encodes tuning to visual features and, without any anatomical supervision, predicts morphological cell type and synaptic connectivity. Extending this: apply the same readout-weight embedding strategy to population-level data where each "neuron" is a census tract, yielding a spatial barcode that encodes the tract's multi-modal exposure profile and can be queried to predict health outcomes not included in training (out-of-distribution generalization by analogy).

Added value to my researchMy spatiotemporal kernel density estimation and MGWR work could be replaced by a learned spatial embedding from a pretrained encoder: instead of specifying the kernel bandwidth by cross-validation, learn it from the data-rich cities and transfer the bandwidth field to data-poor regions. This is directly analogous to the paper's finding that orientation tuning can be recovered in silico with 4° median error — spatial heterogeneity could be similarly recovered without dense in situ surveys.

③ Extend the modulation module to capture socioeconomic and behavioral context in accessibility models

Extending this paperThe paper's modulation module encodes the mouse's internal state (arousal, locomotion) as a dynamic representation that gates the visual core. This is the clearest analogy to behavioral context in human mobility: accessibility is not only a function of the built environment but of who is traveling, under what socioeconomic conditions, and at what time of day. Extending the paper: replace pupil dilation and treadmill speed with income quintile, transit-mode availability and time-of-day, building a behavioral modulation module that modulates a spatial accessibility core accordingly.

Added value to my researchMy 2SFCA and R2SFCA service-area models treat demand as static. Incorporating a learned modulation module — conditioned on individual-level socioeconomic signals from mobile-phone records — would produce a demand-aware, dynamically adjusted accessibility score that captures temporal and equity dimensions simultaneously, directly extending my telehealth and digital divide research.

④ Use the out-of-distribution generalization result as a benchmark design principle for explainable spatial ML

Extending this paperThe paper's most striking result is that a model trained purely on natural videos generalizes to Gabor patches and dot kinematograms — qualitatively different stimulus classes. The mechanism is that the foundation core learns invariant nonlinear transformations from pixel space to cortical representation space. A methodological extension: use the paper's CCnorm evaluation framework as a cross-domain fidelity metric for geospatial models, measuring whether a model trained on one city's transport data produces valid predictions in cities with entirely different urban morphologies (the geospatial analog of stimulus-domain shift).

Added value to my researchMy explainable ML work (SHAP, MGWR, GWR ensembles) struggles with distribution shift across spatial contexts. Adopting the paper's evaluation protocol — training on one spatial domain and measuring calibrated predictive accuracy in held-out domains — would provide a rigorous framework for reporting spatial generalizability in my facility-location and health-access prediction work, strengthening reproducibility and open-science claims.

⑤ Bridge the paper's functional connectomics predictions to physics-informed graph learning over transport networks

Extending this paperThe paper demonstrates that its readout weights predict synaptic connectivity beyond what physical proximity of axons and dendrites explains — i.e., functional similarity is a better predictor of connection probability than spatial proximity alone. This principle — functional barcode beats proximity — maps directly onto urban network science: nodes in a city's transport or service-facility graph that share similar functional demand profiles should be more likely to co-activate or share users, regardless of geographic distance. Extending the paper's paradigm: encode each facility node's functional barcode (demand profile over time) and use it to predict co-usage edges in a mobility graph.

Added value to my researchMy physics-informed graph learning research aims to inject spatial conservation laws into GNN message-passing. The paper's functional barcode provides the ideal node feature initialization: instead of raw spatial coordinates, initialize each node with its demand-profile embedding and impose flow-conservation as a physics prior, unifying my facility-location optimization and graph-learning lines into a single framework. This has direct application to EV charging-station efficiency-vs-equity optimization where functional similarity across stations (usage pattern barcodes) should govern edge weights in the optimization graph.
