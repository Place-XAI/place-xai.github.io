Standardized heat islands and persistence drive modeled urban heat events

Weilin Liao · Linying Wang · Xiaoping Liu · Duo Chan · Dan Li Published 2025-08-01 DOI 10.1038/s44284-025-00290-2 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

The urban–rural gap in heat-event occurrence is not driven by the mean urban heat island (UHI) alone. Using simulations from two global Earth-system models, the authors show that up to 94% of urban–rural differences in hot-day occurrence are explained by the standardized mean UHI effect — the mean UHI normalized by local temperature variance. For multi-day heat events, temperature persistence (day-to-day autocorrelation) becomes a second decisive factor. Cities with a large standardized UHI and high persistence face disproportionately more extreme-heat exposure.

★

Why It Matters

  • Reframes how we attribute urban heat risk. It is not enough to know a city is warmer on average — what controls extra heat events is the mean UHI relative to how variable the local temperature is. Two cities with the same UHI can have very different heat-event burdens.
  • Adds the time dimension that single-day metrics miss. By isolating temperature persistence for multi-day events, the work explains why some cities suffer prolonged, dangerous heat spells that simple UHI maps cannot predict.
  • Gives a transparent, decomposable diagnostic. Mean, variance and persistence are separated into interpretable terms, so planners can see which statistical lever (cool surfaces vs. variability vs. stagnation) matters most for their city.
  • Targets adaptation where it counts. Cities with high standardized UHI and high persistence are flagged as priority hotspots for heat-mitigation investment.

Core Methods

  • Statistical decomposition of heat-event occurrence: express urban–rural differences in hot-day probability (δHDP) in terms of the temperature mean, variance and persistence, isolating the contribution of each.
  • Standardized mean UHI (δZ): normalize the mean UHI by local temperature standard deviation, producing a dimensionless driver that collapses much of the urban–rural hot-day signal onto a single relationship (Fig. 2).
  • Persistence modeling for multi-day events: introduce lag-1 autocorrelation (AC) assumptions to capture day-to-day temperature memory, then quantify how persistence amplifies multi-day heat-event differences (Fig. 3).
  • Predictive validation: evaluate prediction error (RMSE of predicted δHDP) across bins of urban–rural lag-1 AC difference (Fig. 4), showing where the persistence term is essential.
  • Two-model cross-check: repeat the analysis in CESM2 and GFDL to confirm the mean/variance/persistence framework is not model-specific.
