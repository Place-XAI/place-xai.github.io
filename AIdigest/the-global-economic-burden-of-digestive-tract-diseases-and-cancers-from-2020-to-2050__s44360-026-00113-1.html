The global economic burden of digestive tract diseases and cancers from 2020 to 2050

Xiaojie Huang · Qingwei Zhang · Haoliang Cui · Zeyu Li · Jianyi Zhang · Yiming Song · Jingjing Chen · Guifen Chen · Xiaolu Lin · Xiaobo Li · Wanyin Deng Published 2026-04-15 DOI 10.1038/s44360-026-00113-1 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Digestive tract diseases and neoplasms (DTDNs) will impose a US$26.1 trillion global economic burden from 2020 to 2050 — equivalent to a 0.65% annual GDP tax — across 190 countries. Cirrhosis and chronic liver diseases alone account for 30.2% of this burden. The burden is highly unequal: upper-middle-income countries bear 46.2% of consequences, disease patterns shift from infection-related to lifestyle-related as income rises, and physical capital losses dominate in wealthy nations while human capital losses dominate in poor ones. Targeted prevention strategies, particularly for liver diseases, could yield large economic returns globally.

Why It Matters

  • First comprehensive economic quantification of digestive disease burden. Prior work quantified disability-adjusted life years (DALYs) but rarely monetized them at macroeconomic scale; this study translates health loss into GDP-equivalent impact for all 190 countries through 2050.
  • Reveals the hidden dominance of liver disease. Cirrhosis and chronic liver diseases (30.2% share) substantially outweigh more commonly publicized digestive cancers — a surprise finding that redirects prevention priority.
  • Exposes income-stratified double burden. Low-income countries lose primarily human capital (workforce morbidity/mortality), while high-income countries lose physical capital through treatment costs — a distinction invisible to DALYs alone.
  • Shifts from infectious to lifestyle disease as income rises. The model captures a structural transition in the burden composition with economic development, providing a predictive signal for where to intervene as countries grow.
  • All data and code are open. The full modeling pipeline is released on GitHub (hxj0594/HAM_DTDNs), enabling replication and extension to other disease groups.

Core Methods

  • Health-augmented macroeconomic (HAM) model: a multi-channel macroeconomic framework that converts GBD-derived health burden into GDP impact by routing disease shocks through three parallel channels: (1) educational attainment / human capital accumulation, (2) workforce participation and labour productivity, and (3) treatment cost impacts on physical capital accumulation.
  • Counterfactual scenario design: economic burden is defined as the GDP gap between a status quo scenario (with DTDNs) and a disease-eradication counterfactual — a cleaner and more policy-relevant framing than pure cost-of-illness accounting.
  • Educational heterogeneity: Mincer-equation wage returns to schooling are interacted with disease-induced absenteeism and mortality to model how DTDNs differentially erode human capital across income groups with different education profiles.
  • Income-stratified analysis: all outputs are disaggregated by World Bank income group (low, lower-middle, upper-middle, high) and by the 16 individual DTDN conditions, enabling decomposition of the global total.
  • 30-year projection horizon: GBD prevalence and incidence are projected forward under current trends through 2050 and fed into the economic model annually, accumulating discounted burden in current international dollars.
  • Bootstrap uncertainty: confidence intervals propagate uncertainty from GBD estimates (95% uncertainty intervals) through the economic model using bootstrap resampling.

① Spatial disaggregation: from national averages to subnational health-economic maps

Extending this paperThe HAM model operates at the national level, masking enormous intra-country heterogeneity in both disease burden and economic exposure. A natural extension is to downscale the model to subnational administrative units (provinces, prefectures) using mobile phone data and gridded population products as spatial allocation weights, producing high-resolution maps of DTDN economic burden. This would directly address the paper's own acknowledgment that upper-middle-income countries' 46.2% aggregate share hides enormous within-country variation — especially within China and India.

Mobile phone data, which I already use for mobility-flow modelling, can allocate national disease burden estimates to grids — creating a spatial economic burden layer that I could overlay with 2SFCA-derived healthcare accessibility surfaces to identify areas where both access and burden are simultaneously worst. The resulting "double deprivation" index would be a novel spatial health equity metric.

② Embed income-inequality dynamics: how within-country inequality shapes the burden composition over time

Extending this paperThe HAM model uses average per-capita GDP to represent countries but ignores income distributions within countries. Yet the paper's own finding — that physical-capital losses dominate in high-income countries — implies that the wealthy subpopulation within any country drives the capital-loss channel while poor subpopulations drive the human-capital channel. Extending the model with Gini-indexed income distributions or quintile-level health expenditure would disaggregate the two channels, producing a within-country inequality-weighted burden estimate and revealing how domestic inequality amplifies or attenuates the aggregate GDP impact.

Added value to my researchThis connects to my interest in spatial entropy and inequality metrics. The decomposition framework (capital vs. human capital loss by income quintile) can be operationalized as a spatial entropy measure over income groups — a method I could apply to understand how health burden inequality tracks urban agglomeration and spatial sorting. It would also integrate naturally with my MGWR/GWR toolkit for spatially varying regressions.

③ Couple the HAM model to urbanization trajectories: projecting how city growth reshapes DTDN burden

Extending this paperThe paper projects burden under current disease trends but does not account for how rapid urbanization will alter the infection-to-lifestyle disease transition it documents. As countries urbanize, dietary transitions accelerate colorectal cancer risk while sanitation improvements reduce infection-driven cirrhosis — yet the HAM model treats these as exogenous GBD trends. Coupling the HAM model with shared socioeconomic pathway (SSP) urbanization projections would allow scenario testing of how different urbanization speeds change the DTDN burden composition through 2050, especially for the large lower-middle-income countries where both urbanization and disease transition are rapid.

Added value to my researchThis bridges the HAM model into my urban analytics domain. Urbanization rates are a spatial predictor I can extract from remote sensing time series and mobile phone data. Linking SSP-based urban expansion scenarios to disease-burden projections creates a spatially explicit health-economic forecast that complements my research on healthy cities and 15-minute neighborhoods — adding a long-run macroeconomic dimension to proximity-based accessibility analysis.

④ Apply explainable ML to decompose cross-country variation in DTDN economic burden

Extending this paperThe paper produces country-level burden estimates and groups them by income tier, but does not use a data-driven approach to identify which features (alcohol prevalence, urbanization rate, obesity prevalence, health system capacity, education level) best explain cross-country variance in DTDN burden. An XGBoost or random-forest model trained on these features, interpreted with SHAP values, would reveal non-linear interactions — for instance, whether alcohol and education together predict liver disease burden better than either alone, or whether health system capacity moderates the relationship between disease prevalence and economic cost.

⑤ Integrate healthcare accessibility barriers into the treatment-cost channel of the HAM model

Extending this paperThe HAM model assumes that treatment costs are incurred — but does not model whether populations can actually access treatment. In low-income countries, where the paper shows the treatment-cost channel is nearly zero (1% of burden), this may partly reflect the inability to access care rather than its absence. Extending the model with a treatment uptake function calibrated to geographic healthcare accessibility (facility density, travel time, out-of-pocket costs) would reveal the "missing burden" from unmet treatment need and better separate the human-capital loss from the capital-cost channel.

Added value to my researchThis is the most direct bridge between the HAM model and my 2SFCA/R2SFCA healthcare accessibility research. My R2SFCA-derived accessibility scores for digestive disease-relevant facilities (gastroenterology clinics, hepatology centers) could serve as the treatment uptake calibration surface. Regions with very low R2SFCA scores would effectively have a "treatment discount factor" close to zero, shifting their burden from capital losses toward pure human capital losses — revealing a spatial mechanism the current model treats as income-group invariant.
