Connectivity in urbanscapes can cause unintended flood impacts from stormwater systems

Vinh Ngoc Tran · Valeriy Y. Ivanov · Weichen Huang · Kevin Murphy · Fariborz Daneshvar · Jeff H. Bednar · G. Aaron Alexander · Jongho Kim · Daniel B. Wright Published 2024-08-27 DOI 10.1038/s44284-024-00116-7 Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Most urban flood-control solutions are local in design and ignore how built infrastructure connects floods across space and time. Using the 2014 Southeast Michigan flood as a testbed, the study shows that stormwater infrastructure which neglects flood spatial connectivity can be ineffective — even producing inundation in places that received no local rainfall. Different network configurations, including interfaces with natural channels, strongly change upstream surcharge, manhole overflow and inundation. Urban flood design and warning must therefore treat the watershed as an interconnected system, not a collection of independent cells.

Why It Matters

  • Reframes a hidden failure mode of flood engineering. It shows that "fixing" flooding locally can simply move water elsewhere via the pipe network, causing inundation downstream or upstream that no rain gauge would predict.
  • Connects gray infrastructure to emergent hazard. Manholes, outfalls and pipe layout are not passive conduits — their connectivity topology actively shapes where and how deep a city floods.
  • Challenges warning systems. Rainfall-triggered alerts miss connectivity-driven flooding; a neighborhood can flood with zero local rain, exposing a blind spot in current early-warning logic.
  • Grounded in a real disaster. The 11 August 2014 Warren, Michigan event — one of the costliest in the region — makes the connectivity argument concrete and policy-relevant for stormwater redesign.

Core Methods

  • Coupled surface–subsurface flood modeling: a high-resolution physics-based simulation jointly resolves overland inundation and the piped stormwater network for the STORM2014 event.
  • Connectivity scenario experiments: different network connection configurations — including interfaces between the stormwater system and natural channels — are systematically varied to isolate their effect on flooding.
  • Surcharge & manhole-overflow diagnostics: upstream surcharge, overflowing manholes and resulting inundation depth are tracked at outfalls and surcharged manholes (Fig. 3).
  • No-local-rain attribution: the model demonstrates inundation arising from network conveyance in the absence of local rainfall, isolating connectivity as the driver.
  • Inundation–area relationship: the simulated flood is summarized as a relationship between inundation depth and flooded area (Fig. 4), translating mechanism into a usable design metric.

① Turn the stormwater network into a physics-informed graph surrogate

Extending this paperThe paper's evidence comes from a single ~600 GB physics-based simulation of one event — accurate but far too costly to run across many cities or scenario sweeps. A direct extension: train a physics-informed graph neural surrogate on the pipes-manholes-outfalls topology (nodes = manholes/junctions, edges = pipes/channels) so the model learns the paper's surcharge-propagation dynamics and predicts connectivity-driven flooding in seconds, with mass/energy conservation imposed as soft constraints to keep the "no-local-rain" mechanism physical.
