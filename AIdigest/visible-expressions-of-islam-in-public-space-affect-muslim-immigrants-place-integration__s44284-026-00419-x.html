Visible expressions of Islam in public space affect Muslim immigrants' place integration

Pazit Ben-Nun Bloom · Amit Birenboim · Nonna Mayer · Gizem Arikan Published 2026-04-17 DOI 10.1038/s44284-026-00419-x Source nature.com ↗
Core Conclusion

Contrary to the common worry that visible Islam pushes Muslim immigrants to withdraw, in-situ exposure to embedded Islamic symbols increases approach-oriented behavior — measured from GPS tracks as slower walking pace, lower acceleration and lingering near the cue — and raises self-reported place attachment. Twin naturalistic augmented-reality field experiments in Paris (N=151) and London (N=147) show medium effect sizes (d ≈ 0.51–0.63, P<0.05), and parallel original surveys (N=403 + 400) confirm that everyday neighbourhood visibility of Islamic symbols is positively associated with place attachment. The pattern holds across two cities with opposite policy stances on religious visibility, suggesting the effect is not an artefact of a single regulatory context.

Why It Matters

  • Replaces opinion with causal evidence on a charged policy debate. Bans on minarets, headscarves and visible religious display are argued over with little rigorous data; this is a randomized, in-situ causal test of how such symbols actually shape immigrant behaviour and belonging.
  • Behaviour, not just attitudes. Place integration is operationalized as observable approach/avoidance movement captured by GPS (pace, acceleration, dwell), going beyond self-report and its social-desirability bias.
  • Two contrasting regimes, one result. Paris (assertive secularism / laïcité) and London (multicultural accommodation) yield the same direction of effect, strengthening external validity beyond a single national policy.
  • Method as transferable instrument. A purpose-built augmented-reality mobile app injects identical, randomly assigned visual cues onto identical real urban routes — a reusable design for studying person–environment effects in the wild.
  • Direct governance relevance. Findings speak to how cities regulate religious diversity in shared public space and to the integration consequences of visibility policies.

Core Methods

  • Randomized naturalistic field experiment: participants walk a fixed real-world route while an AR app overlays either Islamic symbols (treatment) or non-religious cues (control), with random assignment for causal identification.
  • GPS behavioral telemetry: approach-oriented behaviour quantified from movement traces — walking pace, acceleration, and dwell time near the cue — as objective proxies for the approach/avoidance system.
  • Place-integration construct: conceptualized as a proximal, cue-driven person–environment process with two facets — behavioral approach and attitudinal–perceptual place attachment/representation.
  • Effect estimation: treatment effects reported as Cohen's d with significance tests; balance tests and behavioral regression models in Supplementary.
  • Survey corroboration: cross-sectional surveys associate everyday neighbourhood symbol visibility with place attachment and (in London) social identification, triangulating the experimental result.
  • Cross-context replication: identical protocol run in Paris and London to test robustness across opposing religious-visibility policy regimes; pre-analysis plan with documented deviations.

① Scale the AR cue-injection design from a fixed route to a city-wide GPS panel

Extending this paperThe paper's evidence rests on two short identical routes with ~150 participants per city — a clean but spatially thin sample. A direct extension: turn the AR app into a longitudinal, city-wide passive-GPS panel where naturally occurring religious-visibility (mosques, signage, dress) is geocoded along participants' real daily trajectories, letting the approach/avoidance signal be estimated continuously across neighbourhoods rather than at one staged cue. Methods from countrywide natural-experiment designs on the built environment show how to scale from a route to a population while preserving causal leverage.

Added value to my researchThis is exactly the GPS-trajectory + behavioral-outcome pipeline I use; pairing cue exposure with dwell/pace gives me a transferable "micro-accessibility of belonging" measure that I can fold into spatiotemporal kernel-density and exposure models for health geography.

② Generalize the approach/avoidance metric into an objective intergroup-comfort sensor

Extending this paperThe paper innovates by reading walking pace, acceleration and dwell as approach vs. avoidance. That same behavioral grammar has been used to detect racial avoidance on streets. Extending this paper: build a unified, validated movement-based comfort index spanning religious, ethnic and class cues, so the construct is not bound to Islamic symbols alone and can be benchmarked against other field-experimental avoidance measures.

Added value to my researchAn objective, sensor-derived comfort index is a clean dependent variable for my explainable-ML work — I can attribute which streetscape/POI features drive approach vs. avoidance, linking perception to the spatial accessibility of welcoming public space.

③ Couple symbol exposure with measured segregation and mixing

Extending this paperThe paper treats neighbourhood visibility as the stimulus but does not connect it to the structural segregation of the surrounding city. Extending it: overlay the experimental routes and survey neighbourhoods onto measured socioeconomic/ethnic mixing, asking whether the place-attachment boost from visible Islam is amplified in mixed "rings/pockets" or muted in highly segregated enclaves — turning a person-level effect into a contextual, neighbourhood-conditioned one.

Added value to my researchThis bridges to my urban-analytics line on segregation and job–housing networks: I can use network community detection and spatial entropy to define the mixing context, then test exposure effects as a function of that structure.

⑤ Link place attachment to downstream health and accessibility outcomes

Extending this paperThe paper stops at place attachment and approach behaviour. Extending it: treat these as mediators toward downstream outcomes — does cue-driven approach translate into greater use of nearby services, more walking, or better mental-health indicators for immigrant populations? Pairing the AR/GPS protocol with health and service-use measures would test whether symbolic inclusion has measurable public-health payoffs, much as inequality-in-amenity studies link environment to well-being.
